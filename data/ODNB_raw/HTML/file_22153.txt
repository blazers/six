<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Phillips,  George Searle</span> <span class="st"> [<em>pseud. </em>January Searle]</span>  (1816&#150;1889), <span class="occ">journalist and writer</span>, was born in Peterborough in January 1816, and educated at Trinity College, Cambridge, although he does not appear to have taken a degree. He moved to the United States, where he worked on the staff of the <span class="italic">New York World</span> and the <span class="italic">New York Herald</span>. On returning to England in 1837 he married and settled in Sturton, Lincolnshire, where he began a career as a lecturer. He had one son. In 1844 he moved to Leeds, and in 1845 undertook the editorship of the <span class="italic">Leeds Times</span>. The following year he was appointed principal lecturer of the People's College at Huddersfield, and in 1854 was made lecturer to the Yorkshire Union of Mechanics' Institutes and Literary Societies. During this time Phillips also edited the <span class="italic">Truth Seeker</span> with F. R. Lees. He was acquainted with most of the naturalists and literati of the north of England; among his works published under the pseudonym January Searle are <span class="italic">Chapters in the History of a Life</span> (1850), <span class="italic">Leaves from Sherwood Forest</span> (1851), <span class="italic">Country Sketchbooks of Pastoral Scenes</span> (1851), <span class="italic">The Life, Character, and Genius of Ebenezer Elliott, the Corn-Law Rhymer</span> (1851), a <span class="italic">Memoir of William Wordsworth</span> (1852), and some discourses on the countryside.<br>
<br>In 1860 Phillips returned to the United States, where he worked with Charles A. Dana on the <span class="italic">Chicago Tribune</span>. He later moved with Dana to become literary editor of the <span class="italic">New York Sun</span>. In May 1873, suffering from partial dementia, he was confined to the Trenton Insane Asylum in New Jersey. He was moved to the Morristown Asylum, New Jersey, in April 1876, where he stayed until his death on 14 January 1889. His wife had also spent several years in a lunatic asylum in Wakefield.<br>
<br>A &#145;prolific and graceful writer&#146;, Phillips was also the author of a novel, <span class="italic">Gypsies of the Danes' Dyke</span> (1864), and edited, among other books, <span class="italic">The Memorials of Pel. Verjuice</span>, by Charles Reece Pemberton. He also published various pamphlets and verse, and was a voluminous contributor to periodical literature.<br>
<br>A. F. Pollard<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Victoria Millar </div>
