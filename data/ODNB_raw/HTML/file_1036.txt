<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Bagnall,  Gibbons</span>  (1719&#150;1800), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman and poet</span>, was the only son of Gibbons Bagnall (1688&#150;1743) of Windsor, vintner, and his first wife, Elizabeth Harries. He was admitted to Balliol College, Oxford, on 12 July 1738, and gained his BA on 30 April 1741. He afterwards went to King's College, Cambridge, where he took his MA in 1760. After taking holy orders Bagnall became vicar of Holm Lacy in Herefordshire, and headmaster of the free school at Hereford. He had married Sarah Reeve of Windsor in 1757.<br>
<br>Bagnall was collated on 27 May 1760 to the prebend of Piona Parva in Hereford Cathedral, and on 1 August 1767 to the prebend of Barsham in the same cathedral establishment. He also held for some time the rectory of Upton Bishop, Herefordshire; and in 1783 he was presented to the nearby vicarage of Sellack. He died on 31 December 1800, in his eighty-second year.<br>
<br>Bagnall's most important published works were in verse. The first was <span class="italic">Education: an Essay</span> (1765). A more ambitious literary work was a verse translation, in two volumes, of F. de Salignac de La Mothe F&eacute;nelon's account of the adventures of Telemachus, son of Ulysses. However, this failed to capture the &#145;cadenced prose&#146; of F&eacute;nelon, according to a contemporary critic  (<span class="italic">A New Catalogue</span>, 1799).<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Robert Brown </div>
