<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Quesnel <span class="st"> [Quesuel]</span>,  Peter</span>  (<em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>1320), <span class="occ">Franciscan friar, canonist, and pastoral theologian</span>, is known only through his popular handbook, <span class="italic">Directorium iuris in foro conscientiae et juridiciali</span>, where he is referred to in at least one early manuscript as a Franciscan friar. Bale states that a copy of his work was in the library of Norwich Cathedral priory, on the basis of which he was later believed to be a Franciscan of Norwich, but without supporting evidence. The identification of Quesnel with John the Canon (Juan Marbres, canon of Tortosa), made in the colophons of the first and second books of the first edition of the <span class="italic">Quaestiones super physica</span> of Canonicus (Padua, 1475), is without foundation. The facts that he used the <span class="italic">Summa confessorum</span> of Johann von Freiburg, written about 1300, and that his book was in circulation in England by about 1350, when Master Simon Bredon (<i>d</i>. 1372) had a copy (now MS 223 in the library of Merton College, Oxford), show that he must have been at work about 1320. The work was intended to be a practical guide to the administration of the sacraments in the light of up-to-date canon law, and in most manuscripts was accompanied by a full index. Besides Johann von Freiburg's work, Quesnel made extensive use of that of the thirteenth-century canonist Ram&oacute;n de Pe&ntilde;afort. In the fifteenth century, his work became popular throughout Europe; it survives in at least sixteen manuscripts, and was known to the Franciscan bibliographers in five more in Spain, Italy, France, Germany, and Bohemia.<br>
<br>Jeremy Catto </div>
