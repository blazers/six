<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Fairholt,  Frederick William</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1813, <em>d.</em> 1866), <span class="occ">artist and antiquary</span>, was born in London and baptized on 18 July 1813 at St Anne's, Soho, the sixteenth and only surviving child of a Prussian immigrant, whose name, Fahrholz, was Anglicized when he settled in London, and his wife, Sarah, the daughter of a Spitalfields silk weaver of Scottish origin named Dugweel or Dugwell. Initially Fairholt worked in his father's trade as a tobacco manufacturer. He developed an early interest in literature and drama and, from the age of twelve, received regular drawing lessons. Intrigued by woodcuts in books of border ballads and Scottish legends, he contributed his first wood-engraving to <span class="italic">The Mirror</span>. In 1831 he won the silver Isis medal of the Society of Arts for a watercolour copy of a landscape drawing. He became a drawing teacher, though he also gained employment as an assistant scene-painter. Illness led him to print colouring, for which he earned 10<i>s</i>. a week. John Jackson employed him to copy Hogarth prints for &pound;1 a week. In 1835 he became an assistant to the wood-engraver Stephen Sly (earning &pound;3 to &pound;6 weekly), through whom he began to draw and engrave for Charles Knight's publications, notably the <span class="italic">Penny Magazine</span> and <span class="italic">London</span>.<br>
<br>Fairholt's first major commission was for around 200 drawings on wood for J. Jackson and W. A. Chatto's <span class="italic">Treatise on Wood Engraving</span> (1839). From 1839 he was employed as artist and writer for the <span class="italic">Art Union</span> (from 1848 the <span class="italic">Art Journal</span>), for which he served as an assistant editor under Samuel Carter Hall. His fidelity as a draughtsman and his antiquarian knowledge, particularly of medieval art and customs, were much in demand for the illustration of learned publications. He drew and wrote regularly for the Society of Antiquaries (of which he was elected fellow in 1844), the British Archaeological Association (member), and the Numismatic Society (elected member in 1845). His illustrated antiquarian works include Charles Roach Smith's <span class="italic">The Antiquities of Richborough</span> (1850), Thomas Wright's <span class="italic">Archaeological Album</span> (1845), and <span class="italic">Miscellanea graphica</span> (1854&#150;7) by Lord Londesborough, whom he accompanied to southern France and Rome in 1856 and later to Egypt. Among Fairholt's best-known works as author and illustrator are <span class="italic">The Lord Mayors' Pageants</span> (1843&#150;4) and <span class="italic">Costume in England</span> (1846), and, as editor, <span class="italic">A Dictionary of Terms in Art</span> (1854). His dexterity enabled him to etch, coins especially, direct on the plate without preliminary drawing.<br>
<br>A methodical worker, Fairholt signed his engravings &#145;F. W. F.&#146; Kind, unassuming, and companionable, he had a wry sense of humour that was reflected in his caricature drawing. Since he strongly disliked the country, he spent his busy life chiefly in London, where he died from tubercular consumption on 3 April 1866, at 22 Montpelier Square, Brompton Road, Kensington. He was buried in Brompton cemetery. He bequeathed his Shakespearian collection to Shakespeare's birthplace in Stratford upon Avon and other material to the Society of Antiquaries and the British Museum.<br>
<br>Joanna Selborne </div>
