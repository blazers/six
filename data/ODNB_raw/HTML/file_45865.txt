<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Vardill <span class="st"> [<em>married name </em>Niven]</span>,  Anna Jane</span>  (1781&#150;1852), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was born on 19 November 1781 in London, the only child of the Revd Dr John Vardill (1749&#150;1811) and his wife (<i>d</i>. 1826). Her father, who was born in New York state, moved to England in 1774. He was a loyalist spy, a professor, and a clergyman who served as rector of Skirbeck and Fishtoft in Lincolnshire, from 1791 until his death in 1811. He wrote at least one play, <span class="italic">The Unknown</span>, which was performed at the Surrey Theatre in 1819.<br>
<br>Vardill grew up in Galloway, London, and Lincolnshire. Her father provided the strongest influence on her education. In the preface to her first published work, <span class="italic">Poems and translations, from the minor Greek poets and others, written chiefly between the ages of ten and sixteen, by a lady</span> (1809), she states that &#145;[a] most indulgent father &hellip; found amusement in familiarizing his only child with the Poets of Antiquity&#146;  (Vardill, iv). This first published volume went to three editions. It contains precocious translations of Anacreon, Sappho, Theocritus, Horace, and others, in addition to original works such as &#145;The rights of woman, a burlesque essay&#146;, in which Vardill cites a wide range of supposed authorities on the subject and satirizes both sexes before concluding that woman's role should be that of prime minister to man's king.<br>
<br>Vardill published <span class="italic">The Pleasures of Human Life: a Poem</span>, in 1812. Like much of her work, this poem is an effective imitation of a particular style&#151;Augustan, in this case&#151;and a tribute to her father, who had died the previous year. From 1813 to 1822, as &#145;V&#146;, she produced a number of works in the style of various Romantic poets, including Walter Scott, Robert Southey, and Lord Byron, for the <span class="italic">European Magazine</span>. Her most famous contribution to the magazine was a continuation of Samuel Taylor Coleridge's &#145;Christabel&#146;, published in April 1815, over a year before Coleridge's original appeared in print. She had heard the poem read aloud in December 1814 by Henry Crabb Robinson, a family friend who later became her mother's executor. &#145;Christobell&#146; demonstrates Vardill's remarkable ability to imitate contemporary poetic forms, allowing the previously inaccessible Romantic idiom to filter into popular literary culture.<br>
<br>After her marriage to James Niven (<i>d</i>. 1830) in 1822, Vardill moved from London to his estate at Kirkcudbright in Scotland. Their only child, Agnes, was born in 1825. After her husband's death Vardill returned to England, where she divided her time between London and Skipton until her death in Skipton on 4 June 1852.<br>
<br>Megan A. Stephan </div>
