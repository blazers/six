<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Close,  John</span> <span class="st"> [<em>called </em>Poet Close]</span>  (1816&#150;1891), <span class="occ">writer of doggerel</span>, was born at Gunnerside, Swaledale, Yorkshire, and baptized there on 11 August 1816. He was the son of Jarvis Close (<i>d</i>. 1853), a butcher and Wesleyan preacher, and his wife, Elizabeth. While assisting his father in the butchery trade, Close began to issue verse-tracts and fly-sheets in the 1830s, including <span class="italic">The Satirist, or, Every Man in his Humour</span> (1833). In 1846 he established himself as a printer in Kirkby Stephen, and <span class="italic">The Poetical Works &hellip; of John Close</span> appeared in 1860.<br>
<br>Close's assiduity in writing verse about people and affairs in his neighbourhood attracted the patronage of the local nobility, despite his lack of any real literary talent. Owing to the support of Lord Carlisle, Lord Lonsdale, and other gentlemen, in April 1861 Close was awarded a civil-list pension of &pound;50 on the recommendation of Lord Palmerston. The award provoked a question in the House of Commons  (<span class="italic">Hansard 3</span>, 162, 2 May 1861, 1375&#150;6), and much amused comment in the London press; on 3 June 1861 it was cancelled, and by way of compensation Close received &pound;100 from the Royal Bounty Fund.<br>
<br>Close was married, and had a daughter and two sons. After the events of 1861 he continued to issue &#145;pamphlets of metrical balderdash, interspersed with documents relating to his wrongs&#146;  (<span class="italic">DNB</span>), selling them from a bookstall at Bowness-on-Windermere. Among his subsequent works were <span class="italic">The Wise Man of Stainmore</span> (1864) and <span class="italic">Bowness Church Bells and other Poems</span> (1872). He died at Kirkby Stephen, Westmorland, on 15 February 1891, and was buried in the local cemetery. He is referred to as Poet Close in &#145;Ferdinando and Elvira, or, The Gentle Pieman&#146;, one of W. S. Gilbert's <span class="italic">Bab Ballads</span> (1869).<br>
<br>Douglas Brown </div>
