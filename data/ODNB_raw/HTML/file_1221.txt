<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ball,  Peter</span>  (<em>c.</em>1638&#150;1675), <span class="occ">physician</span>, was the third son and one of the seventeen children of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/58098/', "Sir Peter Ball (bap. 1598, d. 1680)")</script>, lawyer, and his wife, Ann, daughter of Sir William Cooke, of Gloucestershire. The astronomer <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/1224/', "William Ball")</script> was his elder brother. Peter was admitted to the Middle Temple, London, in 1652 and called to the bar in 1657. In 1659, at the age of twenty, he was entered as a medical student at Leiden, but proceeded to Padua, where he took the degree of doctor of philosophy and physic in 1660. To celebrate this feat, a series of laudatory verses entitled <span class="italic">Apollinare sacrum</span> (1660) was published in Padua.<br>
<br>Ball was admitted as an honorary fellow of the College of Physicians in London in December 1664. He was elected a fellow of the Royal Society in 1663, and was one of the council in 1664, 1666, and 1669. In 1667 he became a member of the committee charged with producing a catalogue of the library and manuscripts of Arundel House. While at his father's residence, Mamhead House in Devon, in October 1665, Ball, together with his brother William, made the observation of Saturn mentioned under William Ball. Ball died in July 1675; he was buried on the 20th of that month in the round of the Temple Church.<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Michael Bevan </div>
