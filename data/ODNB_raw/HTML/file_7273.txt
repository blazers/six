<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Davies,  William</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1820), <span class="occ">bookseller</span>, of obscure origins, was assistant to the elder <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/4302/', "Thomas Cadell (1742&#150;1802)")</script>, bookseller and publisher, when he was chosen by him in 1793 as a partner for his youthful son <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/4303/', "Thomas Cadell the younger (1773&#150;1836)")</script> [<i>see under </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/4302/', "Cadell, Thomas")</script>] in the management of his business. From that time the business traded as Cadell and Davies, and Cadell the younger left the management of the business to his partner until Davies fell ill in 1813. During these years the firm continued its reputation for generosity to authors. Regardless of G. Steevens's comment to Bishop Percy in 1797 that Cadell and Davies, &#145;in spite of all their boasts, are not allowed to be at the head of their trade in the line of publication&#146;  (Nichols, <span class="italic">Illustrations</span>, 7.30), the business seems to have been thriving at the end of the century. In 1800 the United States congress commissioned Cadell and Davies to supply 5000 dollars' worth of books, journals, and maps which would form the basis for the Library of Congress. The firm continued to publish Burns's poetical works, as well as Boswell's <span class="italic">Life of Johnson</span>, although in 1797 they declined the opportunity to publish what became Jane Austen's <span class="italic">Pride and Prejudice</span>. A contemporary bookseller noted in the <span class="italic">Aldine Magazine</span> that while some in the book trade found Davies somewhat &#145;haughty&#146;, he was in truth a &#145;straightforward man of business&#146; who was &#145;possessed of superior abilities&#146;, although he later &#145;became too adventurous and liberal in his literary purchases&#146;  (Besterman, xi). Davies was a yeomanry stockkeeper of the Stationers' Company in 1800 and 1803 and a livery stockkeeper in 1812 and 1818. He and his wife, Jessy 
    (or Jesse; 1777/8&#150;1854)
  , had eight children. He died on 28 April 1820, leaving his family less well provided for than might have been expected. His wife died at Bushey, Hertfordshire on 14 October 1854.<br>
<br>H. R. Tedder<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Catherine Dille </div>
