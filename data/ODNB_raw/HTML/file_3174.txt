<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Bradfield,  Henry Joseph Steele</span>  (1805&#150;1852), <span class="occ">colonial official and author</span>, was born on 18 May 1805 in Derby Street, Westminster, London, where his father, Thomas Bradfield, was a coal merchant. In 1825 he published <span class="italic">Waterloo, or, The British Minstrel: a Poem</span>. He had trained as a surgeon, and on 26 April 1826 left England aboard the schooner <i>Unicorn</i> with Lord Cochrane's expedition to Greece, where he took part in several engagements on land and sea. After his return he published <span class="italic">The Athenaid, or, Modern Grecians, a Poem</span> (1830) and <span class="italic">Tales of the Cyclades, Poems</span> (1830), and in 1839 he edited a work entitled <span class="italic">A Russian's Reply to the Marquis de Custine's &#145;Russia&#146;</span>.<br>
<br>On 1 September 1832 Bradfield received a commission as <i>sous-lieutenant</i> in the Belgian <i>Bataillon &eacute;tranger</i> and was appointed to the 1st regiment of lancers. At one time he held a commission in the Royal West Middlesex militia. He was appointed stipendiary magistrate in Tobago on 31 December 1835, and was transferred to Trinidad on 13 May 1836. He was reappointed to the southern or Cedros district on 13 April 1839, but soon returned to England following a quarrel with another colonial officer. In 1841 he returned to the West Indies as private secretary to Colonel Macdonald, lieutenant-governor of Dominica, and in 1842 he acted as colonial secretary in Barbados. Charges connected with the earlier quarrel were, however, renewed, and the government cancelled his appointment.<br>
<br>From then on Bradfield lived very precariously, drawing on his moderate literary talents: among some contributions he made to the <span class="italic">Gentleman's Magazine</span> were articles on &#145;The last of the Paleologi&#146; in January 1843, and a &#145;Memoir of Major-General Thomas Dundas and the expedition to Gaudaloupe&#146; later the same year. Latterly, he was almost a professional beggar; he committed suicide by swallowing prussic acid, and was found dead in the St Albans Hotel, Charles Street, Westminster, on 11 October 1852.<br>
<br>G. C. Boase<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Lynn Milne </div>
