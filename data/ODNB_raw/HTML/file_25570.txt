<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Freine,  Simund  de</span> <span class="st"> [Simon  de Fraxino]</span>  (<em>d.</em> before 1228?), <span class="occ">poet</span>, from a family connected with Sutton Freen, Herefordshire, was a canon of Hereford by <i>c</i>.1190 and bore the title <i>magister</i>. Two poems were addressed by him to Gerald of Wales in, perhaps, 1194&#150;7 (extant in LPL, MS 236, folios 166&#150;166b; the texts are also in BL, Cotton MS Vitellius E.v (sixteenth-century), and Cambridge, Corpus Christi College, MS 400, page 119, which give thirty-one lines omitted in the Lambeth manuscript). One poem urges Gerald to stay in Hereford, commending the extensive studies of its cathedral school; one warmly defends him against Abbot Adam of Dore's criticism. Simund wrote two substantial Anglo-Norman poems in heptasyllabic lines. His adaptation of Boethius's <span class="italic">Consolation of Philosophy</span>, <span class="italic">Le roman de philosophie</span>, is extant in BL, Royal MS 20 B.xiv, folios 68<i>v</i>&#150;77<i>v</i>; Bodl. Oxf., MS Douce 210, folios 51<i>v</i>&#150;59<i>v</i>; and BL, Add. MS 46919, folios 107&#150;16. Simund's life of St George, <span class="italic">La vie de Saint Georges</span>  (Paris, Biblioth&egrave;que Nationale, MS Fr. 902, fols. 108&#150;17), was perhaps written for Archbishop Baldwin's preaching of the third crusade in 1188. Both poems include Simund's acrostic signature in their first twenty lines. They were probably written under the patronage of Bishop William de Vere who may be the provenance of extra information in the anti-Muslim <span class="italic">Georges</span>. If so, any further writings by Simund between de Vere's death in 1198 and his own, implicit in a charter datable to 1224&times;8, are unknown.<br>
<br>Jocelyn Wogan-Browne </div>
