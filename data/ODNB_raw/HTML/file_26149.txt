<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Spiers,  Alexander</span>  (1807&#150;1869), <span class="occ">lexicographer</span>, was born at Gosport in Hampshire, the son of Isaac Spiers, merchant. He studied in England, in Germany, and in Paris. On the advice of the poet Andrieux (<i>d</i>. 1833), he settled in Paris as a professor of English, and found employment at the &Eacute;cole de Commerce, at the &Eacute;cole des Ponts et Chauss&eacute;es, at the &Eacute;cole des Mines, and at the Lyc&eacute;e Bonaparte. He spent fourteen years on his <span class="italic">General English and French dictionary, newly composed from the English dictionaries of Johnson, Webster, Richardson, &amp;c., and from the French dictionaries of the French Academy, of Laveaux, Boiste, &amp;c.</span> (1846). He submitted it to the University of Giessen, which awarded him the degree of PhD in 1849. It proved superior to its predecessors and was at once &#145;autoris&eacute; par le conseil de l'instruction publique&#146;, on 3 July 1846. The twenty-ninth edition, in two volumes, appeared in 1884 (remodelled by H. Witcomb, Spiers's successor at the &Eacute;cole des Ponts et Chauss&eacute;es). An abridgement, under the title of <span class="italic">Dictionnaire abr&eacute;g&eacute; anglais&#150;fran&ccedil;ais et fran&ccedil;ais&#150;anglais, abr&eacute;g&eacute; du dictionnaire g&eacute;n&eacute;ral de M. Spiers</span>, was published in 1851 and supplied to almost every school and <i>lyc&eacute;e</i> in France. He married, on 29 September 1853 at St Giles, Camberwell, Victoire Dawes (1826&#150;1885), daughter of John Newman, architect. They had five sons.<br>
<br>In November 1857 Spiers brought an action against L&eacute;on Contanseau and his publishers, Longmans &amp; Co., for pirating his dictionaries in a work entitled <span class="italic">A Practical Dictionary of the French and English Languages</span>. However, Vice-Chancellor Sir William Page Wood (afterwards Lord Hatherley), in his decision on 25 February 1858, maintained that, though great use of Spiers's books had been made without due acknowledgement, as such publications could not be entirely original a charge of piracy could not be sustained (<span class="italic">Weekly Reporter</span>, 1857&#150;8, 352&#150;4; <span class="italic">The Times</span>). Spiers's other publications included a <span class="italic">Manual of Commercial Terms in English and French</span> (1846), a <span class="italic">Study of the English Prose Writers, Sacred and Profane</span> (1852), and a <span class="italic">Study of English poetry, a choice collection of the finest pieces of the poets of Great Britain</span> (1855). They were all issued in both English and French editions in London, Paris, and America. Spiers also printed and edited for French students Sheridan's <span class="italic">School for Scandal</span> and <span class="italic">The Essays of F. Bacon, Viscount St. Albans</span> (1851).<br>
<br>Spiers was nominated an <i>agr&eacute;g&eacute; de l'universit&eacute;</i>, <i>officier de l'instruction publique</i>, <i>examinateur &agrave; la Sorbonne</i>, and <i>inspecteur g&eacute;n&eacute;ral de l'universit&eacute;</i>. He received the cross of the L&eacute;gion d'honneur from Napoleon III. He died at the rue de la Faisanderie, avenue de l'Imperatrice, Paris, on 27 August 1869.<br>
<br>G. C. Boase<span class="italic">, rev. </span> John D. Haigh </div>
