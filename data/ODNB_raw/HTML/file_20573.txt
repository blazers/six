<div class="para">
<span class="headword">&Oacute; Fithcheallaigh,  Muiris</span> <span class="st"> [Maurice O'Fihely, Mauritius  de Portu]</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1513), <span class="occ">theologian and archbishop of Tuam</span>, was a native either of Clonfert, co. Galway, or of Baltimore, co. Cork, and became a conventual Franciscan friar about 1475. He subsequently studied at Oxford. The chapter at Cremona named him regent of studies at the Franciscan school in Milan in 1488. He became regent master of theology at Padua in 1491, and continued to lecture publicly on Scotistic theology there until at least 1505. He took part in the deposition of the minister-general, &AElig;gidius Delphinus, in the first general chapter of the conventuals, held at Rome in 1506. On 26 June in the same year he was provided to the archbishopric of Tuam by Pope Julius II, who consecrated him in Rome. He continued to reside in Italy, and was present at the Fifth Lateran Council in 1512, signing the acts of the first two sessions. At length he departed for Ireland, but died at Galway on 25 May 1513, and was buried at the Franciscan friary there.<br>
<br>&Oacute; Fithcheallaigh is chiefly known as the editor of many of the works of Duns Scotus. He edited, with omissions, chapter headings, expansions, and explanatory notes, the following treatises attributed to the Doctor Subtilis: <span class="italic">De primo principio</span>; <span class="italic">Theoremata</span>; <span class="italic">Expositio in XII libros Metaphysicorum</span>; <span class="italic">Quaestiones in Metaphysicam Aristotelis</span>; <span class="italic">Commentarium in libros I et II sententiarum</span>; <span class="italic">De formalitatibus</span>; and <span class="italic">Collationes</span>. These were published between 1497 and 1517. Although his additions to Scotus's own words make his editions at times problematic, his explanatory notes are often very helpful, since Scotus himself is frequently unclear. &Oacute; Fithcheallaigh was not only an editor, but also an expositor, critic, and author. Among his expositions, published between 1497 and 1519, are <span class="italic">Annotationes in quaestiones metaphysicales Scoti</span>; <span class="italic">Expositio in quaestiones metaphysicales Scoti</span>; and <span class="italic">Expositio quaestionum Doctoris Subtilis in quinque universalia Porphyrii</span> (or <span class="italic">Expositio in quaestiones dialecticas J. Duns Scoti</span>). He wrote a short treatise entitled <span class="italic">Enchyridion fidei</span> (or <span class="italic">De rerum contingentia et divina predestinatione</span>), dedicated to Gerald Fitzgerald, eighth earl of Kildare (<i>d</i>. 1513), which was published at Venice in 1505. He also produced, while lecturing at Padua, a version in hexameters of the four books of the <span class="italic">Sentences</span>, called <span class="italic">Compendium veritatum</span> (Venice, 1505). Extending himself beyond the works of Scotus, &Oacute; Fithcheallaigh also edited the <span class="italic">Expositio in Metaphysicam</span> of Antonius Andreae, and began an edition of the works of Fran&ccedil;ois de Meyronnes (finally published in 1520). The <span class="italic">Distinctiones ordine alphabetico</span> sometimes attributed to him were the work of a Friar Maurice of the thirteenth century.<br>
<br>A relative, <a name="cosubject_20572"></a><span class="headword">Domhnall &Oacute; Fithcheallaigh</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1505)<span class="occ"></span>, was the author of some annals in Gaelic, dedicated to Florence O'Mahony. These &#145;Irish Annals&#146; were seen in manuscript by Sir James Ware in London in 1626, but are now lost.<br>
<br>S. F. Brown </div>
