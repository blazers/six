<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Wratislaw,  Theodore William Graf</span>  (1871&#150;1933), <span class="occ">poet and civil servant</span>, was born on 27 April 1871 in Rugby, Warwickshire, the son of Theodore Marc Wratislaw (1831&#150;1919), solicitor, and Sarah Townsend. Educated at Rugby School from 1885 to 1888, he went into his father's office but loved poetry rather than the law, though in 1893 he passed his solicitors' finals. He published two slim volumes at his own expense in Rugby in 1892 and moved to London to mix (uncommittedly) in the &#145;Uranian&#146; circle centring on the <span class="italic">Artist and Journal of Home Culture</span>. <span class="italic">Caprices</span> (1893), with dedicatees such as Charles Kains Jackson, Lord Alfred Douglas, Oscar Wilde, and Gleeson White, and poems such as &#145;To a Sicilian Boy&#146; (replaced at proof on the objection of a journalist), associates his name with decadent ideals, but three years later the 120 copies of the edition had not sold out.<br>
<br>Wratislaw returned to his father's office but continued to contribute to the <span class="italic">Yellow Book</span> and <span class="italic">The Savoy</span>, and published <span class="italic">Orchids</span> in 1896, again in a limited edition. In 1895 he entered the estates office at Somerset House, London, where he did what he described in a 1914 letter as &#145;penal servitude&#146; until December 1930  (unpubd letter, 3 March 1914). On 20 September 1899 he married Sara Caroline Ester Harris (<i>b</i>. 1875/6), daughter of Elias Harris, a stock- and sharebroker from Cape Town, Cape Colony. Sara died on 22 September 1901, and on 9 April 1908 he married Theodora Russell (<i>b</i>. 1875/6), a widow, the daughter of Robert Percival Banks, the former vicar of Drumbeg, Sutherland. They divorced in 1913, and on 19 May 1915 he married Ada Ross (1884/5&#150;1942), daughter of Thomas Philip Ross, an insurance manager. The daughter of his first marriage, Isolde Eleanor Josephine Wratislaw (1901&#150;1984), worked in the consular service in Italy, ending her career as British consul-general in Palermo and Milan.<br>
<br>Wratislaw lived long enough to see his inclusion by A. J. A. Symons in <span class="italic">An Anthology of 'Nineties Verse</span> (1928). Perhaps too close in style and subject to Arthur Symons to achieve a truly independent voice, he has remained an essential component of the picture of 1890s poetry. His yearning for literature remained until his death, at his home, York Lodge, Ashley Road, Walton-on-Thames, Surrey, on 13 September 1933. A correspondent in <span class="italic">The Times</span> of 18 September 1933 commented that &#145;the beautiful voice that lent added grace to his poems is silent as the night&#146;.<br>
<br>R. K. R. Thornton </div>
