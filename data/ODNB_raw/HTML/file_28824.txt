<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Watkins,  Charles Frederick</span>  (1795&#150;1873), <span class="occ">writer and Church of England clergyman</span>, was born on 16 January 1795 at Corsley, Wiltshire, the youngest son of the Revd William R. Watkins of Court Coleman and rector of Port Eynon, Glamorgan. He was educated at Christ's Hospital, London, and in 1810 he joined the frigate <i>Hotspur</i> as midshipman and was engaged in teaching the midshipmen mathematics and natural science, but left the service at the peace of 1815. He entered Christ's College, Cambridge, as a &#145;ten-year man&#146; on 29 January 1818 and was ordained as a literate; although he was admitted for holy orders, he never took the BD degree. After serving curacies at Downton, Wiltshire (1818&#150;19), and Windsor (1820&#150;21), he was appointed in 1822 as warden of Farley Hospital, Salisbury, where he stayed until 1832. During this time (1822&#150;32) he also held the post of curate at West Grimstead and Plaitford, Wiltshire.<br>
<br>Watkins was interested in geology and formed a collection of cretaceous fossils, some of which are in museums at Oxford and Cambridge and in the British Museum, London. In April 1832 he became vicar of Brixworth, Northamptonshire, and held this office until his death. While living there he communicated to the Royal Society an <span class="italic">Account of Aurora borealis of 17 Nov 1848</span>  (<span class="italic">PRS</span>, 5 1843&#150;50, 809). In keeping with his scientific interests he also wrote <span class="italic">A Treatise on the Leading Causes of Pleasure and Delight in the Human Mind</span> (1841) and <span class="italic">A Scientific and General Vindication of the Mosaic History of the Creation</span> (1867). He also demonstrated an attention to social issues and published various prose pamphlets, including <span class="italic">An address to the labouring population, on the folly and wickedness of burning agricultural property</span> (1831). His works of poetry include <span class="italic">Sacred Poems</span> (1829), <span class="italic">The Human Hand, and other Poems</span> (1852), and <span class="italic">The Twins of Fame, or, Wellington and Buonaparte: a National Poem</span> (1854). Watkins died at Brixworth on 15 July 1873 and was survived by his wife, Elizabeth, and at least one son, Justinian Charles Secundus Watkins (<i>b</i>. 1845).<br>
<br>T. G. Bonney<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Rebecca Mills </div>
