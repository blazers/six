<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Plarr,  Victor Gustave</span>  (1863&#150;1929), <span class="occ">poet and librarian</span>, was born on 21 June 1863 at Le Kupferhammer, near Strasbourg, the only son of Dr Gustavus Plarr (<i>d</i>. 1892), a distinguished Alsatian mathematician and foreign corresponding member of the British Association from 1857 to 1892, and Mary Jane Tomkins, who was English. The family moved to Scotland after their home was burnt in the Franco-Prussian war of 1870&#150;71. Plarr was educated at Madras College, St Andrews, Fife, at Tonbridge School, Kent, and at Worcester College, Oxford, where he took a second in modern history in 1886 and proceeded MA in 1897.<br>
<br>He spent four years as literary journalist and translator before becoming a librarian, from 1890 at King's College, London, and from 1897 until his death at the Royal College of Surgeons; it is here that Ezra Pound memorably pictures him as Monsieur Verog &#145;Among the pickled foetuses and bottled bones&#146; in his poem <span class="italic">Hugh Selwyn Mauberley</span>  (Pound, 202). On 10 August 1892 he married Helen Marion (1866/7&#150;1930), daughter of Albert Shaw, with whom he had a daughter, Marion, in 1893.<br>
<br>Plarr's poetic and professional publications indicate the balance of his life. He published in both books of the <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/theme/95480', "Rhymers' Club")</script> in 1892 and 1894 and brought out his major collection of poetry, <span class="italic">In the Dorian Mood</span>, in 1896. He published <span class="italic">The Tragedy of Asgard</span> in 1905, and with Pound's encouragement contributed to the second and third books of the Poets' Club in 1911 and 1913.<br>
<br>Plarr edited the fourteenth and fifteenth editions (1895 and 1899) of <span class="italic">Men and Women of the Time</span>, a predecessor of <span class="italic">Who's Who</span>, and continued to publish occasional poems and essays while engaged on catalogues and lists connected with his professional work, notably his <span class="italic">Lives of the Fellows of the Royal College of Surgeons</span> (published posthumously in 1930). In 1914 he was the eccentric memorialist of his friend Ernest Dowson. He is remembered as charming, stooping, bald, and usually smoking. He died of bronchitis at his home, 7 Laurel Road, Wimbledon, Surrey, on 28 January 1929, and was buried in Hampstead cemetery.<br>
<br>R. K. R. Thornton </div>
