<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ranken,  Alexander</span>  (1755&#150;1827), <span class="occ">Church of Scotland minister and author</span>, was born in Edinburgh on 28 February 1755. At the age of fifteen he entered the university of his native city and after graduating in arts began to study divinity in 1775. On 28 April 1779 he was licensed to preach, and in the same year became assistant to the pastor of St Cuthbert's, Edinburgh. He remained there two years, when, on 17 August 1781, he was appointed minister of the parish of Cambusnethan, Lanarkshire. On 12 November 1782 he married Euphemia Thomson (<i>d</i>. 1822); they had two children, Margaret (1783&#150;1786) and Andrew (1785&#150;1851). In July 1785, he was invited by the provost and magistrates of Glasgow to become minister of St David's in the town. There he received praise for the insight, chasteness, and simplicity of his sermons. In April 1801 Glasgow University gave him the degree of DD.<br>
<br>Besides topical homilies and memorial sermons for members of the royal family, Ranken was chiefly known for two published works, <span class="italic">Institutes of Theology</span> (1822), and especially his nine-volume <span class="italic">History of France from the Time of its Conquest by Clovis to the Death of Louis XVI</span> (1802&#150;22). The <span class="italic">History</span> is organized thematically, with separate chapters on topics such as the arts, laws, and political affairs. The first three volumes received strong but fair criticism from Henry Hallam in the <span class="italic">Edinburgh Review</span> for the arrangement and Ranken's poor understanding of his Latin sources, as well as for being &#145;shamefully destitute of references&#146;  (<span class="italic">EdinR</span>, 6.220). Marking his prominence in the kirk, in 1811 Ranken was appointed moderator of the general assembly of the Church of Scotland. He died in Glasgow on 23 February 1827 and was buried there.<br>
<br>Michael Kugler </div>
