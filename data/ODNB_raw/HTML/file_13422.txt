<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hodges,  Edward Richmond <span class="st"> [Edward Richard]</span></span>  (1825?&#150;1881), <span class="occ">orientalist</span>, was probably the person of that name baptized on 2 October 1825 at St Mary Magdalen, Bermondsey, London, the son of Edward and Ann Hodges. While a London apprentice, he became a student of Hebrew, and, after being for a short time a scripture reader, was sent as a missionary by the London Society for Promoting Christianity amongst the Jews, first to Palestine, and afterwards to Algeria (until 1856). A few years later he left the society, and for some time he acted as a minister of the Reformed Episcopal church.<br>
<br>Hodges was best-known as a scholar and teacher of oriental languages, and assisted George Smith (1840&#150;1876) in his cuneiform researches. He wrote numerous magazine articles, and ten entries on Middle Eastern and Indian languages in the 1873 supplement to the arts and sciences division of Charles Knight's <span class="italic">English Cyclop&aelig;dia</span>. He was also a competent editor; he thoroughly revised, annotated, and updated Henry Craik's <span class="italic">Principia Hebraica</span> (2nd edn, 1863) and Isaac Cory's <span class="italic">Ancient Fragments of Ph&#339;nician &hellip; and other Authors</span> (new [3rd] edn, 1876), as well as W. J. Mickle's translation, <span class="italic">The Lusiad</span>, of Cam&otilde;es's Portuguese epic poem for Bohn's <span class="roman">Standard Library</span> (5th edn, 1877). At the time of his death he was preparing a translation of the Armenian history by Moses of Khorene and a study of the cult of Mithras.<br>
<br>Hodges died of chronic bronchitis and asthma at his home, 43 Regina Road, Finsbury Park, Middlesex, on 9 May 1881, aged fifty-five, leaving a widow and six children.<br>
<br>W. A. J. Archbold<span class="italic">, rev. </span> R. S. Simpson </div>
