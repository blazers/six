<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Paterson,  William</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1716&#150;1760), <span class="occ">colonial official and playwright</span>, was born in Scotland. In February 1716 he matriculated at the College of Edinburgh. After college he succeeded his brother James Paterson as master at Edinburgh grammar school before following his friend James Thomson to London. He became a member of the Society for the Encouragement of Learning in 1735, and a freemason in 1737.<br>
<br>Paterson's tragedy <span class="italic">Arminius</span>, whose hero and villain were thinly disguised portraits of, respectively, the prince of Wales and Walpole, was refused production by the lord chamberlain in 1740, but was printed in trade and subscription editions in that year. Patrick Murdoch, Thomson's biographer, claimed that Paterson was sometimes Thomson's amanuensis and that <span class="italic">Arminius</span> was banned unread because the submitted text was in the same hand as Thomson's already banned <span class="italic">Edward and Eleonora</span>. Thomson affectionately portrayed Paterson as a pensive, imaginative nature-lover in <span class="italic">The Castle of Indolence</span> (1748, I.lvii&#150;lix).<br>
<br>By 1742 Paterson was in the service of Lord Tweeddale, secretary of state for Scotland. When the secretaryship was abolished Paterson became surveyor-general of customs for Barbados and the Leeward Islands, salary &pound;400 p.a.; his warrant to start duty in Bridgetown, Barbados, is dated 29 May 1746. A letter by George Grenville, 3 September 1748, says that Paterson was appointed &#145;for Thomson's sake&#146;  (TNA: PRO Chatham MSS, 34). Murdoch claimed that Thomson was nominal holder of this post, procured for him by Lord Lyttelton.<br>
<br>Paterson's letters from Barbados to the board of customs advocate imperial expansion in the West Indies and, <i>inter alia</i>, offer shrewd economic analyses of the sugar and slave trades. He believed that prohibitions and high duties were &#145;the smugglers' harvest&#146;  (Bodl. Oxf., MS North A.6: 5 July 1751, 18 Dec 1758). His last reported visit to London was in April 1760. It is not known when he died.<br>
<br>James Sambrook </div>
