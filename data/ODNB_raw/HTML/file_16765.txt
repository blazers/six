<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lister,  Thomas</span>  (<em>c.</em>1559&#150;1628), <span class="occ">Jesuit</span>, was apparently a younger son of Christopher Lister of Midhope, Yorkshire, and Ellen, daughter and coheir of John Clayton of Clayton Hall, Leyland, Lancashire, where he may have been born. He entered the English College at Rome on 15 September 1579, joined the Society of Jesus on 20 February 1583, being fellow novice with Vitelleschi, afterwards general of the society, and graduated DTh at Pont-&agrave;-Mousson in 1592. Lister was sent to England, and worked with Edward Oldcorne in the Worcestershire district, based at Hindlip, the home of Thomas Habington. He apparently used the surname Butler as an alias. Because of Lister's claustrophobia and fear of priest's holes, the Jesuit superior, Henry Garnet, employed him as a socius. But Lister's nervous energy, violent headaches, excitability, and neuroses made him a difficult companion, unsuited to the clandestine English mission. He spent some time in Flanders, but was once again in England at the turn of the century. Garnet wrote that Lister's troubles stemmed not so much from &#145;weakness of character&#146; as from &#145;a disturbed mind and lack of responsibility&#146;  (Caraman, 233). On 3 June 1610 Lister was professed of the four vows. In 1621 he was superior of the Oxford district.<br>
<br>Lister was the author of &#145;Treatise of schism&#146;, in which he maintained that the appellant priests who refused to acknowledge the archpriest's jurisdiction were <i>ipso facto</i> deprived of their ecclesiastical powers, and ought to be treated as schismatics. This work, which caused much disquiet among the secular clergy, was extensively circulated in manuscript. It was first published in Christopher Bagshaw's <span class="italic">Relatio compendiosa</span> of 1601.<br>
<br>Lister died at Li&egrave;ge on 19 February 1628, and was buried there.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Mark Nicholls </div>
