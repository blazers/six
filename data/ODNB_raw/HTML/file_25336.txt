<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Shepherd,  Richard</span>  (1731/2&#150;1809), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman and theological writer</span>, was baptized on 10 October 1732 at Mareham-le-Fen, Lincolnshire, the son of Henry Shepherd (<i>d</i>. 1764), vicar of that parish, and his wife, Susanna. He matriculated from Corpus Christi College, Oxford, on 1 December 1749 at the age of seventeen. He graduated BA (1753), MA (1757), BD (1765), and DD (1788), and was elected probationary fellow of his college in 1760. After considering a military career he took orders in the Church of England and eventually was appointed chaplain to Thomas Thurlow, bishop of Lincoln and later of Durham; Thurlow's nomination gained Shepherd the archdeaconry of Bedford in July 1783. He was elected a fellow of the Royal Society in May 1781, and in 1788 he was Bampton lecturer at Oxford. In 1792 he was instituted to the rectory of Wetherden and Helmingham in Suffolk by Lord Chancellor Thurlow, brother of the bishop, and he held these preferments until his death.<br>
<br>Shepherd published a wide selection of writings, including poetry and drama, although he came to concentrate on theology. His poetry was not unsuccessful: his <span class="italic">Ode to Love</span> (1755?) was reprinted in 1760 and <span class="italic">The Nuptials</span> (1761) went into three editions. Most of his poetic and dramatic pieces were included in the two-volume <span class="italic">Miscellanies</span> (1776). Several of Shepherd's theological works went into two or more editions, including his response to Soame Jenyns in <span class="italic">The Review of a Free Enquiry</span> (1759), <span class="italic">The Requisition of Subscription to the Thirty-Nine Articles</span> (1771), and <span class="italic">Reflections on the Doctrine of Materialism</span> (1779), addressed to Joseph Priestley. Shepherd published regularly throughout his life; his last piece was <span class="italic">No False Alarm, or, A Sequel to Religious Union</span> (1808). He died at the parsonage at Wetherden on 3 January 1809. No details are known of his wife, with whom he had a son, Henry, born in 1792.<br>
<br>W. P. Courtney<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Emma Major </div>
