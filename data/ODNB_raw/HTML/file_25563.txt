<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Simmons,  Bartholomew</span> <span class="st"> [<em>pseud. </em>Harold]</span>  (1804&#150;1850), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was born at Kilworth, co. Cork, the eldest son of an English exciseman, and a Miss Luddy of Kilworth. He was educated at Birmingham School and Quigley's school, both in Cork. Simmons's first poems were published shortly after his twentieth birthday in <span class="italic">Bolster's Magazine</span>, to which he was a regular contributor during the next three years, during which time he also contributed, sometimes under the pseudonym Harold, to <span class="italic">Blackwood's Magazine</span>, which printed his most famous poem &#145;Napoleon's Last Look&#146;.<br>
<br>Simmons could not, however, live by his poetic gifts alone, and so in 1830 entered the excise branch of the civil service, a sinecure obtained with the help of his father's old landlord Lord Mountcaskell. But Simmons did not cease to write altogether, and was a key member of Lady Blessington's literary circle, whose collected poems were published in London in 1843.<br>
<br>Unmarried to the end of his life, Simmons died of cancer on 21 July 1850 at his lodgings in 29 Acton Street, Gray's Inn Road, London, his death hastened by the shock from an explosion that occurred while he was sailing up the Thames. He was buried in Highgate cemetery. Thanks to the repeated anthologization of his poem on Napoleon, and to Christopher North's eulogistic reference to his poetic gift in the <span class="italic">Noctes Ambrosianae</span>, Simmons's name was remembered well into the nineteenth century.<br>
<br>Jason Edwards </div>
