<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Parr <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Taylor]</span>,  Louisa Sarah Ann</span>  (1848?&#150;1903), <span class="occ">novelist</span>, was born in London, the only child of Matthew Taylor, a naval officer. She spent her early years at Plymouth, where she was educated. Her writing career began in 1868, when she published a story, &#145;How it all happened&#146;, under the pseudonym of Mrs Olinthus Lobb, in <span class="italic">Good Words</span>. It proved popular, appearing in a French version as a <i>feuilleton</i> in the <span class="italic">Journal des D&eacute;bats</span>, the editor apologizing for departing from his rule of never printing translations. At the request of the queen of W&uuml;rttemberg it was translated into German, and it was issued in the United States in pamphlet form. On 16 November 1869 she married George Parr, a surgeon and collector of china, prints, fans, and early editions, and the couple settled down to a comfortable life in Kensington, London.<br>
<br>In 1870 Louisa Parr published <span class="italic">Dorothy Fox</span>, a novel of Quaker life, which created such a frenzy in the United States that one publisher paid &pound;300 for the advance sheets of her next novel, <span class="italic">The Prescotts of Pamphillon</span> (1874). But neither <span class="italic">The Prescotts</span> nor its successor, <span class="italic">The Gosau Smithy</span> (1875), compared with what is generally recognized as her <i>tour de force</i>, <span class="italic">Adam and Eve</span>, published in 1880. A story of Cornish smuggling life about the roguish Adam, and Eve, his London cousin, Parr's plot is founded on incidents related in Jonathan Couch's <span class="italic">History of Polperro</span> (1871), and it was written on location in Cornwall after extensive trips through Italy, Algeria, and France. She followed this success with four more novels&#151;<span class="italic">Robin</span> (1882), <span class="italic">Loyalty George</span> (1888), <span class="italic">Dumps and I</span> (1891), and <span class="italic">The Squire</span> (1892)&#151;before writing her last, <span class="italic">Can this be Love?</span> (1893), the story of a young woman persecuted by her snobbish lover and his vulgar relatives. Parr also contributed stories to magazines&#151;most notably &#145;The Neap Reef&#146; to <span class="italic">Good Words</span> in 1871&#151;and she composed a short life of her fellow novelist Dinah Mulock Craik, which appeared in <span class="italic">Women Novelists of Queen Victoria's Reign</span> (1897). The main characteristics of her work are a sense of humour and a pleasing style, though she could write pointedly about women's oppression. However, she was always at her best in dealing with the sea.<br>
<br>Louisa Parr died, after a week's illness, from bronchitis on 2 November 1903 at her home at 18 Upper Phillimore Place, Kensington, London. Her husband had predeceased her; they had no children.<br>
<br>Elizabeth Lee<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Katharine Chubbuck </div>
