<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Mayer,  Samuel Ralph Townshend</span>  (1841&#150;1880), <span class="occ">founder of the Free and Open Church Association and writer</span>, second son of Samuel Mayer, solicitor, of Gloucester, was born at Gloucester in August 1841. As he grew up he is said to have borne a remarkable resemblance to the poet Keats, not least in his poor health. He was for several years a frequent contributor to the Gloucester newspapers, and to many serial publications. In 1859 he published a novelette, <span class="italic">Amy Fairfax</span>. Moving to London, he founded and, from 1866 until February 1872, was secretary of the Free and Open Church Association, advocating the abolition of pew-rents. He edited the <span class="italic">Churchman's Shilling Magazine</span>, the <span class="italic">Illustrated Review</span> from January to June 1871, the <span class="italic">Free and Open Church Advocate</span> (3 vols., 1872&#150;7), and was proprietor and editor of the <span class="italic">St. James's Magazine</span> for a short period in 1875. In 1868 he was the editor of the first report of the Metropolitan Conservative Working Men's Association, and in 1870 he established the Junior Conservative Club, in conjunction with J. B. Payne. In <span class="italic">The Origin and Growth of Sunday Schools in England</span> (1878), and <span class="italic">Who Was the Founder of Sunday Schools? Being an Inquiry</span> (1880), he attempted to prove that whatever credit belonged to Robert Raikes as the founder of those institutions should be shared with the Revd Thomas Stock. With J. C. Paget he published <span class="italic">Afghanistan</span> in 1878, examining its history, geography, and ethnography.<br>
<br>Mayer died at his home in Crown Terrace, Mortlake Road, Richmond, Surrey, on 28 May 1880 and was buried in Gloucester cemetery. His wife, Gertrude Mary (1839&#150;1932), daughter of John Watson Dalby, whom he married in 1868, was a friend of Leigh Hunt and B. W. Procter (Barry Cornwall). She wrote a number of novels, including <span class="italic">Sir Hubert's Marriage</span> (3 vols., 1876), and in 1894 she published <span class="italic">Women of Letters</span> (2 vols.), essays which had previously appeared in <span class="italic">Temple Bar</span>. She and Mayer had been contributors to the journal, of which she subsequently became the editor.<br>
<br>G. C. Boase<span class="italic">, rev. </span> K. D. Reynolds </div>
