<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Powis,  William Henry</span>  (1808&#150;1836), <span class="occ">wood-engraver</span>, was born in London. He learned his craft from George Wilmot Bonner and soon became established as &#145;one of the best wood-engravers of his time&#146;  (Chatto and Jackson, 544). Linton, who worked alongside him for a year, observed that &#145;most noticeable in Powis's work is &hellip; that everything has been cut at once. In this he differed notably from [John Orrin] Smith, who depended on after-toning&#146;  (Linton, 194). With Bonner, Powis produced some facsimile engravings after Hans Holbein's work for Francis Douce's <span class="italic">The Dance of Death</span>, printed at the Chiswick Press in 1833; contemporaries deemed his contributions to be of &#145;remarkable excellence&#146;  (Linton, 193).<br>
<br>After leaving Bonner, Powis undertook work for John Jackson, who, according to Linton, often signed Powis's engravings. He produced many engravings of animals for Jackson, including several in James Northcote's <span class="italic">Fables</span> (1833), such as <span class="italic">Elephant</span> and <span class="italic">Wolf</span>, and in Edward Turner Bennett's <span class="italic">The Gardens and Menageries of the Zoological Society Delineated</span> (1830&#150;31), for instance, <span class="italic">Palm Squirrel</span> and <span class="italic">Barn Owl</span>. Powis was probably best known for his topographical views, many of them biblical scenes. A number adorned Richard Westall and John Martin's <span class="italic">Illustrations of the Bible</span> (1833). Linton thought such landscapes &#145;perhaps the best &hellip; for their size up to that time engraved in wood&#146;  (Linton, 194). Certainly the publishers regarded Powis's work as excellent, paying him, according to Chatto and Jackson, 15 guineas instead of the more usual fee of 12, for his engraving of <span class="italic">The Deluge</span>, after Martin. Later, Powis engraved William Harvey's illustration to accompany the poem &#145;Vesuvius: Across the Bay&#146; in Charles Latrobe's <span class="italic">The Solace of Song</span> (1837). His contemporaries thought him less gifted in his figure subjects, and Linton considered them &#145;weakly drawn&#146;  (Linton, 194).<br>
<br>Throughout his working life Powis was established in London. The London trade directories record him working from 94 St John Street Road (1832&#150;4) and later from 59 St John Street Road (1836). Although many early sources, including Samuel Redgrave, suggest that Powis's early death at the age of twenty-eight in 1836 was caused by overwork, Linton claimed that he had always been unhealthy and probably died of consumption. There are proof impressions of the engraver's work in the British Museum and the Victoria and Albert Museum, London; these are characteristically signed either &#145;W. H. POWIS, Sc&#146; or &#145;W. POWIS, Sc.&#146;<br>
<br>Susanna Avery-Quash </div>
