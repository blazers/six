<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Babell,  William</span>  (1689/90&#150;1723), <span class="occ">musical arranger and harpsichordist</span>, was the son of a bassoonist in the Drury Lane Theatre orchestra, and was taught by his father, by John Christopher Pepusch, and possibly by Handel. A proficient violinist, harpsichordist, and organist, he was active in London's professional musical circles and apparently played in the private band of George I. For some years until his death he was organist at All Hallows, Bread Street.<br>
<br>Babell's fame as a harpsichordist derived from his arrangements of popular operatic arias for the instrument, a practice he originated and which won him an international reputation. Charles Burney criticized him for converting the music of Handel (among others) into:<blockquote>
<i>showy</i> and brilliant lessons, which by mere rapidity of finger in playing single sounds, without the assistance of taste, expression, harmony, or modulation, enabled the performer to astonish ignorance, and acquire the reputation of a great player at a small expence.</blockquote>Others regarded him more favourably; his published harpsichord works are considered to provide &#145;a valuable insight into early 18th-century practices of ornamentation and extemporization&#146;  (<span class="italic">New Grove</span>). He also wrote concertos for the violin and small flute.<br>
<br>Babell died at Canonbury, Islington, on 23 September 1723 at the age of thirty-three, his early death being attributed to his &#145;intemperate habits&#146;. He was buried in All Hallows Church.<br>
<br>J. A. F. Maitland<span class="italic">, rev. </span> K. D. Reynolds </div>
