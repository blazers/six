<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Watts,  Walter Henry</span>  (1776&#150;1842), <span class="occ">journalist and artist</span>, was born in the East Indies, the son of a captain in the Royal Navy. He was sent to England at an early age and placed in a school in Cheshire. A talented artist, he initially devoted himself to the study of drawing and painting. In 1808 he was a member of the Society of Associated Artists in Watercolours. He was primarily known as a miniature painter, and from 1808 to 1830 exhibited at the Royal Academy. In 1816 he was appointed miniature painter to Princess Charlotte.<br>
<br>Watts was unable to earn his living from painting alone, and in 1803 became a parliamentary reporter on the staff of the <span class="italic">Morning Post</span>, joining the <span class="italic">Morning Chronicle</span> in the same capacity about 1813. In 1826 he managed the reporting department of the <span class="italic">Representative</span>, but returned to the <span class="italic">Morning Chronicle</span> in the following year. In 1837 he was co-founder and first chairman of the Newspaper Press Benevolent Association (1837&#150;47), an ultimately unsuccessful organization which aimed at providing pensions for journalists and small grants to assist them in emergencies. Watts also continued to act as a parliamentary reporter for the <span class="italic">Morning Chronicle</span> until ill health forced him to retire in 1840.<br>
<br>Throughout this time Watts wrote and edited fifteen volumes of the <span class="italic">Annual Biography and Obituary</span> (1817&#150;31). He also contributed fine art criticism to the <span class="italic">Literary Gazette</span>, and is credited with the authorship of a rejoinder to Martin Shee's <span class="italic">Rhymes on Art</span> (1805). Watts, who never married, died at his lodgings at Earl's Court Terrace, Old Brompton, London, on 4 January 1842.<br>
<br>E. I. Carlyle<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Victoria Millar </div>
