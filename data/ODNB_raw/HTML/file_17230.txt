<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lutwyche,  Thomas</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1674, <em>d.</em> 1734), <span class="occ">lawyer</span>, was baptized on 21 September 1674, the first surviving son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/17229/', "Sir Edward Lutwyche (1634&#150;1709)")</script>, justice of the common pleas, and his wife, Anne (<i>d</i>. 1722), daughter of Sir Timothy Tourneur. Lutwyche was a king's scholar at Westminster School, and was elected to Christ Church, Oxford, where he matriculated on 4 July 1692, but took no degree. He was called to the bar at the Inner Temple in 1697, was reader there in 1715, and treasurer of the inn in 1722. Lutwyche was made QC towards the end of Queen Anne's reign, and was an able lawyer. He was married to Elizabeth, daughter of William Bagnall of Bretforton, Worcestershire; they had two sons and three daughters.<br>
<br>A member of the October Club, Lutwyche was returned as MP for Appleby by Lord Thanet in 1710, and held the seat until 1722. He was MP for Callington, Cornwall, between 1722 and 1727, and for Agmondesham, Buckinghamshire, from 1728 to his death. A high tory, Lutwyche delivered, on 6 November 1723, a strong speech in parliament against the bill for laying a tax upon papists. In 1725 he was one of the organizers of the trial of Lord Chancellor Macclesfield; and he was said to have refused the post of attorney-general in succession to Sir Robert Raymond.<br>
<br>Lutwyche died on 13 November 1734 and was buried at the Inner Temple Church. Among his papers were some manuscript reports of &#145;select cases, arguments and pleadings&#146; in the queen's bench in the reign of Queen Anne. These were first published in 1781 as a supplement to the authoritative <span class="italic">Modern Reports</span> of cases since the accession of Charles II.<br>
<br>J. M. Rigg<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Robert Brown </div>
