<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Shirley,  John</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1648, <em>d.</em> 1679), <span class="occ">author</span>, was baptized at St Botolph, Aldersgate, London, on 18 August 1648, one of the sons of John Shirley, bookseller, and his wife, Anne. He matriculated from Trinity College, Oxford, on 17 March 1665, becoming a scholar in 1667. He graduated BA on 18 February 1668 and proceeded MA on 28 November 1671. His ability was soon recognized and, according to his brother Benjamin, he wrote a life of Sir Walter Ralegh as the preface to an edition of Ralegh's <span class="italic">History of the World</span>. In 1673 he acted as <i>terrae filius</i>, a privileged orator of the university, and was made a fellow of his college. But within the probationary period he was expelled for immoral conduct and returned to London. He married the daughter of an innkeeper of Islington and made his living by writing and by correcting for the press. He died in Islington on 28 December 1679 and was buried there on 30 December.<br>
<br>He was not the only John Shirley writing at that time. <a name="cosubject_25429"></a><span class="headword">John Shirley</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1678&#150;1682), <span class="occ">author</span>, who claimed to be a doctor of medicine, wrote two medical books, <span class="italic">A Short Compendium of Chirurgery</span> (1678) and <span class="italic">The Art of Rowling and Bolstring</span> (1682), providing advice on how to deal with wounds, ulcers, and fractures.<br>
<br>John Shirley (<i>fl</i>. 1685&#150;1688), writer, was author of <span class="italic">The Famous History of Palmerin of England</span> (1685), an adventure story with a hero fighting giants and wild bears while crossing Europe, and two lively popular works in praise of women, <span class="italic">The Illustrious History of Women</span> (1686) and <span class="italic">The Triumph of Wit</span> (1688), which had reached an eighth edition by 1724. His name occurs with the preface to <span class="italic">A true account of the enterprise of the confederate princes against the Turks and Hungarian rebels</span> (1685). That work was published by William Thackery, Duck Lane, London, who also published many works by <a name="cosubject_25431"></a><span class="headword">John Shurley</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1681&#150;1702), <span class="occ">writer</span>, who sometimes used the name of J. S. Gent. His range of works included an account of the life of Guy, earl of Warwick (1681), the story of Reynard the fox in heroic verse (1681), <span class="italic">Ecclesiastical History Epitomized</span> (1682), an account of the life of Jesus, and <span class="italic">Visions of Don Francisco de Quevedo Vellegass</span> (1682). <span class="italic">The Compleat Courtier, or, Cupid's Academy</span> (1683) provided a popular lively collection of love songs, poems, epistles, and dialogue, and his <span class="italic">Young Man's Resolution of the Maiden's Request</span> (1695) followed a similar theme. His final work, an abridgement by J. S. Gent, was the <span class="italic">History of the Renowned and Valiant Prince Amadi of Gaul</span> (1702).<br>
<br>F. D. A. Burns </div>
