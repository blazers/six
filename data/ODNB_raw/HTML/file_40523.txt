<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Weston,  James</span>  (1688?&#150;1748?), <span class="occ">stenographer</span>, hailed from Edinburgh; nothing is known of his parentage or early life. He was probably employed taking shorthand at trials in London in the 1710s. Established as a shorthand &#145;professor&#146; by 1722, he claimed to have taught more than 200 pupils by mid-1724, many by post, waging that no Westonian writer could be matched for speed. He courted publicity through a protracted and costly advertising campaign puffing his services and <span class="italic">Stenography Compleated</span> (1727), the most lavish shorthand book yet printed. A projected engraved shorthand <span class="roman">Bible</span> never materialized, but in 1730 he published part of the prayer book in shorthand, and in 1743 a reformulation of his system, as well as crib sheets and copybook materials, some in manuscript. His system's appropriation of Theophilus Metcalfe's 1630s alphabet, its dependence on arbitrary symbols and awkward rules, and its ambiguous, inelegant written results all militated against its lasting success; few Westonian manuscripts survive. &#145;Warlike&#146; and &#145;blustering&#146; Weston's significance in shorthand history lies rather in having &#145;done service by making a noise and raising attention to shorthand&#146;  (<span class="italic">Private Journal</span>, 1.73; 2.327), his &#145;warm and proud temper&#146;  (<span class="italic">British Journal</span>, 143, 1725, 4) occasioning public spats and competitions with stenographer rivals.<br>
<br>Timothy Underhill </div>
