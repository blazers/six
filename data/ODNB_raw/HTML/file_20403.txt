<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Nunn family</span>  (<em>per. </em><em>c.</em>1815&#150;1847), <span class="occ">hymn writers</span>, were the children of John Nunn of Colchester. The eldest son, another <a name="cosubject_65808"></a><span class="headword">John Nunn</span>  (1782&#150;1861)<span class="occ"></span>, was born in Colchester on 23 May 1782 and was educated at St John's College, Cambridge, from 1802, graduating BA in 1806. He married Elizabeth, daughter of William Tipton of Shrewsbury. After serving various curacies he became domestic chaplain to the earl of Galway (1849&#150;53) and rector of Thorndon, Suffolk, in 1854, where he died on 15 April 1861; he was buried at Thorndon. Nunn was a friend and fellow curate of Patrick Bront&euml;. In 1817 he published <span class="italic">Psalms and Hymns</span>, which was reprinted several times, the last edition appearing in 1861. It contained several hymns by Nunn himself, but these soon passed out of use. Nunn's sister, <a name="cosubject_20403"></a><span class="headword">Marianne Nunn</span>  (1778&#150;1847)<span class="occ"></span>, born on 17 May 1778, also wrote hymns and is remembered for her adaptation of John Newton's hymn &#145;One there is above all others, O how he loves&#146; to the Welsh tune &#145;Ar hyd y nos&#146;. The original appeared in John Nunn's <span class="italic">Psalms and Hymns</span>; in various variant versions it remained popular as a children's hymn into the early twentieth century. Marianne Nunn died, unmarried, in 1847. Her younger brother, <a name="cosubject_20404"></a><span class="headword">William Nunn</span>  (1786&#150;1840)<span class="occ"></span>, was born on 13 May 1786 and educated at St John's College, Cambridge, graduating BA in 1814 and MA in 1817. Ordained priest in 1814, he served as the incumbent of St Clement's Episcopalian Chapel in Manchester from 1818. He married Elizabeth, daughter of Philip Vaughan of Kidwelly, on 14 June 1819; they had at least two sons. William Nunn wrote several hymns, of which the most popular were &#145;O could we touch the sacred lyre&#146; and &#145;The gospel comes, ordained of God&#146;. Both appeared in his 1827 <span class="italic">Selection of Psalms and Hymns</span>. He died in Manchester on 9 March 1840, and was buried at All Saints' Church there.<br>
<br>Rosemary Mitchell </div>
