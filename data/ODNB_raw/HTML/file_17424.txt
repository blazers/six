<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Macdonald,  Alexander</span>  (1755&#150;1837), <span class="occ">Roman Catholic priest and Scottish Gaelic scholar</span>, was born in the west highlands of Scotland. Aged eleven he was received into the Roman Catholic seminary of Bourblach, in North Morar, by Bishop Hugh Macdonald. Alexander Macdonald was later sent to the Scots College in Rome, where, aged twenty-three, he was ordained priest by dispensation. In 1782 he returned to Scotland and, being a good student of Gaelic, he was placed at Balloch, near Drummond Castle, Perthshire, to attend the highlanders resident in that mission. Ten years later he was appointed missionary of the Gaelic chapel in Blackfriars' Wynd, Edinburgh. Afterwards he returned to Balloch, and eventually he built a chapel at Crieff, Perthshire, where he passed the remainder of his life, except for a short interval in 1827&#150;8, when he took charge of the congregation at Leith.<br>
<br>A talented classical as well as Gaelic scholar, Macdonald was employed to give the Latin significations of the words for two letters of the alphabet in the <span class="italic">Dictionarium Scoto-Celticum: a Dictionary of the Gaelic language</span>, published under the direction of the Highland Society of Scotland in 1828. He himself published <span class="italic">Phingateis, sive, Hibernia liberata, epicum Ossianis poema, e Celtico sermone conversum, tribus praemissis disputationibus, et subsequentibus notis</span> (1820), dedicated to Augustus Frederick, duke of Sussex. Macdonald died at Crieff on 13 July 1837.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Philip Carter </div>
