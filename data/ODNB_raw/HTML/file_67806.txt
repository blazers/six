<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hunt,  Martita</span>  (1900&#150;1969), <span class="occ">actress</span>, was born in Buenos Aires, Argentina, on 30 January 1900, to Alfred Hunt and his wife, Marta, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Burnett. She came to England to attend Queenwood boarding-school, Eastbourne, and she then trained for a theatrical career under Dame Genevi&egrave;ve Ward and Lady Benson. After acting at Liverpool repertory theatre and with a touring company, she made her London d&eacute;but for the Stage Society in Ernst Toller's <span class="italic">The Machine Wreckers</span> at the Kingsway in May 1923. She spent the next six years working in the West End, giving performances as the Principessa della Cercola in W. Somerset Maugham's <span class="italic">Our Betters</span> (Globe, 1924) and as Mrs Linden in Ibsen's <span class="italic">A Doll's House</span> (Playhouse, 1925), interspersed with engagements at club theatres such as the Q and the Arts and, in 1926, a short season of Chekhov plays at the small Barnes Theatre, directed by Victor Komisarjevsky, who cast her as Charlotta Ivanovna, the eccentric governess in <span class="italic">The Cherry Orchard</span> and as Olga in <span class="italic">Three Sisters</span>.<br>
<br>In September 1929 Hunt joined the Old Vic company under Harcourt Williams, and in the following eight months played a challenging succession of Shakespearian roles (the Nurse in <span class="italic">Romeo and Juliet</span>, Portia in <span class="italic">The Merchant of Venice</span>, the Queen in <span class="italic">Richard II</span>, Helena in <span class="italic">A Midsummer Night's Dream</span>, Portia in <span class="italic">Julius Caesar</span>, and, opposite John Gielgud, Rosalind in <span class="italic">As You Like It</span>, Lady Macbeth in <span class="italic">Macbeth</span>, and Queen Gertrude in <span class="italic">Hamlet</span>), together with the parts of B&eacute;line in Moli&egrave;re's <span class="italic">The Imaginary Invalid</span>, Queen Elizabeth in Shaw's <span class="italic">The Dark Lady of the Sonnets</span>, and Lavinia in his <span class="italic">Androcles and the Lion</span>. With an arresting appearance and a dominant stage presence, she proved most effective as strong, tragic characters, her Gertrude in <span class="italic">Hamlet</span> being accounted by some critics the finest they had seen.<br>
<br>Returning to the West End, Hunt featured in an extraordinary range of productions in the 1930s and during the war years, notably as Edith Gunter in Dodie Smith's <span class="italic">Autumn Crocus</span> (Lyric, 1931), the countess of Rousillon in <span class="italic">All's Well that Ends Well</span> (Arts, 1932), Lady Strawholme in Ivor Novello's <span class="italic">Fresh Fields</span> (Criterion, 1933), Liz Frobisher in John Van Druten's <span class="italic">The Distaff Side</span> (Apollo, 1933), Barbara Dawe in Clemence Dane's <span class="italic">Moonlight is Silver</span> (Queen's, 1934), Theodora in Elmer Rice's <span class="italic">Not for Children</span> (Fortune, 1935), Masha in Chekhov's <span class="italic">The Seagull</span> (New, 1936), Emilia in <span class="italic">Othello</span> (when she rejoined the Old Vic briefly in 1938), the Mother in Garcia Lorca's <span class="italic">Marriage of Blood</span> (Savoy, 1939), Miss Havisham in a dramatization of Dickens's <span class="italic">Great Expectations</span> at the Rudolf Steiner Hall (1939), L&eacute;onie in Cocteau's <span class="italic">Les parents terribles</span> (Gate, 1940), Mrs Cheveley in Wilde's <span class="italic">An Ideal Husband</span> (Westminster, 1943), and Cornelia in Webster's <span class="italic">The White Devil</span> (Duchess, 1947). During the same period Hunt made innumerable films, the majority unworthy of her talents, though some, like <span class="italic">Good Morning, Boys</span> (1937), <span class="italic">Trouble Brewing</span> (1939), <span class="italic">The Man in Grey</span> (1943), and <span class="italic">The Wicked Lady</span> (1945), achieved considerable popularity and enabled her to sparkle in supporting or cameo roles, and in David Lean's <span class="italic">Great Expectations</span> (1946) she took the opportunity to develop her stage characterization of Miss Havisham into an unforgettable study in alienation. For all this resourcefulness, stardom continued to elude her until, in 1948, an American management hired her to play Countess Aurelia, the eponymous heroine of Giraudoux's <span class="italic">The Madwoman of Chaillot</span>, for its English-speaking premi&egrave;re, a compelling performance which won her a Tony award on her Broadway d&eacute;but, but sadly left London audiences relatively unmoved three years later. Ironically, this very success served only to typecast her as the <i>grande dame</i> or patrician grotesque in a string of subsequent films, including <span class="italic">Anna Karenina</span> (1948), <span class="italic">Lady Windermere's Fan</span> (1949), <span class="italic">Folly to be Wise</span> (1952), <span class="italic">The March Hare</span> (1956), <span class="italic">Anastasia</span> (1956), <span class="italic">The Admirable Crichton</span> (1957), <span class="italic">The Prince and the Showgirl</span> (1957)&#151;contributing a witty reprise of her stage performance as the Grand Duchess in its source, Rattigan's <span class="italic">The Sleeping Prince</span> (Phoenix, 1953)&#151;<span class="italic">The Brides of Dracula</span> (1960), <span class="italic">Becket</span> (1964), and <span class="italic">The Unsinkable Molly Brown</span> (1964). After 1950 her stage appearances became more infrequent and she made her last as Ang&eacute;lique Boniface in <span class="italic">Hotel Paradiso</span>, an adaptation from Feydeau, alongside Alec Guinness at the Winter Garden in May 1956. She died of bronchial asthma at her home, 7 Primrose Hill Studios, Fitzroy Road, Hampstead, London, on 13 June 1969.<br>
<br>Donald Roy </div>
