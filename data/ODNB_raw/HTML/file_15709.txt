<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Knell,  William Adolphus</span>  (1801&#150;1875), <span class="occ">marine painter</span>, was born in Carisbrooke, Hampshire, in 1801, the son of William Knell and Jane Richardson. He lived in London and first exhibited at the Royal Academy of Arts in 1825 with <span class="italic">The Royal Squadron at Spithead</span>. Initially adopting the popular Dutch seventeenth-century marine style, in mid-career he shifted to an English manner to maintain his share of the market. The results were lighter and less heavy-handed pictures marked by translucent seas and broader handling in the skies. He specialized in inshore scenes of England, France, and the Low Countries, but many of his major works depicted naval action. Eight drawings by Knell, reproduced in coloured aquatint by Newton Smith Fielding, illustrated the <span class="italic">Epitome, Historical and Statistical, Descriptive of the Royal Naval Services of England</span> by E. Miles and Lieutenant L. Miles, published in 1841.<br>
<br>Knell contributed twenty-nine pictures to the Royal Academy from 1825 to 1866, forty-four to the British Institution from 1825 until 1867, and twenty to the Society of British Artists. He entered <span class="italic">The Destruction of Toulon by the British, 18 June 1793</span> and <span class="italic">The Battle off Cape St. Vincent, 14 February 1797</span> to the Westminster Hall competition of 1847. The latter, described as &#145;another masterly naval battle, treated with a spirit of effect worthy of praise&#146;  (<span class="italic">Art Union</span>, 272), was one of only four pictures purchased by the Westminster commissioners for the nation that year, for &pound;200. He painted at Queen Victoria's &#145;special command&#146;  (Millar, 133) <span class="italic">The Naval Review at Spithead, 11 August 1853</span> (1854; Royal Collection) for &pound;150 and <span class="italic">The Arrival of Prince Albert, 6 February 1840</span> (1840; Royal Collection), which was engraved by William Miller (1796&#150;1882) for the <span class="italic">Art Journal</span> (1 June 1857, p. 188) and the <span class="italic">Royal Gallery of Art</span> (2.25). Other major works include <span class="italic">Pilot Boats off Dover</span> (exh. RA, 1847; Victoria Art Gallery, Bath), and <span class="italic">The Arrival of the Princess Alexandra at Gravesend, 1863</span> (Danish Royal Collection).<br>
<br>Knell married Susannah Williams (<i>n&eacute;e</i> Cross), a widow, on 22 August 1846, when he was described as a widower. Their eldest son, William Callcott Knell (<i>fl</i>. 1848&#150;1879), produced more vigorous and heavily worked marine scenes than his father and another son, Adolphus Knell (1855&#150;1880), similarly turned away from the Dutch marine manner to produce oils likened to Turner sketches. John Henry Knell, who exhibited four pictures at the Royal Academy in 1833 and 1834, was probably Knell's brother. Knell died at his London home, 329 Kentish Town Road, on 9 July 1875, and was buried in the Abney Park cemetery, Stoke Newington.<br>
<br>A number of Knell's pictures are in public collections in Derby, Edinburgh, Hull, Newcastle, York, and the National Maritime Museum, Greenwich, which owns twelve oils.<br>
<br>Jason Rosenfeld </div>
