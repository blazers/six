<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Cox,  Elizabeth</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1671&#150;1688), <span class="occ">actress</span>, was possibly the spinster of twenty-two who married John Bateman of St Paul's, Covent Garden, on 30 November 1661. Nothing is known of the early life of Betty Cox, whose first recorded performance was Lydia in William Wycherley's <span class="italic">Love in a Wood</span>, for the King's Company in March 1671. Early in her career she was popular as chaste, but witty heroines in roles such as Palmyra in Dryden's <span class="italic">Marriage &agrave; la mode</span>, Violetta in his <span class="italic">The Assignation</span>, as well as Constantia in Thomas Duffet's farce <span class="italic">The Amorous Old Woman</span>. When the company moved into the larger Drury Lane Theatre, Cox appears to have broadened her range, playing Octavia in Nathaniel Lee's <span class="italic">Nero</span> in 1674. She became a respected player of tragic heroines such as Desdemona, Indamore in Dryden's <span class="italic">Aureng-Zebe</span>, and probably the title role in Lee's <span class="italic">Sophonisba</span>, to which she spoke the epilogue in performances at London and Oxford.<br>
<br>We have no record of Cox's London appearances between 1676 and October 1681, when the epilogue to Lee's <span class="italic">Mithridates</span>, which she spoke with Cardell Goodman, implies that this was her return to the stage and has her declare &#145;<i>the Devil's in 't if I'me past my Prime</i>&#146;. The <span class="italic">Satire to Julian</span> (1683) and other scandal verses identify Goodman and Cox as lovers. However, there are no further recorded performances from Cox. On 7 April 1683 Charles Killigrew, manager of the new United Company, was ordered by the lord chamberlain to answer her petition, possibly an unsuccessful bid for entry to the new company. The latest mention of her is in the satiric <span class="italic">Session of the Ladies</span> as &#145;Lord Lumley's cast player the fam'd Mrs Cox&#146; (1688).<br>
<br>J. Milling </div>
