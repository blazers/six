<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Macdonald,  William Russell</span>  (1787&#150;1854), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was in early life editor and part proprietor of <span class="italic">Bell's Life in London</span>, the <span class="italic">Sunday Herald</span>, the <span class="italic">British Drama</span>, and the <span class="italic">Literary Humourist</span>, besides contributing largely to other periodicals. &#145;An entire change of opinion and sentiment&#146;, says his obituarist Thomas Butler, &#145;subsequently induced him to seek other channels for the exercise of his varied literary talents.&#146; He wrote <span class="italic">Christianity, Protestantism, and Popery, Compared and Contrasted</span> (1829), published anonymously, and several poems, including <span class="italic">A Paraphrase of Dodsley's &#145;Economy of Human Life&#146;</span> (1817) and <span class="italic">Fudges in Ireland</span> among other more ephemeral poems. But the most useful of Macdonald's productions were his numerous books for the young, to which he devoted the latter period of his life until he lost his sight. Among them were <span class="italic">The Book of Quadrupeds</span> (1838), <span class="italic">First and Second Lessons for the Nursery</span>, <span class="italic">Simple Tales for the Young</span>, and <span class="italic">The Child's Cheerful Companion</span>. Macdonald died on 30 December 1854 in Great James Street, Bedford Row, London, leaving a widow and two sons.<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Victoria Millar </div>
