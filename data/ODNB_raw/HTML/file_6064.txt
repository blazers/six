<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Coney,  John</span>  (1786&#150;1833), <span class="occ">draughtsman and engraver</span>, was born in Ratcliff Highway, London, and was apprenticed to an architect, but he never followed the profession. Among his early studies were pencil drawings of the interior of Westminster Abbey. In 1805 he exhibited the first of ten works at the Royal Academy, a <span class="italic">Perspective View of Lambeth Palace</span>, from 39 Craven Street, Strand. His first publication was a work entitled <span class="italic">A Series of Views Representing the Exterior and Interior of Warwick Castle</span> (1815). The plates were drawn and etched by himself.<br>
<br>Coney was next employed for fourteen years by Harding to draw and engrave a series of exterior and interior views of the cathedrals and abbey churches of England, intended to illustrate the new edition of Sir William Dugdale's <span class="italic">Monasticon Anglicanum</span>, edited by Sir Henry Ellis (1846). In 1829 he began to engrave cathedrals, town halls, and other buildings in France, the Netherlands, Germany, and Italy, with descriptions in four languages. These were published in London in 1832. The next important work, also engraved and designed by himself, was <span class="italic">The Beauties of Continental Architecture</span> (1843), which included twenty-eight plates and fifty vignettes. Coney was employed by the architect Charles Cockerell to engrave a large view of Rome, and he also engraved some drawings of the Law Courts, Westminster, for Sir John Soane. In addition to the above-mentioned works he was the author of <span class="italic">English Ecclesiastical Edifices of the Olden Time</span> (1842, the plates of which were previously used in Dugdale's <span class="italic">Monasticon</span>) and <span class="italic">Original Drawings of London Churches</span> (1820). Coney died of an enlargement of the heart in Leicester Place, Camberwell, on 15 August 1833. Prints and drawings by him are in the British Museum and the Victoria and Albert Museum in London, the National Gallery of Ireland in Dublin, Manchester City Galleries, and the Nottingham Castle Museum.<br>
<br>L. A. Fagan<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Dennis Harrington </div>
