<div class="para">
<span class="headword">&Oacute; Dubhag&aacute;in,  Se&aacute;n  M&oacute;r</span> <span class="st"> [John  the Great O'Dugan]</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1372), <span class="occ">historian and poet</span>, was the most distinguished in a line of hereditary historians to the &Oacute; Ceallaigh chiefs of U&iacute; Mhaine, in eastern Galway. According to a tradition preserved in the seventeenth-century <span class="roman">Registry of Clonmacnoise</span>, the head of the &Oacute; Dubhag&aacute;in family held estates from the U&iacute; Mhaine at Ballinruane and Ballydugan, Galway, in return for keeping a true record of all grants made to the church of Clonmacnoise. Se&aacute;n M&oacute;r himself was scribe of an early portion of the <span class="roman">Book of U&iacute; Mhaine</span> (or <span class="italic">Hy Many</span>, in BL, Egerton MS 90, fols. 20&#150;24) and author of a number of historical poems, the most famous of which, <span class="italic">Triallam timcheall na F&oacute;dla</span>, minutely details the ruling families of Ulster, Connacht, and Leinster, naming their hereditary jurisdictions, but totally ignores the Anglo-Norman invasion and subsequent colonization of large portions of the country. The poem is incomplete, as his original intention was to discuss the whole island of Ireland, and this may have been one of &Oacute; Dubhag&aacute;in's latest works. Other poems by him deal with the history and genealogy of the &Oacute; Ceallaigh chiefs, eulogize his contemporary, Muircheartach &Oacute; Maolmhuaidh, while reciting the genealogies of the Cine&aacute;l Fiachach of Westmeath, give the succession of the provincial kings of Cashel as far as Domnall M&oacute;r Ua Briain (<i>d</i>. 1194), and expound a method for establishing the date of movable feasts in the church's year.<br>
<br>&Oacute; Dubhag&aacute;in was apparently practising his profession for some time before 1339, a year which saw the earliest datable works by his pupil, &Aacute;dhamh &Oacute; Cian&aacute;in (<i>d</i>. 1373), a Fermanagh historian and prolific scribe. &Oacute; Cian&aacute;in's book of prose genealogies, dating <i>c</i>.1344&#150;5, is described in a colophon as having been copied (in whole or in part) &#145;from the book of his great tutor, that is, Seoan &Oacute; Dubag&aacute;n&#146;  (Bannerman, 143&#150;5). This evidence for Se&aacute;n's activity in the first half of the fourteenth century indicates that he was already <i>ollamh</i> (&#145;court poet&#146; or &#145;historian&#146;) of U&iacute; Mhaine when the chieftain William &Oacute; Ceallaigh issued a general invitation to the bardic classes of all Ireland to attend a Christmas feast at his residence in 1351, perhaps under &Oacute; Dubhag&aacute;in's influence, since it was the first time the host to such a gathering had not been a member of the learned classes himself. About 1365 &Oacute; Dubhag&aacute;in retired &#145;from the delights of the world&#146; to spend his declining years at the priory of St John the Baptist (Crutched Friars) at Rindown, Roscommon. On his death in 1372 he was described in the annals as <i>ardsheanchaidh na h&Eacute;ireann</i> (&#145;chief historian of Ireland&#146;).<br>
<br>Katharine Simms </div>
