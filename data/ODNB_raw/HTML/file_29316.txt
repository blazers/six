<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Whitlock <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Kemble]</span>,  Elizabeth</span>  (1761&#150;1836), <span class="occ">actress</span>, was the third daughter and fifth child of the provincial actors <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/15325/', "Roger Kemble (1722&#150;1802)")</script> and his wife, Sarah Ward (1737&#150;1807), and was born at Warrington, Lancashire, on 2 April 1761. Although she was apprenticed to a mantua-maker she acquired some theatrical experience in the provinces, and appeared at Drury Lane on 22 February 1783 as Portia in <span class="italic">The Merchant of Venice</span>, a part she repeated on 1 March. She subsequently played at York, where Tate Wilkinson, in <span class="italic">The Wandering Patentee</span>, described her as &#145;possessed of marks of merit, but wild as a colt untamed&#146;.<br>
<br>Elizabeth Kemble returned to Drury Lane for two seasons, playing, through the influence of her elder sister <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/25516/', "Sarah Siddons")</script>, Margaret in Philip Massinger's <span class="italic">A New Way to Pay Old Debts</span>, Leonora in Edward Young's <span class="italic">The Revenge</span>, and Elvira in <span class="italic">Love Makes a Man</span>. Critical comment, however, remained ambivalent about her talents; she returned to York, and on 21 June 1785 married Charles Edward Whitlock (<i>d</i>. 1822). He was manager of the Newcastle, Sunderland, Lancaster, and Chester theatres, and from 1785 to 1791 she was one of the chief actresses of his circuit. The couple had three sons, the eldest of whom, Henry Edward Whitlock (1787&#150;1806), later appeared with them in America. After performing in Edinburgh in 1793, the Whitlocks were engaged in the USA, where they spent the following fourteen years, playing in Annapolis, Philadelphia, Charleston, Boston, and New York, and gaining a solid reputation. Her roles were primarily in tragedy: Belvidera in <span class="italic">Venice Preserv'd</span>, Imogen in <span class="italic">Cymbeline</span>, Lady Macbeth, and Lady Randolph in John Home's <span class="italic">Douglas</span>. Although not as talented as her older sister, Sarah, to whom she was inevitably compared, Elizabeth Whitlock evidently had the more beautiful voice, and was the more vivacious in company. John Bernard, who knew her in America, commented that, despite her heavy figure, she &#145;could kindle the sympathies and blind the spectator to every deficiency&#146;.<br>
<br>On 6 October 1807 Elizabeth Whitlock reappeared at Drury Lane, acting the part of Elwina in Hannah More's <span class="italic">Percy</span>. She seems not to have performed again, and she and her husband returned to America for the years 1812&#150;14, before settling at Addlestone, near Chertsey, in Surrey. He died on 3 March 1822, and she some years later, at Addlestone, on 27 February 1836.<br>
<br>K. A. Crouch </div>
