<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Brinkley,  Richard</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1355&#150;1375), <span class="occ">Franciscan friar, theologian, and philosopher</span>, was for a long time mistakenly referred to as Walter Brinkley. Nothing is known of his early life, except that he joined his order in Oxford. Brinkley's philosophical and theological ideas circulated at the University of Paris and Prague University, and were discussed at Prague in connection with those of the mid-fourteenth-century Oxford philosopher Richard Billingham. But though mentioned by W. Senko in connection with an anonymous list of celebrated doctors of Prague University, he is not listed at all in Josef T&#345;&iacute;&#353;ka's catalogue of pre-Hussite Prague University students and scholars. Brinkley's works have only recently been rediscovered, and are just beginning to be discussed by contemporary medievalists. In fact, to date only part 5, chapters 6&#150;10, the tract <span class="italic">De significato propositionis</span> of his <span class="italic">Summa nova de logica</span> (so called by Thomas Rossy, a late fourteenth-century Franciscan) and <span class="italic">De obligationes</span> from part 7 have been critically edited.<br>
<br>Thus far what is known of Brinkley's philosophical views is that he defended a version of metaphysical &#145;realism&#146;, with respect to the ontological status of universals, against the prevailing nominalist views of the day. He defended a perspectivist theory of intellectual cognition, against direct intuitive cognition theories, such as those defended by Albert von Sachsen, William Ockham, or Duns Scotus. Brinkley likewise maintained a variety of reism with respect to the ontological status of the referent of an entire proposition, similar to that of Andr&eacute; Neufch&acirc;teau, sharply criticizing the propositional views of Richard Billingham, William Bermingham, and Richard Feribrigge. Brinkley also held the views that the referent of the phrase &#145;God's existence&#146; (<i>Deum esse</i>) is identical with the referent of the term &#145;God&#146; (<i>Deum</i>), and that the ways God acts internally (<i>ad intra</i>), when understood adverbially, are the same as when he acts externally (<i>ad extra</i>). Brinkley's only complete surviving work, the <span class="italic">Summa nova de logica</span> (composed between 1355 and 1365), exists in two manuscripts: Prague University Library, &#268;R. III. A. 11 (olim 396), fols. 31&#150;104; and Leipzig University Library, MS 1360, fols. 1&#150;105. There also exists a portion of his <span class="italic">Summa</span>, namely the tract <span class="italic">De insolubilibus</span>, in BL, Harley MS 3243; and portions of his <span class="italic">Lectura in libros sententiarum, inc: utrum per aliquam disciplinam vel scientiam</span>, in Paris, Biblioth&egrave;que Nationale, Lat. 16535, fols. 123<i>r</i>&#150;129<i>r</i>, and Lat. 16408, fols. 40<i>r</i>&#150;42<i>r</i>. Brinkley's other two known works, his <span class="italic">Determinationes, inc: sit aliqua conclusio theologiae</span> and his <span class="italic">Distinctiones scholasticae, inc: ad sciendam primam originem et finalem</span>, both seem to be lost. Brinkley exerted some influence on the theological writings of Heinrich Totting of Oyta, Dionysius the Carthusian, Johann Hiltalingen of Basel, Jean de Ripa, Pietro de Candia, Guglielmo Centueri da Cremona, and possibly the logical writings of John Wyclif.<br>
<br>Michael J. Fitzgerald </div>
