<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ray,  Gabrielle</span> <span class="st"> [<em>real name </em>Gabrielle Elizabeth Clifford Cook]</span>  (1883&#150;1973), <span class="occ">actress</span>, was born at Bamford Grange, Cheadle, Stockport, on 28 April 1883, the daughter of an iron merchant, William Austin Cook, and his wife, Annie, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Holden. She made her first stage appearances as a child. In 1893 she took the part of Eveleen in John Hollingshead's short-lived operaticized version of <span class="italic">The Green Bushes</span>, <span class="italic">Miami</span>, in the West End. She also danced in Paul Valentine's Blackpool ballet and appeared as a winsome cupid in <span class="italic">Little Red Riding Hood</span> at Richmond before graduating to almost adult roles in the touring companies of <span class="italic">The Belle of New York</span> and <span class="italic">The Casino Girl</span>. While still in her teens she was taken to London by the George Edwardes organization to understudy Gertie Millar in <span class="italic">The Toreador</span> at the Gaiety Theatre. Having been promoted to the small but visible role as a suggestible ministerial secretary, Thisbe, in the next Gaiety musical, <span class="italic">The Orchid</span> (1903), she moved across to the Prince of Wales Theatre to take over the number three female role in <span class="italic">Lady Madcap</span>, in which she performed the latest Parisian dance craze, La Maxixe, with Dorothy Craske. By the time of the production of <span class="italic">The Little Cherub</span> (1906, in which she played the part of Lady Dorothy Congress) Gabs had become one of the most popular picture-postcard beauties of the day, and her role in that piece&#151;teamed with Zena Dare and Lily Elsie as the picturesque supporting daughters to the chief comedian&#151;reflected her new status. She had good supporting roles in <span class="italic">See See</span> (1906, So-Hie) and <span class="italic">Les merveilleuses</span> (1906, Egl&eacute;) and headed the grisettes as the heels-upping Frou-Frou in <span class="italic">The Merry Widow</span> (1907) before Edwardes cast her up a notch, in a full-scale song-and-dance (and even almost acting) part as the perky Daisy in <span class="italic">The Dollar Princess</span> (1909). Miss Ray went on to appear alongside Phyllis Dare and Olive May in the Gaiety Theatre's <span class="italic">Peggy</span> (1911, Polly Polino), but then took her leave of the stage to marry the almost aristocratic Eric Raymond Loder (1888&#150;1966). The marriage was celebrated at St Edward's Roman Catholic Church, Windsor, on 1 March 1912, but it was not successful. Gabs returned to the stage four years later, attempting to pick up where she had left off, and appeared in Edwardes's production of <span class="italic">Betty</span> (1916, Estelle) and in revue at the Hippodrome, but her moment of stamped and franked fame had passed, and her career soon faded away into variety and pantomime appearances and then into a long real retirement. She died on 21 May 1973 at Holloway Sanatorium, Egham, Surrey.<br>
