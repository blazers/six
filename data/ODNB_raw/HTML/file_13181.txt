<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Heywood,  James</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1687, <em>d.</em> 1776), <span class="occ">writer and poet</span>, son of John Heywood, was born at Cheetham Hill, Manchester, and baptized at Manchester on 21 February 1687. He was educated at the Manchester grammar school. For many years he carried on the business of a wholesale linen draper in Fish Street Hill, London. He was a governor of St Bartholomew's, Christ's, Bridewell, and Bethlem hospitals, and was elected alderman of Aldgate ward, but paid the customary fine of &pound;500 rather than serve the office. In his earlier years he contributed to <span class="italic">The Freethinker</span>, <span class="italic">The Plain-Dealer</span>, and other publications, and a letter of his is printed in no. 268 of <span class="italic">The Spectator</span>. These pieces, with some verses, he collected in a small volume, <span class="italic">Letters and Poems on Several Subjects</span> (1722; 2nd edn, with additions, 1726). The poems had previously been published with the title <span class="italic">Original Poems on Several Occasions</span> (1721). Many of his poems are addressed to &#145;Lucinda&#146;, and other titles include &#145;Advice to a Bashful Lover&#146; and &#145;To a Very Old Batchelor&#146;. His letters, which are mostly humorous, deal with contemporary conduct, especially that between the sexes. Heywood made no secret of his own keen interest in the female sex. He wrote: &#145;For my Part, when I see a beautiful young Lady, the sight of her diffuses a secret Satisfaction, and strikes my Mind with an inward Joy, and causes a sudden cheerfulness, and a Secret Delight&#146;  (Heywood, 51).<br>
<br>Heywood is alluded to by Steele in <span class="italic">The Guardian</span> as a politician and brisk little man, who had the habit of twisting off the buttons of persons he conversed with. He died at his house in Austin Friars on 23 July 1776, aged eighty-nine. His wife, Catherine, referred to in his will, predeceased him.<br>
<br>C. W. Sutton<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Michael Bevan </div>
