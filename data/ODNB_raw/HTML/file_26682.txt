<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Strutt,  Jacob George</span>  (1784&#150;1867), <span class="occ">landscape painter and etcher</span>, was born in Colchester, Essex, on 4 August 1784, the third son in the family of five sons and three daughters of Benjamin Strutt (1754&#150;1827), a lawyer's clerk and election agent, and his wife, Caroline, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Pollett. His father, a Colchester antiquarian and literary figure whose friends included Henry Crabb Robinson, was also a talented painter in oils.<br>
<br>Strutt studied in London, where he married, on 8 November 1813, the writer Elizabeth Byron [<i>see </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/45862/', "Strutt, Elizabeth")</script>]. They had two sons and two daughters. He was a contributor to the Royal Academy, British Institution, and the Society of British Artists, Suffolk Street, between 1819 and 1858. For a few years he practised portrait painting, but from 1824 to 1831 he exhibited studies of forest scenery and was best-known for two sets of etchings published at this time&#151;<span class="italic">&#145;Sylva Britannica&#146;, or, Portraits of Forest Trees Distinguished for their Antiquity</span> in 1822 (reissued in 1838) and <span class="italic">&#145;Deliciae sylvarum&#146;, or, Grand and Romantic Forest Scenery in England and Scotland</span> in 1828.<br>
<br>About 1831 Strutt went abroad and, after residing for a time at Lausanne, settled in Rome, where with his second son and pupil Arthur John Strutt (1819&#150;1888), who was also a landscape painter, he had a studio. From there, he sent to the Royal Academy <span class="italic">The Ancient Forum, Rome</span> (exh. 1845) and <span class="italic">Tasso's Oak, Rome</span> (exh. 1851); in 1851 he returned to England, where he and his wife lived in London. In 1858 he exhibited a view of the Roman campagna, but then his name disappears from exhibition catalogues. He died in 1867. Strutt's portraits of the Revd William Marsh and Philander Chase DD were engraved by J. Young and C. Turner.<br>
<br>F. M. O'Donoghue<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Emily M. Weeks </div>
