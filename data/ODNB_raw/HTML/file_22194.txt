<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Phiston <span class="st"> [Fiston]</span>,  William</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1580&#150;1609), <span class="occ">author and translator</span>, describes himself on the title-page of his <span class="italic">Certain Godly and Very Profitable Sermons of Faith, Hope and Charity</span> (1580), as &#145;of London, student&#146;. No further details about his life are known. However, he could have been the William Phiston who matriculated from Magdalene College, Cambridge, in 1572. Edmund Grindal, archbishop of Canterbury, to whom <span class="italic">Certain Godly Sermons</span> was dedicated, was connected to the same college.<br>
<br>
<span class="italic">Certain Godly Sermons</span>, translated from the Italian (published in Geneva in 1543), indicates Phiston's Calvinist leanings with its emphasis on the word and on understanding &#145;the truth of the gospel&#146;  (Phiston, sig. Aiiij). In an additional epistle the translator praises the queen's ministers for their toleration  (ibid., sig. Aiij). <span class="italic">The Well Spring of Witty Conceit</span>, translated from the Italian in 1584, has no author's dedication but Richard Jones, the publisher, dedicated it to Master David Lewis, doctor of civil laws. The author's preface states his aim as persuasion and he addresses a &#145;most dear friend M. N.&#146;  (sig. Bi). Some material was added to the translation by Phiston. This probably includes &#145;Certain worthy sentences, very meet to be written about in a bed-chamber&#146;, which is written in rhyming couplets.<br>
<br>Phiston's translation, <span class="italic">A Testimonie of the True Church of God</span> from the French original by Simon Voyon, has been tentatively dated to 1560, although the <span class="italic">Short-Title Catalogue</span>'s conjectured date is much later, 1585. This is dedicated to Alexander Nowell, dean of St Paul's. The book is unequivocally protestant in tone. The dedication states that the book is intended for those who cannot afford to purchase Foxe's book of martyrs. Arranged chronologically, the book traces the history and thought of Christian doctors and martyrs, ending with Calvin.<br>
<br>In 1595 Phiston dedicated his translation from the Italian and Latin, <span class="italic">The Estate of the Germaine Empire with the Description of Germanie</span>, to Robert Ratcliffe, earl of Sussex, who was about to travel beyond the seas. The book is a handbook aimed at the diplomat, with tables of precedence and ways in which the states of Germany could be understood. Phiston corrected and amended William Caxton's version of <span class="italic">The Ancient History of the Destruction of Troy</span> in 1597. He had also translated <span class="italic">The School of Good Manners</span> from the French in 1595, and this was published in a newly corrected version in 1609, with a third, unchanged, edition in 1629.<br>
<br>E. Lord </div>
