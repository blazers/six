<div class="para">
<span class="headword">H. A.</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1613), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was the author of <span class="italic">The Scourge of Venus</span>, an erotic narrative poem printed in 1613 and reissued in 1614 and 1620. A. B. Grosart identified him as Henry Austin, from the following remark by Thomas Heywood prefixed to <span class="italic">The Brazen Age</span> (1613):<blockquote>What imperfection soever it have, having a brazen face it cannot blush; much like a pedant about this towne, who, when all trades fail'd, turn'd <i>pedagogue</i>, once insinuating with me, borrowed from me certaine translations of <i>Ovid</i>, as his three books <span class="italic">De arte amandi</span>, and two <span class="italic">De remedio amoris</span> which since, his most brazen face hath most impudently challenged as his own, wherefore I must needs proclaime it as far as <i>Ham</i>, where he now keeps schoole, <i>Hos ego versiculos feci, tulit alter honores</i>, they were things which out of my juniority and want of judgement, I committed to the view of some private friends, but with no purpose of publishing, or further communicating them. Therfore I wold entreate that <i>Austin</i>, for so his name is, to acknowledge his wrong to me in shewing them, &amp; his owne impudence, &amp; ignorance in challenging them. But courteous Reader, I can onely excuse him in this, that this is the <i>Brazen Age</i>. (T. Heywood, <span class="italic">The Dramatic Works of Thomas Heywood</span>, 1874, 3.167&#150;8)</blockquote>Grosart's identification has not, however, been accepted by subsequent editors. <span class="italic">The Scourge of Venus</span> is a reworking of the tale of Cinyras from Ovid's <span class="italic">Metamorphoses</span>, while Heywood is talking about the <span class="italic">Ars amatoria</span> and the <span class="italic">Remedia amoris</span>. Heywood's enemy <a name="cosubject_71687"></a><span class="headword">Austin</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1613)<span class="occ"></span> remains a shadowy figure, and there remains no evidence to reveal his first name, or to link him to the otherwise unknown H. A. of <span class="italic">The Scourge of Venus</span>.<br>
<br>Matthew Steggle </div>
