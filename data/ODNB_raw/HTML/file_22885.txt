<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Pulman,  George Philip Rigney</span>  (1819&#150;1880), <span class="occ">antiquary</span>, was born at Axminster, Devon, on 21 February 1819, the son of Philip Pulman (1791&#150;1871) and his wife, Anne Rigney (1788&#150;1855). In early life he was organist at Axminster parish church and wrote for local newspapers. On 12 December 1848 he married at Cattistock, Dorset, Jane, third daughter of George Davys Ewens of Axminster.<br>
<br>In 1848 Pulman bought a printing and bookselling business at Crewkerne, Somerset, where he settled. For some years he was editor of the <span class="italic">Yeovil Times</span>, and on 10 March 1857 he instituted <span class="italic">Pulman's Weekly News and Advertiser</span>, which for over twenty years he owned and edited. It was the first paper to be established at Crewkerne and soon attained a large local circulation.<br>
<br>In June 1878 Pulman sold his newspaper and business and retired to The Hermitage at Uplyme, between Axminster and Lyme Regis. He died there on 3 February 1880, and was buried at Axminster cemetery on 7 February. His wife and his son, G. B. Pulman, a solicitor at Lutterworth, both survived him.<br>
<br>Pulman was a keen fisherman. At the Great Exhibition of 1851 he won a bronze medal for artificial flies. He wrote the popular work <span class="italic">The Vade-mecum of Fly-Fishing for Trout</span> (1841; 2nd edn, 1846; 3rd edn, 1851), and <span class="italic">The Book of the Axe</span>, published initially in numbers and later, in 1841, as a book. Other editions followed in 1844, 1853, and 1875, the last being &#145;rewritten and greatly enlarged&#146;. It was a piscatorial description of the district along the course of the Axe, and contained histories of the towns and houses on the river's banks. Pulman's interest in language and dialect was reflected in <span class="italic">Rustic Sketches, Being Poems on Angling in the Dialect of East Devon</span> (1842; repr. 1853 and 1871), <span class="italic">Local Nomenclature: a Lecture on the Names of Places, Chiefly in the West of England</span> (1857), and a version of <span class="italic">The &#145;Song of Solomon&#146; in the East Devonshire Dialect</span> (1860). Under the name John Trotandot he published <span class="italic">Rambles, Roamings, and Recollections</span> (1870), which described the country around Crewkerne, and <span class="italic">Roamings Abroad</span> (1878). He used the pseudonym Tickler for <span class="italic">Devonshire Sketches</span> (1869), and also wrote under the pseudonym Elias Tozer.<br>
<br>In the 1840s Pulman published <span class="italic">The Western Agriculturist</span>, a magazine for west-country farmers; he also published <span class="italic">United Counties Miscellany</span> from 1849 to July 1851. He supplied the music for songs entitled &#145;The Battle of Alma&#146; (1854) and &#145;I'll love my love in the winter&#146;, with words by W. D. Glyde, and composed a &#145;Masonic Hymn&#146; and &#145;Psalms, Hymn-Tunes, and Twelve Chants&#146; (1855).<br>
<br>W. P. Courtney<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Ian Maxted </div>
