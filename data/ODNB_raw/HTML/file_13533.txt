<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Holland,  John</span>  (1794&#150;1872), <span class="occ">poet and writer</span>, was born in Sheffield Park, Sheffield, on 14 March 1794, the son of John Holland, optical instrument maker, of Richmond Hill, in the parish of Handsworth, Yorkshire, and his wife, Elizabeth, daughter of Samuel Cox of Staveley, Derbyshire. He was brought up in his father's trade but soon abandoned it for literary pursuits, and was encouraged by James Montgomery, Sheffield's leading writer and evangelist, who became a close friend. As a prominent member of the Wesleyan Chapel in Carver Street, about 1818 he was appointed one of the secretaries of the Sheffield Sunday School Union.<br>
<br>As a young man he produced several volumes of poetry with a religious or moral purpose. Some, such as <span class="italic">Sheffield Park</span> (1820) and <span class="italic">The Village of Eyam</span> (1821), had a local setting. His evangelical writings found a wide audience but are no longer read.<br>
<br>From 1825 to 1832 Holland was, in succession to Montgomery, editor of the radical newspaper the <span class="italic">Sheffield Iris</span>. In 1832 he became editor of the <span class="italic">Newcastle Courant</span>, but he returned to Sheffield in 1833 and acted as co-editor of the <span class="italic">Sheffield Mercury</span> from 1835 until the journal ceased publication in 1848. A lifelong interest in botany and geology led him to the publication of diverse works on local flowers, the cultivation of gooseberries, a historical survey of fossil fuels and the coal trade of Great Britain (1835), and the impressive <span class="italic">A Treatise on the Progressive Improvement and Present State of the Manufactures in Metals</span> (1831&#150;49). He is chiefly remembered today for his historical and topographical writings, especially the <span class="italic">History, Antiquities and Description of the Town and Parish of Worksop</span> (1826) and the <span class="italic">Tour of the Don</span> (1837), and for various memoirs of the lives of poets, clergymen, and other notables, including <span class="italic">Memorials of Sir Francis Chantrey &hellip; in Hallamshire and Elsewhere</span> (1851) and <span class="italic">Memoirs of the Life and Writings of James Montgomery</span> (7 vols., 1854&#150;6). He also wrote numerous sermons, hymns, and articles for <span class="italic">Notes and Queries</span> and <span class="italic">The Reliquary</span>, and for magazines and newspapers.<br>
<br>In acknowledgement of Holland's journalistic services an annuity of &pound;100 was subscribed for by ten gentlemen of Sheffield and presented to him in 1870. He died, unmarried, at his residence at The Mount, Sheffield, on 28 December 1872 and was buried in the churchyard of the neighbouring parish of Handsworth.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> David Hey </div>
