<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Langrish,  Browne</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1759), <span class="occ">physician</span>, was born in Hampshire, and first educated as a surgeon. In 1733 he was in practice at Petersfield, Hampshire, and published <span class="italic">A New Essay on Muscular Motion</span>, which discussed the structure of muscles and the phenomena of muscular contraction. In 1734 he became an extra licentiate of the College of Physicians, and began practice as a physician. He was elected a fellow of the Royal Society in 1734, and in 1735 published <span class="italic">The Modern Theory and Practice of Physic</span>, which described experiments in the analysis of excreta and the examination of the blood. He practised in Winchester or Basingstoke, Hampshire, and in 1746 published <span class="italic">Physical experiments on brutes, in order to discover a safe and easy method of dissolving stone in the bladder</span>. Experiments on cherry laurel water (prussic acid) were made, and Langrish concluded that it could be used in medicine with advantage. An unpublished letter (1747) to the Royal Society on oleaginous warm baths was published in an article in 1938, with critical comments from the authors F. T. Gardner and C. D. Leake  (<span class="italic">Annals of Medical History</span>, 131&#150;5).<br>
<br>Langrish delivered the Croonian lectures on muscular motion to the Royal Society in 1747, and they were published in 1748. In the same year he graduated MD, and published a work of thirty-five pages on smallpox which showed extensive reading and acute clinical observation. He died at Basingstoke, Hampshire, on 12 November 1759.<br>
<br>Norman Moore<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Jean Loudon </div>
