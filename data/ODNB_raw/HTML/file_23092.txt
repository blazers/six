<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ramsay,  John</span>  (1802&#150;1879), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was born on 16 August 1802 in Kilmarnock, the son of James Ramsay, carpet weaver, and his wife, Jean, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Fulton. He received a limited education, during which he resided occasionally with a grandfather at Gulillard, near the village of Dundonald, Ayrshire. He was apprenticed in carpet weaving in Kilmarnock, and soon began to compose verses while working at the loom. Subsequently he became a grocer in Kilmarnock, by which time he is described as &#145;married [with] a rising family&#146;  (Paterson, 405). His entry in the Scottish register of deaths gives his wife's name as Elizabeth Templeton. However, meeting with reverses he relinquished the business, and for fifteen years he travelled through Scotland selling his poems. Finally he became the agent of a benevolent society in Edinburgh.<br>
<br>While a carpet weaver Ramsay contributed verses to the <span class="italic">Edinburgh Literary Journal</span>, then edited by Henry Glassford Bell. In 1836 he published his collected poems under the title of <span class="italic">Woodnotes of a Wanderer</span>, which reached a second edition in 1839. &#145;The Eglinton Park Meeting&#146;, the leading piece in the volume, is a humorous description in <i>ottava rima</i> of a review of the Ayrshire yeomanry by the marquess of Hastings in 1823. &#145;Address to Dundonald Castle&#146;, in somewhat laboured heroic couplets, is energetic and picturesque. As late as 1871 Ramsay published a volume entitled <span class="italic">Gleanings of the Gloamin</span>. He died at 495 St Vincent Street, Glasgow, on 11 May 1879.<br>
<br>T. W. Bayne<span class="italic">, rev. </span> James How </div>
