<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Larpent, Sir  George Gerard</span>, first baronet, and Baron de Hochepied in the Hungarian nobility  (1786&#150;1855), <span class="occ">politician</span>, was born in London on 16 February 1786, the younger son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/16077/', "John Larpent (1741&#150;1824)")</script>, inspector of plays, and his second wife, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/65588/', "Anna Margaretta Larpent (1758&#150;1832)")</script>, daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/22569/', "Sir James Porter")</script>, diplomatist. On 13 October 1813 he married Charlotte (1792&#150;1851), third daughter of William Cracroft of the exchequer office. In 1819 he was granted permission to assume the Hungarian title of Baron de Hochepied, which came through his mother's line.<br>
<br>Larpent entered the East India house of Cockerell and Larpent at an early age, became chairman of the Oriental and China Association, and deputy chairman of the St Katharine's Dock Company. Through his role in Cockerell and Larpent he became a major recipient of slave compensation. The firm acted as commission agents in both the West India and East India trades and held mortgages in Mauritius in particular. Larpent (and other partners in Cockerell and Larpent) were awarded compensation for ownership of 509 enslaved people following the <span class="roman">Emancipation Act</span> of 1833. In 1847&#150;8 Cockerell, Larpent &amp; Co. had &#145;&pound;200,000 of their &pound;900,000 assets locked up in Mauritius sugar estates and extensive interests in indigo and sugar plantations and a teak forest in India&#146;  (Chapman, 112&#150;13).<br>
<br>In May 1840 Larpent unsuccessfully stood for parliament at Ludlow as a whig, and in April 1841, at Nottingham; but in June 1841 he was returned at the head of the poll for Nottingham with Sir John Cam Hobhouse. On 23 August 1841 he was created a baronet. He retired from parliament in August 1842, pending the result of a petition presented against his return. In 1847 he unsuccessfully contested the City of London. He gave evidence in 1848 to the select committee or sugar and coffee planting. He wrote two pamphlets on trade and edited the descriptive journals written home by his half-brother, the lawyer <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/16075/', "Francis Seymour Larpent (1776&#150;1845)")</script>; he also edited the history of Turkey written by his grandfather, Sir James Porter, continuing it and adding a memoir (1854).<br>
<br>Larpent's first wife died on 18 February 1851 at Bath, leaving two sons and a daughter; he married, secondly on 17 July 1852, Louisa Martha, daughter of George Bailey of Lincolnshire; she died at Marseilles on 23 March 1856. They had a son, Seymour George Larpent. Sir George Larpent died at Conduit Street, Mayfair, London, on 8 March 1855.<br>
<br>Anita McConnell </div>
