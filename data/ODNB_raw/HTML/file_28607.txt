<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Walsh,  Edward</span>  (1756&#150;1832), <span class="occ">physician</span>, was born in Waterford, Ireland, the eldest son of John Walsh (1720&#150;1785), a merchant of Ballymountain House, co. Waterford. <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/28618/', "Robert Walsh (1772&#150;1852)")</script> was his younger brother. After early education at Waterford, he studied medicine at Edinburgh and at Glasgow, where he graduated MD in 1791.<br>
<br>Before leaving Waterford, Walsh founded a literary society there, an account of which by him appeared anonymously in the <span class="italic">British Magazine</span> in 1830  (pp. 99&#150;105). A poem by him won a silver medal offered by this society, and was also successful some years later after it had been appropriated by one of the competitors for the Dublin College Historical Society medal  (ibid., 102). In 1792 Walsh published a poem, <span class="italic">The Progress of Despotism: a Poem on the French Revolution</span>, which was dedicated to Charles James Fox, and a year later a collection of his work entitled <span class="italic">Bagatelles</span> appeared. About the same time he published in the <span class="italic">Anthologia Hibernica</span> a proposal for a universal alphabet.<br>
<br>Walsh began his professional career as medical officer on a West Indian packet. He was afterwards physician to the forces in Ireland during the 1798 rising, and was present at the battles in Wexford in 1798, and at the surrender of Humbert at Ballinamuck. Walsh also served in the Holland expedition of 1799, of which he later wrote an account, and at the attack on Copenhagen (2 April 1801), where his hand was shattered. He was afterwards sent with the 49th regiment to Canada, where he spent some years studying Native American life. He collected a vast amount of information for a statistical history of Canada, but never published the work. He was present during most of the battles of the Peninsular War, and at Waterloo, and also served in the Walcheren expedition. He held for some time the post of president of the medical board at Ostend. He died on 7 February 1832 at Summerhill, Dublin.<br>
<br>D. J. O'Donoghue<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Patrick Wallis </div>
