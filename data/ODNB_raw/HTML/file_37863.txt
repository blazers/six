<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Price,  Dennis</span> <span class="st"> [<em>real name </em>Dennistoun John Franklin Rose Price]</span>  (1915&#150;1973), <span class="occ">actor</span>, was born on 23 June 1915 in Ruscombe, Berkshire, the younger son and second of the three children of Brigadier-General Thomas Rose Caradoc Price (1875&#150;1949), an army officer descended from a Cornish baronet's family, the Prices of Trengwainton, and his wife, Dorothy Patience, daughter of Sir Henry Verey, official referee of the Supreme Court of Judicature. He was educated at Radley College, Oxfordshire, and read theology at Worcester College, Oxford, where he joined the Oxford University Dramatic Society. On leaving Oxford without a degree he studied for the stage at the Embassy Theatre School, and made his London d&eacute;but at the Queen's Theatre on 6 September 1937 with John Gielgud in Shakespeare's <span class="italic">Richard II</span>.<br>
<br>In 1939 Price married the actress Joan Schofield, daughter of Major-General Arthur Cecil Temperley of Beaconsfield, Buckinghamshire. They had two daughters. The marriage was soon undermined by Price's homosexuality and alcoholism.<br>
<br>After being invalided out of the Royal Artillery in 1942, Price joined the company of No&euml;l Coward, who was so impressed by his charm that in 1943 he gave him the leading role of Charles Condamine in his play <span class="italic">Blithe Spirit</span>, at the Duchess Theatre in London. In 1944 he won his first starring role on screen in <span class="italic">A Canterbury Tale</span>, directed by Michael Powell. Tall, handsome, and urbane, he swiftly became one of Britain's leading international film stars. He played the title role in <span class="italic">The Bad Lord Byron</span> (1948), was chosen by Ivor Novello for the lead in the Technicolor film of Novello's musical <span class="italic">The Dancing Years</span> (1949), and reached the peak of his career with <span class="italic">Kind Hearts and Coronets</span> (1949), in which he was supremely sardonic as the heartless murderer of eight ducal cousins, all played by Alec Guinness.<br>
<br>Joan Schofield divorced Price in 1950, and the British film recession brought a sharp decline in his popularity. On 19 April 1954 he was found unconscious in his gas-filled flat in Egerton Gardens, Kensington, but the dramatic publicity generated by his attempted suicide led to a revival in his fortunes. He starred on the South African stage in <span class="italic">Separate Tables</span> (1957), made an acclaimed Broadway d&eacute;but as Hector Hushabye in <span class="italic">Heartbreak House</span> (1959), and had further notable film roles in <span class="italic">Private's Progress</span> (1955), <span class="italic">The Naked Truth</span> (1957), <span class="italic">I'm All Right, Jack</span> (1959), <span class="italic">Tunes of Glory</span> (1960), and <span class="italic">Tamahine</span> (1963).<br>
<br>In 1965 Price's nonchalant interpretation of the butler Jeeves in the BBC television series <span class="italic">The World of Wooster</span> delighted its creator, P. G. Wodehouse, who felt that Price had &#145;that essential touch of Jeeves mystery&#146;.<br>
<br>Troubles with the Inland Revenue caused Price's strategic withdrawal in 1966 from his London home in Curzon Street, Mayfair, to settle on the channel island of Sark. He was declared bankrupt in 1967. He died from cirrhosis of the liver at the Princess Elizabeth Hospital, St Martin's, Guernsey, on 6 October 1973.<br>
<br>Michael Thornton<span class="italic">, rev. </span>
