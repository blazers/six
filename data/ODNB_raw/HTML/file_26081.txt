<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Spark,  Thomas</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1655, <em>d.</em> 1692), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman and classical scholar</span>, was baptized on 21 June 1655 at Northop, Flintshire, the youngest child of Archibald Spark (<i>c</i>.1610&#150;1670), vicar of Northop, and his wife, Katharine Jones. From Westminster School, where he was a king's scholar between 1668 and 1672, Spark hopefully addressed Joseph Williamson with Latin poems on &#145;Rome and other subjects&#146;  (<span class="italic">CSP dom.</span>, <i>1670</i>, 643). He was elected to Christ Church, Oxford, in May 1672 (matriculating on 17 July), held a Bostock exhibition, and was tutored by Richard Roderick. He graduated BA in 1676 and proceeded MA in 1679. He enjoyed Dean John Fell's generosity but disparaged it in a begging letter sent to his old schoolmaster Richard Busby. Fell nominated him to deliver the first annual Bodleian oration, endowed by John Morris, on 8 November 1682. He was Greek praelector (1682&#150;3) and senior censor (1684).<br>
<br>Spark's published scholarship is unimpressive. His editions of Herodian (1678) and Zosimus (1679)&#151;possibly two of the &#145;New year books&#146; whose publication Fell sponsored&#151;deserve the same criticism Thomas Hearne makes of his edition of Lactantius (1684): &#145;a poor Performance, the Text being very uncorrect and the Notes from MSS. very mean, he having taken no pains to collate them accurately&#146;  (<span class="italic">Remarks</span>, 2.71). A Latin poem by Spark appears in <span class="italic">Pietas universitatis Oxoniensis &hellip; Caroli II</span> (1685). Two rather longer ones were printed posthumously in <span class="italic">Musarum Anglicanarum analecta</span> (vol. 2, 1699): the first on the restoration of St George's hall, Windsor Castle, with a concluding attack on Shaftesbury and rebellion; the second on Christ Church's newly recast bell Great Tom.<br>
<br>Wood describes Spark as &#145;confident and forward without measure&#146;  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span>, 2.893), and, as chaplain to Lord Chancellor George Jeffreys, his advancement in the church may have seemed assured. He became prebendary of Lichfield (9 April 1686), rector of Ewhurst, Surrey (1 March 1687), and of Norton, Leicestershire, and prebendary of Rochester (2 June 1688). However, on 12 September 1687 Oxford's convocation denied Jeffreys's request, by 118 votes to 53, that Spark and his other chaplain, Luke de Beaulieu, receive DD degrees. Spark did become BD on 18 February 1688 and eventually obtained his DD degree in 1691. That year he published in Oxford <span class="italic">A Sermon Preached at Guildford</span>. He married Dorothy Blaker at Chidnam, Sussex, on 16 April 1691. Having gone to Bath for his health, he died there on 7 September 1692 and was buried in Bath Abbey four days later. Wood attributes his early death to &#145;his Excesses and too much Agitation in obtaining Spiritualities&#146;  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span>, 2.893). His widow was granted letters of administration on 31 October 1692.<br>
<br>Hugh de Quehen </div>
