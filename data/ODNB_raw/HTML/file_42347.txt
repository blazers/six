<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Cumming,  Constance Frederica  Gordon-</span>  (1837&#150;1924), <span class="occ">travel writer</span>, was born on 26 May 1837 at Altyre, Moray, twelfth child and youngest daughter of Sir William Gordon Gordon-Cumming of Altyre and Gordonstown, second baronet (1787&#150;1854), and his first wife, Eliza Maria (<i>d</i>. 1842), eldest daughter of John Campbell the younger of Shawfield and Islay. In addition to her eleven siblings (one of them the lion hunter <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/6899/', "Roualeyn Gordon-Cumming")</script>) Constance had three half-siblings from her father's second marriage. She was educated at home and at school in Fulham, Middlesex. In 1867 she was invited to spend a year with a married sister in India and this proved the start of twelve years of travel and a longer period of travel writing. She was well off and well connected, and &#145;her globe trotting took on the air of a series of rather far-flung social calls&#146;  (Robinson, 94). After a year in India with her sister and brother-in-law she spent <span class="italic">Two Happy Years in Ceylon</span> (2 vols., 1892), where she knew the bishop of Colombo. She then found herself <span class="italic">At Home in Fiji</span> (2 vols., 1881), the guest of its first governor. In Fiji she met and accepted the invitation of the bishop of Samoa to join him on his rounds, much enjoying <span class="italic">A Lady's Cruise in a French Man-of-War</span> in the South Seas (2 vols., 1882). A visit to the <span class="italic">Granite Crags</span> of California (1884) was followed by <span class="italic">Wanderings in China</span> (2 vols., 1886). After a brief return to Britain in 1879 she set off again for California at the invitation of General and Mrs Ulysses Grant, later finding herself among the <span class="italic">Fire Fountains</span> of Hawaii (2 vols., 1883).<br>
<br>In later years Gordon-Cumming settled in Scotland, where she spent some time arranging for exhibition the numerous watercolours she had produced in her travels and working on travel books and articles which have been described as &#145;almost unreadable, so informative are they&#146;  (Middleton, 5). However, her time and her pen were increasingly taken up with furthering the development of numeral type for blind and sighted Chinese in Mandarin-speaking areas of China, for she had been inspired by her visit to William Hill Murray's mission to blind people on her visit to China. She died in Crieff, Perthshire, on 4 September 1924.<br>
<br>Gordon-Cumming's reputation as a traveller has sunk somewhat and she is not now regarded as being on a par with her acquaintance Isabella Bird. Nor are her competent watercolours the equal of the paintings of her friend Marianne North. Her style of writing is so turgid that her works are generally left out of travel anthologies. None the less, the distances she travelled, the exotic locations she reached, and the sheer quantity of her output gain her a place in the history of travel.<br>
<br>Elizabeth Baigent </div>
