<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lane,  James Hunter</span>  (1806/7&#150;1853), <span class="occ">physician and medical journalist</span>, was admitted a licentiate of the Royal College of Surgeons of Edinburgh in 1829, and graduated MD at Edinburgh University in 1830.<br>
<br>Lane was honorary physician to the Cholera Hospital, Liverpool, during the epidemic of 1831&#150;2, for which he received the thanks of the board of health, and he was physician to the Lock Hospital of the infirmary there in 1833. In 1834 he collaborated with James Manby Gully in a translation, <span class="italic">A Systematic Treatise on Comparative Physiology</span>, from the German of Frederick Tiedemann of Heidelberg. Lane contributed many articles to the medical papers, and for some time edited the <span class="italic">Liverpool Gazette</span>, which later became the <span class="italic">Monthly Archives of the Medical Sciences</span>. In so doing he became well known for his &#145;outspoken and fearless criticism&#146; on health questions. In 1840 he was appointed senior physician of the Lancaster Infirmary, and in the same year he brought out his <span class="italic">Compendium of materia medica and pharmacy, adapted to the London pharmacopoeia, embodying all the new French, American, and Indian medicines, and also comprising a summary of practical toxicology</span>. This was a work of considerable worth in its day. Lane was shortly afterwards elected president of the Royal Medical Society of Edinburgh. He was also a fellow of the Linnean Society.<br>
<br>For the last few years of his life Lane lived in London at 58 Brook Street, Grosvenor Square, and had an excellent practice. His death at Chain Pier Lodge, Brighton, on 23 June 1853, was caused by ascites.<br>
<br>Thomas Seccombe<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Patrick Wallis </div>
