<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lyte,  Thomas</span>  (1568&#150;1638), <span class="occ">genealogist</span>, was born on 17 April 1568, the son and heir of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/17301/', "Henry Lyte (1529?&#150;1607)")</script>, botanist and antiquary of Lyte's Cary, Charlton Mackrell, Somerset, and his second wife, Frances (<i>d</i>. 1589), daughter of John Tiptoft of London. His commonplace book records that he was educated at Sherborne School, Clifford's Inn, and the Middle Temple. Wood claims he spent &#145;several years in academicals&#146; at Oxford without taking a degree  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span>, 3rd edn, 1815, 2.649). He was twice married: on 2 February 1592 he married Frances (<i>d</i>. 1615), daughter of Henry Worth of Worth, Devon; and about 1620 Constance, daughter of George Huntley of Boxwell, Gloucester, and widow of Nicholas Baskerville and Sir John Sidney, became his second wife. His marriages resulted in seven sons and six daughters. The family lived at Lyte's Cary, where Lyte repaired the chapel in 1631, adding heraldry and tablets describing his extensive pedigree. He was active in Somerset politics from 1628 until his death, primarily as a justice of the peace.<br>
<br>Lyte was a devotee of British antiquities, particularly genealogy, and was admired by Camden  (<span class="italic">Britannia</span>, 1695, col. 61). Some of his manuscripts are published in the Harleian Society volume <span class="italic">Wiltshire Visitation Pedigrees</span> (1954). In 1605 he wrote &#145;Britaines monarchie&#146;, a manuscript dedicated to King James whose ancestry it traced. It begins with the &#145;Brittish genealogies and the historie of Brute&#146;, this mythological inclusion justified as the &#145;order which Antiquitie hath left and we by tradition have received&#146;  (BL, Add. MS 59741, fol. 8). The king's descent then continues through the Romans, Saxons, Danes, Normans, Scots, and Picts.<br>
<br>Lyte's principal work was an illustrated copy of the royal genealogy, presented to the king at Whitehall on 12 July 1610. The chart, &#145;contayning the bredth and circumference of twenty large sheets of Paper&#146;, was hung at court &#145;in an especiall place of eminence&#146;  (A. Mundy, <span class="italic">Briefe Chronicle</span>, 1611, 477&#150;8) and engraved in copper for better preservation. It has disappeared, although Lyte's ink copy survives. It assures James of his &#145;most rightfull inheritance&#146;  (BL, Add. MS 48343) by comparisons with Henry VII; the controversial figure of Mary, queen of Scots, is barely mentioned. The king rewarded Lyte with the extravagant gift of a miniature royal portrait set in gold and diamonds, preserved in the British Museum. It is described as &#145;the finest Jacobean jewel in existence&#146;  (H. C. Smith, <span class="italic">Jewellery</span>, 1908, 303&#150;4). Lyte died at Lyte's Cary on 18 September 1638 and was buried the next day at Charlton Mackrell among his forebears.<br>
<br>Peter Sherlock </div>
