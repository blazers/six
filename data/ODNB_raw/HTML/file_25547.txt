<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Sillery,  Charles Doyne</span>  (1807&#150;1837), <span class="occ">poet</span>, born at Athlone, Ireland, on 2 March 1807, was the son of an Irish artillery officer, Captain Charles Doyne Sillery, a native of Drogheda, co. Louth, who died of wounds received at the battle of Talavera in 1809, and Catherine, youngest daughter of Barclay Fyfe of Leith, Midlothian. She later married James Watson of Tonthey Hall, Berkshire. The younger Charles entered the navy at an early age, serving as a midshipman on a voyage to China and India. Delicate health prevented him from following a naval career, and in 1828 he settled in Edinburgh in order to study surgery at the university there. The university records make no mention of him after 1829. Besides three small religious works, entitled respectively <span class="italic">A Discourse on the Sufferings of Our Saviour</span> (1833), <span class="italic">An Essay on the Creation of the Universe</span> (1833), and <span class="italic">The Man of Sorrows</span>, published posthumously, he published the following volumes of verse: <span class="italic">Vallery, or, The Citadel of the Lake</span> (1829), <span class="italic">Eldred of Erin</span> (1830), <span class="italic">The Royal Mariner and other Poems</span> (1834), and <span class="italic">The Exiles of Chamouni</span> (1834). He also contributed verses to the <span class="italic">Edinburgh Literary Journal</span>. He died in Edinburgh on 16 May 1837 as a result of pulmonary consumption. Several of his poems have been included in Scottish literary anthologies.<br>
<br>D. J. O'Donoghue<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Rebecca Mills </div>
