<div class="para">
<span class="headword">King,  John</span>  (1696&#150;1728), <span class="occ">classical scholar and physician</span>, was born at Adstone, Northamptonshire, on 5 August 1696, the eldest son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/15575/', "John King (1652&#150;1732)")</script>, Church of England clergyman, and his second wife, Elizabeth, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Aris (1665/6&#150;1727), widow of the Revd John Eston. He was educated at Eton College and King's College, Cambridge, where he was elected a fellow, graduating BA (1719) and MA (1722). Although he did not take a medical degree, King established himself as a physician in St George's parish, Stamford, Lincolnshire. He soon acquired a great reputation and on 12 August 1724 he was elected to the scholarly and conversational Spalding Gentlemen's Society. Three years later he married Lucy, daughter of Thomas Morice, paymaster of the British forces at Lisbon, his intention then being to settle in London under the direction of his brother-in-law, the physician John Freind.<br>
<br>In 1722 King published an <span class="italic">Epistola ad Johannem Freind</span>, an attack on the remarks of Daniel Wilhelm Triller on Hippocrates' treatises on epidemics. King's edition of <span class="italic">Euripidis Hecuba, Orestes et Phoenissae</span> in the original Greek with Latin translation appeared in 1726, the product of nearly five years' work. In 1748 Thomas Morrell published for use at Eton the same three plays, together with <span class="italic">Alcestis</span>, in which he gave nearly the whole of King's translation and notes. Morrell's contemporary, the antiquary Roger Gale, took a less generous view of King's ability which he thought more that &#145;of a trader than a scholar&#146;  (Gale, 3.80). King intended to move to London but before this could be accomplished he died at Stamford from fever on 12 October 1728. He was buried at Pertenhall, Bedfordshire, his father's former living. He was survived by his wife and their only son, also John, who became rector of Pertenhall (1752&#150;1800) and a fellow of King's College, Cambridge, before his death, aged eighty-five, on 6 October 1812.<br>
<br>W. P. Courtney<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Philip Carter </div>
