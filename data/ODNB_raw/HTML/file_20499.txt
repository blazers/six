<div class="para">
<span class="headword">&Oacute; Cobhthaigh family</span>  (<em>per. </em>1415&#150;1586), <span class="occ">Gaelic poets</span>, were hereditary poets settled during the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries in the barony of Rathconrath, Westmeath. Their work was frequently cited by other poets. Members of the family wrote works which survive or are mentioned in chronicles.<br>
<br>
<a name="cosubject_74855"></a><span class="headword">An Clasach &Oacute; Cobhthaigh</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1415)<span class="occ"></span> was a famous poet and man of learning. Maeleachlainn &Oacute; Cobhthaigh (<i>d</i>. 1429), son of An Clasach, was killed by Edmond Dalton, who had conquered his district. <a name="cosubject_74854"></a><span class="headword">Domhnall &Oacute; Cobhthaigh</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1446)<span class="occ"></span>, another son of An Clasach, was killed, with his two sons, on the island called Cr&oacute;inis in Lough Ennell, Westmeath, by Art &Oacute; Maelsheachlainn and the sons of Fiacha MacGeoghegan. Domhnall was famous as a soldier as well as a poet. One of his poems, of 168 verses, is extant: &#145;Aire riot a mhic Mhurchadha&#146; (&#145;Be cautious, oh son of Murchadh!&#146;). It urges the Leinstermen to resist the English.<br>
<br>
<a name="cosubject_74856"></a><span class="headword">Aedh &Oacute; Cobhthaigh</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1452)<span class="occ"></span>, described by O'Clery as a learned poet, kept a house of hospitality. He died of the plague at Fertullagh, Westmeath. Thomas &Oacute; Cobhthaigh (<i>d</i>. 1474) and Murchadh &#145;the lame&#146; &Oacute; Cobhthaigh (<i>d</i>. 1478) are both mentioned in the chronicles as ollavs (professors of poetry). <a name="cosubject_74857"></a><span class="headword">Tadhg &Oacute; Cobhthaigh</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1554)<span class="occ"></span>, son of another Aedh, wrote a poem of sixty-eight verses in praise of the cross, beginning &#145;Crann seoil na cruinne an chroch naomhtha&#146; (&#145;The holy cross is the mast of the world&#146;), and 100 verses on the death of Brian O'Connor Failghe; both are extant. He was probably also the author of the poem in praise of Manus, son of Black Hugh O'Donnell, beginning &#145;Cia re ccuirfinn s&eacute;d suirghe&#146; (&#145;Who sends gifts of courtship&#146;). It contains twenty stanzas, for each of which O'Donnell gave the poet a mare.<br>
<br>
<a name="cosubject_74858"></a><span class="headword">Uaithne &Oacute; Cobhthaigh</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1556)<span class="occ"></span>, son of William, was murdered, with his wife, at Ballinlig, Westmeath, in 1556. He wrote a poem of 156 verses in praise of James, earl of Desmond, beginning &#145;M&oacute; n&aacute; iarla ainm Sh&eacute;mais&#146; (&#145;Greater than an earl is the name of James&#146;), and a theological poem of 160 verses, beginning &#145;Fada an cuimhne so ar ch&oacute;ir nD&eacute;&#146; (&#145;Long be this remembrance on the justice of God&#146;). <a name="cosubject_77077"></a><span class="headword">Diarmait &Oacute; Cobhthaigh</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1584)<span class="occ"></span> wrote a lament of 150 verses for Uaithne which begins &#145;D&aacute; n&eacute;ll orchra &oacute;s iath Uisnigh&#146; (&#145;Two clouds of woe over the land of Uisneach&#146;). He also wrote five theological poems: &#145;D&iacute;on cloinne a n-&eacute;cc a n-athar&#146; (&#145;Safeguard of children in the death of their father&#146;), a poem of 160 verses; &#145;Fi&uacute; a bheatha b&aacute;s tighearna&#146; (&#145;The cost of life the death of a lord&#146;), of 156 verses; &#145;Mairg as aidhne a n-aghaidh breithimh&#146; (&#145;Alas! pleader is facing the judge&#146;), of 148 verses; &#145;Mairg nach taithigh go teagh r&iacute;ogh&#146; (&#145;Alas! that I did not go to the king's house&#146;), of 156 verses; and &#145;Deacair aidhneas earca r&iacute;ogh&#146; (&#145;A powerful argument the tributes of a king&#146;), of 160 verses. Copies of all these are extant, and some are in the collection of the Royal Irish Academy.<br>
<br>
<a name="cosubject_74859"></a><span class="headword">Muircheartach &Oacute; Cobhthaigh</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1586)<span class="occ"></span> wrote a poem on salvation, of 140 verses, beginning &#145;Dlighidh liaigh leigheas a charaid&#146; (&#145;The right of a physician is the cure of his friend&#146;); one of 148 verses on the death of Garrett Nugent, baron of Delvin, beginning &#145;Mairg is d&aacute;ileamh don digh bhr&oacute;in&#146; (&#145;Alas! that sorrow is attendant on drink&#146;); another, on Christopher Nugent, fourteenth Baron Delvin, of 184 verses, beginning &#145;Geall re hiarlacht ainm bar&uacute;n&#146; (&#145;The name baron is the promise of an earldom&#146;); and one of 124 verses on William Nugent, beginning &#145;Do ghn&iacute; cl&uacute; &aacute;it oighreachda&#146; (&#145;Place of inheritance gives reputation&#146;).<br>
<br>
<a name="cosubject_74860"></a><span class="headword">Donnchadh &Oacute; Cobhthaigh</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1584)<span class="occ"></span> wrote a composition of three Irish verses on penitence, beginning &#145;Acht mar uisge d'&eacute;is a leata&#146; (&#145;Just as water, after it has frozen&#146;), as a translation of three elegiac couplets, three hexameters, and three pentameters in Latin, beginning &#145;Lympha coacta gelu&#146; (&#145;Liquid, condensed by cold&#146;), which he entered into a blank half-column of Oxford, Bodleian Library, MS Rawlinson B 505, a collection of Irish saints' lives (fol. 89<i>v</i>b).<br>
<br>Norman Moore<span class="italic">, rev. </span> D&aacute;ibh&iacute; &Oacute; Cr&oacute;in&iacute;n </div>
