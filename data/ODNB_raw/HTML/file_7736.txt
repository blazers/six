<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Dodd,  George</span>  (1808&#150;1881), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was a journeyman author employed in supplying Victorian publications with the type of factual article for which there was a vast demand. Nothing is known of his parentage and education. During nearly half a century he was known as an industrious and painstaking writer. An aptitude for presenting statistics in an attractive form made him a useful assistant to the publisher Charles Knight. He wrote numerous articles on industrial art in the <span class="italic">Penny Cyclopaedia</span>, the <span class="italic">English Cyclopaedia</span>, and supplements. He edited and wrote much of the <span class="italic">Cyclopaedia of the Industry of All Nations</span> (1851). He contributed to the <span class="italic">Penny Magazine</span>, to <span class="italic">London</span>, to <span class="italic">The Land we Live in</span>, and to several other of Knight's serial publications. Some of his papers were collected and published in volumes, under the titles of <span class="italic">Days at the Factories, or, The Manufacturing Industry of Great Britain Described</span> (1843; repr. 1967) and <span class="italic">Curiosities of Industry</span> (1852). For Knight's <span class="italic">Weekly Volumes</span> he provided an account of <span class="italic">The Textile Manufactures of Great Britain</span> (6 vols., 1844&#150;6). The work by which he was probably best known was an elaborate volume on <span class="italic">The food of London: a sketch of the chief varieties, sources of supply &hellip; and machinery of distribution, of the food for a community of two millions and a half</span> (1856). On Knight's retirement as a general publisher, Dodd joined Messrs Chambers, contributing to their serial publications. He also compiled <span class="italic">Chambers's Handy Guide to London</span> (1862) and <span class="italic">Chambers's Handy Guide to the Kent and Sussex Coasts</span> (1863). For over thirty years he contributed one or more papers annually to the <span class="italic">Companion to the [British] Almanac</span>. He also wrote on locks (1853), the Crimean War (1856), and the Indian &#145;revolt&#146; (as he unusually called it) (1859), and he published two useful summaries of economic progress: <span class="italic">Railways, Steamers, and Telegraphs: a Glance at their Recent Progress and Present State</span> (1867) and <span class="italic">Dictionary of Manufactures, Mining, Machinery, and the Industrial Arts</span> (1871). Dodd was found dead at his home, 133 Torriano Avenue, Kentish Town, London, on 20 or 21 January 1881; his death was, the coroner noted, &#145;accelerated by cold weather&#146;.<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> H. C. G. Matthew </div>
