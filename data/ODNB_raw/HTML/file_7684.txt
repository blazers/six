<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Disney,  John</span>  (1677&#150;1730), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman and moral reformer</span>, was born at Lincoln on 26 December 1677, the son of Daniel Disney (1656&#150;1734) of Swinderbury and his wife, Catherine Fynes (<i>d</i>. 1690). None of his four brothers and four sisters survived childhood. He was educated at grammar school and at a dissenting academy in Lincoln. On 20 May 1698 he married Mary Woodhouse (1677&#150;1763), daughter and heir of William Woodhouse. They had nine children, including John Disney (1700&#150;1771), father of the Unitarian minister, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/7685/', "John Disney (1746&#150;1816)")</script>.<br>
<br>On 19 November 1702 Disney entered the Middle Temple but, according to his biography by his grandson John Disney, published in Kippis's <span class="italic">Biographia Britannica</span>, his legal training was intended only to equip him for service as a JP. He served effectively on the commission of the peace for Lindsey, Lincolnshire, but was removed from the bench at least twice during tory purges in 1711&#150;12. Active as a layman and as a clergyman in the work of the SPCK and the societies for the reformation of manners, he helped establish charity schools in Lincoln and wrote extensively in defence of reform. He published two essays exhorting JPs to enforce the laws against immorality and profaneness; he wrote <span class="italic">A View of Ancient Laws Against Immorality and Profaneness</span> (1729) and planned a &#145;<i>Corpus legum de moribus reformandis</i>&#146;. He also published several sermons, a genealogy of the house of Hanover, and devotional works in both prose and verse.<br>
<br>Disney, who was brought up a dissenter, explained to Archbishop Wake in 1718 that, &#145;as soon as I came of age, I betook myself to the Church of England, and that upon principle and conviction&#146;  (Kippis, 5.257). He was ordained deacon on 15 March 1719 and priest on 20 September 1719 by the bishop of Lincoln, Edmund Gibson, and was immediately afterwards presented to the Lincolnshire livings of Croft and Kirkby-on-Bain. In 1720 he was admitted as a fellow commoner of Magdalene College, Cambridge, and became MA in 1722. In the same year he resigned his country benefices, and was appointed to the important living of St Mary's, Nottingham. He died at Nottingham on 3 February 1730 and was buried near the altar in St Mary's.<br>
<br>J. H. Overton<span class="italic">, rev. </span> John Spurr </div>
