<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Conway,  Roger</span>  (<em>d.</em> <em>c.</em>1360), <span class="occ">Franciscan friar and theologian</span>, was a native of Conwy in north Wales, who probably entered the order in Chester, and was a member of the custody of Worcester. He studied at Oxford and was DTh by 10 February 1355, when&#151;as guardian of the Franciscan convent in Worcester&#151;he received papal permission to live in the London Greyfriars, &#145;for the spiritual recreation of himself and of the nobles of England, who are said to frequent the friary in great numbers&#146;  (<span class="italic">CEPR letters</span>, 3.563). He received the royal alms on behalf of the London Greyfriars on 4 February 1356 and became lector in theology at this convent, subsequently acting (<i>c</i>.1357&#150;8) as provincial of the Friars Minor in England. In London he preached on the poverty of Christ, and was the most prominent, and frequently quoted, defender of mendicant poverty against the attacks of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/9627/', "Richard Fitzralph")</script>, archbishop of Armagh (<i>d</i>. 1360). He is probably identical with the Franciscan who in 1356 took up the challenge, when Fitzralph wagered his <span class="roman">Bible</span> that no friar could find a scriptural text that would prove their claims concerning Christ's mendicancy. With the provincial of the English Dominicans, John Tatenhall, Conway in 1358 led a delegation of friars to Avignon, where they petitioned the pope to compel the English bishops to punish those who led the faithful astray by slandering the friars and by attacking papal powers to grant them pastoral privileges.<br>
<br>Conway's <span class="italic">Defensio religionis mendicantium</span>, a treatise on the hearing of confessions by the friars, was previously regarded as a reply to Fitzralph's <span class="italic">Defensio curatorum</span>, but is now recognized as a refutation of the latter's <span class="italic">De audientia confessionum</span>, a treatise on the papal bull <span class="italic">Vas electionis</span> (1321), composed during the hearing in Avignon <i>c</i>.1358. Incorporating material written before 6 December 1352, since in chapter 7 Clement VI is mentioned as the present pope, whereas in chapter 5 the pope is named as Innocent VI (<i>r</i>. 1352&#150;62), it consists primarily of a defence of the mendicant privilege of hearing confessions, though it also examines the question of dominion.<br>
<br>Conway further composed a <span class="italic">Tractatus de regalia Christi</span>, also known as <span class="italic">Quaestiones tres de Christi paupertate et de dominio temporali</span> (&#145;Three questions on Christ's poverty and on the nature of the dominion he enjoyed in this life&#146;). The work is of interest as suggesting modifications in earlier, primarily Franciscan, concepts of mendicant poverty&#151;presumably because Conway was now arguing on behalf of all four orders of friars&#151;while strenuously resisting Fitzralph's attack on the entire mendicant state.<br>
<br>A further work on mendicant pastoral privileges attributed to Conway, <span class="italic">Intellectus fratrum de constitutione Vas electionis quoad negativam ibidem definitam</span>, is largely identical with the second part of the <span class="italic">Defensio mendicantium</span>. In his writings Conway shifted the emphasis away from poverty and mendicancy, and back to pastoral issues. He procured on 14 July 1359 from Innocent VI a bull reaffirming the papal confirmation of the privileges contained in <span class="italic">Vas electionis</span>. According to John Bale, who gives no source, Conway died in London in 1360 and was buried in the choir of the Franciscan church there. He gave at least three, possibly four, manuscripts containing patristic, homiletic, canonistic, and exegetical works to his native convent in Chester (three are now in the library of Gray's Inn, London), but sold a copy of Thomas Aquinas's commentary on part of the <span class="italic">Sentences</span> of Peter Lombard to Lewis Charlton (<i>d</i>. 1369), a fellow theologian who was later bishop of Hereford.<br>
<br>Katherine Walsh </div>
