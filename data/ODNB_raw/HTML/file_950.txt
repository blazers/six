<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ayrton,  William</span>  (1777&#150;1858), <span class="occ">impresario and writer</span>, was born in London on 24 February 1777, one of the fourteen children of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/948/', "Edmund Ayrton (1734&#150;1808)")</script>, composer, and his wife, Ann (1739&#150;1800), daughter of Benjamin Clay. He was unsuccessful in winning the Gresham professorship of music in 1801; however, in 1807 he was elected a fellow of the Society of Antiquaries, and in 1813 he became a founder member of the Philharmonic Society. In 1817 he was manager of the Italian opera at the King's Theatre, Haymarket. For this season, he went to Paris to engage some singers who were then little known, but whose reputations he helped to establish, including Camporese, Crivelli, and Pasta. With them, he introduced to England works by Paer and Cimarosa and, with particular success for twenty-three performances, Mozart's <span class="italic">Don Giovanni</span> (in Italian). A contentious lawsuit at the end of the season with the litigious lessee of the theatre, Edmund Waters, in which Ayrton sued for &pound;1200, was settled in his favour for &pound;700. His second season, in 1821 (by which time Waters was bankrupt), introduced Rossini's <span class="italic">La gazza ladra</span> to England (also in Italian). Ayrton contributed criticism to the <span class="italic">Morning Chronicle</span> (1813&#150;26) and <span class="italic">The Examiner</span> (1837&#150;51); but his most important journalistic work was as editor of <span class="italic">The Harmonicon</span> (1823&#150;33), an invaluable record of the times that carried full, excellent reviews and included substantial music examples. He also edited <span class="italic">The Sacred Minstrelsy</span> (1834), a two-volume anthology of sacred music for domestic use, <span class="italic">Knight's Musical Library</span> (8 vols., 1834&#150;7), and <span class="italic">The Madrigalian Feast</span> (1838). Among his songs are the popular vocal duets &#145;Fair and Fair&#146; and &#145;Oh! Oh! Memory&#146;. He became a fellow of the Royal Society in 1837. On 17 May 1803 he married Marianne, daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/682/', "Samuel Arnold")</script>; they had at least one son. He died at his home in Bridge Street, Westminster, London, on 8 May 1858 and was buried in Kensal Green cemetery.<br>
<br>John Warrack </div>
