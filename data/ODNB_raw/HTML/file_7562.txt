<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Vere, Sir  Aubrey  De</span> <span class="st"> [<em>formerly </em>Aubrey Vere Hunt]</span>, second baronet  (1788&#150;1846), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was born on 28 August 1788, at Curragh Chase, Adare, co. Limerick, the eldest son of Sir Vere Hunt, first baronet (<i>d</i>. 1818), and his wife, Eleanor (<i>d</i>. 1820), only daughter of William Cecil Pery, Lord Glentworth, bishop of Limerick. He had a private tutor at Ambleside, Westmorland, and then went to Harrow School where he was a contemporary of Byron and Peel. On 12 May 1807 he married Mary (<i>d</i>. 1856), the eldest daughter of Stephen Edward Rice of Mount Trenchard, co. Limerick, and the sister of his closest friend. They had three daughters, two of whom died in adolescence, and five sons, including the poet <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/32797/', "Aubrey Thomas de Vere (1814&#150;1902)")</script>, and the classical scholar <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/32798/', "Sir Stephen De Vere (1812&#150;1904)")</script>.<br>
<br>De Vere succeeded to the baronetcy in 1818, and was a popular landlord. A loyalist, strongly in favour of Catholic emancipation, however, he failed to be elected when he stood for parliament about 1820. He contributed to <span class="italic">Harmonica</span> (1818), and his first major work was <span class="italic">Julian the Apostate</span>, published in 1822 while he was living in London. Another drama, <span class="italic">The Duke of Mercia</span>, followed in 1823.<br>
<br>Nevertheless, De Vere was mainly known as a sonneteer. &#145;The true basis of power&#146; and the &#145;Lamentation of Ireland&#146; were noted. He took the name by which he is known, De Vere, by letters patent on 15 March 1832. About this time, he became close to Wordsworth, who called his sonnets, attached to <span class="italic">Song of Faith</span> (1842), &#145;the most perfect of our age&#146;  (<span class="italic">Mary Tudor</span>, 1884). The drama <span class="italic">Mary Tudor</span> (1847) was written during De Vere's final illness; he died at Curragh Chase on 5 July 1846.<br>
<br>[Anon.]<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Jessica Hinings </div>
