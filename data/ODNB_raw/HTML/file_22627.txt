<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Pottinger,  Israel</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1759&#150;1761), <span class="occ">playwright</span>, of whose early life nothing is known, was first an apprentice to a bookseller named Worral. He then set up for himself in Paternoster Row, London, and planned a variety of periodicals. One of them, the <span class="italic">Busy Body</span>, was published three times a week for 2<i>d</i>. at the Dunciad, Paternoster Row. Oliver Goldsmith contributed to it in 1759. Not meeting with much success, Pottinger then opened a circulating library near Great Turnstile, Holborn, and at Islington performed for a time George Alexander Stevens's popular <span class="italic">Lecture on Heads</span>. He subsequently suffered from a mental disorder, but supported himself in his lucid intervals through his writing. In 1761 he published an unacted comedy called <span class="italic">The Methodist</span>, which he described as &#145;a continuation or completion of the plan of [Samuel] Foote's <span class="italic">Minor</span>&#146;. It was a scurrilous attack on George Whitefield. A third edition appeared within the year. In the same year a farce by Pottinger, entitled <span class="italic">The Humorous Quarrel, or, The Battle of the Greybeards</span>, was performed at Southwark fair, and subsequently published. <span class="italic">The Duenna</span>, a comic opera in three acts, a parody on R. B. Sheridan's play, published in 1776 and &#145;acted by his majesty's servants&#146;, is supposed to have been by Pottinger. A new edition appeared within the year. He was probably also the author of <span class="italic">The Critic</span> (1780), a political burlesque of Sheridan's play of the same name.<br>
<br>G. Le G. Norgate<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Michael Bevan </div>
