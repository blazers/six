<div class="para">
<span class="headword">M&aacute;eldub <span class="st"> [Maildulf]</span></span>  (<em>supp. </em><em>fl. </em>mid-7th cent.), <span class="occ">abbot</span>, may have been the eponymous founder and first abbot of the monastery of Malmesbury. He is a shadowy figure whose very existence is questionable. The earliest reference to him is by Bede, who in his <span class="italic">Historia ecclesiastica</span> states merely that Aldhelm (<i>d</i>. 709/10) was abbot of the monastery <i>quod</i> <i>Maildubi</i> <i>Urbem nuncupant</i> (&#145;which they call the <i>urbs</i> [that is, monastery] of M&aacute;eldub&#146;; Bede, <span class="italic">Hist. eccl.</span>, 5.18). M&aacute;eldub is clearly an Irish name, and Bede might seem to imply that M&aacute;eldub was the founder of a monastery there; an implication seemingly supported by place-name forms such as &#145;Meldubesburg&#146;. An anonymous Irishman, in a letter to Aldhelm, states that Aldhelm had been &#145;nourished by a certain holy man of our race&#146;  (<span class="italic">Aldhelm: Prose Works</span>, letter 6). Subsequent authors such as William of Malmesbury deduced from such evidence that Aldhelm had been taught at Malmesbury by an Irish scholar named M&aacute;eldub, who happened to be the monastery's founder. But Aldhelm nowhere mentions any M&aacute;eldub, and other forms of the name, such as that attested in a charter deemed by some scholars to be trustworthy&#151;namely &#145;Meldunesburg&#146; (<span class="italic">AS chart.</span>, S 71)&#151;suggest that the eponymous founder was named M&aacute;eld&uacute;n rather than M&aacute;eldub. Nothing further is known of M&aacute;eld&uacute;n and his relation to Aldhelm cannot be ascertained. The fact remains, however, that there are various Old Irish words in the <span class="roman">&Eacute;pinal-Erfurt glossaries</span> (<i>c</i>.700), which some scholars have associated with Malmesbury. If M&aacute;eldub's existence could be proved beyond doubt, the fact of an Irish abbot in a monastery in Wessex in the seventh century would be of the greatest significance.<br>
<br>Michael Lapidge </div>
