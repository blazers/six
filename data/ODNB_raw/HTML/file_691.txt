<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Arnot <span class="st"> [<em>formerly </em>Pollock]</span>,  Hugo</span>  (1749&#150;1786), <span class="occ">author</span>, was born Hugo Pollock on 8 December 1749 at Leith, near Edinburgh, the son of Duncan Pollock (<i>d</i>. 1765), merchant, and his wife, Christian Arnot (<i>d</i>. 1773). He changed his name to Arnot on succeeding to his mother's property of Balcormo, Fife. On 8 July 1770 he married Margaret, daughter of William Bennet, excise officer; the couple had five sons and four daughters, three of whom died in infancy. His daughter Christian (1771/2&#150;1852) married <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/23336/', "Peter Reid")</script> with whom she had a son, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/23327/', "David Boswell Reid")</script>, chemist. He became an advocate on 5 December 1772, but his legal career faltered on account of his reputation for irritability, sarcasm, and caustic language. In 1776 he published the satirical <span class="italic">Essay on Nothing</span>, first delivered as a paper to the Edinburgh Speculative Society. His <span class="italic">History of Edinburgh</span> appeared in 1779&#151;a second edition was printed in 1816&#151;and his <span class="italic">Collection of Celebrated Criminal Trials in Scotland</span> was published in 1785; both works were pirated in Ireland. Arnot published the <span class="italic">Collection</span> at his own expense in defiance of the Edinburgh booksellers, and the gross proceeds were &pound;600. In addition he wrote numerous papers on local politics, and in <span class="italic">A Letter to the Heritors</span> (1775) he opposed plans to raise local taxes. His efforts are also said to have prevented the building of the South Bridge in Edinburgh for ten years. Prematurely aged by asthma, Arnot died on 20 November 1786 at his home on St Andrew's Meuse, Edinburgh, and was buried in South Leith cemetery on 25 November. He was a favourite subject with the Edinburgh caricaturist John Kay, who took full advantage of the extreme slimness of Arnot's figure.<br>
<br>[Anon.]<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Philip Carter </div>
