<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Quick,  Henry</span>  (1792&#150;1857), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was born on 4 December 1792 in Zennor, Cornwall, the son of Henry Quick (1754&#150;1805), a miner, and his wife, Margery, <i>n&eacute;e</i> George (1759&#150;1834). Quick spent his life in Zennor, and from an early age wrote rugged verses for the countryside. He attended school until about the age of eight, and his first work was printed in 1826. From 1830 until his death he commemorated in verse all the local calamities and crimes, usually closing each poem with a religious exhortation. Most of his works were printed as broadsides, which he distributed along with the popular journals procured monthly from Penzance which supplied his income. On 25 October 1835 he married Jane Rowe (<i>c</i>.1767&#150;1855), a widow much older than himself, because he &#145;needed someone to care for him&#146;  (Pool, 3).<br>
<br>In 1836 Quick wrote his <span class="italic">Life and Progress</span> in eighty-nine verses. He also printed <span class="italic">A New Copy, &amp;c., on the Glorious Coronation of Queen Victoria</span> (1838), <span class="italic">A New Copy of Verses on the Scarcity of the Present Season and Dreadful Famine in Ireland</span> (1848), and similar minor works both in verse and prose.<br>
<br>An engraving by R. T. Pentreath depicts Quick in curious costume, with a printed sheet in his hand and a basket under his arm, distributing his poems and pamphlets.  (Millett, 36). He died of tuberculosis at his home, Mill Hill Down, Zennor, on 9 October 1857, and was buried in the local churchyard three days later.<br>
<br>G. Le G. Norgate<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Megan A. Stephan </div>
