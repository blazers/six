<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Dibben,  Thomas</span>  (1677/8&#150;1741), <span class="occ">Latin poet and Church of England clergyman</span>, was born in London, the son of Richard Dibben. He was admitted to Westminster School on the foundation in 1692, and elected in 1696 aged eighteen to a scholarship at Trinity College, Cambridge, from where he graduated BA (1699), MA (1703), BD (1710), and DD (1721). On 16 July 1701 he was instituted to the rectory of Great Fontmell, Dorset. He was chaplain to John Robinson, bishop of Bristol and lord privy seal, with whom in 1712 he went to the peace congress of Utrecht. On Robinson's being translated to the see of London in 1714 Dibben was collated to the precentorship of St Paul's Cathedral. He represented the diocese of Bristol in the convocations of 1715 and 1727.<br>
<br>Dibben published two sermons, one of which was preached at Utrecht before the plenipotentiaries in March 1712 on the anniversary of Queen Anne's accession. He acquired considerable celebrity as a Latin poet. He wrote one of the poems printed at Cambridge on the return of William III from the continent in 1697, and translated Matthew Prior's <span class="italic">Carmen seculare</span> for 1700 into Latin verse. Prior later complimented the work of his friend and former schoolfellow, claiming that &#145;most accurate judges will find the translation exceed the original&#146;  (M. Prior, <span class="italic">Poems</span>, 1733, preface). By this date Dibben was a resident of Poultry Compter in the City of London having, as a result of mental ill health, abandoned his family and fortune. He died there on 5 April 1741.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Philip Carter </div>
