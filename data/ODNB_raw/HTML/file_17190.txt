<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lund,  John</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1785), <span class="occ">humorous poet</span>, of Pontefract, is said to have been a barber in that town. Little else is known of his life, although a contemporary declared that his satires &#145;would not disgrace the pen of a Churchill&#146;  (Boothroyd, 495). In 1771 Lund published <span class="italic">The mirrour: a poem, in imitation of C. Churchill, to which are added three tales, in the manner of Prior</span>. In 1777 he published <span class="italic">A collection of original tales in verse, in the manner of Prior; to which is added a second edition of &#145;Ducks and pease, or, The Newcastle rider&#146;</span>. The story is rudimentary, being that of a rider (that is, bagman) who, when airing himself as a person of quality, is suddenly confronted by his master; but it proved extremely popular, passing through numerous editions down to 1838 and was reprinted in Richardson's <span class="italic">Table Book</span> . In 1779 there followed <span class="italic">A collection of oddities in prose and verse, serious and comical, by a very odd author</span>. Also often attributed to Lund is a piece entitled <span class="italic">The Bath Comedians</span>, but its publication date of 1753 casts doubt on the matter. The British Library refers to this piece's author simply as &#145;L., J. M.&#146;.<br>
<br>Thomas Seccombe<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Grant P. Cerny </div>
