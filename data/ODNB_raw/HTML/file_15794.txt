<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Knox,  William</span>  (1789&#150;1825), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was born on 17 August 1789 at Firth, parish of Lilliesleaf, Roxburghshire, the eldest son of Thomas Knox, a farmer, and his wife, Barbara (<i>n&eacute;e</i> Turnbull), the widow of Mr Pott of Todrig. After schooling at Lilliesleaf parish school and Musselburgh grammar school he farmed unsuccessfully at Wrae, near Langholm, Dumfriesshire, from 1812 to 1817. He &#145;became too soon his own master&#146;, wrote Scott, &#145;and plunged into dissipation and ruin&#146;  (<span class="italic">Journal</span>, 29&#150;30). His farming career over, Knox returned to his parents at Todrig. In 1820 the family settled in Edinburgh, and Knox became a writer with the support of John Wilson and Scott, who frequently gave him money.<br>
<br>As well as contributing to journals such as the <span class="italic">Literary Gazette</span>, Knox published <span class="italic">The Lonely Hearth</span> (1818), <span class="italic">The Songs of Israel</span> (1824), and <span class="italic">The Harp of Zion</span> (1825). &#145;Mortality&#146;, in <span class="italic">The Songs of Israel</span>, was reputedly a favourite with President Lincoln. He died in Edinburgh, a few days after a stroke, on 12 November 1825.<br>
<br>T. W. Bayne<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Sarah Couper </div>
