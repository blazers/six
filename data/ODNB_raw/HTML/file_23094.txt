<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ramsay,  Laurence</span>  (<em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>1577&#150;1588), <span class="occ">poet</span>, consistently used his verses to castigate puritan sectarians and Catholics alike. About 1577 he venomously attacked &#145;the pestilent Pelagians, Arians, and Anabaptists, besides Atheists, &amp; Papists&#146; in a poem of eighty-two seven-line stanzas in the form of a monologue spoken by Satan entitled <span class="italic">The practise of the divell &hellip; in his papistes, against the true professors of Gods holie worde, in these our latter dayes</span>, published in London by J. Charlewood for Timothie Rider. In 1579 the same bookseller distributed a broadside by Ramsay, entitled &#145;A Short Discourse of Mans Fatall End with an Unfaygned, Commendation of Syr Nicholas Bacon&#146;, which begins &#145;Since God hath fyxt our dayes and yeares, to live and else to dye&#146;. This thirty-six-line poem, written in couplets and commemorating the death of Bacon, celebrates the dead knight in part for the fact that &#145;no patch of popish mynde in him was ever found&#146;. In later life Ramsay seems to have been attached to the household of Robert Dudley, earl of Leicester, who favoured an aggressively anti-Catholic foreign policy. After Leicester's death, Ramsay wrote &#145;Ramsies farewell to his late lorde and master therle of Leicester, which departed this worlde at Cor'burye the 4 Sept. 1588&#146;, which was licensed to Edward Aggas on 7 October 1588. No copy is now known. Also lost is Ramsay's &#145;Wisshinge and Wouldynge&#146;, for which Edward White obtained a licence on 5 August 1583. It was possibly a poem resembling &#145;I would and I would not&#146; by Nicholas Breton.<br>
<br>The <span class="italic">Dictionary of National Biography</span> identifies Ramsay with the Laurence Ramsay who joined a body of sectarians meeting at Faversham, Kent, in 1550 and who advocated Anabaptism and Pelagianism  (Strype, <span class="italic">Ecclesiastical Memorials</span>, 2/1, 370). However, in the light of Ramsay's explicit attacks on &#145;Anabaptistes and &hellip; Pelagians&#146; as &#145;sectes diabolicall&#146;, either this identification seems unlikely, or represents a religious position that (twenty years later) Ramsay had come to regret  (Ramsay, <span class="italic">Practise of the Divell</span>).<br>
<br>Cathy Shrank </div>
