<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Prior, Sir  James</span>  (<em>c.</em>1790&#150;1869), <span class="occ">writer</span>, son of Matthew Prior, was born at Lisburn, co. Antrim, about 1790. He entered the navy as a surgeon, and sailed from Plymouth in the <i>Nisus</i> frigate on 22 June 1810 for the Cape of Good Hope, Mauritius, the Seychelles Islands, Madras, and Java (at the capture of which by the British in September 1811 he was present). This journey he described in a <span class="italic">Voyage in the Indian Seas</span> (1820). His next expedition (1812&#150;13), in the same frigate, was to South Africa, Brazil, and other areas, and was described in a <span class="italic">Voyage Along the Eastern Coast of Africa</span> (1819).<br>
<br>Prior was present at the surrender of Heligoland and, in 1815, at the surrender of Napoleon. He then became staff surgeon to the Chatham division of the Royal Marines, and to three of the royal yachts. In 1817 he married Dorothea, widow of E. James. She died at Oxford Terrace, Hyde Park, London, on 28 November 1841.<br>
<br>Prior was appointed assistant to the director-general of the medical department of the navy, and on 1 August 1843 he was created deputy inspector of hospitals. In 1847 he married Caroline, widow of Charles H. Watson. He was knighted at St James's Palace on 11 June 1858. In 1830 he had been elected to the Royal Irish Academy, the Athenaeum, and the Society of Antiquaries. For many years before his death he lived at 20 Norfolk Crescent, Hyde Park, London; but he died at Brighton on 14 November 1869, survived by his widow who died on 14 December 1881 aged eighty-five.<br>
<br>Prior's chief works were biographies. His <span class="italic">Memoir of the Life and Character of Edmund Burke</span> (1824) ran to several editions and was quite well regarded. His <span class="italic">Life of Oliver Goldsmith</span> (2 vols., 1837) and his <span class="italic">Miscellaneous Works of Goldsmith</span> (4 vols., 1837) were assiduously compiled. When John Forster (1812&#150;1876) brought out <span class="italic">The Life and Adventures of Oliver Goldsmith</span> (1848) he was accused by Prior of wholesale plagiarism, an accusation which Forster consistently denied. Washington Irving, in his <span class="italic">Life of Goldsmith</span> (1849), admitted his obligations to &#145;the indefatigable Prior&#146;, who none the less denounced him in <span class="italic">Goldsmith's Statue</span>.<br>
<br>W. P. Courtney<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Elizabeth Baigent </div>
