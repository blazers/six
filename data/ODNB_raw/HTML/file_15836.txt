<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lablache,  Frederick</span>  (1815&#150;1887), <span class="occ">singer</span>, eldest son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/15837/', "Luigi Lablache (1795&#150;1858)")</script> and his wife, Teresa Pinotti, was born in Naples on 29 August 1815, and trained by his father. He made his d&eacute;but in 1835 at the Th&eacute;&acirc;tre Italien, Paris, as the Count in Bellini's <span class="italic">La sonnambula</span>, and his London stage d&eacute;but at the Lyceum in Donizetti's <span class="italic">L'elisir d'amore</span> (16 November 1837). Until 1852 he sang bass-baritone parts in some of his father's London and Paris seasons; at Naples in 1836 he created the part of Edoardo III in Donizetti's <span class="italic">L'assedio di Calais</span>. Most of his career was in the British Isles. In the late 1840s he appeared in Jenny Lind's highly successful provincial tours, managed by Michal Balfe; the critic J. W. Davison then called him &#145;a clever intelligent artist&#146;. He also toured with his wife, the contralto <a name="cosubject_15835"></a><span class="headword">Fanny Wyndham Lablache</span> <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Frances Wilton]</span>  (1821?&#150;1877)<span class="occ"></span>, a Scot who had studied at the Royal Academy of Music in London (1836&#150;7) and then made her d&eacute;but at Covent Garden (<span class="italic">Quasimodo</span>, February 1836), afterwards appearing at Her Majesty's. They both retired from the stage about 1865 and taught at the Royal Academy of Music. Fanny died in Paris on 23 September 1877, Frederick at their home, 51 Albany Street, Regent's Park, London, on 30 January 1887. Their son Luigi (or Louis), an actor, died in 1914.<br>
<br>G. C. Boase<span class="italic">, rev. </span> John Rosselli </div>
