<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Herring,  Francis</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1628), <span class="occ">physician</span>, a native of Nottinghamshire, matriculated from Christ's College, Cambridge in 1582, graduating BA in 1585, and MA in 1589. On 3 July 1599, two years after obtaining his MD, he was admitted a fellow of the College of Physicians. He was censor in 1609, 1618, 1620, 1623, 1624, 1626, and 1627. He was named an elect on 5 June 1623.<br>
<br>Herring's first published work was <span class="italic">Anatomyes of the True Physition and Counterfeit Mounte-Banke</span> (1602), a translation from the work in Latin by Johann Oberndoerffer. He took the opportunity to add, by way of appendix, &#145;A short discourse, or discovery of certaine stratagems, whereby our London-empericks have bene observed strongly to oppugne, and oft times to expugne their poore patients purses&#146;.<br>
<br>Herring's later writings concentrate on the plague and on anti-Catholic propaganda. In <span class="italic">Certaine rules, directions or advertisements for this time of pestilentiall contagion: with a caveat to those that weare impoisoned amulets</span> (1603, rev. edns 1625 and 1665), he asserted that although the plague was a punishment from God, there were practical precautions that could be taken. These included cleaning the streets, burying the dead outside the city, fumigation, and keeping people away from public places. He also noted that arsenic amulets were of no use. In 1604 he published <span class="italic">A Modest Defence of the Caveat Given to the Wearers of Impoisoned Amulets</span>.<br>
<br>Herring's other interest was verse of an anti-Catholic bent. His <span class="italic">Pietas pontificia</span> (1605), dealing with the Gunpowder Plot, appeared in several editions and was translated into English twice, as <span class="italic">Popish Pietie</span> (1610) and <span class="italic">Mischeefes Mysterie</span> (1617). Herring died in the early part of 1628.<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Sarah Bakewell </div>
