<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Richardson <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Beaumont; <em>other married name </em>Ashburnham]</span>,  Elizabeth</span>, <i>suo jure</i> baroness of Cramond  (1576/7&#150;1651), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was the eldest of the ten children of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/47987/', "Sir Thomas Beaumont (d. 1614)")</script> [<i>see under </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/47915/', "Beaumont, Huntingdon")</script>] of Stoughton, Leicestershire, and St Botolph, Aldersgate, London, and his wife, Catherine (<i>d</i>. 1621), daughter and heir of Sir Thomas Farnham of Stoughton and his wife, Helen. On 27 November 1594 she married, as her first husband, John Ashburnham (<i>c</i>.1571&#150;1620) of Ashburnham, Sussex, who was knighted in 1604. They had ten children, six of whom lived to adulthood, including John (1603&#150;1671) and William (<i>d</i>. 1679). Elizabeth's husband's death on 29 June 1620 left the family in financial straits, but his widow was able to exercise considerable influence at court since Mary, countess of Buckingham, mother of the favourite, was a cousin. Elizabeth's attempts to gain advancement for her son-in-law Sir Edward Dering ended eventually in a baronetcy on 1 February 1627. A letter to the duke of Buckingham of the same year indicates that she enjoyed the society of his wife, of Lady Carlisle, and of the queen  (<span class="italic">CSP dom.</span>, <i>1627&#150;8</i>, 326); in 1627 she also waited on the queen of Bohemia in The Hague.<br>
<br>On 14 December 1626 Elizabeth married, as his second wife, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/23584/', "Sir Thomas Richardson (bap. 1569, d. 1635)")</script>, later lord chief justice, and through his influence was created baroness of Cramond on 29 February 1628, an occurrence which apparently elicited &#145;many gibes and pasquinades &hellip; for the amusement of Westminster Hall&#146;  (Campbell, 2.15). On 9 September 1629 she was granted an annual pension of &pound;300 for the duration of her life. In 1645 her collection of prayers, <span class="italic">A Ladies Legacie to her Daughters</span>, was printed. The text was in three parts; the first had been written in 1625 and the second in 1635. Richardson gave a manuscript copy of her prayers to her eldest daughter, Elizabeth Cornwallis, in 1635 (E. Sussex RO, ASH 3501), but a Folger Shakespeare Library manuscript (MS V.a.511), headed &#145;Instructions for my children or any other Christian&#146;, is dated 1606, indicating that she began her writing of mother's advice many years earlier. She died, in her seventy-fifth year, at Covent Garden, London, and was buried next to her first husband on 3 April 1651 at St Andrew's, Holborn.<br>
<br>Victoria E. Burke </div>
