<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Herdson,  Henry</span>  (<em>b. </em><em>c.</em>1611), <span class="occ">writer on mnemonics and memory</span>, matriculated as a sizar from St John's College, Cambridge, in 1626 and graduated BA in 1629&#150;30. According to Venn he may have been rector of Harkstead, Suffolk, in 1632. As self-styled professor of the art of memory (at least &#145;by Publick Authority&#146;, if not according to any more conventional body), he taught in London, at or near to the Green Dragon by St Antholin Church (just east of St Paul's). Shortly before his books appeared (though possibly as early as 1647) he made an appearance at the church of St Dunstan-in-the-East, where he accosted Dr Thomas Fuller as the latter was leaving the pulpit. Fuller, describing the event some ten years later, wrote: &#145;when I came out of the pulpit of St Dunstan's East, one (who since wrote a book thereof) told me in the vestry, before credible people, that he in Sydney College had taught me the art of memory&#146;. Fuller denied this, saying that he did not remember ever having seen Herdson before&#151;&#145;which, I conceive, was a real refutation!&#146; In his <span class="italic">Life of Thomas Fuller</span> (1874) Bailey describes Herdson (perhaps uncharitably but hardly inaccurately) as &#145;the Memory-mountebank&#146;.<br>
<br>Herdson's books both appeared in 1651; the two are generally found bound together. The first, whose dedication is dated 21 October 1651, is the Latin <span class="italic">Ars mnemonica, sive, Herdsonus Bruxiatus, vel, Bruxus Herdsoniatus</span>; this is in fact no more than a republication of part of Adam Brux's <span class="italic">Simonides redivivus</span> (1610). The second (this one in English), is <span class="italic">Ars Memoriae; the Art of Memory Made Plaine</span>, a summary of the principles of the art of mnemonics. According to Herdson, his is an art based on simple precepts; but he is insistent that it should not be less esteemed on these grounds&#151;he considers that &#145;he must needs be malicious and unworthy, that will contemne this Art of Memory for the meanness of the Fundaments thereof&#146;. This second book was reprinted in full in the second edition (1813) of Gregor von Feinaigle's <span class="italic">Art of Memory</span>. Though considered (by Herdson himself at least) his greatest work, it has not been much thought of by posterity; even Feinaigle described it as &#145;in small compass&#146;.<br>
<br>Daniel Hahn </div>
