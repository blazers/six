<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Kettlewell,  Samuel</span>  (1822&#150;1893), <span class="occ">theological writer</span>, born on 31 March 1822, was the son of the Revd William Kettlewell, rector of Kirkheaton, near Huddersfield, and his wife, Mary Midgeley. He matriculated on 24 October 1846 at Durham University, and graduated as a licentiate of theology in 1848. He was ordained deacon in the same year, and priest in 1849 by the bishop of Ripon. He then became a curate at Leeds under Walter Farquhar Hook, and in 1851 he was appointed vicar of St Mark's, Leeds. This, his only incumbency, he resigned in 1870 to devote himself to literary work. He had already published a <span class="italic">Catechism on Gospel History</span> (1851; 3rd edn, 1878), and two works suggested by the controversy over Irish disestablishment: <span class="italic">A Short Account of the Reformation in Ireland</span>, and <span class="italic">Rights and Liberties of the Church</span> (both 1869). His energies were now mainly devoted to his work on Thomas &agrave; Kempis, and in 1877 he published <span class="italic">The Authorship of the &#145;De imitatione Christi&#146;</span>, followed in 1882 by <span class="italic">Thomas &agrave; Kempis and the Brothers of Common Life</span> (2 vols.; 2nd edn, 1884). These two books were the fruit of much research in England, Holland, and Belgium, and remain of importance for &agrave; Kempian studies. In 1888 Kettlewell published <span class="italic">The Basis of True Christian Unity</span> (2 vols.), and in 1892 a translation of the <span class="italic">De imitatione</span>. He had received the Lambeth MA in 1860, and in 1892, in recognition of his work, he was granted the Lambeth DD, the queen countersigning his diploma. He died on 2 November 1893 at his residence, Kesselville House, Eastbourne, where he had retired in 1870; he was twice married, his second wife, Margaret, surviving him.<br>
<br>A. F. Pollard<span class="italic">, rev. </span> H. C. G. Matthew </div>
