<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Wace</span>  (<em>b. </em>after 1100, <em>d.</em> 1174<span class="mult">x</span>83), <span class="occ">historian and poet</span>, was born soon after 1100 on Jersey. Some have considered Wace to have been of noble birth, following a suggestion by Gaston Paris that Toustain, chamberlain of Duke Robert (I) of Normandy, was Wace's maternal grandfather; this has not been substantiated, nor has the misnomer Robert Wace, which dates back to the eighteenth century. According to autobiographical passages in his last work, the <span class="italic">Roman de Rou</span> (<i>c</i>.1160&#150;74), Wace was taken to Caen as a young boy for religious training. He continued his studies in the &Icirc;le-de-France, later returning to Caen where he devoted himself to a literary career; in the <span class="italic">Rou</span>, he refers to himself as a <i>clerc lisant</i> (a reading or teaching cleric), a role whose definition has generated debate among scholars. Although none of the <i>sirventes</i> (lyric poems, often of a satiric or political nature) which Wace says that he wrote appears to survive, three saints' lives are extant from the earlier part of his career: the <span class="italic">Vie de Sainte Marguerite</span> (<i>c</i>.1130&#150;40), the <span class="italic">Conception Nostre Dame</span> (<i>c</i>.1130&#150;40, but probably after the <span class="italic">Marguerite</span>), and the <span class="italic">Vie de Saint Nicolas</span> (<i>c</i>.1150).<br>
<br>In 1155, Wace completed the <span class="italic">Roman de Brut</span>, the oldest extant Old French chronicle of the early kings of Britain. Although Wace called the 14,866 line <span class="italic">Brut</span> a translation, it is more than a translation of a single work. Based largely on the vulgate and first variant versions of Geoffrey of Monmouth's <span class="italic">Historia regum Britanniae</span> (<i>c</i>.1138), the <span class="italic">Roman de Brut</span> contains material from oral sources and possibly from other written sources as well, including the earliest extant reference to King Arthur's round table. In his Middle English adaptation of Wace's <span class="italic">Brut</span> (<i>c</i>.1200), Layamon reports that Wace offered the <span class="italic">Roman de Brut</span> to Eleanor of Aquitaine, consort of Henry II; however, no other evidence has yet been found to support Layamon's claim.<br>
<br>Wace's chronicle of the Norman dukes which he dedicated to Eleanor of Aquitaine and Henry II, the <span class="italic">Roman de Rou</span> (&#145;The Romance of Rollo&#146;), was less popular than his Arthurian chronicle, less stylistically homogeneous, but more synthetic and original. In the <span class="italic">Rou</span>, Wace drew on Latin sources, including the Norman histories of William of Jumi&egrave;ges, William of Poitiers, and Dudo of St Quentin, and the English histories of William of Malmesbury and Eadmer of Canterbury, as well as on oral sources. Unlike Wace's <span class="italic">Brut</span> and the saints' lives which are continuous narratives in octosyllabic rhymed couplets, the <span class="italic">Roman de Rou</span> contains four parts, the first two in alexandrines and the last two in octosyllables. The first part, known as the <span class="italic">Chronique ascendante des ducs de Normandie</span> (315 lines), traces the projected scope of the history in reverse chronological order, from Henry II back to Rollo; the second (4425 lines) describes the founding of the duchy by Rollo, and the history of the early dukes to the peace of 965; the third (11,440 lines) continues the narrative from 965 to the imprisonment of Robert Curthose by his brother, Henry I, following the battle of Tinchebrai in 1106. The fourth part (750 lines), dedicated to the career of the pirate Hasting, is thought to have been the first draft of the poem, later abandoned, and not part of the final poem as Wace intended. Early in the third part of the <span class="italic">Rou</span>, Wace records Henry II's gift of a prebend in Bayeux, which he may have received in payment for the <span class="italic">Brut</span> or as incentive to work on the <span class="italic">Rou</span>; documentary evidence suggests that Wace received the prebend between 1165 and 1169. At the end of the <span class="italic">Rou</span>, Wace reports that the monarch had grown less generous, and that he was removing Wace's commission in favour of a poet named Maistre Beneeit, most often identified as Beno&icirc;t de Ste Maure, author of the <span class="italic">Roman de Troie</span> (<i>c</i>.1160) and the <span class="italic">Chronique des ducs de Normandie</span> (<i>c</i>.1174&#150;80). The transfer may have taken place in or around 1174, since the last recorded event in the <span class="italic">Chronique ascendante</span> can be dated to 1174, but Henry II's son, Henry, the Young King (<i>d</i>. 1183), is mentioned as still alive.<br>
<br>Wace played an important role in the development of the French language through his use of an especially large and varied vocabulary and in the development of Old French narrative, primarily Arthurian romance.<br>
<br>Jean Blacker </div>
