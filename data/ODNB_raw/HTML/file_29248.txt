<div class="para">
<span class="headword">White,  James</span>  (1803&#150;1862), <span class="occ">writer</span>, born in Midlothian in March 1803, was the younger son of John White of Dunmore in Stirlingshire, and his wife, Elizabeth, daughter of John Logan of Howden in Midlothian. After studying at Glasgow University he matriculated from Pembroke College, Oxford, on 15 December 1823, graduating BA in 1827. He served as curate of Hartest-cum-Boxsted in Suffolk, and on 27 March 1833 he was instituted vicar of Loxley in Warwickshire.<br>
<br>In April 1829 White married Rosa (1805&#150;1882), only daughter of Colonel Charles Fitzmaurice Hill. They had two sons and five daughters. On her father's death in 1811 Rosa inherited two farms in Bonchurch on the Isle of Wight. A clause in the will prevented the land from being developed. In 1836 James White had this clause revoked by a private act of parliament, thus enabling him to sell off much of the land for building purposes. He resigned his living in 1839 and retired to the Isle of Wight to write.<br>
<br>White had already written some essays, and between 1845 and 1847 he produced a succession of Scottish historical tragedies, works of some merit, though only moderately successful. Among them were <span class="italic">The Earl of Gowrie</span> (1845) and <span class="italic">Feudal Times</span> (1847). Another tragedy, <span class="italic">John Savile of Haysted</span> (1847), was acted at Sadler's Wells Theatre, London, in 1847. At a later time he brought out several historical sketches of a popular character, written with considerable power of generalization. The best known is <span class="italic">The Eighteen Christian Centuries</span> (1858), which reached a fourth edition in 1864.<br>
<br>White possessed a charming style, and interested his readers by his clearness of thought and his ability in selecting and arranging detail. He was a friend of Charles Dickens, whom he met about 1845 and who thought <span class="italic">The Earl of Gowrie</span> &#145;a work of remarkable genius&#146;  (<span class="italic">Letters of Charles Dickens</span> to White, 24 Feb 1846, in <span class="italic">Letters of Charles Dickens</span>, 4.505). In 1849 Dickens rented a house at Bonchurch for some months on James White's recommendation to be near him. <span class="italic">John Savile of Haysted</span> was dedicated to Dickens. White also translated some of Schiller's works, and wrote a joint biography of Burns and Scott (1858), and various other works. White died in Bonchurch on 26 March 1862, his wife surviving him, and was buried in the churchyard of Bonchurch parish church on 31 March 1862.<br>
<br>E. I. Carlyle<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Janet Tedman </div>
