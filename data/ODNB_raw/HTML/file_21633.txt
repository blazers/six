<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Paxton,  John</span>  (<em>c.</em>1740&#150;1780), <span class="occ">painter</span>, was born in Edinburgh, the third child of John Paxton (<i>c</i>.1697&#150;1787) of Berwickshire and his wife, Helen (<i>d</i>. 1783), daughter of William Adams, an Edinburgh printer. John Paxton senior was chief clerk to Archibald Stewart, a rich Edinburgh wine merchant of Berwick origin, MP, and lord provost of Edinburgh at the time of the Jacobite rising of 1745. Paxton was a student at Foulis art academy in Glasgow and subsequently studied in Rome. He was one of the original members of the Incorporated Society of Artists, and signed their declaration roll in 1766. In that year he sent to their exhibition from Rome <span class="italic">Samson in Distress</span>. In 1769 and 1770 he exhibited portraits at the Royal Academy, and in the latter year settled in Charlotte Street, Rathbone Place, London, where he had considerable practice as a portrait painter.<br>
<br>Paxton continued to exhibit with the Society of Artists, of which he was director in 1775, sending chiefly portraits, but also scriptural, classical, and historical subjects. His brother <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/53259/', "Sir William Paxton")</script>, merchant and banker, must have convinced him that he would find ample and lucrative employ for his artistic talents in India. In March 1776 he asked the directors of the East India Company if he might go &#145;to the East Indies to provide for himself in the way of his profession&#146;  (BL OIOC). In 1777 the portraitist Catherine Read, then active in Madras, received a not very appreciative account of her colleague from one of her correspondents: &#145;We have now another [artist] nam'd Paxton, but he is a very indifferent hand and yet gets employment&#146;  (Archer, 471).<br>
<br>Paxton painted a portrait of Signorina Zamperini as Cechina. A portrait by him of his fellow pupil James Tassie is in the Scottish National Portrait Gallery at Edinburgh. Paxton is alluded to in the fable of &#145;The Bee-Flower&#146; in John Langhorne's <span class="italic">The Fables of Flora</span> (1771). He died, unmarried, in Bombay on 19 February 1780. In <span class="italic">RA Exhibitors</span>, Graves lists further works exhibited between 1802 and 1807 by John Paxton, but these were presumably by a different artist of the same name.<br>
<br>L. H. Cust<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Willem G. J. Kuiters </div>
