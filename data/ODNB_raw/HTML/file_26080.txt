<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Spalding,  William</span>  (1809&#150;1859), <span class="occ">author</span>, was born in Aberdeen on 22 May 1809, son of James Spalding, advocate, of Aberdeen, and his wife, Frances Read. He graduated MA at Marischal College, Aberdeen, in 1827 and was afterwards writer to the signet for some years in Edinburgh, where he passed advocate in 1833. In the same year he published a notable <span class="italic">Letter on Shakespere's Authorship of the Two Noble Kinsmen</span>, which was reissued in 1876 and 1994. He made an extensive study of Shakespearian and Elizabethan drama, and in the 1840s contributed several articles on the subject to the <span class="italic">Edinburgh Review</span>. On 22 March 1838 he married Agnes Frier; they had a daughter, Mary, who was his constant literary companion and amanuensis.<br>
<br>After having failed to get the chair of logic at the University of Edinburgh in 1836, Spalding was elected to the chair of rhetoric and <i>belles-lettres</i> there on 2 November 1840. In 1841 he published <span class="italic">Italy and the Italian Islands</span> (3 vols.), the fruits of his earlier travels in Italy. On 22 October 1845 he became professor of logic, rhetoric, and metaphysics at St Andrews, a post he held until his death on 16 November 1859, at South Street, St Andrews, after a long period of delicate health.<br>
<br>Spalding was short and plain in appearance and he suffered from chest complaints. He was a popular, if exacting, teacher and wrote on numerous literary, biographical, and historical subjects. His most important work, other than those mentioned above, was his <span class="italic">History of English literature, with an outline of the origin and growth of the English language</span> (1853) which went to numerous English editions and was translated into German. His work in many subjects was much praised in his lifetime and in the years immediately following his death.<br>
<br>J. M. Rigg<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Elizabeth Baigent </div>
