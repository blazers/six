<span class="headword">Mare,  William  de la</span> <span class="st"> [William  de Mara]</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1272&#150;1279), <span class="occ">Franciscan friar and theologian</span>, is of unknown origins. Little is known of the events of his life. He studied theology in Paris, where in the early 1270s he commented on the <span class="italic">Sentences</span> (probably 1272&#150;4) and then was regent master (probably 1274&#150;5). The <span class="italic">Sentences</span> commentary (books 1 and 2, ed. H. Kraml, 1989, 1995; manuscripts include Florence, Biblioteca Nazionale, Z. MS Conv. soppr. A.2.727, and Toulouse, Biblioth&egrave;que Municipale, MS 252, which also contain parts of books 3 and 4) is a largely unoriginal work, closely based on Bonaventure's commentary and also using the commentary by John Pecham, who may have been William's teacher. William also preached sermons in Paris and produced a set of <i>quaestiones disputatae</i>.<br>
<br>William de la Mare then returned to England. Recorded as preaching to the Franciscan convent at Lincoln, between 1277 and 1279 he produced a critique of Aquinas, his <span class="italic">Correctorium fratris Thomae</span>, claiming that many of Thomas's positions&#151;for instance, that man has no direct intellectual cognition of singulars, that it cannot be demonstrated that the world had a beginning in time, and that the intellective soul is the one substantial form of a man&#151;were heretical. William wrote this <span class="italic">Correctorium</span> before August 1279. Later he extended and revised it  (Vatican City, Biblioteca Apostolica Vaticana, MS Vat. lat. 4413). A much shorter form of the work (<span class="italic">Declarationes magistri Guilelmi de la Mare OFM de variis sententiis S. Thomae Aquinatis</span>, ed. F. Pelster, 1956, from Assisi, Biblioteca Comunale, MS 174), edited as William's original version, has been shown to be an abbreviation by an unknown writer. The <span class="italic">Correctorium</span> was very influential. In 1282, at their general chapter at Strasbourg, the Franciscans decided that members of their order should not read Aquinas's <span class="italic">Summa</span> unless accompanied by the <span class="italic">Correctorium</span>. For the Dominicans' part, four detailed replies to it had been written before 1284&#151;the authors of these anonymous works were probably Jean de Paris, Robert Orford, Richard Knapwell, and William of Macclesfield. A text of William's <span class="italic">Correctorium</span> is found within one of these replies, which quotes it so as to respond to it (<span class="italic">Le correctorium corruptorii quare</span>, ed. P. Glorieux, 1927).<br>
<br>William de la Mare was also one of the most learned and judicious of the thirteenth-century scholars who tried to improve the text of the <span class="roman">Vulgate Bible</span>. Using his knowledge of both Greek and Hebrew, he wrote a <span class="italic">Correctorium Bibliae</span>  (Vatican City, Biblioteca Apostolica Vaticana, MS Vat. lat. 4240), in which he sensibly refrains from assuming that the Latin text must be corrected whenever it departs from the Hebrew or Greek. He also used his linguistic knowledge to write <span class="italic">De Hebraeis et Graecis vocabulis glossarum Bibliae</span>  (Toulouse, Biblioth&egrave;que Municipale, MS 402; Florence, Biblioteca Laurenziana, MS Plut. xxv sin. cod. 4).<br>
<br>John Marenbon </div>
