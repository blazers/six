<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lindley <span class="st"> [Lyndeley]</span>,  William</span>  (1739&#150;1819), <span class="occ">architect</span>, was born on 4 June 1739, possibly at Heath, near Wakefield, the eldest of five children. About 1754 he joined the office of the important provincial architect John Carr of York, as draughtsman; he engraved Carr's stables at Harewood House, Yorkshire, in 1758.<br>
<br>A neo-classical architect, Lindley derived his simplified and unadventurous style from James Paine, Robert Adam, and Carr himself. After twenty years with Carr he advertised his architectural skills in the <span class="italic">York Chronicle</span> on 18 October 1773. As a result, the corporation of Doncaster appointed him to design a new theatre in 1774, which he completed in 1774&#150;5 (dem.); soon afterwards he moved to Doncaster and was made a freeman in 1783. For the corporation Lindley undertook whatever was required in the way of public architecture: he enlarged Paine's mansion house (1800&#150;01, 1805) and Carr's racecourse grandstand (1804; dem. 1969), improved the town hall (1784&#150;6; dem. 1846), and designed a gaol (1779; dem.) and a dispensary (1793&#150;4; dem. 1969). He was also responsible for civic architecture in Sheffield, where he designed the market (1784&#150;6; dem. 1851) and the Ecclesall court house and gaol (1791; dem.).<br>
<br>Lindley also built domestic architecture, lining the streets of Doncaster with handsome late Georgian houses; one, his own, provided lodging for the prince of Wales when he was attending the races. He designed others at Sheffield, Bawtry, Knaresborough, and Leeds. Wakefield gave him the opportunity to design a &#145;new&#146; town of stately terraces centred on a classical church (1791&#150;5; with Charles Watson). His grandest town house was undoubtedly Denison Hall (1786&#150;8), built for John Denison, one of Leeds's wealthiest merchants. Lindley went on to design alterations to Denison's country house, Ossington Hall, Nottinghamshire (1788&#150;90, 1805&#150;6; dem. 1963). Lindley's letters to Bryan Cooke of Owston, Yorkshire, in which he constantly pressed Cooke to build a new house by reference to his work for others, enable a number of other country houses to be attributed to him. These include: Ferham House, Rotherham (1781), for Jonathan Walker; Thundercliffe Grange (Grange Hall), near Ecclesfield (1794&#150;5), for the fourth earl of Effingham; and Newhill Hall, Wath-on-Dearne (1784&#150;5; dem.), for John Payne.<br>
<br>A bachelor uncle, whose sister, nephews, and nieces inherited his estate, Lindley never sought office. He paid &#145;as a gentleman&#146; in guineas  (Stapleton, 37) and, sure of his worth, was direct with patrons. His mortification at receiving a dismissal at the hands of a workman was sharply expressed in a letter to St Andrew Warde of July 1798. Lindley had several pupils: in 1790 Charles Watson, the son of an architect, entered his office, before becoming a partner in 1792. John Woodhead became Lindley's partner about 1810, and William Hurst, another partner, was articled <i>c</i>.1815. William Lindley died on 28 February 1819, probably at his home, 20 South Parade, Doncaster, and was buried in the parish church on 1 March.<br>
<br>Angus C. Taylor </div>
