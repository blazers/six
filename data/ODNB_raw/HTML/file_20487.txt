<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ockham,  Nicholas</span>  (<em>d.</em> <em>c.</em>1320), <span class="occ">Franciscan friar, theologian, and philosopher</span>, was born in Ockham in Surrey. The course of his career suggests that he was born in the early 1240s. After joining the Franciscan order he was sent to Paris for his first theological studies, between 1270 and 1274, most likely attending the lectures of Roger Marston. He was subsequently closely linked to the so-called neo-Augustinians, adherents of the intellectual tradition of Alexander of Hales and Bonaventure, of whom Marston was one. It is probable that Ockham was a bachelor of the <span class="roman">Bible</span> at Oxford in 1278 and 1279 and a bachelor of the <span class="italic">Sentences</span> from 1280 to 1282. Finally he became the eighteenth regent master of the Franciscan school at Oxford, probably between 1286 and 1288. Almost nothing is known of his later life, but it has been argued that he died about 1320.<br>
<br>Scholarly research in the late twentieth century has produced ten manuscript copies of Nicholas Ockham's commentary on the <span class="italic">Sentences</span>. Questions, or parts thereof, from books 1&#150;4 have been edited in various books and journals. Fifteen <i>quaestiones disputatae</i> belonging to Nicholas are to be found divided between codex 158 and codex 196 of the municipal library of Assisi. Among these disputed questions is the &#145;Question on the plurality of forms&#146;. This dispute, attributed to William Ockham by a number of early historians, is in fact the response of Nicholas to Thomas Sutton's &#145;Treatise against the plurality of forms&#146;. Some of the other disputed questions fall naturally into groups, such as the four questions that are united in the edition entitled <span class="italic">Quaestiones disputatae &#145;De dilectione Dei&#146;</span> (1981).<br>
<br>S. F. Brown </div>
