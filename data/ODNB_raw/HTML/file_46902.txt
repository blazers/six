<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Fage,  Mary</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1637), <span class="occ">poet</span>, has until very recently been known only as the &#145;wife of Robert Fage the younger, Gentleman&#146;, as she is identified on the title-page of <span class="italic">Fames Roule</span> (1637), her one published work. It is, however, possible that she was the daughter of Edward Fage (<i>d</i>. 1638) of Doddinghurst, Essex, that she married her kinsman Robert Fage, and that this kinsman was the Ro: Fage who published a translation of <span class="italic">Peter Ramus of Vermandois &hellip; his Dialectica</span> (1632): the preliminary matter of this book indicates that the author had Essex connections.<br>
<br>
<span class="italic">Fames Roule</span> was published in 1637, with a dedicatory poem by Thomas Heywood. It is a collection of over 400 acrostic verses and anagrams, playing on the names and titles of eminent figures at court, and arranged in order of precedence, beginning with Carolus Stuarte: &#145;Av! Sol's Tru Trace&#146;. Its sheer volume (nearly 300 quarto pages), even for a period that delighted in word play and in which the acrostic and anagram were often overworked, is extraordinary.<br>
<br>Despite the work's familiarity with the court structure, and its tone of support for the embattled Caroline court, it has not been possible to place either Mary or Robert Fage there, and there are occasional gaps and mistaken identifications, which could suggest that the author lived at one remove from her subject. <span class="italic">Fames Roule</span> might have been an effort to secure patronage through a species of shotgun flattery, although Fage's attempt to wrest meanings from names and titles suggests another context for <span class="italic">Fames Roule</span>, the genre of prophecy: in this case, the reading of fortunes and secular prophecy that was to appeal to so many women in the latter part of the seventeenth century.<br>
<br>Betty S. Travitsky </div>
