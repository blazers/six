<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Thurlow,  Edward  Hovell-</span>, second Baron Thurlow  (1781&#150;1829), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was born on 10 June 1781 in the Temple, London, the first son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/27408/', "Thomas Thurlow (1737&#150;1791)")</script>, bishop of Durham, and his wife, Anne (<i>d</i>. 1791), daughter of William Bere of Lymington, Hampshire. He was educated at Charterhouse School and at Magdalen College, Oxford, matriculating in 1798, and graduating MA in 1801. He succeeded his uncle, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/27406/', "Lord Chancellor Edward Thurlow (1731&#150;1806)")</script>, to the barony of Thurlow in 1806, but did not take his seat in the House of Lords until 1810. In tribute to his grandmother, he took the additional name Hovell by royal licence on 8 July 1814.<br>
<br>In accordance with a custom frequent at this time, on 30 December 1785, then aged four, Thurlow was made one of the principal registrars of the diocese of Lincoln, and in 1788 he was made clerk of the custodies of idiots and lunatics. Later, he was appointed clerk of the presentations in the petty bag office (1796), patentee of commissions in bankruptcy (1803), and clerk of the Hanaper (1821). He retained all of these positions until his death.<br>
<br>On 13 November 1813 Thurlow married an actress, Mary Catherine (<i>d</i>. 1830), eldest daughter of James Richard Bolton, attorney; they had three sons, of whom Edward Thomas (1814&#150;1857) succeeded his father in the title.<br>
<br>Hovell-Thurlow was a great admirer of Sidney, and his first publication was a private edition of the <span class="italic">Defence of Poesy</span>. His original poetry was published in several collections, along with some classical translations (1814) and an attempt to continue Shakespeare's <span class="italic">Tempest</span> in <span class="italic">Angelica, or, The Rape of Proteus</span> (1822). The reception of his work tended to be lukewarm or hostile. The <span class="italic">Edinburgh Review</span> (1814) lamented: &#145;his Lordship &hellip; loves the Muse with a warmth which makes us regret that the passion is not mutual&#146;, and Byron parodied him cruelly. On the other hand, Lamb praised his sonnet &#145;To a Bird, that Haunted the Waters of Lochen, in the Winter&#146;, which ends:<blockquote>
<div>Nature is always wise in every part.<br>
</div>
</blockquote>Hovell-Thurlow died on 4 June 1829 in Regency Square, Brighton.<br>
<br>Jessica Hinings </div>
