<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Thelwall,  Algernon Sydney</span>  (1795&#150;1863), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman and teacher of elocution</span>, was born in Cowes, Isle of Wight, the eldest son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/27167/', "John Thelwall (1764&#150;1834)")</script>, the political reformer, and his wife, Susan Vellum (<i>d</i>. 1816). Educated by private tuition, he was admitted a pensioner at Trinity College, Cambridge, in 1813 and, having been elected a scholar in 1816, graduated BA as eighteenth wrangler in 1818. He was admitted at the Middle Temple in 1816, but was ordained priest in 1819, becoming English chaplain and missionary to the Jews in Amsterdam from 1819 to 1826. Returning to England he became curate of Blackford, Somerset, in 1828, marrying on 11 November 1828, at Islington, Georgiana Anne, eldest daughter of a Mr Tahourdin.<br>
<br>Thelwall was a prominent evangelical, whose published <span class="italic">Sermons</span> (1833) revealed a greater awareness of the importance of the church in its corporate capacity than had been common among evangelicals of the preceding generation. His polemical works included a scriptural refutation of Irvingism (1834) and an exposure of the iniquity of the opium trade with China (1839). He was a founder of the Trinitarian Bible Society, of which he was secretary from 1836 to 1847 and again in 1855. During 1842&#150;3 he was minister of the Bedford Chapel, Bloomsbury, and was curate of St Matthew's, Pell Street, from 1848 to 1850 and again during 1852&#150;3, but he never held a benefice. A determined anti-Catholic, he contributed attacks on Tractarianism to the series of <span class="roman">Brighton Protestant Tracts</span>, and came to wider notice as reporter of the proceedings of the protestant committee convened in 1845 to oppose a state grant to the Catholic seminary at Maynooth.<br>
<br>From 1850 until his death Thelwall was lecturer in elocution and public reading at the theological department of King's College, London. His <span class="italic">The Reading Desk and the Pulpit</span> (1861) argued for the importance to clergymen of his subject. Thelwall died at his home, 43 Torrington Square, London, on 30 November 1863. Three of his sons were admitted at Trinity College, Cambridge.<br>
<br>M. C. Curthoys </div>
