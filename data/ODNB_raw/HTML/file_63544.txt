<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ibach,  Josias</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1679&#150;1696), <span class="occ">brass founder</span>, is thought to be the son of Constantin Ibach, the organist at St Pankratius at Stade, on the River Elbe. (Stade was then the capital of the Swedish duchy of Bremen and Verden.) Constantin Ibach had married there in 1640; his family came from Meissen and were organ builders, which is probably how Josias&#151;named after his grandfather&#151;learned brass founding and pipe making. The young Josias Ibach emigrated to London, obviously attracted by work opportunities after the great fire in 1666; he was subsequently naturalized in 1694. Christ Church, Newgate Street, records the burials of two members of an &#145;Ibeck&#146; family, Hester on 2 January 1677 and John on 4 November 1679, who may well be related to him.<br>
<br>In 1679 Ibach cast the bronze statue of Charles II on horseback that Tobias Rustat commissioned from Grinling Gibbons to stand in the courtyard of Windsor Castle. Christopher Wren noted that &#145;the horse at Windsor was first cut in wood by a German and then cast by one Ibeck a founder in London&#146;  (Bolton and Hendry, 22, letter no. 7, 9 Sept 1682). Clearly at that time he still regarded himself as a citizen of Stade, since on one of the horse's hoofs he inscribed &#145;Josias Ibach Stada Bramensis 1679 Fundit&#146;. In 1689 Ibach recast and repaired the original brass shells on the fountain in the privy garden at Hampton Court, for which he was paid &pound;121. At Chatsworth House he assisted Jean Tijou and was paid &pound;17 for seventeen &#145;brass vauses gilt&#146; in 1692 with another &pound;60 the following year in part payment for an &#145;artificial tree of brass for a fountain&#146;  (Thompson, 36, 124)&#151;which was a copper willow tree designed to surprise visitors with a sudden shower&#151;together with further payments for minor repairs in 1696. His place and date of death are not known.<br>
<br>Philip Lewin </div>
