<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Mac Duinnshl&eacute;ibhe <span class="st"> [MacDonlevy]</span>,  Cormac</span>  (<em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>1459), <span class="occ">physician and translator of medical texts</span>, was descended from a royal family of twelfth-century Ulster (modern counties Down and Antrim), who were overthrown by the Anglo-Norman invaders. A branch of the family migrated to T&iacute;r Conaill (modern co. Donegal) where they became hereditary physicians to the &Oacute; Domhnaill. Mac Duinnshl&eacute;ibhe calls himself <i>basiller a fisigeacht</i> (&#145;bachelor of medicine&#146;; BL, Arundel MS 333, fol. 113<i>v</i>25), a qualification recorded in a number of the scribal colophons that name him as translator, one of which  (NL Ire., MS G 12, p. 30b13&#150;19) also credits him with a degree in arts. The identity of the university from which he graduated is not specified.<br>
<br>Nine translations from Latin into Irish are attributed to Mac Duinnshl&eacute;ibhe, all of them undated. British Library, Arundel MS 333, fols. 112&#150;13, is a holograph copy of his translation of a section of the <span class="italic">Diete particulares</span> of Isaac Judaeus (<i>d</i>. 959), written for Deinis &Oacute; Eachthigheirn, member of a Munster family, and belongs, apparently, to the second half of the fifteenth century. His translation of Gualterus Agilon's (<i>fl</i>. <i>c</i>.1250) <span class="italic">De dosibus medicinarum</span> (published as S. Sheahan, <span class="italic">An Irish Version of Gaulterus &#145;De dosibus&#146;</span>, 1938), made for Diarmaid (mac Domhnaill) &Oacute; Leighin, member of an important medical family in Munster, was completed by 29 March 1459, the date of the anonymous scribe's colophon to the copy of the work found in British Library, Harley MS 546, fols. 1<i>r</i>&#150;11<i>r</i>.<br>
<br>Three of Mac Duinnshl&eacute;ibhe's works comprise translations of treatises by the distinguished physician and professor of medicine in the University of Montpellier, Bernard de Gordon (<i>c</i>.1258&#150;<i>c</i>.1318). His rendering of Gordon's <span class="italic">De pronosticis</span> (1295) (Dublin, Royal Irish Academy, MS 439 (3 C 19), fols. 241&#150;88) had been completed by <i>c</i>.1468, the date of writing of the digest of that work found in National Library of Ireland, MS G 11, pp. 425&#150;38. His major work, a translation of Gordon's monumental <span class="italic">Lilium medicine</span> (1303&#150;5; Dublin, Royal Irish Academy, MS 443 (24 P 14), pp. 1&#150;327), which achieved great popularity among the medical fraternity of the Gaelic-speaking world, was completed by 1482, the date of writing of the copy of the work contained in British Library, Egerton MS 89, fols. 13<i>r</i>a1&#150;192<i>v</i>b13. The earliest extant copy of his rendering of Gordon's short treatise, <span class="italic">De decem ingeniis curandorum morborum</span> (1299) (National Library of Ireland, MS G 12, pp. 23a1&#150;30b19), is evidently of early sixteenth-century date.<br>
<br>Four further works comprise: a translation of the first tractate of the French surgeon Gui de Chauliac's <span class="italic">Chirurgia</span> (1363) (NL Ire., MS G 453, fols. 110&#150;27; TCD, MS 1436, pp. 17a&#150;35a), a brief extract from which occurs in British Library, Arundel MS 333, fol. 37<i>v</i>a17&#150;21 in a scribal hand dated 18 March 1514 (fol. 35<i>v</i>20&#150;29); a rendering of Bertrandus's <span class="italic">Lucidarius, vel, Almagest</span>, a unique copy of which is found in Oxford, Corpus Christi College, MS 129, fols. 56<i>v</i>14&#150;57<i>v</i>2  (cf. Bodl. Oxf., MS Digby 79, fols. 176<i>r</i>1&#150;177<i>r</i>16), written in 1527; a translation of a section of the <span class="italic">Rosa Anglica</span> (<i>c</i>.1314) of John Gaddesden, the earliest extant copy of which (NL Ire., MS G 12, pp. 14&#150;22) is evidently of early sixteenth-century date; and, finally, a translation of an opusculum of St Thomas Aquinas, <span class="italic">De operationibus occultis naturae</span>, a unique copy of which is found in the sixteenth-century manuscript (NL Scot., Adv. MS 72.1.12, fol.10<i>r</i>a1&#150;10<i>v</i>b36).<br>
<br>Aoibheann Nic Dhonnchadha </div>
