<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Goodridge,  Henry Edmund</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1797, <em>d.</em> 1864), <span class="occ">architect</span>, was baptized on 26 July 1797 at St Michael's Church, Bath, the son of James Goodridge, a leading builder in the city who carried out much of the development in the suburb of Bathwick, and his wife, Anna Buck. He was apprenticed to John Lowder, then architect to the city, and supplemented this training by visits to France <i>c</i>.1818 and later, in 1829, to Italy. After setting up independently in the city <i>c</i>.1820, he developed a successful practice both in Bath itself and in the neighbouring areas of Somerset and Wiltshire. Harvey Lonsdale Elmes, subsequently the architect of St George's Hall in Liverpool, was an assistant in his office from 1834 to 1837. On 10 July 1822 Goodridge married Matilda Yockney of Upper East Hayes, Bath, with whom he had five children, one of whom, Alfred Samuel Goodridge, continued his father's practice after he retired about 1855.<br>
<br>Goodridge's best-known work is the Lansdown tower on the edge of Bath, built in 1824&#150;7 for the celebrated William Beckford, who had settled in the city in 1823; and this and his own residence, Montebello (later Bathwick Grange), on Bathwick Hill (1828&#150;30), are compositions of great originality, combining a picturesque irregularity of form with an eclectic mixture of Greek and italianate detail. Others of his early works are more conventionally neo-classical in manner, but he also designed a number of churches in the Gothic style, some of which&#151;notably that in Rode Hill, Somerset (1822&#150;4)&#151;are highly, indeed bizarrely, individual, while his Devizes Castle, Wiltshire (<i>c</i>.1840), is in a neo-Norman idiom. In his entry for the competition for the Houses of Parliament (1835) he proposed an octagonal House of Commons and a House of Lords in the form of a &#145;baronial hall with minstrel gallery&#146;  (Colvin, <span class="italic">Archs</span>., 415).<br>
<br>Goodridge's &#145;great passion&#146; was &#145;the picturesque in landscape gardening&#146;, which he exercised in the grounds of his Bathwick villa  (Goodridge, 5). He exhibited occasionally at the Royal Academy and became a fellow of the Royal Institute of British Architects &#145;at a very early period of [the] Institution&#146;  (ibid.). He died at Belle Vue Villa, Bathwick Hill, on 26 October 1864 and was buried at Lansdown cemetery, Bath.<br>
<br>Peter Leach<span class="italic">, rev. </span>
