<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Otway,  Caesar</span> <span class="st"> [<em>pseud. </em>Terence O'Toole]</span>  (1780&#150;1842), <span class="occ">religious journalist and writer on Ireland</span>, was born in co. Tipperary, the son of Loftus Otway, a merchant. He entered Trinity College, Dublin, on 6 December 1796, aged sixteen, and graduated BA in 1801. After being ordained he was successively curate of a number of country parishes. In 1836 he was appointed to the assistant chaplaincy of Leeson Street Magdalen Chapel, Dublin, where he became one of the leading evangelical preachers. He was twice married, first to Frances, daughter of the Very Revd James Hastings, dean of Achonry, and second to Elizabeth, daughter of W. Digges La Touche of Sans Souci. Elizabeth and Caesar Otway had two sons and two daughters.<br>
<br>In 1825 Otway and Joseph Henderson Singer, later bishop of Meath, launched the <span class="italic">Christian Examiner</span>, the first Irish religious magazine to promote the cause of the established church. It was through Otway's encouragement that William Carleton began his literary career by writing for the periodical. Otway was an enthusiastic antiquary and an admirer of Irish scenery, and published <span class="italic">A Tour in Connaught</span> (1839) and <span class="italic">Sketches in Erris and Tyrawley</span> (1841). He co-operated with the landscape painter and antiquary George Petrie in the first volume of the <span class="italic">Dublin Penny Journal</span>, for which he wrote under the pseudonym Terence O'Toole and was also a contributor to the <span class="italic">Dublin University Magazine</span>. Ill health prevented him from writing his projected history of Ireland, and from editing the works of Sir James Ware. He died in Dublin on 16 March 1842.<br>
<br>D. J. O'Donoghue<span class="italic">, rev. </span> David Huddleston </div>
