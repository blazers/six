<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Mason,  James</span>  (1778/9&#150;1827), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was the son of James Mason, draper, of St John's Hill, Shrewsbury, and his wife, Sarah, daughter of Arthur Heywood, banker in Liverpool. Mason lived in Shrewsbury all his life. He was captain of the Shrewsbury Volunteers, and was involved in politics and literature. He was a supporter of Charles James Fox, advocating the abolition of slavery and Roman Catholic emancipation. In 1804 his <span class="italic">Considerations on the Necessity of Discussing the State of the Irish Catholics</span> appeared. This was followed by <span class="italic">A Brief Statement of the Present System of Tythes in Ireland, with a Plan for its Improvement</span>. He took part in the Shrewsbury election of 17 October 1806, and next year issued <span class="italic">A Letter to the Electors of Shrewsbury</span>. His other political pamphlets were <span class="italic">Observations on Parliamentary Reform</span> (1811) and <span class="italic">A Review of the Principal Arguments in Favour of Restricting Importation, and Allowing the Exportation of Corn</span> (1814).<br>
<br>Mason's published literary work included a tragedy, <span class="italic">The Natural Son</span> (1805), which should be distinguished from Richard Cumberland's earlier comedy with the same title, and in 1809 he issued two volumes of <span class="italic">Literary Miscellanies</span>. The first contained &#145;Mortimer&#146;, a novel in a series of letters, and translations from the classics; the second contained two tragedies, &#145;The Renown&#146; and &#145;Ninus&#146;, and two comedies, &#145;The School for Husbands&#146; (an original play, unlike John Ozell's translation from Moli&egrave;re) and &#145;The School for Friends&#146;. A comedy, under the same name as the last, by Marianne Chambers, was produced at Drury Lane in December 1805, and published the same year. In 1810 Mason published a verse translation, <span class="italic">The Georgicks of Publius Virgilius Maro</span>. Robert Watt, in his <span class="italic">Bibliotheca Britannica</span>, also attributes to him, probably wrongly, <span class="italic">A Plea for Catholic Communion in the Church of God</span> (1816).<br>
<br>Mason died at Shrewsbury on 27 April 1827, aged forty-eight.<br>
<br>G. Le G. Norgate<span class="italic">, rev. </span> H. C. G. Matthew </div>
