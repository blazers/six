<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Rabisha,  William</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1625&#150;1661), <span class="occ">cook and author</span>, may have been of a Yorkshire family: his surname appears to be a variant of the West Riding name Robertshaw or Robertshay. In the preface to his <span class="italic">The Whole Body of Cookery Dissected</span> (1661), he states that he had worked as a cook in aristocratic and ambassadorial households in England and abroad since before the civil war. The book claims to be written &#145;for the instruction of young Practitioners&#146;  (A sig. 4<i>v</i>) rather than for private householders. It begins with bills of fare for very grand dinners, of between forty and seventy separate dishes, and continues with recipes on an equally lavish scale, for instance for caviar, baked swan, and roast porpoise. It concludes with a modernized text (perhaps taken from John Murrell's <span class="italic">Two Bookes of Cookerie and Carving</span>, published in 1631) of the <span class="italic">Boke of Keruynge</span>, which had originally been printed by Wynkyn de Worde in 1508; and with a description of George Neville's vast installation feast of 1468, which was purportedly taken from &#145;the Records of the <i>Tower</i>&#146;  (Rabisha, 159) but which was possibly taken from the text printed as <span class="italic">The Great Feast at the Intronization of &hellip; George Neuell</span> (<i>c</i>.1570).<br>
<br>The tone of Rabisha's book is not only antiquarian but pointedly royalist. It is dedicated to five women with royalist connections and refers to the author's experience as a cook &#145;in the late Kings Court, of ever blessed memory&#146;  (A sig. 3<i>r</i>). It is therefore unclear whether Rabisha the cook was also the Captain William Rabisha who brought the news of the fall of Wexford from Cromwell to parliament in 1649 and who wrote the heterodox <span class="italic">Adam Unvailed, and Seen with Open Face</span> (1649) and a tract on Christopher Love's plot of 1651. The royalism of the cookery book must have been meant either to distance the author from his parliamentarian past, or to distance him from a parliamentarian namesake.<br>
<br>John Considine </div>
