<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Colquhoun,  John</span>  (1805&#150;1885), <span class="occ">writer on sport</span>, second son of Sir James Colquhoun, third baronet (1774&#150;1836), of Luss and Janet, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Sinclair (1781&#150;1846) [<i>see </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/5987/', "Colquhoun, Janet")</script>], the religious author, was born in Charlotte Square, Edinburgh, on 6 March 1805. Together with his elder brother he was educated first at Edinburgh high school, subsequently at a private school in Lincolnshire run by the Revd Grainger of Winteringham, and finally at the University of Edinburgh. An ensign in the 33rd regiment from 1826, in 1828 he was in the wilds of Connaught as a lieutenant; there he had plenty of hard work and a full share of adventures in the way of protecting the excise, or &#145;still-hunting&#146;, as it was called. In 1829 he was gazetted into the 4th dragoon guards but sold out the following year.<br>
<br>On 29 January 1834 Colquhoun married Frances Sarah (1813&#150;1877), fourth daughter of Ebenezer Fuller Maitland of Park Place, Henley-on-Thames; they had four sons and five daughters, among them <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/41040/', "Lucy Bethia Walford")</script>. Frances herself, when fourteen years old, contributed to the <span class="italic">Hymns for Private Devotion, Selected and Original</span> (1827), a completion by her mother, Bertha Fuller Maitland, of Henry Kirke White's fragment beginning &#145;Much in sorrow, oft in woe&#146;, and this completion was widely accepted for church use. A small volume of her poems was published in 1876 under the title <span class="italic">Rhymes and Chimes</span>.<br>
<br>Colquhoun was always a keen sportsman and an accurate observer of nature; but in his sympathy for the Scottish highlands' wilder days and for the preservation of endangered fauna, he confessed to &#145;having sunk the sportsman in the &#147;amateur naturalist&#148;&#146;  (Colquhoun, <span class="italic">The Moor and the Loch</span>, preface to 3rd edn, 1851). During his life he visited nearly every district of Scotland, so that his opportunities for observation were especially favourable. In 1840 he embodied his experiences in <span class="italic">The Moor and the Loch</span>, which speedily took a high rank among books on Scottish sport. The fourth edition (1878) contained many additions, notably the most valuable portions of some other books written in the meantime: <span class="italic">Rocks and Rivers</span> (1849); <span class="italic">Salmon Casts and Stray Shots</span> (1858); and <span class="italic">Sporting Days</span> (1866). The fifth edition (1880) added an autobiographical preface; two more editions followed by 1888. Besides these works Colquhoun wrote two lectures, &#145;On the fer&aelig; natur&aelig; of the British islands&#146; (deploring the extinction of Scottish species due to &#145;improvement&#146;), and &#145;On instinct and reason&#146;, which were published in 1873 and 1874 respectively. Colquhoun died on 27 May 1885 at his home, 1 Royal Terrace, Edinburgh, after a short illness.<br>
<br>J. A. F. Maitland<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Julian Lock </div>
