<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Quin,  Edward Turnly</span>  (1762&#150;1823), <span class="occ">journalist</span>, the son of Henry Turnly Quin of Roscommon, was born in Ireland and entered Trinity College, Dublin, as a pensioner in June 1779, proceeding BA in 1787. On leaving Dublin, according to one account he &#145;embraced a military life, and distinguished himself in the German service&#146;  (Gilliland, 1.511), while another version has it that he went to France &#145;to teach the pugilistic art&#146;  (<span class="italic">A Biographical Dictionary of the Living Authors of Great Britain and Ireland</span>, 285). He married Ann Delhoste at St Pancras old church on 18 September 1793; they had a son, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/22958/', "Edward Quin (1794&#150;1828)")</script>. Having settled in London as a journalist and reporter of debates, in 1803 he became editor of <span class="italic">The Traveller</span> (incorporated in 1822 with <span class="italic">The Globe</span>), an evening paper which, though intended primarily for commercial travellers, attracted a wider readership and was noted for its advocacy of political reform. As editor, Quin published the earliest of Leigh Hunt's essays (1804). In 1805 he was elected to the common council of the City of London as deputy for the ward of Farringdon Without. Though a protestant himself, Quin was an eloquent defender of civil rights for Catholics, and a speech he delivered in the common council in 1807 against a motion opposing the admission of Roman Catholics into the army was published that year under the title <span class="italic">Catholic Vindicator</span>. By 1808 he was also the proprietor of three weekly papers, the <span class="italic">Mirror of the Times</span>, <span class="italic">The Englishman</span>, and the <span class="italic">British Mercury</span>. Financial difficulties forced him into temporary retreat, but he later returned to journalism as editor of <span class="italic">The Day</span>, the auctioneers' paper, until its amalgamation with the <span class="italic">New Times</span> in 1817. He died at his home in Sheerness, Kent, on 7 July 1823.<br>
<br>G. Martin Murphy </div>
