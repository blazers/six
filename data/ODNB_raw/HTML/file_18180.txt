<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Martin,  Frederick Joseph</span>  (1830&#150;1883), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was born at Geneva, Switzerland, on 19 November 1830 and educated in Heidelberg, Germany. Nothing is known of his parents, other than the fact that his father was also called Frederick Joseph Martin. He moved to England quite young and worked as a teacher, and then from 1856 was secretary and amanuensis to Thomas Carlyle, whom he helped in his historical researches. On 17 January 1856 he married Susannah Styles (<i>b</i>. 1835), daughter of William Styles, a farmer; over the next decade they had two sons and three daughters.<br>
<br>Martin started a short-lived biographical magazine called <span class="italic">The Statesman</span>, in which he began an account of Carlyle's early life, but as his subject did not approve, he discontinued it. He inaugurated the <span class="italic">Statesman's Year-Book</span> in 1864, and in 1879 Benjamin Disraeli, the prime minister, struck by its usefulness, gave him a pension of &pound;100 a year. He continued to supervise the <span class="italic">Year-Book</span> until December 1882, when he was compelled by ill health to give it up and was replaced by J. Scott Keltie. In the intervening years he had produced an enormous amount of research, including a <span class="italic">Life of John Clare</span> (1865), volume two of <span class="italic">The National History of England</span> (1873), and a variety of histories and reference books in relation to finance and commerce. He wrote largely for various papers and was an occasional contributor to <span class="italic">The Athenaeum</span>. Only a few weeks after his retirement from the <span class="italic">Year-Book</span>, Martin died at his home, 22 Lady Margaret Road, Kentish Town, London, on 27 January 1883. He was buried at Highgate cemetery on 1 February.<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Jessica Hinings </div>
