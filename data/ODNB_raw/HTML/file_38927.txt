<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Gregg,  Hilda Caroline</span> <span class="st"> [<em>pseud. </em>Sydney C. Grier]</span>  (1868&#150;1933), <span class="occ">novelist and short-story writer</span>, was born at Badgington (Bagendon), North Cerney, Gloucestershire, on 20 June 1868, the eldest of the four daughters of Sarah Caroline Frances French (<i>d</i>. 1913) and John Robert G. Gregg (<i>d</i>. 1882), later vicar of Deptford. Hilda Gregg was brought up in a family with strong religious convictions and Irish protestant connections. Her father, descended from a long line of Ulster clergymen, was described by Gregg's cousin Winifred Peck in her autobiography, <span class="italic">A Little Learning</span>, as &#145;an Irish clergyman of, I imagine, the strictest sect of Ulster <span class="roman">Old Testament</span> Protestants&#146;. Her brother, John Allen Fitzgerald Gregg (1873&#150;1961), became archbishop of Armagh, while her sister, Katherine (<i>b</i>. 1869), one of the first women doctors to qualify in Britain, undertook medical missionary work in Japan and India. Hilda Gregg was educated privately, her only academic distinction being an honorary MA from London University. Her first story was published in the <span class="italic">Bristol Times</span> in 1886, the year in which she moved to 27 St Anne's Road, Eastbourne, to look after her widowed mother. She began writing for a living, spurred on by financial need as well as by a subsequent success in a short-story competition run by <span class="italic">Cassell's Family Magazine</span>. Over the next three decades her short fiction and novels featured in a variety of genteel literary periodicals, including <span class="italic">Cassell's</span>, <span class="italic">Argosy</span>, the <span class="italic">Lady's Realm</span>, and the <span class="italic">Girl's Own Paper</span>. In 1894 Gregg sent her first novel, <span class="italic">In Furthest Ind</span>, a fictional memoir of a seventeenth-century Englishman's adventures in India, on speculation to the Edinburgh firm of William Blackwood, who published it in 1895 under the pseudonym of Sydney C. Grier. Blackwood remained her publisher throughout her writing career. <span class="italic">In Furthest Ind</span>, praised for its seemingly first-hand knowledge of locale and its skilful dialogue, set the tone and style for Gregg's subsequent works, which she produced at a rate of one a year until 1925. Gregg followed <span class="italic">In Furthest Ind</span> with <span class="italic">His Excellency's English Governess</span> (1896), a historical romance set in Baghdad, and then with <span class="italic">An Uncrowned King</span>, serialized in <span class="italic">Blackwood's Magazine</span> between December 1895 and September 1896, and <span class="italic">A Crowned Queen</span> (1898), both historical romances set in the Balkans. Other locales featured in her thirty-three novels included Ethiopia (<span class="italic">Peace with Honour</span>, 1897), Bengal (<span class="italic">Like another Helen</span>, 1899), Afghanistan (<span class="italic">The Wardens of the Marches</span>, 1901), and Sicily (<span class="italic">One Crowded Hour</span>, 1912). Gregg died on 22 June 1933 at 48 St Leonards Road, Eastbourne, her home since 1926, leaving an estate valued at slightly over &pound;4200.<br>
<br>David Finkelstein </div>
