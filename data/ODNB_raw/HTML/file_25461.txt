<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Short,  Thomas</span>  (<em>c.</em>1690&#150;1772), <span class="occ">physician</span>, was born in the south of Scotland, and, after graduating in medicine, settled in practice at Sheffield.<br>
<br>In 1713 William Steel told him the secret, which he afterwards published, of making cerated glass of antimony as a cure for dysentery. He made several journeys to visit the mineral springs of Yorkshire and of other parts of England, becoming an expert on the medicinal uses and analysis of water. Following thirty years of research in the chemical analysis of mineral waters, in 1766 he published the first attempt to systematize tests, entitled <span class="italic">Institutes, or, An Introduction to the Examination of Mineral Waters</span>. Prior to this he had published <span class="italic">A Rational Discourse on the Inward Use of Waters</span> (1725). In 1750 Short published <span class="italic">New Observations on the Bills of Mortality</span>, in which he continued the work of John Graunt and Sir William Petty, and treated the subject in relation to observations he had made on the effect of the air on health. He spent eighteen years on this work.<br>
<br>Short wrote several other works on medicine, among them studies on the medical effects of tea, milk, sugar, and spirits. In 1767 he published <span class="italic">A Comparative History of the Increase and Decrease of Mankind</span>, in which he advocated early marriages, denounced alcohol &#145;as a Stygian poison&#146;, and collected much historical and medical information. He died in 1772.<br>
<br>Norman Moore<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Patrick Wallis </div>
