<span class="headword">Livingston <span class="st"> [Livingstone]</span>,  James, of Barncloich</span>, first Viscount Kilsyth  (1616&#150;1661), <span class="occ">nobleman</span>, was born on 25 June 1616, the younger son of Sir William Livingston of Kilsyth (<i>d</i>. 1627), a lord of session from 1609 and vice-chamberlain of Scotland from 1613, and his second wife, Margaret, daughter of Sir John Houston of Houston. On 23 April 1647 he was served heir to his brother's grandson William Livingston of Kilsyth. He suffered substantial losses during Montrose's campaign in 1645, and his house was burned and his lands plundered&#151;apparently by both English and Scottish forces&#151;in 1650&#150;51. He was imprisoned in Edinburgh in 1651, excepted from Cromwell's <span class="roman">Act of Pardon and Grace</span> (1654), and fined &pound;1500 (later reduced to &pound;800). After the Restoration, in recognition of his loyalty to the crown, he was raised to the peerage, as Viscount Kilsyth and Lord Campsie, on 17 August 1661. He acted as a commissioner of supply and as commissioner to parliament for Stirlingshire in 1661. Kilsyth and his wife, Euphame, daughter of Sir David Cunningham of Robertland, whom he had married on 10 December 1639, had four children: James, second Viscount Kilsyth, died in 1706 and was succeeded by his brother, William (1650&#150;1733), who, having engaged in the Jacobite rising of 1715, was forced to seek safety abroad and suffer forfeiture of his estates; the title died with him, in Rome. Elizabeth married Major-General Robert Montgomery, fifth son of Alexander, sixth earl of Eglinton; her sister, Anne (<i>b</i>. 1648), died unmarried. Kilsyth died in London on 9 September 1661.<br>
<br>T. F. Henderson<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Alison G. Muir </div>
