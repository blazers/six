<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hey,  Richard</span>  (1745&#150;1835), <span class="occ">essayist and mathematician</span>, was born on 11 August 1745 at Pudsey, near Leeds, the son of Richard Hey (1702&#150;1766), drysalter, and his wife, Mary, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Simpson (1702&#150;1768). He was the younger brother of the <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/13161/', "Revd John Hey DD (1734&#150;1815)")</script> and of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/13163/', "William Hey FRS (1736&#150;1819)")</script>. He matriculated at Magdalene College, Cambridge, in 1764, became a scholar in 1765, and graduated BA in 1768 as third wrangler, obtaining the chancellor's medal. In 1771 he took the degree of MA as fellow of Sidney Sussex College, and in 1779 was created LLD by royal mandate. On 8 November 1771 he was called to the bar at the Middle Temple. There is a fiat, dated 7 December 1778, for his admission to the court of arches, but no warrant was issued. He was admitted into Doctors' Commons, but obtaining no practice retired from the bar.<br>
<br>Hey had started his literary career with <span class="italic">Observations on the Nature of Civil Liberty and the Principles of Government</span>, (1776), but his chief work was the <span class="italic">Dissertation on the Pernicious Effects of Gaming</span> (1783; 3rd edn 1812) by which he gained a prize of 50 guineas offered through the University of Cambridge. In 1784 Hey gained a second prize, offered by the same anonymous donor, for his <span class="italic">Dissertation on Duelling</span>, which also reached a third edition in 1812, and his <span class="italic">Dissertation on Suicide</span> gained him a third prize of 50 guineas (1785). The three dissertations were published together in 1812. In 1792 Hey published <span class="italic">Happiness and Rights</span>, in reply to Paine's <span class="italic">Rights of Man</span>. He also wrote a tragedy in five acts called <span class="italic">The Captive Monarch</span> (1794) and a novel entitled <span class="italic">Edington</span> (1796). Hey had been fellow and tutor of Magdalene College from 1782 until his marriage in May 1796 to Martha, the daughter of T. Browne. Hey's last work was <span class="italic">Some principles of civilisation, with detached thoughts on the promotion of Christianity in British India</span> (1815). He had at various times contributed papers to the <span class="italic">Philosophical Transactions</span> and other magazines, and he also assisted in editing a pamphlet which gives a scientific account of an Egyptian mummy, with anatomical and other details. Hey died on 7 December 1835 at Hertingfordbury, near Hertford.<br>
<br>R. E. Anderson<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Rebecca Mills </div>
