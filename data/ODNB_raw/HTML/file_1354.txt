<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Barclay,  William</span>  (1797/8&#150;1859), <span class="occ">miniature painter</span>, was born possibly in London and was also possibly the son of William Barclay (<i>fl</i>. 1764&#150;1769), miniature painter, of Tottenham, Middlesex. He entered the Royal Academy Schools on 15 January 1820, aged twenty-two, under the title &#145;William Barclay Jnr&#146;. He established himself as a miniature painter, practising in London and Paris throughout the period 1830 to 1859.<br>
<br>Barclay specialized in making copies of the works of the great Italian masters in the Mus&eacute;e du Louvre in Paris, as well as producing portrait miniatures on ivory in the style of, but considerably weaker than, Sir William Ross. A large full-length miniature in watercolour on ivory of Dom Pedro, duque do Bragan&ccedil;a, and Dom Lu&iacute;s, duque do P&ocirc;rto, demonstrating this affinity with Ross, was acquired by Queen Victoria in 1843 (Royal Collection). Barclay's miniature of Mrs Bacon (1836) and two miniatures of unknown sitters are in the Victoria and Albert Museum, London. Two of these are signed &#145;Barclay&#146; in his characteristic fashion. He exhibited portrait miniatures and some copies in watercolour at the Paris Salon between 1831 and 1859, as well as at the Royal Academy between 1832 and 1856. He died in 1859, possibly in Paris. A successful miniature painter of his time, Barclay has been classified by most recent commentators as a competent but second-rank artist.<br>
<br>V. Remington </div>
