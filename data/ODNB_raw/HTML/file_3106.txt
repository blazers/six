<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Boyd,  Hugh Stuart</span>  (1781&#150;1848), <span class="occ">Greek scholar</span>, was born at Edgware, Middlesex, on 23 March 1781, the son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/3105/', "Hugh Macaulay Boyd (1746&#150;1794)")</script> and his wife, Frances, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Morphy. His father had been born Hugh Macaulay, but prior to his son's birth had assumed the surname of his maternal grandfather, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/47302/', "Hugh Boyd (1690&#150;1765)")</script>, of Ballycastle, Ireland.<br>
<br>As a boy in Hampstead, Boyd studied Greek with a tutor named Spowers. He was admitted a pensioner of Pembroke College, Cambridge, on 24 July 1799 and matriculated on 17 December 1800, but left the university without taking a degree. He once claimed that he could repeat 3280 &#145;lines&#146; of Greek prose and 4770 lines of Greek verse.<br>
<br>On 16 September 1805 Boyd married Ann Wilson Lowry (<i>d</i>. 1834), daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/17103/', "Wilson Lowry")</script>, the engraver and geologist. They had one child, Ann Henriette (1811&#150;1901), and lived in various furnished houses in England, financed by his Irish estates. In 1811 he contracted ophthalmia, which led to complete blindness. A move in 1825 to Great Malvern and a reading of Elizabeth Barrett's <span class="italic">Essay on Mind</span> (1826) led in 1827 to their correspondence and friendship. She read Greek aloud to him, and he tutored her in the language. The friendship flourished in 1831&#150;2 when both were living in Sidmouth.<br>
<br>Boyd's original publications included <span class="italic">Luceria, a Tragedy</span> (1806) rejected by Drury Lane, and <span class="italic">A Malvern Tale and other Poems</span> (1814). His translations included <span class="italic">Select Passages from the Works of St Chrysostom, St Gregory Nazianzen etc</span> (1810) and the <span class="italic">Agamemnon of Aeschylus</span> (1803). <span class="italic">Thoughts on an Illustrious Exile</span> (1825) extolled the Waldensian Christians and praised Napoleon's religious toleration, while Boyd's staunchly Anglican, anti-papist stance is expressed in <span class="italic">The Fathers not Papist with Select Passages and Tributes to the Dead</span> (1834). Despite his pedantry and vanity Elizabeth Barrett supported the lonely widower after 1834, when he lived in London, first at St John's Wood, then at 21 Downshire Hill, Hampstead, and then again at St John's Wood. Her 1844 volume, which made her fame, recalled in &#145;Wine of Cypress&#146; Boyd's hospitality at Malvern. In September 1846 she confided to him, uniquely, her impending elopement. Latterly Boyd suffered from acute lumbago; he died at 1 Hawley Place, Kentish Town, London, from a paralytic stroke, on 10 May 1848 and was buried in Highgate cemetery. Elizabeth Barrett Browning published three sonnets in his memory.<br>
<br>William Hunt<span class="italic">, rev. </span> John D. Haigh </div>
