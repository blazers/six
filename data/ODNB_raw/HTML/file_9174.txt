<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Farneworth,  Ellis</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1713, <em>d.</em> 1763), <span class="occ">translator</span>, was baptized on 21 October 1713, at Mugginton, Derbyshire, the son of Ellis Farneworth (<i>bap</i>. 1675), vicar, and his wife, Mary. He attended Chesterfield School under William Burrow and in 1725 was admitted to Eton College as king's scholar. He then proceeded to Cambridge, matriculating as a member of Jesus College on 10 November 1730. In 1734 he took his degree of BA, and in 1738 that of MA. In 1755 he was acting as curate to John Fitzherbert, vicar of Ashbourne, Derbyshire, and on 27 December 1758 he became vicar of Rostherne, Cheshire, by the influence of William Fitzherbert of Tissington, Derbyshire, brother of his former vicar. In October 1762 he was instituted to the rectory of Carsington, Derbyshire, at the instance of his friend, the Hon. James Yorke, dean of Lincoln.<br>
<br>Farneworth's first two translations from Italian, <span class="italic">The Life of Pope Sixtus the Fifth &hellip; Translated from the Italian of Gregorio Leti</span> (1754) and <span class="italic">The history of the civil wars of France &hellip; a new translation from the Italian of Henrico Caterino Davila</span> (1758), respond to the widespread contemporary interest in historical works. While the first translation claims to be &#145;far from &hellip; literal&#146;  (p. xi), reacting to criticism of being too diffuse, Farneworth opted for a closer rendering of the second. His most ambitious project was <span class="italic">The works of Nicholas Machiavel &hellip; newly translated from the originals: illustrated with notes, anecdotes, dissertations, and the life of Machiavel</span> (1762). Farneworth's edition includes a variety of sources: <span class="italic">The Prince</span>, for example, is followed, chapter by chapter, by Frederick II of Prussia's <span class="italic">Examen du Prince de Machiavel</span>, the so-called &#145;anti-Machiavel&#146;, and is prefaced by Voltaire's essay on the text. The purpose of the apparatus is thus explained by Farneworth: &#145;the Editor may truly say, that no pains have been spared to make it acceptable to the Public&#146;  (p. vii). Although Farneworth's works were not profitable, his translation of Machiavelli, which he was obliged to &#145;hawk round the town&#146;  (Nichols, <span class="italic">Lit. anecdotes</span>, 2.391&#150;3), went through a second edition in 1775 and by 1814 sold at a high price  (Chalmers, 128). Under the pseudonym Philopyrphagus Ashburniensis, Farneworth contributed a humorous account of Powell, the fire-eater, to the <span class="italic">Gentleman's Magazine</span> for February 1755. Farneworth died at Carsington on 25 March 1763.<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Antonella Braida </div>
