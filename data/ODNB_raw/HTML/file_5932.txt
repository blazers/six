<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Collingwood,  Roger</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1495&#150;1517), <span class="occ">mathematician</span>, originated in the diocese of Durham, of unknown parentage. He was admitted as a questionist in May 1495 to the University of Cambridge, probably to Queens' College, graduating BA in 1496 and MA in 1499. During his tenure of a fellowship at Queens' in 1497 to 1510, Collingwood resided partly at Cambridge, where he held the first known mathematics lectureship in 1501&#150;2, 1504&#150;7 (receiving a fee of &pound;4 in 1506), and 1514&#150;17, and partly on the continent, where he was permitted to study canon law. He was living away from Cambridge in 1502&#150;3, in Paris, and in 1507&#150;11.<br>
<br>Collingwood's lectureship had probably been instituted by his teacher, Bishop Richard Foxe, patron of humanism and founder of Corpus Christi College. He was dean of chapel at Queens' in 1503&#150;4, was appointed rector of Albury in Surrey in 1509, and served his college as senior proctor in 1513. Collingwood was also a practising mathematician and under the name of Carbo-in-ligno left a treatise in manuscript, &#145;Arithmetica experimentalis&#146;, dedicated to Bishop Foxe, and preserved in the library of Corpus Christi College. Nothing is known of Collingwood's subsequent life nor of the circumstances of his death.<br>
<br>A. M. Clerke<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Anita McConnell </div>
