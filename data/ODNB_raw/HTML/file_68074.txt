<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Man,  Judith</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1640), <span class="occ">translator</span>, was the daughter of Peter Man, solicitor to Thomas Wentworth, earl of Strafford. By her own account, Man was brought up in Strafford's household where she received some education, including competence in the French language. All evidence regarding her life and interests comes from her own hand, namely from the prefatory materials to her translation of John Barclay's <span class="italic">Argenis</span>, which was originally published in Latin, and had been translated into English by both Kingsmill Long (in 1625) and Robert le Grys (in 1628). Man's translation, <span class="italic">Epitome of the History of Faire Argenis and Polyarchus</span>, printed in 1640, was taken from an epitome of Barclay's text written by Nicolas Co&euml;ffeteau, bishop of Marseilles, and dedicated to Wentworth's eldest daughter, Anne, apparently as a tribute to the &#145;Schoole of Vertue&#146;  (Man, sig. A2<i>v</i>) where she had been educated. Her translation is very literal, and stresses the virtuous femininity of the heroine, Argenis, rather than the political allegory for which Barclay's work was renowned, although Barclay's commitment to monarchy would have made it a suitable text for Wentworth's royalist household. From the evidence of the dedication it is possible to infer that the translation doubled as an exhortation to Anne Wentworth to marry. One of the more interesting aspects of Man's text is her self-presentation as an author. She sees the text as having a specifically didactic purpose, but views translation as a fitting activity for a woman because it creates diversion and provides self-improvement: &#145;so &hellip; I might make my selfe, so much the more perfect, in the French tongue&#146;  (ibid., sig. A6<i>v</i>). Man authorizes her literary undertaking by referring to other romance texts translated by women: &#145;who have traced me the way, witnesse the translation into French of Sir Philip Sidney's <span class="italic">Arcadia</span>, the <span class="italic">New Amarantha</span> and <span class="italic">Urania</span>&#146;  (ibid., sig. A7<i>r</i>). Of Man's later life or writing, nothing is known.<br>
<br>Danielle Clarke </div>
