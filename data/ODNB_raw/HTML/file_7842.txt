<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Dornford,  Josiah</span>  (1762/3&#150;1797), <span class="occ">translator and writer</span>, was the son of Josiah Dornford of Deptford, Kent, a member of the court of common council of the City of London, and the author of several pamphlets on the corporation's affairs and the reform of debtors' prisons. His half-brother was <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/7841/', "Joseph Dornford")</script>, Church of England clergyman. He matriculated at Trinity College, Oxford, on 23 May 1781, aged eighteen, and graduated BA in 1785 and MA in 1792. He later moved to the University of G&ouml;ttingen, where he took the degree of LLD. He was called to the bar at Lincoln's Inn. In 1790 he published in three volumes <span class="italic">An Historical Development of the Present Political Constitution of the Germanic Empire</span>, translated from the German of J. S. P&uuml;tter; the work was probably undertaken at G&ouml;ttingen, where P&uuml;tter was professor of laws. Dornford also published in Latin a small volume of academic exercises by another G&ouml;ttingen professor, the philologist Christian Gottlob Heyne, who, in a preface to this publication, speaks of Dornford as a &#145;learned youth&#146; who had &#145;gained the highest honours in jurisprudence in our academy&#146;. Dornford's only other known work is <span class="italic">The Motives and Consequences of the Present War Impartially Considered</span> (1793), a pamphlet written in defence of William Pitt's administration. In 1795 he was named inspector-general of the army accounts in the Leeward Islands, and the record of this appointment shows that he had served as one of the commissaries to Lord Moira's army. He died at Martinique on 1 July 1797.<br>
<br>J. M. Scott<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Philip Carter </div>
