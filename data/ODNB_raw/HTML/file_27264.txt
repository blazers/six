<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Thompson,  Henry</span>  (1797&#150;1878), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was born in Surrey. He was admitted to St John's College, Cambridge, as a pensioner in 1818, graduating BA in 1822 and proceeding MA in 1825. In 1820 he competed for Sir William Browne's medal, receiving an extra prize for a Latin ode, and in 1824 he obtained the first members' prize for a Latin essay. He married on 30 June 1823 Anne Harrison, daughter of the Revd James Bell, vicar of Lympne, Kent. In December 1823 he was ordained deacon, and priest in December 1827. After being successively curate of St George's, Camberwell, Surrey (1824&#150;7), St Mary's, Salehurst, Sussex (1827&#150;8), and Wrington, Somerset (1828&#150;53), he was appointed by George Henry Law, bishop of Bath and Wells, to the vicarage of Chard, Somerset, on 14 September 1853, where he resided until his death on 29 November 1878. He left two sons&#151;Christopher, and Henry Bell (<i>d</i>. 1887), who had served as his curate at Chard and whom he presented to the vicarage of Tatworth, Somerset.<br>
<br>Thompson was a man of very conservative instincts. In the words of his friend Edward Augustus Freeman, whom he first met at Hannah More's house at Barley Wood, he &#145;seemed to look at everything in 1878 with exactly the same eyes with which he looked on things in 1839&#146;. At the same time, Freeman adds, &#145;he showed us that past generation in its best colours&#146;. He was a good classical scholar and knew Hebrew and German.<br>
<br>Thompson was the author of <span class="italic">Davidica: Twelve Practical Sermons on the Life of David</span> (1827), <span class="italic">Pastoralia: a Manual of Helps for the Parochial Clergy</span> (1830), which is of interest for its recommendation that the sick should be encouraged to make special confession, <span class="italic">The Life of Hannah More</span> (1838), which has been found useful by later biographers, and <span class="italic">Concionalia: Outlines of Sermons for the Christian Year</span> (1853; 2nd ser., 1871). He published editions of Horace (1853) and Virgil (1854; 3rd edn, 1862), and also contributed most of the classical articles to the <span class="italic">Encyclopaedia metropolitana</span> (1824), several of which he afterwards published separately. In 1845 he translated Schiller's <span class="italic">Maid of Orleans</span> and <span class="italic">William Tell</span>, and in 1850 edited a volume of <span class="italic">Original Ballads by Living Authors</span>, to which E. A. Freeman was a contributor of nine poems. Thompson also contributed to the anthologies of religious verse <span class="italic">Lyra sanctorum</span> and <span class="italic">Lyra eucharistica</span>, and to the <span class="italic">Churchman's Companion</span>.<br>
<br>E. I. Carlyle<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Triona Adams </div>
