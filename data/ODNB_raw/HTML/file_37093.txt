<div class="para">
<span class="headword">St Victor,  Achard  of</span>  (<em>c.</em>1100&#150;1171), <span class="occ">abbot of St Victor and bishop of Avranches</span>, was born in England and educated in France, as revealed in an early collection of Victorine epitaphs&#151;&#145;Huius oliva domus, Anglorum gloria cleri&#146;&#151;and in another, later, epitaph, which begins, &#145;Anglia me genuit, docuit me Gallia&#146;  (Bonnard, 1.204). He became an Augustinian canon at St Victor, Paris, probably in the lifetime of Master Hugh (<i>d</i>. 1141), the founder of the school of St Victor. As one of Hugh's successors in that school, Master Achard took part in the theological controversies of the 1150s, emerging as a sharp critic of Peter Lombard.<br>
<br>In 1155 Achard became abbot of St Victor: several letters and charters attest his financial vigilance and the practical support that he received from the English pope, Adrian IV (<i>r</i>. 1154&#150;9). His election in 1157 to the see of S&eacute;es&#151;which had an Augustinian chapter&#151;was vetoed by Henry II. But on 27 March 1161 Achard was consecrated bishop of Avranches, and in that capacity was on much improved terms with the king. He assisted at the baptism of Henry's daughter Eleanor in 1161, and at the translations of dukes Richard I and II of Normandy at F&eacute;camp in 1162 and of Edward the Confessor at Westminster in 1163. As a diocesan bishop he was on good terms with his learned and active neighbour Arnulf, bishop of Lisieux (<i>d</i>. 1184). His later epitaph suggests that he had a good knowledge of canon law: &#145;legis doctorem tenuit illa&#146; (namely Gallia). Achard died on 29 March 1171. He was a generous patron of Lucerne (in the diocese of Avranches), the Premonstratensian house in which his tomb and a fine, though damaged, contemporary effigy can still be seen.<br>
<br>Achard's two theological treatises, the <span class="italic">De Trinitate</span> and the <span class="italic">De discretione animae spiritus et mentis</span> (both ed. E. Martineau, 1987), probably belong to the 1140s or the early 1150s. They reflect the debates and use the terminology of contemporary Paris in a way that the work of Master Hugh does not; yet Achard is doctrinally conservative, and essentially unsympathetic to the drift towards organized secular schools and (within his lifetime) the University of Paris. His doctrinal position is further and usefully clarified in fifteen sermons  (<span class="italic">Sermons in&eacute;dits</span>, ed. J. Ch&acirc;tillon, <span class="roman">Textes Philosophiques du Moyen &Acirc;ge</span>, 17, 1970), which presumably belong to his years as abbot and bishop.<br>
<br>Margaret Gibson<span class="italic">, rev. </span>
