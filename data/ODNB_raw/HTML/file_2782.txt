<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Boissier,  George Richard</span>  (1790/91&#150;1858), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman</span>, was the son of Robert Boissier of Penshurst, Kent, and his wife, Barbara Goring. He was educated at a school at Kirby Hill, Yorkshire, and matriculated at Magdalene College, Cambridge, in 1824. He graduated BA in 1828, the same year in which he published anonymously <span class="italic">Notes on the Cambridgeshire Churches</span>. Drawing its terminology from Thomas Rickman's <span class="italic">An Attempt to Discriminate the Styles of Architecture in England</span> (1817), it was an unusually early work of ecclesiology. Boissier's comments concerning unsuitable later additions to medieval churches, such as singing galleries, anticipated the arguments of the Ecclesiological Society, as did too the central message of his preface. Here he complained that the churches of Cambridgeshire and other counties were sadly neglected, describing them rather colourfully as &#145;cold, comfortless, unhealthy; the haunts of colds, catarrhs, and rheumatism; the receptacle frequently for filth, and the abode of toads and reptiles&#146;  (Boissier, <span class="italic">Notes</span>, 13).<br>
<br>Boissier was ordained deacon on 2 March 1828, and priest on 14 June 1829. He was curate of Chiddingstone, Kent, in 1828, and subsequently became the priest-in-charge of Oakfield, Penshurst. He died at Penshurst on 23 June 1858.<br>
<br>Rosemary Mitchell </div>
