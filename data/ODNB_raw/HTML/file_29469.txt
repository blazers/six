<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Peterborough,  William  of</span>  (<em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>1190), <span class="occ">Benedictine monk and theologian</span>, was a native of Peterborough and a monk of Ramsey Abbey. He was a prolific author of works of popular theology, of which the following are known from the bibliographers Henry Kirkestede, John Leland, and John Bale: <span class="italic">Distinctiones theologiae</span>; <span class="italic">Sermones notabiles</span>, in two volumes; <span class="italic">Homeliae in cantica</span>; <span class="italic">Euphrastica</span>; <span class="italic">Opus partium</span>; and <span class="italic">Interpretaciones vocabulorum s. scripturae</span>. The title of the first of these works indicates a use of the scholastic method, and his own title <i>magister</i> confirms that he had studied in the schools. The only work that survives is <span class="italic">Euphrastica</span>, an explanation of difficult passages in the scriptures. 100 problems are taken, starting with the <span class="roman">Old Testament</span> and working through to the <span class="roman">New Testament</span>. They include: &#145;whether the impossible is possible to God, or not&#146;  (no. 31); &#145;how it was that one evangelist said &#147;before the cock crows you will denounce me thrice&#148;, while another said &#147;&hellip; twice&#148;&#146;  (no. 60); and &#145;where Christ was in the forty days after his Passion&#146;  (no. 80). The work secures William's place among a group of monastic writers who &#145;wrote elaborate and lengthy commentaries on the <span class="roman">Bible</span> that contain little that is original&#146;  (Hunt, 27).<br>
<br>Edmund King </div>
