<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Peyton,  Thomas</span>  (1595&#150;1626), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was born at Royston, Hertfordshire, the son and heir of Thomas Peyton. On 26 November 1613 he was admitted a student of Lincoln's Inn. He was a studious and devout young man who published the first part of a poem entitled <span class="italic">The Glasse of Time in the First Age, Divinely Handled by Thomas Peyton of Lincolnes Inne, Gent</span> (1620), with every intention of a continuation. The volume opens with addresses in verse to King James, Prince Charles, Lord Chancellor Bacon, and the reader. The poem consists of 168 stanzas, of varying lengths, in heroic verse and relates the story of the Fall, as told in the <span class="roman">Bible</span>. There are many classical allusions and digressions into contemporary religious topics. Peyton writes as a champion of the established church and a warm opponent of the puritans.<br>
<br>In 1623 Peyton continued the work in a second volume, entitled <span class="italic">The Glasse of Time in the Second Age</span>, and brought the scriptural narrative up to Noah's entrance into the ark. A further continuation was promised but never written. The two parts together are titled <span class="italic">The Glasse of Time in the Two First Ages</span> and consist of 164 pages, interspersed with woodcuts. Some of the episodes in Peyton's poem&#151;notably his descriptions of paradise and of Lucifer&#151;very faintly suggest some masterly passages on the same subject in Milton's <span class="italic">Paradise Lost</span> but the resemblances are not close enough to render it probable that Milton was acquainted with his predecessor's efforts (see <span class="italic">North American Review</span>, October 1860). Peyton died in 1626.<br>
<br>[Anon.]<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Joanna Moody </div>
