<span class="headword">Gibson <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Cullum]</span>,  (Susannah) Arethusa</span>  (1814&#150;1885), <span class="occ">society hostess and political activist</span>, was born on 11 January 1814 at Southgate Green, Bury St Edmunds, the only child of Revd Sir Thomas Gery Cullum, eighth baronet (1777&#150;1855), and his first wife, Mary Anne, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Eggers (<i>d</i>. 1830). Much of her childhood was spent in Italy. On 23 February 1832 she married <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/10637/', "Thomas Milner Gibson (1806&#150;1884)")</script>, whose radicalism she shared, even when their opinions on specific issues differed.<br>
<br>Witty and energetic, Gibson channelled her ability into conventional entertaining and charity work. She needed more stimulus, however, which she created in her eclectic salons, attended by diplomats, writers, politicians, and, after 1848, European exiles. Regular guests included Dickens, Thackeray, Hugo, Lady Morgan, the Disraelis, Cobden, and Louis Napoleon. Dickens in turn placed her, &#145;for Anti Corn Law Leaguality&#146;, among his own ideal guests  (<span class="italic">Letters of Charles Dickens</span>, 4.106). Her hectic public life was for twenty-five years (1833&#150;57) complicated by pregnancies, despite her husband's lengthy absences on his yacht, although characteristically she resumed her activities as quickly as possible. The early deaths of four of her eight children deeply depressed her. Allegedly in 1838&#150;9 she had an affair with Sir George Wombwell, third baronet (1792&#150;1855).<br>
<br>Gibson's rebelliousness and belief in individual freedom became focused after meeting Mazzini in 1844. Through her fund-raising skills, her many influential contacts, and her practical assistance with correspondence she helped for many years to promote Italian nationalism. She enlivened her frequent European travels with thrilling hints of conspiracies, and in 1857 dramatically appeared in a Genoa theatre wearing the Italian tricolour. In the 1850s she became an advocate of mesmerism and spiritualism, and in 1860 held an eventful s&eacute;ance for the spiritualist D. D. Home. Although she once described her latest child (1851) as &#145;This new enemy of the Pope&#146;  (Disraeli, <span class="italic">Letters</span>, 5.2084 n. 1), she later converted to Roman Catholicism. Her last years were spent mostly in Paris, which she found more stimulating than England. Her husband died (on board his yacht) in 1884. She outlived all but two of her children and died at her home, 11 avenue du Bois de Boulogne, Paris, on 23 February 1885; she was buried at Bury St Edmunds, Suffolk, on 3 March.<br>
<br>Mary S. Millar </div>
