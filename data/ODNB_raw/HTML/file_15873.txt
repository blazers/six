<div class="para">
<span class="headword">L&oacute;egaire Lorc</span>  (<em>supp. </em><em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>300 <span class="smallcap">BC</span>), <span class="occ">legendary king of Leinster</span>, was the son of Augaine M&aacute;r and is said to have been also king of Ireland. He was described as <i>senathair Laigen</i>, &#145;the ancestor of the Leinstermen&#146;, and all kindreds who regarded themselves as true Laigin (Leinstermen) traced their descent back to L&oacute;egaire Lorc. Lorcmag, &#145;the plain of Lorc&#146;, was used by medieval literati to describe the territory of the Leinstermen. The epithet <i>lorc</i> is variously explained as meaning &#145;fierce, dumb, tongue-tied&#146;. The trait of dumbness, often associated with deities, links L&oacute;egaire Lorc with his renowned grandson and other ancestor of the Laigin, Labraid Loingsech, whose original name was M&oacute;en, &#145;the dumb one&#146;. They may ultimately be regarded as one character.<br>
<br>L&oacute;egaire Lorc is one of the main protagonists in the original legend of the Laigin, <span class="italic">Orgain Denna R&iacute;g</span>, &#145;The destruction of Dind R&iacute;g&#146;, which in its earliest written form may date to the seventh century. Cobthach C&oacute;el Breg, &#145;Cobthach the Thin of Brega&#146;, one of the reputed ancestors of the midland and northern dynasty, the U&iacute; N&eacute;ill, became jealous of L&oacute;egaire and of his kingship of Leinster. The legend describes Cobthach and L&oacute;egaire as brothers. Cobthach tricked L&oacute;egaire into believing that he was dead and when L&oacute;egaire came to grieve over his brother's body which was laid out in a chariot, Cobthach stabbed and mortally wounded him. An alternative version relates how Cobthach killed L&oacute;egaire at the battle of Carman, the site where the kings of Leinster were inaugurated in the medieval period. This foul deed perpetrated by Cobthach is described as <i>fingal</i>, &#145;kin slaying&#146;, and is used to explain the continuous enmity between Cobthach's descendants, the U&iacute; N&eacute;ill, and L&oacute;egaire's descendants, the Laigin. This act of kin slaying was avenged by L&oacute;egaire's grandson, Labraid Loingsech, who returned from exile, seized the kingship of Leinster, and burned Cobthach in an iron house at Dind R&iacute;g (claimed to be a site on the River Barrow near Leighlinbridge, co. Carlow).<br>
<br>Edel Bhreathnach </div>
