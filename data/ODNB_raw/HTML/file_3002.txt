<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Bourn,  Thomas</span>  (1771&#150;1832), <span class="occ">schoolteacher and educational writer</span>, was born in Hackney, Middlesex, on 19 April 1771, and was educated at a private school in Well Street. He became a teacher at the school of the Revd S. Palmer until 1791 when he became a teacher of writing and geography in the private girls' schools run by William Butler (1748&#150;1822), a Quaker. He married Butler's daughter in 1796 and had a family of eleven children.<br>
<br>Bourn published <span class="italic">A Concise Gazetteer of the most Remarkable Places in the World</span> (1807; 3rd edn, 1822), a compilation of over 900 pages which sought to make geography more attractive to learners by associating places with historical occurrences, famous persons, and remarkable facts. Drawing upon the educational works of his father-in-law (whose <span class="italic">Arithmetical Questions &hellip; for the Use of Young Ladies</span> he edited in 1829) and the biographical compilations of his friend Stephen Jones, Bourn sought to present useful knowledge in an appealing way, to draw young female readers away from the dangerous frivolities of popular novels. Bourn claimed to use a conversational method of teaching in preference to learning by rote: &#145;Young people require to be entertained as well as admonished&#146;  (<span class="italic">Concise Gazetteer</span>, viii). He died at his house in Mare Street, Hackney, Middlesex, on 20 August 1832.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> M. C. Curthoys </div>
