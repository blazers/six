<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Nutter,  William</span>  (1759?&#150;1802), <span class="occ">engraver and draughtsman</span>, is said to have been a pupil of John Raphael Smith and may have worked on stipples published under Smith's name in the 1780s. His name appears on a few prints published by Smith after 1785, including Smith's own design <span class="italic">The Moralist</span> (1787). He went on to engrave many good dotted plates after contemporary artists, twelve of them for the printseller Emanuel Matthias Diemar or his widow. <span class="italic">The Benevolent Cottager</span> (1788) after Francis Wheatley, <span class="italic">Sunday Morning, a Cottage Family Going to Church</span> (1795) after William Redmore Bigg, and <span class="italic">Rosebud, or, The Judgment of Paris</span> (1796) after Richard Westall, are typical enough of his better plates. <span class="italic">The Destruction of the Bastille</span> (1792) after Henry Singleton and <span class="italic">The Burial of General Fraser</span> (1794) after John Graham were unusually large and ambitious historical subjects. Nutter was a prolific engraver of the leading miniaturist Samuel Shelley, including those prepared for <span class="italic">The Cabinet of Genius</span> (1787), a collection of poems each adorned with an original design by Shelley. Much of Nutter's other output consisted of portraits, but he exhibited some allegorical designs at the Royal Academy in 1782 and 1783. He died, unmarried, at his residence in Somers Town, London, on 14 March 1802 and was buried in the dissenters' ground of Whitefield's Tabernacle, Tottenham Court Road. In his will he left &pound;50 in trust for &#145;the charity called the Sick Mans Friend at Whitefields Chapel&#146;  (fol. 420<i>v</i>) besides other small gifts to his professional colleagues.<br>
<br>Timothy Clayton and Anita McConnell </div>
