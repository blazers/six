<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Peebles,  David</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1530&#150;1576), <span class="occ">composer</span>, was a canon at the Augustinian priory of St Andrews until the Scottish Reformation of 1559&#150;60. In 1571 Robart Stewart, commendator of St Andrews and a prominent reformer, granted Peebles and his spouse, Katherine Kynneir (<i>d</i>. 1592), a charter of land within the grounds of St Andrews, suggesting that he had remained in his former monastery. According to marginalia in the Wode partbooks, as &#145;ane of the cheiff musitians into this land&#146; he was commissioned by James Stewart, former prior of St Andrews, natural son of James V and future earl of Moray and regent of Scotland, to &#145;set thrie pairts to the tenor&#146; of certain of the psalms in a &#145;plane and dulce&#146; style thereby forgoing &#145;the curiosity of musike&#146;. Perhaps as a consequence of this stricture, Peebles &#145;wes not earnest&#146; to complete what he may have regarded as a menial task, but the tenacity of the prior's agent Thomas Wode was eventually repaid with an extremely accomplished set of 105 psalm settings. These form the core of the Wode partbooks. Although Wode's manuscript remained unpublished, some of Peebles's psalms appeared in Edward Millar's psalter of 1635, where he is numbered among &#145;the primest musicians that ever this kingdome had&#146;. The Wode partbooks also preserve two Latin motets by Peebles. According to Wode's marginalia, Peebles had composed his elegant four-part polyphonic setting of &#145;Si quis diligit me&#146; about 1530 and presented it to James V, who &#145;being a musitian &hellip; did lyke it verray weill&#146;. In 1576 Robart Stewart commissioned Peebles to set a protestant Latin version of <span class="roman">Psalm 3</span>, <span class="italic">Quam multi domine</span>. This dynamic work eloquently demonstrates Peebles's continuing acquaintance with contemporary compositional practices. He was dead by 1592, the date of his wife's will, which describes her as Peebles's &#145;relict&#146; and also mentions two lawful sons, Andrew and Thomas.<br>
<br>D. James Ross </div>
