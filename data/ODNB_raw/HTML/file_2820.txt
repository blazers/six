<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Bond,  Daniel</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1725, <em>d.</em> 1803), <span class="occ">painter and japanner</span>, sometimes called John Daniel Bond, was baptized on 6 July 1725 at Stroud, Gloucestershire, the son of William Bond, bookseller and bookbinder, and probably Mary Duks; he was the elder brother of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/57290/', "Capel Bond (bap. 1730, d. 1790)")</script>, composer. His uncle the Revd Daniel Bond was usher of the Crypt School in Gloucester, and it is likely that he was educated there. Nothing is known of Bond's early career until 1761, when under the name Daniel Bond he exhibited a landscape drawing after Vernet at the Society of Artists in London.<br>
<br>From 1762 to 1769 Bond exhibited over thirty landscape paintings at the Free Society of Artists in London, winning the second premium prize for landscape in 1764. His work was described as highly finished, with figures, in the style of George Lambert and Gaspar Poussin. It is possible that during this period he taught drawing; in 1763 James Butler exhibited <span class="italic">A Drawing of a Landscape after Mr. Bond of Birmingham</span> at the Free Society of Artists. Bond exhibited only twice after 1769, showing one painting in 1775 and two in 1780, both at the Free Society of Artists. He also provided nine illustrations for William Shenstone's <span class="italic">Works in Verse and Prose</span> (1764) and the tailpiece for the fourth edition of Robert Dodsley's <span class="italic">Cleone: a Tragedy</span> (1765), all engraved by Charles Grignion. Besides painting on canvas, Bond was employed decorating japanned wares for the papier m&acirc;ch&eacute; producer Henry Clay in Birmingham. The dates of his employment here are not known. He became a freeman of the city of Gloucester on 2 February 1789, when he was described as a painter and japanner of Birmingham.<br>
<br>Bond was married; it is possible his wife was Susannah Hodgetts (<i>fl</i>. 1758&#150;1803), to whom a Daniel Bond was married on 1 October 1758 at St Philip's, Birmingham, and who together had three daughters. Bond's personal circles were noted by contemporaries as &#145;confined&#146;  (<span class="italic">GM</span>, 1803). He does not appear to have had any contact with influential groups in Birmingham concerned with the manufacture of wares, such as the Lunar Society. He died on 18 December 1803 at his home, at Hagley Row, Edgbaston, Birmingham. In the months following his death there was a small flurry of interest in the paintings owned by his widow in the <span class="italic">Gentleman's Magazine</span>, although no record of a public sale has been traced. A large landscape by Bond is in Aston Hall, Birmingham.<br>
<br>Nicholas Grindle </div>
