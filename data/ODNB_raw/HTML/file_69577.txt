<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lane,  A.</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1695&#150;1700), <span class="occ">grammarian</span>, is known only through the evidence provided by the two grammars he has left us. He styles himself MA on the title-page of <span class="italic">A Rational and Speedy Method of Attaining to the Latin Tongue</span> (1695), but no record survives of his attendance at university. From Lane's prefatory epistle dedicated to Sir Richard Reynell, lord chief justice of Ireland, we learn that Lane had been encouraged to use his linguistic ideas to teach Latin to Reynell's eight-year-old son. As a result of the son's progress, &#145;several Persons of Quality thought fit (on your Lordship's Recommendation) to put their Children under the same conduct&#146;  (<span class="italic">A Rational and Speedy Method</span>, sig. A2<i>r</i>). By 1700, according to the title of his <span class="italic">A Key to the Art of Letters</span> (1700), he was &#145;A. Lane, M.A, late Master of the Free School of <i>Leominster</i> in <i>Herefordshire</i>, now Teacher of a private School at <i>Mile-end-green</i> near <i>Stepney</i>&#146;.<br>
<br>Lane's two books show that he was familiar with logic and grammatical theories ranging from Priscian to Port-Royal, but his contribution to knowledge lies in the original adaptation of his material; <span class="italic">Method</span> has, in addition to a Latin grammar, the first &#145;universal&#146; grammar in English, &#145;containing such Precepts as are common to all Languages&#146;  (title-page). His philosophy was continued into the <span class="italic">Key</span> since the rules &#145;are the same in all Languages how different soever they be&#146;  (preface, x).<br>
<br>R. D. Smith </div>
