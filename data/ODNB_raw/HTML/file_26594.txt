<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Storer,  Thomas</span>  (<em>c.</em>1571&#150;1604), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was the son of John Storer, a citizen of London. He entered Christ Church, Oxford, in 1587, graduating BA on 27 March 1591 and MA on 13 May 1604.<br>
<br>Wood writes that, while at Oxford, Storer &#145;was held in great renown for his most excellent vein in poesy&#146;, and contributed &#145;verses printed in several books made occasionally by members of the University&#146;  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath Oxon.</span>, 750&#150;51). Among Storer's occasional poems can be numbered a prefatory poem to William Vaughan's <span class="italic">Golden Grove</span> (1600), and some Latin epigrams in the 1603 collection <span class="italic">Academiae Oxoniensis pietas</span>, devoted, like the rest of the volume, to celebrating the accession of King James. Wood adds that Storer also published some &#145;Pastoral Aires and Madrigals&#146;  (ibid.), which &#145;were afterwards remitted into a book called &#147;<span class="roman">England's Helicon</span>&#148;&#146;  (ibid.), but as Thomas Seccombe noted, this appears to be a mistake  (<span class="italic">DNB</span>). While Storer's work does not appear in <span class="italic">England's Helicon</span> (1600), about twenty extracts from his poem <span class="italic">The Life and Death of Thomas Wolsey</span> were used by the anthologist Robert Allott in <span class="italic">England's Parnassus</span> (1600).<br>
<br>
<span class="italic">The Life and Death of Thomas Wolsey, Cardinall &hellip; by Thomas Storer, Student of Christ Church in Oxford</span> is the work by which Storer is known. It was published in 1599, and consists of three parts all in rhyme royal stanzas, subtitled &#145;Wolseius aspirans&#146;, &#145;Wolseius triumphans&#146;, and &#145;Wolseius moriens&#146;. Throughout the poem, Wolsey's ghost tells the story of his own rise and fall, in a manner which superficially resembles Thomas Churchyard's poem on the same theme in the 1587 edition of the <span class="italic">Mirror for Magistrates</span>.<br>
<br>Something of Storer's intellectual milieu can be seen from the volume's links with other writers. Among those who wrote commendatory verses for it were Charles Fitzgeffrey, Edward and Thomas Michelborne, and John Sprint, a Christ Church colleague. The volume is dedicated to John Howson, the queen's chaplain. Storer seems to have regarded the earl of Northumberland as either a current or a prospective patron, to judge from the lavish praise of him introduced into the narrative (ll. 905&#150;17). Among those who admired the poem are Storer's colleague Alberic Gentilis  (<span class="italic">Laudes academiae Perusinae et Oxoniensis</span>, 1605, 41).<br>
<br>According to Wood, Storer died in London in November 1604, and was buried in the church of St Michael Bassishaw. This cannot be confirmed from the registers, which rather puzzlingly record the death of a Thomas Storer, son of John, on 4 January 1599  (Clark, 165). Clearly, in the light of Storer's later works, this cannot be the poet.<br>
<br>Matthew Steggle </div>
