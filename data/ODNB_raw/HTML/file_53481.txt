<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Campsall,  Richard</span>  (<em>c.</em>1280&#150;<em>c.</em>1330), <span class="occ">theologian</span>, was born at Campsall, in the West Riding of Yorkshire, probably between 1280 and 1285. He first appears as a bachelor in arts at Balliol College, Oxford; by 1306 he was regent master in arts at Merton College; a papal letter of 1317 addresses him as bachelor in theology, and by 1322 he was a master in theology. Between 1306 and 1322 he is named repeatedly in Merton rolls. On 10 January 1326, as bursar and procurator, Campsall was locum tenens for the chancellor of the university in a dispute with the mayor of Oxford, who had moved the town pillory: Campsall negotiated a new location for it and absolved the mayor from the chancellor's excommunication. On his death <i>c</i>.1330 five volumes owned by Campsall went to Merton: a glossed psalter, two works by Augustine, one by Hugh of St Victor, and &#145;some works&#146; by Anselm, all of them missing when the catalogue of 1350&#150;60 was drawn up. Campsall was buried in the college chapel at Merton; his tombstone has been securely identified, despite having been moved, and despite the disappearance of both a brass cross &#145;on steps&#146; and of the inscription, still visible in the seventeenth century: &#145;Orate pro anima Magistri Ricardi de Camsale sacre pagine Professor &hellip; hic jacet tumulatum&#146;  (Wood, 1.23&#150;4). Campsall should not be confused with a namesake who was rector of St Martin's, Canterbury.<br>
<br>The catalogue of St Augustine's Abbey, Canterbury, ascribes two works to Campsall&#151;<span class="italic">Questiones super tres libros phisicorum</span> and <span class="italic">Notabilitates eiusdem super omnes libros phisicorum</span>&#151;but neither is known to be extant. Campsall's certain writings are a series of twenty disputed questions of c.1306 on the <span class="italic">Prior Analytics</span>; a disputed question, <span class="italic">Whether Matter Can Be Without Form</span>; aphorisms <span class="italic">On Contingency and Foreknowledge</span>, extant in one manuscript, but also in a Bologna printing of 1496 from a slightly variant manuscript base; and a short essay on the universal. All have been published by Synan. There survives also a brief treatment of &#145;Whether one can act against one's conscience&#146;  (Etzkorn, 322). But a truncated <span class="italic">Logica Campsale Anglici, valde utilis et realis contra Ocham</span>, long ascribed to Campsall, cannot be his. Modern scholars suggest that Campsall's works may be supplemented through citations by contemporaries of his opinions which probably stem from a lost writing on the <span class="italic">Sentences</span>. He is credited with a solution of paralogisms on the Trinity (&#145;The Father is God; the Son is God; therefore, the Father is the Son&#146;), first by invoking the &#145;rule of Anselm&#146;, that in divinity all is one unless blocked by a relation, and second by pronouncing such syllogisms invalid owing to the &#145;fallacy of accident&#146;. In the summary division of fourteenth-century thinkers into Scotists and Ockhamists, Campsall may count as mildly Ockhamist  (Synan, 2.3, 4), or alternatively Ockham as Campsallist  (Tachau, &#145;The influence of Richard Campsall&#146;, 123).<br>
<br>Edward A. Synan </div>
