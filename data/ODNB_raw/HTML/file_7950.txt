<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Dove,  Nathaniel</span>  (1711&#150;1754), <span class="occ">writing master</span>, trained in London under Philip Pickering, a writing master in Paternoster Row. Almost nothing is known of his life, except that he described himself as &#145;Master of the Academy at Hoxton&#146;  (Heal, 46) and was one of the schoolmasters recommended in T. Dilworth's <span class="italic">A New Guide to the English Tongue</span>. He is chiefly known for his book <span class="italic">The Progress of Time</span> (1740), where verses on the seasons and months are composed in a mixture of calligraphic styles, including &#145;German&#146; text and &#145;Gothic&#146; hands. Dove's designs for these plates were engraved by Thomas Gardener and the work was dedicated to Prince George, the eldest son of the prince of Wales. Between 1738 and 1740 Dove also contributed twenty designs to George Bickham's luxury engraved copybook <span class="italic">The Universal Penman</span> (1733&#150;41), and a survey of these shows his facility in a variety of hands, including the businesslike round hand and the more ornamental square and German texts. Although he was evidently fond of decorating his texts with flourishes, the greater part of his work in <span class="italic">The Universal Penman</span> was of a more practical orientation, providing exemplary formats for tradesmen's bills and accounts. According to William Massey's conclusion, in his <span class="italic">The Origins &amp; Progress of Letters</span> (1763), these performances must have recommended Dove to his final lucrative position as a clerk in the &#145;Victualling-office, on Tower-hill&#146;  (Massey, 2.76). It was while in this employment that he died in 1754.<br>
<br>Lucy Peltz </div>
