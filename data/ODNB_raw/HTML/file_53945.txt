<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hengler,  Jenny Louise</span>  (<em>b. </em>1849), <span class="occ">equestrian performer</span>, was born on 23 March 1849, at Lewes, Sussex, the second daughter of the circus proprietor <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/12926/', "(Frederick) Charles Hengler (1820&#150;1887)")</script> and his wife, Mary Ann Frances Sprake (<i>c</i>.1828&#150;1902). She performed as a juvenile artiste in her father's arenas from an early age, her first recorded appearance being in the 1856&#150;7 winter season at Bradford, in the pantomime. A great beauty, she became one of the most celebrated <i>haute &eacute;cole</i> riders of her day, setting the standard against which others were judged. In November 1865 the <span class="italic">Liverpool Daily Post</span> called her &#145;the most remarkable feminine attraction of the day&#146;, and at the end of the season observed that, &#145;It is feared that when we see her next she will be much grown, but the flower will be expanded and more beautiful&#146;.<br>
<br>On 6 May 1874 Hengler married the putative Polish count Waldemar Alexander Oscar Kamienski, a rider who had been billed as Alexander Oscar at Hengler's since 1869. The wedding was not widely reported, and she never rode in any of her father's arenas again; however, as Jenny Louise Hengler-Oscar, she appeared in her husband's International circus, being noted at Newcastle upon Tyne in 1882. The Kamienskis later went to the United States, where Count Kamienski ran a horse training and riding school at Beretta College. They lived in Astoria, Long Island, with their three children, Philip, Charles, and Beatrice. She visited Britain in 1892, on the occasion of her sister Lydia's marriage, but nothing is known of her after this date.<br>
<br>John M. Turner </div>
