<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Philipps,  Jenkin Thomas</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1755), <span class="occ">translator and tutor</span>, was of Welsh origin. He studied at the University of Basel, where in 1707 he delivered a Latin oration, &#145;Uses of travel&#146;, which was published in London in 1715. He appears to have held a place in the London court of George I, according to the dedication to his <span class="italic">Discours touchant l'origine et le progr&egrave;s de la religion chr&eacute;tienne parmi la nation Britannique</span> (1716?), which was written in Latin and French. Philipps was a highly accomplished linguist and worked as a private tutor from 1717 to 1720. He expounded his methods in <span class="italic">A compendious way of teaching ancient and modern languages, formerly practised by &hellip; T. Faber &hellip; now with little alteration successfully executed in London</span> (2nd edn, 1723). By 1726 he was tutor to the children of George II, and wrote for their use <span class="italic">An Essay towards a Rational Grammar; with Easy Rules in English, to Learn Latin</span> (1726; 3rd edn, 1741). His royal pupils included William Augustus, duke of Cumberland, for whom he published several Latin epistles, and he remained his private tutor when the boy attended lessons at Westminster School.<br>
<br>On 13 November 1732 Philipps was appointed historiographer royal, with a stipend of &pound;200, a position that he retained until his death. He translated a number of historical works from German into English, and translated from German the Russian catechism by Theofan, bishop of Pskov and Novgorod. He compiled a history of the laws and constitutions of northern European states, which was published in 1752. He died a widower in London on 22 February 1755.<br>
<br>Charlotte Fell-Smith<span class="italic">, rev. </span> S. J. Skedd </div>
