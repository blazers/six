<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Laing,  Alexander</span>  (1778&#150;1838), <span class="occ">antiquary</span>, the illegitimate son of an Aberdeen advocate named Michie, was born at Coull, Aberdeenshire. He was reasonably well educated and highly talented, but his career was hampered by an erratic temperament. For some years before his death he was employed as an itinerant bookseller and stationer, which earned him the nickname Stashie Laing. The first of Laing's antiquarian writings, <span class="italic">The Caledonian itinerary, or, A tour on the banks of the Dee, with historical notes from the best authorities</span>, was published at Aberdeen in 1819. During the next three years Laing edited an annual, the first two issues of which were entitled <span class="italic">The Eccentric Magazine</span>, and the third <span class="italic">The Lounger's Commonplace Book</span>; this was a collection of anecdotes and curiosities. In 1822 he published <span class="italic">Scarce Ancient Ballads Never before Published, with Notes</span>, and in the following year a similar collection, entitled <span class="italic">The Thistle of Scotland</span>. In 1828 he published his chief work, <span class="italic">The Donean Tourist, Interspersed with Anecdotes and Ancient National Ballads</span>, a useful collection of folklore.<br>
<br>Laing's last work was <span class="italic">An Cluaran Albannach: a repository of ballads, many never before published, to which are appended copious notes, historical, biographical, illustrative, and critical</span> (1834). Laing died at Boltingstone, a roadside inn between Tarland and Strathdon, and was buried in the churchyard of Coldstone, Aberdeenshire.<br>
<br>Thomas Seccombe<span class="italic">, rev. </span> H. C. G. Matthew </div>
