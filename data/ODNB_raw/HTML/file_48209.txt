<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Markham,  Ernest</span>  (1881&#150;1937), <span class="occ">gardener and writer on horticulture</span>, was born at Newbottle, Northamptonshire, on 8 March 1881, the third son in the family of at least three sons and four daughters of William Markham (1852/3&#150;1946), agricultural labourer and shepherd, and his wife, Dinah Diana, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Astley (1853&#150;1922). From his youth he worked in a succession of large gardens: Newbottle Manor, Northamptonshire; Crown Court East, Worcester; The Dell, King's Norton, Birmingham; and Wortley Hall, Sheffield. Having been the leading foreman at King's Walden Bury, Hitchin, he became head gardener to Lady Chichester at Arlington Court, Devon, about 1900. From 1905 to 1910 he worked for Lady Marcus Beresford at Bishopsgate, Englefield Green, Surrey. He was a widower at the time of his marriage, at Whittlesey, Cambridgeshire, on 10 July 1912, to Miriam Elizabeth (1887&#150;1953), daughter of William Price, coachman.<br>
<br>In 1914 Markham became head gardener to William Robinson (1838&#150;1935) of Gravetye Manor, near East Grinstead, Sussex, living with Miriam, his wife, at The Moat, a cottage on the estate. He loved Gravetye and directed more than a dozen gardeners in the productive and ornamental departments. From 1928 the ageing Robinson conferred upon him sole responsibility for managing and developing the garden, and at Robinson's death Markham was left with the life possession of Moat cottage. He continued to manage the famous Gravetye Garden, refusing many offers of employment elsewhere. In 1933 the Royal Horticultural Society recognized his services to horticulture by making him an associate of honour, and in June 1935 he read a paper on ornamental climbing plants to the society.<br>
<br>Markham contributed regularly to <span class="italic">Gardening Illustrated</span> and wrote two books, <span class="italic">The Large and Small Flowered Clematis and their Cultivation in the Open Air</span> (1935) and <span class="italic">Raspberries and Kindred Fruits</span> (1936). His contributions to the later editions of William Robinson's <span class="italic">English Flower Garden</span> included sections on lilies and clematis. He kept a large collection of clematis varieties and is commemorated by the hybrid clematis &#145;Ernest Markham&#146; and <i>Clematis macropetala</i> &#145;Markham's Pink&#146;. He died of a heart attack on 6 December 1937 at the Queen Victoria Cottage Hospital, East Grinstead, survived by his wife; he was buried at West Hoathly, Sussex, on 9 December. Obituaries described him as a modest and diffident man with an encyclopaedic knowledge of plants.<br>
<br>A. A. Sclater </div>
