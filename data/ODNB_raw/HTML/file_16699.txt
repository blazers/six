<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lindsay,  George</span>, third Lord Spynie  (<em>d.</em> 1671), <span class="occ">nobleman</span>, was the second child, and second son, of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/16681/', "Alexander Lindsay, second Lord Spynie (d. 1646)")</script>, and his second wife, Lady Margaret, only daughter of George Hay, first earl of Kinnoull and lord chancellor of Scotland (1622&#150;34). He was served heir to his father in 1646. Like his father he was a staunch supporter of Charles I. He opposed the surrender of the king to the English parliament in 1647 and, as colonel of the Stirling and Clackmannan horse, supported the engagement negotiated with him in December 1647. In December 1650 the Scottish parliament, then reconciling a number of former engagers, appointed him colonel of foot for Angus (Forfarshire), though he did not make his peace with the kirk until early 1651. He was among the former engagers admitted to the committee for regulating the army in March 1651, and led his regiment into England in Charles II's 1651 campaign. Captured at the battle of Worcester, he was imprisoned in the Tower of London on 16 September 1651. His estates were forfeited on the proclamation of the Cromwellian act of grace and pardon, from which he was excepted, at the Mercat Cross of Edinburgh on 5 May 1654. The forfeiture was later changed to a heavy fine which, with other financial obligations apparently incurred in the royal cause, left him greatly in debt. With the death of Ludovic Lindsay, sixteenth earl of Crawford, in November 1652, he was served his heir, although he did not inherit the earldom.<br>
<br>Following the Restoration Spynie's losses were placed before a commission of enquiry, in 1661, and he secured royal protection from arrest for debt. He sat in parliament in 1663, 1669, and 1670. He died insolvent and without heirs in January 1671, and was buried in Holyrood on 21 January.<br>
<br>T. F. Henderson<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Alison G. Muir </div>
