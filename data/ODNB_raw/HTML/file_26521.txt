<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Mackenzie,  Mary Elizabeth Frederica  Stewart-</span>, Lady Hood  (1783&#150;1862), <span class="occ">chief of clan Mackenzie</span>, eldest of the six daughters and coheirs of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/14126/', "Francis Humberston Mackenzie, Baron Seaforth (1754&#150;1815)")</script>, and Mary (1754/5&#150;1829), daughter of Baptist Proby, dean of Lichfield, and brother of Lord Carysfort, was born at Tarnadale on 27 March 1783. In 1801 she went with her family to Barbados, where her father was governor; she met there, and married at Bridgetown, on 6 November 1804, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/13677/', "Sir Samuel Hood (1762&#150;1814)")</script>, vice-admiral of the white. They returned to Britain, where Lady Hood established a firm friendship with Sir Walter Scott, despite her whig sympathies; he described her as having &#145;the spirit of the chieftainess in every drop of her blood&#146;  (Lockhart, 306). In 1814 she accompanied her husband to the East Indies on his appointment as commander-in-chief. She travelled extensively in India, and claimed to be the first British woman to shoot a tiger in that country. Sir Samuel Hood died on 24 December 1814, and in the following year his widow succeeded to the family estates on the death of her father, his four sons having predeceased him. She thus became the chief of clan Mackenzie. The failure of the male line when there should be a deaf clan chief had been predicted in an old curse, and was apparently fulfilled in this generation.<br>
<br>On 21 May 1817 Lady Hood married James Alexander Stewart of Glasserton (1784&#150;1843), elder son of Admiral Keith Stewart (<i>d</i>. 1795), third son of Alexander Stewart, sixth earl of Galloway. On their marriage the name Mackenzie was added to that of Stewart. They had three sons and three daughters. Her husband was MP for Ross and Cromarty from 1831 to 1837, governor of Ceylon in 1837&#150;40, and lord high commissioner of the Ionian Islands from 1840 to 1843. Her granddaughter, by her son, Keith William (1818&#150;1881), was the hostess <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/51948/', "Mary Jeune, Lady St Helier")</script>.<br>
<br>Ill health and the financial difficulties which threatened to overwhelm the Seaforth estates prevented Mary Stewart-Mackenzie from leading the Mackenzies at Scott's pageant to welcome George IV to Scotland in 1822. Her financial difficulties were resolved only on the marriage of her third daughter, Louisa, to William Baring, second Lord Ashburton, in 1858 [<i>see </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/50780/', "Baring, Louisa")</script>]. She died at Brahan Castle, Ross-shire, on 28 November 1862, her second husband having died in 1843; a lengthy procession of mourners, led by pipers, followed her coffin for the 20 miles to Fortrose Cathedral where she was buried.<br>
<br>T. F. Henderson<span class="italic">, rev. </span> K. D. Reynolds </div>
