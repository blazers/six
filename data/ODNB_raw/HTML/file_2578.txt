<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Blake, Sir  Francis</span>, second baronet  (1736/7&#150;1818), <span class="occ">political writer</span>, was the eldest surviving son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/2577/', "Sir Francis Blake, first baronet (1707/8&#150;1780)")</script>, of Twisell Castle, Norham, co. Durham, mathematician, and his wife, Isabel (<i>c</i>.1710&#150;1741), second daughter and coheir of Samuel Ayton of West Herrington, co. Durham. He was educated at Westminster School, and possibly at the Middle Temple from June 1755. On 15 January 1756 he was admitted to Trinity Hall, Cambridge, from where he graduated LLB in 1763. He became second baronet on the death of his father on 29 March 1780 and served as high sheriff of Northumberland from 1784 to 1785. In the following years he published his three most significant studies of state finances <span class="italic">The Efficacy of a Sinking Fund of one Million per annum Considered</span> in 1786, <span class="italic">The Propriety of an Actual Payment of the Public Debt Considered</span> in 1786, and <span class="italic">The True Policy of Great Britain Considered</span> in 1787. These, and other pieces, were republished collectively under the title <span class="italic">Political Tracts</span>, first at Berwick in 1788 and again at London in 1795.<br>
<br>Blake spent some &pound;80,000 on building Fowberry Tower on the banks of the River Till; his project was never completed and the structure was sold to George Colley, an agriculturist, in 1807 for &pound;45,000. Blake married Elizabeth Douglas on 15 April 1772. Their eldest son, also named Francis (1774/5&#150;1860), represented Berwick in parliament between 1826 and 1834 and published severe criticisms on the conduct of the House of Lords in relation to the corn laws. The elder Sir Francis died on 22 May 1818 at Twisell Castle; his son inherited the title and died on 10 September 1860, aged eighty-five.<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Philip Carter </div>
