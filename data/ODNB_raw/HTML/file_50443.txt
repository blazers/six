<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Cellach mac M&aacute;ele Coba</span>  (<em>d.</em> 658), <span class="occ">joint high-king of Ireland</span>, belonged to Cen&eacute;l Conaill, the branch of the U&iacute; N&eacute;ill settled in what is now Donegal. His mother may have been Croinsech, daughter of &Aacute;ed Find, king of Osraige, and he may have been married to Dathnat (or Domnait). Cellach appears to have been joint ruler of Cen&eacute;l Conaill with his brother <a name="cosubject_50443"></a><span class="headword">Conall C&oacute;el mac M&aacute;ele Coba</span>  (<em>d.</em> 654) from the death of their uncle, Domnall mac &Aacute;eda, in 642. The brothers' authority over Cen&eacute;l Conaill was challenged by their first cousin, &Oacute;engus mac Domnaill, but they defeated and killed him in 650. Their status as high-kings of Ireland was accepted by the main twelfth-century regnal lists but not in the late seventh-century text <span class="italic">Baile Chuinn</span> (&#145;The frenzy of Conn&#146;), a regnal list of Tara (the formal title of the high-kings) in the form of a prophecy. The silence of this source is, however, far from conclusive, since it was composed during the reign of F&iacute;nsnechtae Fledach (<i>r</i>. 675&#150;95); he belonged to the U&iacute; N&eacute;ill of Brega and <span class="italic">Baile Chuinn</span> is notably hostile to the claims of the rival Cen&eacute;l Conaill.<br>
<br>The chronicle of Ireland (the &#145;common stock&#146; of the annals of Ulster, annals of Tigernach, and <span class="italic">Chronicum Scotorum</span>) was uncertain about this period, for it notes under 643: &#145;Here there is doubt as to who reigned after Domnall. Some historiographers say that four kings, namely Cellach and Conall C&oacute;el and the two sons of &Aacute;ed Sl&aacute;ine, namely Diarmait and Blathmac [Bla&iacute;mac], ruled in shared reigns&#146;  (<span class="italic">Ann. Ulster</span>, s.a.).<br>
<br>At least broadly this may be accurate: these were the allies of Domnall mac &Aacute;eda in his great victory at Mag Roth in 637 and would be the natural heirs of his power after his peaceful death in 642. Diarmait mac &Aacute;eda Sl&aacute;ine was clearly the most active ruler in the midlands, winning a major battle against Guaire Aidne of the Connachta and his Munster allies in 649. The probability, therefore, is that, whatever the titular succession to the kingship of Tara, effective authority was shared between Cen&eacute;l Conaill, led by the brothers Cellach and Conall C&oacute;el, and S&iacute;l n&Aacute;eda Sl&aacute;ine (the U&iacute; N&eacute;ill of Brega), also led by two brothers, Diarmait and Bla&iacute;mac (or Blathmac). Indeed, the alliance between these two branches of the U&iacute; N&eacute;ill, at opposite ends of the crescent of U&iacute; N&eacute;ill kingdoms stretching across the northern half of Ireland from the River Liffey to Donegal, dominated most of the seventh century.<br>
<br>Conall C&oacute;el was killed in 654, probably by Diarmait mac &Aacute;eda Sl&aacute;ine; from then until his own death in 658 Cellach seems to have reigned alone. According to one set of annals, Cellach's death took place at Brug na Bo&iacute;nne, the Neolithic passage grave at Newgrange on the Boyne (Meath).<br>
<br>T. M. Charles-Edwards </div>
