<span class="headword">Vere <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Cecil]</span>,  Anne  de</span>, countess of Oxford  (1556&#150;1588), <span class="occ">courtier</span>, was born on 5 December 1556, the daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/4983/', "William Cecil, first Baron Burghley (1520/21&#150;1598)")</script>, and his second wife, Mildred (1526&#150;1589) [<i>see </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/46675/', "Cecil, Mildred")</script>], daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/6155/', "Sir Anthony Cooke")</script>. Anne presumably gained the equivalent of a grammar school education in the cultivated Cecil household. Writing to Lord Burghley in 1577, the German educator Johannes Sturm (Sturmius) expressed his understanding that Anne &#145;speaks Latin also&#146;  (4 Dec 1577, <span class="italic">CSP for.</span>, <i>1577&#150;8</i>, no. 469).<br>
<br>In 1569 Anne was contracted to marry Philip Sidney, but by July 1571 she was engaged to Burghley's ward, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/28208/', "Edward de Vere, seventeenth earl of Oxford (1550&#150;1604)")</script>. Oxford's biographers have speculated that Burghley engineered the match (despite his protestations to the contrary), yet Anne may have been an attractive as well as accomplished young lady. Her brother, Robert Cecil, thought so, at least; writing from dingy lodgings at Ostend in 1587 he observed that &#145;If my lady of Oxford were here, her bewty wold quickly be marred&#146;  (TNA: PRO, SP 84/21, fol. 242). De Vere married Anne at Westminster Abbey on 19 December 1571, with Queen Elizabeth and many prominent courtiers in attendance. The match, however, proved disastrous; Oxford denied the paternity of his daughter Elizabeth, who was born on 2 July 1575 while he travelled on the continent, and he lived apart from his wife until some time in 1582.<br>
<br>During the separation Anne maintained cordial relations with the queen; this is evidenced in her exchange of new year's gifts with her sovereign every year from 1575 to 1588. The countess received five book dedications, all between 1575 and 1581. In his dedication to Anne, Geoffrey Fenton described his <span class="italic">Golden Epistles</span> (1575) as a &#145;morall discourse&#146; mingled with &#145;texts of divinitie&#146;  (sig. *2). The last two dedications are translated expositions of <span class="roman">Ephesians</span>, the first of Niels Hemmingsen's exposition, by Abraham Fleming (1580), the second, an anonymous translation of Chrysostom (1581). In between are two books by de Vere retainers who had already dedicated works to the earl. George Baker terms Anne &#145;his singular good Lady&#146;  (sig. A2) in the dedication to his translation <span class="italic">Newe Jewell of Health</span> (1576), from the work by Conrad Gesner. Similarly, John Brooke's <span class="italic">Christian Discourse</span> (1578), is dedicated to &#145;his singuler good ladie and mistres, the countesse of Oxenforde&#146;. Brooke praises her humility and courtesy as well as her &#145;good inclination to vertue and godlinesse&#146;  (sig. A2, A3<i>v</i>). Baker praises her &#145;wit, learning, and authoritie&#146;  (sig. A2<i>v</i>), Fenton her &#145;learning and judgement&#146;  (sig. *2), and Fleming her regard for learning and &#145;zealous love to religion&#146;  (sig. *3).<br>
<br>BL, Lansdowne MS 104 preserves copies in Lord Burghley's hand of two letters that the countess sent to her husband in December 1581; in both she professes her devotion to de Vere and her desire for reconciliation. Burghley has corrected several readings in the second letter. After her reconciliation with Oxford, Anne gave birth to three more daughters: Bridget (<i>b</i>. 6 April 1584), Susan (<i>b</i>. 26 May 1587), and Frances, who died an infant on 12 September 1587. The couple's only son, styled Lord Bolebec (Bulbeck), died within four days of his birth in May 1583. Anne died at Greenwich on 5 June 1588 and was buried at Westminster Abbey on 25 June.<br>
<br>In <span class="italic">Pandora</span> (1584), John Southern credited Anne with four sonnets and two quatrains memorializing her dead son. All six poems, however, are couched in Southern's uniquely arhythmic meters, which are neither accentual nor syllabic. The content of all six draw heavily as well on his favourite poet, Desportes. They could perhaps be translations of Anne's elegies in Latin or another language, but are probably prosopopoeias outright.<br>
<br>Steven W. May </div>
