<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Streat,  William</span>  (1599/1600&#150;1666<span class="mult">x</span>9), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman</span>, was the son of John Streete, merchant, of Dartmouth, Devon. Having entered Exeter College, Oxford, early in 1617, he matriculated on 8 May 1621 aged twenty-one, graduated BA on 31 January 1622, and proceeded MA on 10 June 1624. He was ordained and became rector first, in 1630, of St Edmund on the Bridge, Exeter, and then, in 1632, of South Pool, Devon.<br>
<br>According to Anthony Wood, after 1641 Streat sided with the presbyterians, and &#145;preached very bitterly&#146; against Charles I and the royalists, &#145;blasting them with the name of Bloody papists&#146;. Later he &#145;became a desperate enemy to, and continually preached against&#146; the exiled Charles II, spreading from his pulpit satirical propaganda from parliamentarian newspapers and pamphlets  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span>, 3.728). Yet his only publication, <span class="italic">The dividing of the hooff, or, Seeming-contradictions throughout sacred scriptures, distinguish'd, resolv'd and apply'd</span> (1654), while it acknowledges the power of &#145;his Highness&#146; (Cromwell), placed by God as a steward, and looks forward to the thousand-year rule of the saints, regrets the storms and madness among God's people, &#145;our spirituall divisions in points of religion&#146;. A substantial 496-page work, it attempts to reconcile 295 ostensibly contradictory texts from the <span class="roman">Old</span> and <span class="roman">New</span> testaments. Appealing to the conciliatory tradition of John Jewel and William Perkins, it seeks to &#145;cast out that evill spirit of contradiction among professors of the Gospel&#146; and &#145;to bring a poor creature into Gods chamber of presence&#146;  (Streat, dedications).<br>
<br>Streat remained at South Pool, and conformed in 1662. At an unknown date he had married Amy, and in his will, drawn up on 14 June 1666, he named four of their sons, Robert, Joseph, William, and John, and a daughter Sarah, and mentioned five other children, dividing among them property in Dartmouth and a lease in South Allington. He died some time before 7 December 1669, when probate was granted to his executors William and John Streete.<br>
<br>Vivienne Larminie </div>
