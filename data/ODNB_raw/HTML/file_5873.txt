<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Coleman,  William Higgins</span>  (<em>c.</em>1816&#150;1863), <span class="occ">botanist</span>, was born in Middlesex, the son of John Coleman and his wife, Constance. Following early education at the Blue Coat School, Hertford, he went in February 1832 to St John's College, Cambridge, where he graduated BA in 1836 and MA in 1838. He was ordained deacon and priest at Lincoln in 1840. During his sojourn at Cambridge Coleman became a compulsive compiler of floristic lists, and in 1839 discovered the great pignut, <i>Bunium bulbocastanum</i> L., a species new to the British Isles. In the summer of 1836 he obtained a scholastic post near East Grinstead, Sussex, where he remained for six months. There he produced his best-known plant list, the manuscript of which was edited by T. C. G. Rich and published in 1994 under the title <span class="italic">W. H. Coleman's Flora of East Grinstead (1836)</span>. During 1837 Coleman moved to Essex, where he taught at Dedham grammar school, returning to Hertford in the following year and possibly becoming a master at Christ's Hospital, Hertford.<br>
<br>In 1841, in association with the Revd Richard Holden Webb (1806&#150;1880), the compilation of a flora of Hertfordshire was announced. In the survey that followed Coleman recognized the specific status of the river water-dropwort <i>Oenanthe fluviatilis</i>, and identified the hybrid sedge <i>Carex boenninghauseniana</i> Weihe as new to the British flora. During 1847 he was teaching in Ashby-de-la-Zouch, Leicestershire, and in 1849 moved to Dunster, Somerset. Meanwhile, Webb had published the first part of <span class="italic">Flora Hertfordiensis</span>, citing Coleman as the senior co-author. The book contained innovations which were utilized by later local flora writers and set a new standard for the geographical study of British plants.<br>
<br>Back in Leicestershire and with the aid of the Revd Andrew Bloxam (1801&#150;1878) Coleman studied the critical genus <i>Rubus</i> (which includes blackberries and raspberries) as well as the floristics of the county. Coleman remained unmarried and died at Burton upon Trent, Staffordshire, on 12 September 1863. His manuscript (with Bloxam) &#145;Flora Leicestrensis&#146; passed to his friend Edwin Brown (1818&#150;1876), and thence to the Leicester Museum.<br>
<br>D. H. Kent </div>
