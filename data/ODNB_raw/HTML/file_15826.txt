<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Kyngesbury <span class="st"> [Kyngusbury]</span>,  Thomas</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1351&#150;1396), <span class="occ">Franciscan friar</span>, was a member of the Franciscan convent at Oxford by 1351, and had taken the degree of DTh there by <i>c</i>.1369. He was twenty-sixth provincial minister of the English Minorites from 1379 or 1380 to 1390 or 1392. At the beginning of the great schism he induced the English Franciscans to take an oath of adherence to Urban VI (<i>r</i>. 1378&#150;89). He was in favour at court; about 1390 Richard II urged Boniface IX (<i>r</i>. 1389&#150;1404) to provide him to the next vacant bishopric, and he preached before Richard on 25 March 1396. In August of that year Robert Morton of Bawtry, Nottinghamshire, escheator and knight of the shire, made a bequest of &pound;10 to him.<br>
<br>Kyngesbury was buried at Nottingham. Although no writings of his remain, he clearly encouraged the study of science in his order. Thus in 1380, at the instance of Joan, princess of Wales (<i>d</i>. 1385), he requested the friar John Somer to write a <span class="italic">kalendarium</span> for the years 1387&#150;1462. He also authorized a treatise on music entitled the <span class="italic">Quatuor principalia musice</span>, which was written by the Oxford Minorite John Tewkesbury (though sometimes erroneously attributed to Simon Tunstede), &#145;with the authority and consent of Friar Thomas de Kyngusbury, Master, Minister of England, A. D. 1388&#146;  (Little, 250&#150;51).<br>
<br>A. G. Little<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Linne R. Mooney </div>
