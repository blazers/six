<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Porter,  Anna Maria</span>  (1778&#150;1832), <span class="occ">novelist</span>, was born on 22 December 1778 at Salisbury, Wiltshire, the daughter of William Porter (1735&#150;1779), surgeon, and his wife, Jane, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Blenkinsop (1745&#150;1831). She was the younger sister of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/22577/', "Sir Robert Ker Porter (1777&#150;1842)")</script>, painter and traveller, and <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/22571/', "Jane Porter (bap. 1776, d. 1850)")</script>, novelist. Anna Maria was educated at Edinburgh with her sister, both deciding from an early age to devote their lives to literature and the arts. When only thirteen, Anna began a series of stories which eventually appeared as <span class="italic">Artless tales, <span class="roman">&#145;by Anna Maria Porter, ornamented with a frontispiece, designed by her brother, R. K. Porter&#146;</span></span> (1793). A second volume, this time with the subtitle &#145;<span class="roman">Romantic effusions of the heart</span>&#146;, was published under her name in 1795. Within another two years she had completed two three-decker novels: <span class="italic">Walsh Colville</span> (1797) and <span class="italic">Octavia</span> (1798?).<br>
<br>In 1807 the Porter family moved to a cottage in Esher, Surrey, and it was here that Anna wrote and published a historical romance, <span class="italic">The Hungarian Brothers</span>, which appeared under her own name after a brief flirtation with anonymity. She had been spurred on, as she later confessed, by the immediate and continuing success of her sister's <span class="italic">Thaddeus of Warsaw</span> (1803). Anna's novel was soon translated into French, and later English editions made appearances until as late as 1832.<br>
<br>Anna Porter's other works included: <span class="italic">Don Sebastian, or, The House of Braganza</span> (1809), <span class="italic">Ballad Romances and other Poems</span> (1811), <span class="italic">The Recluse of Norway</span> (1814), <span class="italic">Tales of Pity on Fishing, Shooting and Hunting</span> (1814), <span class="italic">The Knight of St John</span> (1817), <span class="italic">Roche Blanche, or, The Hunters of the Pyrenees</span>, and at least two other novels on which both sisters' names appeared. Jane said of Anna that &#145;the quickness of her perceptions gave her an almost intuitive knowledge of everything she wished to learn&#146;, while Mrs S. C. Hall described her as blonde, and by nature gay.<br>
<br>After their mother's death, the Porter sisters moved to London in 1831, Anna's novel <span class="italic">The Barony</span> having recently appeared in the usual three-volume format. They went to visit their brother William Ogilvie Porter, who practised medicine at Bristol, and to the family's dismay, Anna fell ill there. Despite the best efforts of her brother, she died of typhus fever on 21 September 1832, and was buried in St Paul's churchyard, Bristol.<br>
<br>Eric Quayle </div>
