<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Pope <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Leigh; <em>other married name </em>Wheatley]</span>,  Clara Maria</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1767, <em>d.</em> 1838), <span class="occ">flower painter</span>, was born in London, and baptized on 12 April 1767 at St Andrew by the Wardrobe, London, the youngest daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/16385/', "Jared Leigh (1724&#150;1769)")</script>, a proctor in Doctors' Commons and an amateur artist, and his wife, Elizabeth (1729/30&#150;1807). She married first, probably in the mid-1780s, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/29182/', "Francis Wheatley RA (1747&#150;1801)")</script>, portrait, landscape, and genre painter, with whom she had one son and three daughters; and second, on 25 June 1807 at St George's, Hanover Square, London, as his third wife, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/22528/', "Alexander Pope (1763&#150;1835)")</script>, actor and miniature painter.<br>
<br>The portraits of Clara Maria by Wheatley, for whose fancy pictures she was also a model, show a woman of great charm and elegance. The determination of her character, first to provide for her debt-laden family when Wheatley's health declined by becoming a teacher of drawing and later, against the advice of her friends, to marry Pope, is observed by Joseph Farington RA from the mid-1790s. With the encouragement and assistance of Farington and of the architect John Soane, who befriended her, she contributed to supporting her family by teaching and painting, finding a generous pupil in Princess Sophia of Gloucester and patronage in the close at Salisbury and exhibiting miniatures, portraits, and genre scenes at the Royal Academy from 1796. After 1812, when she showed a portrait of the renowned soprano Madame Catalani (engraved), she exhibited, until her death, examples of the fruit and flower paintings on which her reputation rests.<br>
<br>Following the Dutch tradition well known in late eighteenth-century England through engravings, Mrs Pope's elaborate watercolours include <span class="italic">A Group of Flowers in a Classical Vase</span> (exh. RA, 1818), <span class="italic">Fruit, Flowers and a Squirrel on a Stone Slab</span> (exh. RA, 1829; ex Christies 16 March 1923, lot 3), and <span class="italic">The Flowers of Shakespeare</span> (exh. RA, 1835; Sir John Soane's Museum, London) depicting the bust of Shakespeare in its niche on the staircase of Soane's house encircled by all the flowers mentioned in his works. As a botanical illustrator she worked for Samuel Curtis (1779&#150;1860), florist and proprietor of the <span class="italic">Botanical Magazine</span>, producing spectacular large-scale watercolours, engraved in aquatint and hand-coloured for his <span class="italic">Monograph on the Genus Camellia</span> (1819) and for <span class="italic">The Beauties of Flora</span> (1820) with life-size flowers painted in strong colours set in a paler landscape background. Eleven highly finished watercolours of paeonies painted in 1821 and 1822 (Natural History Museum, London) were never published; <span class="italic">Moss Roses</span> in a Chinese vase (1832) was made into a more delicately coloured lithograph. Mrs Pope's work was well received during her lifetime and the grand manner of her illustrations has ensured her a place in the history of botanical illustration. She died at her home in Store Street, London, on 24 December 1838.<br>
<br>Mary Webster </div>
