<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Blencowe,  William</span>  (1683&#150;1712), <span class="occ">cryptographer</span>, was born on 6 January 1683, the third son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/2634/', "Sir John Blencowe (1642&#150;1726)")</script>, a judge and baron of the exchequer, and his wife, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/41326/', "Anne Blencowe (1656&#150;1718)")</script>, the eldest daughter of the mathematician and cryptographer John Wallis. He matriculated at Lincoln College, Oxford, in 1697 and moved later that year to Magdalen College, where he graduated BA in 1701. On the nomination of Archbishop Thomas Tenison he became a fellow of All Souls College on 21 December 1702, and he was made MA in 1704. He was instructed in the art of deciphering by his maternal grandfather, and for his encouragement received the survivorship of Wallis's pension of &pound;100 p.a., following the latter's death on 28 October 1703. As a matter of course Blencowe succeeded Wallis as decipherer to the government; the statement of a survivor that he applied for the office &#145;unrecommended&#146; cannot therefore be accepted as accurate. His eventual salary for the office was &pound;200 per annum.<br>
<br>In 1709 Blencowe sought a dispensation to permit him to retain his fellowship at All Souls without his taking holy orders. This request was resisted by the warden, Bernard Gardiner, who tried to force Blencowe and Matthew Tindal, another college fellow, to take orders or resign. Following Queen Anne's intervention on Blencowe's behalf, the dispute led to the abolition of the warden's veto on dispensations; thereafter the non-residence of the fellows became a leading characteristic of All Souls College.<br>
<br>In his late twenties Blencowe fell ill with a violent fever, from which he was recovering, when, on 25 August 1712, he shot himself during temporary insanity caused by a relapse. He was buried in All Saints' Church, Northampton, where the monument to his memory records that he was a &#145;man studious of many kinds of learning, particularly of the common law, which he professed and practised with reputation; and of the art of deciphering letters wherein he excelled, and served the public for ten years&#146;.<br>
<br>T. F. Henderson<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Philip Carter </div>
