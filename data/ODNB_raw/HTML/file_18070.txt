<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Markham,  Peter</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1757&#150;1758), <span class="occ">physician and writer</span>, exposed with some force the abuses in the manufacture of bread during the great scarcity of 1757. Nothing is known of Markham's biography or medical qualifications, though his writings did much to attract the attention of parliament to the subject and some of his suggestions were adopted in the <span class="roman">Act for the Due Making of Bread</span> (31 Geo. II c. 29).<br>
<br>Markham wrote three pamphlets on the adulteration of bread by millers and bakers. His first pamphlet, <span class="italic">Poison Detected, or, Frightful Truths, and Alarming to the British Metropolis</span> (1757), published anonymously, dealt with the adulteration of bread but also drew attention to the unwholesome methods of colouring tea and preparing wine and beer. This pamphlet was subsequently counter-attacked by Emmanuel Collins's parody, <span class="italic">Lying Detected, or, Some of the Most Frightful Untruths that Ever Alarmed the British Metropolis</span> (1758). Markham's two other works were published under his own name in the pamphlet war that grew out of the bread controversy. It has been argued that Markham may have had genuine concern for the health of the public but his accounts of the abuses in the food industry were in fact &#145;biased and distorted&#146;  (Drummond and Wilbraham, 225).<br>
<br>W. A. S. Hewins<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Jeffrey Herrle </div>
