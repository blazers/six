<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Evans,  David Morier</span>  (1819&#150;1874), <span class="occ">financial journalist</span>, was the son of Joshua Lloyd Evans, stationer, of Llanidloes, Montgomeryshire. His occupation was given as shorthand writer at the time of his marriage, at Haggerston on 27 June 1840, to Jemima, daughter of Stephen Thomas Swan, baker. He formed an early connection with journalism, and became assistant city correspondent on <span class="italic">The Times</span>, a post that he occupied for several years, and left to assume the direction of financial articles in the <span class="italic">Morning Herald</span> and <span class="italic">Standard</span>. He left <span class="italic">The Standard</span> at the end of 1872, and in the following March, with Tom Hamber, started a paper called <span class="italic">The Hour</span> as an ultra-protestant tory paper. Evans spent his entire fortune on it, being adjudged bankrupt on 19 December 1873 (though he left about &pound;4000 when he died a month later). The paper limped on until 1876 when Disraeli heard of its death with &#145;a pang&#146;  (Escott, 204). Evans's health broke down under the strain of his financial difficulties, and he died, from cirrhosis and general dropsy, at Albion House, King Edward's Road, South Hackney, London, on the morning of 1 January 1874, aged fifty-four. His wife survived him, with several children. He was buried in Abney Park cemetery, Stamford Hill, the funeral being attended by a large number of fellow journalists among whom he was popular.<br>
<br>In addition to his regular work, Evans was connected with several other commercial and financial periodicals, among them the <span class="italic">Bankers' Magazine</span>, to which he was one of the principal contributors, <span class="italic">The Bullionist</span>, and the <span class="italic">Stock Exchange Gazette</span>. He also conducted the literary and statistical departments of the <span class="italic">Bankers' Almanac and Diary</span>. He recognized the significant role of &#145;panics&#146; in nineteenth-century financial history and two of his books are valuable records, anticipating much subsequent historical work: <span class="italic">The Commercial Crisis, 1847&#150;8</span> (1849) and <span class="italic">History of the Commercial Crisis, 1857&#150;8</span> (1859). He also published (anonymously) <span class="italic">The City, or, The Physiology of London Business</span> (1845); <span class="italic">City Men and City Manners</span> (1852); <span class="italic">Facts, Failures, and Frauds</span> (1859); and <span class="italic">Speculative Notes</span> (1864).<br>
<br>Alsager Vian<span class="italic">, rev. </span> H. C. G. Matthew </div>
