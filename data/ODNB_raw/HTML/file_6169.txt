<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Cooke,  John</span>  (<em>d.</em> in or before 1614), <span class="occ">playwright</span>, was the author of a comedy entitled <span class="italic">Greene's Tu quoque, or, The Cittie Gallant</span>, published in 1614 with a preface by Thomas Heywood. Another edition appeared in 1622 and there is also an undated quarto, possibly from as late as 1640. The play was performed at court by Queen Anne's men on 9 and 11 November 1611. The noted clown Thomas Greene took the part of Bubble, the Cittie Gallant, who constantly has on his lips the words &#145;Tu quoque&#146;, hence the origin of the first title. Entered in the Stationers' register under date 22 May 1604 is &#145;to William Cotton, Fyftie epigrams written by J. Cooke, Gent&#146;; two editions of this survive, both undated.<br>
<br>Cooke may have been the John Cooke who stood surety to a loan of &pound;20 to several of the shareholders in the Whitefriars syndicate (manager of the King's Revels company of actors). The loan was made on 16 October 1607 by William Cooke, a haberdasher. A link between Cooke and this company might explain the explicit borrowings in <span class="italic">Tu quoque</span> from <span class="italic">Ram Alley</span>, Lording Barry's Whitefriars play. It has been argued that Cooke may have been writing for the Whitefriars syndicate about the time of <span class="italic">Ram Alley</span> (1607&#150;8) and may simply have reappropriated his own scenes from that play when he wrote <span class="italic">Tu quoque</span>  (Bly, 361).<br>
<br>Heywood's <span class="italic">A Pleasant Conceited Comedie, wherein is Shewed, how a Man may Chuse a Good Wife from a Bad</span> is attributed, in a manuscript note on the title-page of a British Museum copy of the edition of 1602, to Joshua Cooke, whose name is otherwise unknown. John Cooke died before the publication of <span class="italic">Tu quoque</span> in 1614.<br>
<br>A. H. Bullen<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Matthew Steggle </div>
