<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Crookenden,  Isaac</span>  (<em>b. </em>1777), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was born on 19 October 1777, the youngest of nine children of Caleb (<i>b</i>. 1730) and Mary Crookenden; his father was perhaps the shipbuilder of Itchenor, Sussex, whose bankruptcy was recorded in 1789  (<span class="italic">GM</span>, 186). Isaac Crookenden married Elizabeth Pelham Fillary on 25 December 1798 at Arundel; they had one son, Adolphus Pelham (1800&#150;1870). In 1802 Crookenden published <span class="italic">Berthinia, or, The Fair Spaniard</span>, a chapbook of forty-eight pages which also included a second tale. Nine further titles of a similar type are known; one of them, <span class="italic">The Skeleton</span> (1805), describes Crookenden as &#145;Late assistant at Mr Adams' Academy in Chichester&#146;. Frederick Frank identifies Crookenden as &#145;probably the most notorious counterfeiter of legitimate Gothic novels&#146;  (Frank, 59). Thus Crookenden's <span class="italic">The Skeleton</span> was a &#145;refabrication&#146; of the anonymous <span class="italic">Animated Skeleton</span> (1798) and his <span class="italic">Horrible Revenge, or, The Monster of Italy!!</span> (1808) was &#145;another shilling shocker based loosely upon [Edward] Montague's <span class="italic">The Demon of Sicily</span> of 1807&#146;  (ibid., 59). Crookenden also derived materials from Ann Radcliffe, Matthew (&#145;Monk&#146;) Lewis, and Charlotte Dacre to produce his series of &#145;gothic novels in miniature&#146;  (Neuburg, 165). His only deviation from the Gothic mode is <span class="italic">Venus on Earth</span> (1808), an &#145;examination&#146; of the &#145;passion of love&#146;. He worked for four publishers successively: S. Fisher, A. Neil, J. Lee, and R. Harrild. It is difficult to date the end of his career as the later chapbooks are undated: <span class="italic">The Spectre of the Turret</span> is conjecturally dated between 1810 and 1820. None of his work was reviewed, but <span class="italic">The Mysterious Murder</span> (1806) was reprinted in New York in 1827.<br>
<br>Paul Baines </div>
