<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Baker,  Humphrey</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1557&#150;1574), <span class="occ">writer on astrology and arithmetic</span>, whose origins are unknown, was living in London when his almanac was published in 1557. A small volume, printed in black letter, it was entitled <span class="italic">The rules and right ample documentes, touchinge the use and practise of the common almanackes, which are named ephemerides: a briefe &hellip; introduction upon the iudicall astrologie &hellip; with a treatise &hellip; touching the coniunction of the planets &hellip; the hole faithfully and clerely translated into Englysche by Humprey Baker BL</span>; its lengthy title reproduced that of the French original by Oronce Fine, <span class="italic">Les canons &amp; documens tr&egrave;s amples, touchant l'usage et practique des communs almanachz</span> (1556).<br>
<br>Baker's enthusiasm for arithmetic pervades his small primer on that subject, likewise printed in black letter. <span class="italic">The Wellspring of Sciences</span> (1562) went through many editions, with varying titles. That of 1574 was enlarged as <span class="italic">The well springe of sciences, which teacheth the perfect works and practice of arithmeticke &hellip; augmented and amended &hellip; certaine tables of the agreement of measures, and waightes, of divers places in Europe, the one with another, etc</span>, and dedicated &#145;to the Governor, Consuls, Asistentes, &amp;c. of the Company of Merchentes Adventurers&#146;. In this dedication Baker excused himself for not entering fully into the merits of arithmetic, on the grounds that &#145;where good wine is to sell, there need no garlande be hanged out&#146;. The tables of weights and measures were those employed in the main European centres of trade. This style continued up to the edition of 1602; Baker was surely dead by the time that Henry Phillippes issued it as <span class="italic">Baker's arithmetic &hellip; newly corrected and enlarged, and made more plain and easie</span> (1646). This Baker should not be confused with his contemporary, Humphrey Baker (<i>d</i>. 1603), eminent in the Joiners' Company.<br>
<br>Anita McConnell </div>
