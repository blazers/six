<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Austin,  William</span>  (<em>b. </em>1627/8, <em>d.</em> in or before 1677), <span class="occ">poet and classical scholar</span>, was born in the parish of St Saviour, Southwark, the second son in the family of four children of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/917/', "William Austin (c.1587&#150;1634)")</script>, writer and barrister, and his second wife, Anne Bingham. He was educated at Eton College and Christ's College, Cambridge (matriculated 1647; BA 1649), and was admitted on 23 June 1648 to Gray's Inn, where he remained for the rest of his life.<br>
<br>Austin wrote two poems to celebrate the union of Charles II and Catherine of Braganza, which were &#145;presented to their majesties&#146; on their passage down the Thames from Hampton Court to Whitehall (23 August 1662). The first was entitled <span class="italic">A joyous welcome to the most serene, and most illustrious queen of brides Catherin, the royal spouse and consort of Charles II</span> (1662) and the second <span class="italic">Triumphus Hymenaeus, a panegyrick to the king and queen's most sacred majestie, upon their ever to be remembered passing upon the river of Thames</span> (1662). Both poems are full of bombastic eulogy, and are crowded with classical allusions, explained in voluminous notes. In an address to the reader Austin not inaptly refers to &#145;<i>Triumphus hymenaeus</i>&#146; as &#145;this thatcht Tugurium of Poesie&#146;  (p. 39).<br>
<br>In 1664 Austin produced <span class="italic">Atlas under Olympus: an Heroick Poem</span> which is dedicated to Charles II and Monck, duke of Albemarle, and is a fulsome panegyric on their achievements. Almost every line is based on a classical quotation, and Austin's epistle to the reader describes his poetic method: &#145;what could not be afforded you from my own fancy, I have endeavoured to supply, by swelling the Margent with the Allusions of some Authors&#146;  (sig. B3<i>v</i>). Austin's most readable production is a description in verse of the plague of London, entitled <span class="italic"><i>Epiloimia ep&#275;</i>, or, The anatomy of the pestilence. A poem in three parts, describing the deplorable condition of the City of London under its merciless dominion</span> (1666). Austin here dispenses with classical allusions and annotations. Hazlitt also describes a poem on Austin's nephew, &#145;Upon the death of Mr. Anthony Austin, younger son to Sir James Austin knight, who died in the flower of his youth, and in the 22th year of his age, December the 30th, 1676&#146;, by W. A[ustin?] (1677), but this volume cannot be traced.<br>
<br>Austin's precise date of death is uncertain, but his will was proved in 1677. He was buried in the parish church of St Saviour, Southwark, near the monument to his father.<br>
<br>Sidney Lee<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Sarah Ross </div>
