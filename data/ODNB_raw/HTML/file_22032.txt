<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Pether,  Sebastian William Thomas</span>  (1790&#150;1844), <span class="occ">landscape painter</span>, was baptized on 31 August 1794 at St Luke's, Chelsea, the eldest son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/22031/', "Abraham Pether (1756&#150;1812)")</script>, landscape painter, and his wife, Elizabeth (<i>d</i>. in or after 1812). He was a pupil of his father and, like him, painted moonlight views and nocturnal conflagrations. The realism and preoccupation with colour harmonies in these works should have brought him success, but early in life the necessity of providing for a large family forced him into the hands of the dealers, who purchased his pictures for small sums for copying purposes, to which they readily lent themselves, and consequently they were rarely seen at exhibitions. In 1814 Pether sent to the Royal Academy <span class="italic">View from Chelsea Bridge of the Destruction of Drury Lane Theatre</span>, and in 1826 <span class="italic">A Caravan Overtaken by a Whirlwind</span>. The latter was a commission from Sir John Fleming Leicester, but as the subject was not suited to the painter's talent this solitary commission was of no real benefit to him. His life was one long struggle with adversity, which reached its climax when, in 1842, three pictures, which with the help of a frame-maker friend he had sent to the Royal Academy, were rejected.<br>
<br>Pether resembled his father in his taste for mechanical pursuits, and is said to have suggested the idea of the stomach-pump to a surgeon named Jukes. He died, of consumption, at Battersea on 14 March 1844, on which date a subscription was raised for his family.<br>
<br>Pictures attributed to Sebastian Pether frequently appear at sales but often they are copies of his work. <a name="cosubject_68067"></a><span class="headword">Henry Pether</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1828&#150;1865)<span class="occ"></span>, whose landscapes, including moonlight scenes along the Thames, are frequently confused with Pether's, was probably his son.<blockquote>It is not always easy to see where Sebastian's pictures end and Henry's begin, unless they are signed. Henry more frequently used a signature, and his pictures, if less inventive in romantic imagery, are painted with more refinement, and show a sense of topography and local atmosphere. (Maas, 51)</blockquote>
<br>
<br>F. M. O'Donoghue<span class="italic">, rev. </span> J. Desmarais </div>
