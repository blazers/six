<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Stuart,  Jane</span>  (<em>c.</em>1654&#150;1742), <span class="occ">the alleged natural daughter of James II</span>, was, according to an oral tradition first set down thirty years after her death by Samuel Peckover, a prominent Cambridgeshire Quaker, born in Paris, the daughter of James, duke of York [<i>see </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/14593/', "James II and VII")</script>], then in exile, and an unnamed maid of honour to Queen Henrietta Maria, and to have lived at the English court from the Restoration to 1688 without, apparently, any contemporary ever noticing her. The legend of the royal Quaker was lent credence because of James's friendship with William Penn and his generosity to Quakers. What is known about Jane is that she lived in a cellar in Wisbech, earning her living by reaping in the fields in the summer and spinning wool and flax in the winter. She was a mystery to her neighbours, whose curiosity was aroused when she was found reading the <span class="roman">New Testament</span> in Greek.<br>
<br>After the 1688 revolution, when she moved to Wisbech, Jane Stuart is reputed to have evaded questions from the government about the birth of the prince of Wales, whom she had nursed, but this was the last thing William III would have wanted to enquire into. Although several Quakers visited James II at St Germain, there is no record of her ever being there. During the rising of 1715 she was reported as having hired a chaise to take her to Scotland to see her half-brother James III (James Francis Edward). Yet she never visited her &#145;family&#146; at St Germain; nor did James II, a tender father to his natural children, ever mention her. There is no connection between Jane Stuart and a namesake, a widow, who claimed to be a great-granddaughter of James I. Jane Stuart died in Wisbech on 12 September 1742 and was buried in the Quaker burial-ground there. She was unmarried.<br>
<br>Eveline Cruickshanks </div>
