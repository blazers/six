<div class="para">
<span class="headword">May,  John</span>  (<em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>1606&#150;1613), <span class="occ">writer on aulnage</span>, whose origins are obscure, was appointed deputy aulneger about 1606. He published <span class="italic">A Declaration of the Estate of Clothing now Used within this Realme of England</span> with an &#145;Apologie shewing the necessarie use of his office&#146; (London, 1613). This treatise was written to reveal to the duke of Lennox, recently appointed aulneger-general, the &#145;deceits and abuses of the law&#146; used by the manufacturers to evade statutes relating to the wool trade. The deceits occurred at every stage of manufacture, and although the law had provided for &#145;searchers&#146; to look for faulty workmanship in the clothmaker's place of work, this too was abused; the searchers were frequently the clothiers themselves or else their servants. May thus presents a very grim picture where &#145;because of falsehood&#151;everyone in England striveth to wear anything but cloth and the Infidel brusheth bare his garment to count the threads&#146;  (May, 28), but he claims the principal cause &#145;to brede these deceits&#146; is the scattering into the countryside of these very clothiers &#145;where the eye of the officer is not upon their doings&#146;  (ibid., 42). To resolve the situation he urges the duke that the industry should be restricted to towns where suitable places for inspection should be provided, for searching and sealing the cloth. Nothing is known of May's life beyond this publication.<br>
<br>Nancy Ives </div>
