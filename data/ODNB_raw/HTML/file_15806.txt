<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Konig,  Charles Dietrich Eberhard</span>  (1774&#150;1851), <span class="occ">botanist and mineralogist</span>, was born in Brunswick and educated at the University of G&ouml;ttingen, where he studied botany. In 1800 he was invited to England to arrange the natural history collections of Queen Charlotte at Kew Palace. The following year Konig went to work for Sir Joseph Banks in Soho Square, London, as assistant to Jonas Dryander, Banks's librarian. During this period he launched and edited <span class="italic">Annals of Botany</span> in conjunction with Dr John Sims, writing, translating, and illustrating many of the articles. In 1807 he became assistant keeper in the British Museum's department of natural history and modern curiosities, and turned his attention to mineralogy, cataloguing and arranging the whole collection over the next ten years.<br>
<br>In 1813 Konig succeeded Dr Shaw as keeper of the department. He continued to spend most of his time on the minerals, although he did rearrange and name the bird collection in the early 1820s; he was said to show an &#145;excessive contempt for fossils&#146;  (Torrens, 69) in 1819. In spite of this he published a paper on the human skeleton from Guadeloupe in the <span class="italic">Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society</span> in 1814, and the first part of <span class="italic">Icones fossilium sectiles</span> in 1825. Konig also wrote many of the geological articles for Abraham Rees's <span class="italic">Cyclopaedia</span> (1802&#150;20), after September 1811. In 1837 he was relieved of responsibility for botany and zoology, becoming keeper of the museum's mineralogical and geological branch. Konig was a difficult man, and had bad relations with both John E. Gray, keeper of the zoological branch, and Robert Smirke, architect of the new British Museum building. Konig died suddenly at the museum on 29 August 1851, while still in office.<br>
<br>John C. Thackray </div>
