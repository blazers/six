<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Morgan,  John</span> <span class="st"> [<em>known as </em>John Morgan Matchin]</span>  (1688&#150;1733/4), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman and Welsh scholar</span>, was born on 7 February 1688 at Llangelynnin, Merioneth, north Wales, younger son of Edward Morgan (<i>fl</i>. 1651&#150;1700), curate of Llangelynnin, and his wife, Margreta (<i>fl</i>. <i>c</i>.1660&#150;1700). From 1704 to 1708 he studied with his elder brother, Edward (<i>d</i>. 1749)&#151;also to become a cleric and man of letters, at Jesus College, Oxford, where he is thought to have come under the influence of the renowned Welsh antiquary Edward Lhuyd. Having been ordained in 1709, he became curate of Llandegfan, Anglesey (1709&#150;10), and of Llanfyllin, Montgomeryshire (1710&#150;13). In 1713 Morgan left his native Wales to become curate in the village of Matching, Essex, where he served as vicar from 1728 until his death. He was commonly known as John Morgan Matchin, and there is no evidence that he married.<br>
<br>Morgan's interest in Welsh literature and scholarship, which he shared with a number of other Oxford-educated Welshmen of the time, is reflected in a collection of proverbs and colloquialisms and in a verse collection including some of his early work  (NL Wales, Llanstephan MSS 20 and 15). He is thought to be the author of the translations from Tertullian and Cyprian, <span class="italic">Llythyr Tertulian at 'Scapula</span> (1716). He is best known, however, for his short prose work, <span class="italic">Myfyrdodau bucheddol ar y pedwar peth diweddaf</span> (&#145;Devout musings on the four last things&#146;), which appeared in eight editions between 1714 and 1830. Although partly based on an earlier translation from English, <span class="italic">Cadwyn euraidd o bedair modrwy</span> (1706), its clarity of expression and classically influenced economy of style set it apart from other devotional and didactic writing of the period. In three letters addressed to his Oxford contemporary Moses Williams  (BL, Add. MS 14934, fols. 175&#150;7), as well as discussing literary and antiquarian matters Morgan voiced his concern for the educational and spiritual well-being of his fellow countrymen, denouncing the folly of the English-only education policy of the Society for Promoting Christian Knowledge, &#145;the consequence [of which] at last will be barbarism, which necessarily introduces ignorance and irreligion&#146;. These letters eventually came into the hands of the Morrisian circle of writers and antiquarians, making John Morgan a link between Edward Lhuyd and the cultural revival of mid-eighteenth-century Wales. He died at Matching on 28 February 1734 (or possibly 1733) and was buried on 2 March in the chancel of the parish church.<br>
<br>Huw M. Edwards </div>
