<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Thomas,  Joshua</span> <span class="st"> [Josua Tomos]</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1759), <span class="occ">author and Church of England clergyman</span>, was born at Penp&iuml;od, Llanllywenfel, Brecknockshire, early in the eighteenth century. Virtually nothing is known of his early life though he is reputed to have graduated from the Queen's College, Oxford, a fact not recorded by Foster in <span class="italic">Alumni Oxonienses</span>. Following curacies at Nantmel, Radnorshire, and thereafter at Tirabad and Llanwrtyd in his home county, and at Llangamarch and Llanllywenfel, also in Brecknockshire, where he served under Theophilus Evans (1693&#150;1767), he was presented with the living of Merthyr Cynog in 1741; he added Llanbister in the neighbouring county of Radnorshire five years later. He also served as a chaplain to the earl of Powys, before returning to his home parish at Llanllywenfel in 1758. His son, also called Joshua, matriculated at Christ Church, Oxford, in May 1758, graduating BA in 1762 and MA two years later.<br>
<br>The elder Joshua Thomas was principally known as a translator into Welsh of pious and didactic works of an Anglican hue. His earliest published work was a translation of Andrew Jones's <span class="italic">The Black Book of Conscience</span>, which he entitled <span class="italic">Lyfr du y gydwybod</span> (1723), followed, in 1728, by excerpts from Matthew Henry's <span class="italic">Exposition on the Old and New Testaments</span>, and, in 1733, by Archbishop Edward Synge's <span class="italic">An Essay towards Making the Knowledge of Religion Easy</span> and Robert Warren's <span class="italic">The Daily Self-Examiner</span>. Further works were Bishop George Bull's <span class="italic">Discourse Concerning the Spirit of God</span> (<i>c</i>.1740) and a selection from the writings of Dr John Scott of St Giles-in-the-Fields entitled <span class="italic">Yr angenrheidrwydd o gredu gwobrwyon a chosbedigaethau y byd arall tu ag at fod yn wir grefyddol</span> (&#145;The necessity of believing in rewards and punishments in the next world in order to become truly religious&#146;; 1743). His most celebrated and accomplished work was his translation of Scott's <span class="italic">Christian Life</span> entitled <span class="italic">Y Fuchedd Grist'nogol, o'i dechreu i'w diwedd mewn gogoniant</span> (1752), which, stylistically, was of a very high standard.<br>
<br>Although hardly known beyond the borders of Brecknockshire and the diocese of St David's, Thomas typified the orthodox Welsh clergy of his day in his loyalty to a sober Anglicanism which steered well clear of Methodist enthusiasm on the one hand and popish superstition on the other. He died on 23 June 1759 at Llanllywenfel, and was buried there in the parish church.<br>
<br>D. Densil Morgan </div>
