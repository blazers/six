<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Stuart,  James</span>  (1764&#150;1842), <span class="occ">historian and journalist</span>, son of Benjamin Stuart of co. Antrim, was born in Armagh. He was educated at the Royal School, Armagh, and in January 1784 entered Trinity College, Dublin, where he was taught by Dr George Miller. He graduated BA in the spring term of 1789, and enrolled as a student in the Middle Temple in January 1790 but he was never called to the bar. In 1811 he published <span class="italic">Poems on Various Subjects</span>, some of which are on places near Armagh, some on his friends, none of more than occasional interest. In 1812 he became the first editor of the <span class="italic">Newry Telegraph</span>, and from 1815 to 1819 also edited the <span class="italic">Newry Magazine</span>. Here he wrote <span class="italic">Historical Memoirs of the City of Armagh for a Period of 1,373 Years</span>, which was published in 1819. The book combines a short history of Ireland with a history of Armagh. Stuart also included a skilful denunciation of Edward Ledwich's view that St Patrick never existed.<br>
<br>Stuart went to live in Belfast in 1821 and became editor of the <span class="italic">News Letter</span>. Some theological letters by him, which first appeared in this journal, were published in 1825 as <span class="italic">The Protestant Layman</span>. In 1827 he founded and edited the <span class="italic">Guardian and Constitutional Advocate</span>, but ill health soon forced him to give it up. He married Mary Ogle; they had no children. Stuart died in September 1842 in Belfast.<br>
<br>Norman Moore<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Marie-Louise Legg </div>
