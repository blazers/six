<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Walker,  George</span>  (1772&#150;1847), <span class="occ">writer and publisher</span>, was born in Falcon Square, Cripplegate, London, on 24 December 1772. At fifteen he was apprenticed to a bookseller named Cuthell in Middle Row, Holborn, and two years later opened his own business with a capital of a few shillings. He married the daughter of a local tradesman with some property and prospered. He transferred to Great Portland Street, adding a musical publishing department, and finally, as a music publisher solely, moved to Soho Square, and took his son <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/28483/', "George Walker (1803&#150;1879)")</script> into partnership.<br>
<br>Walker wrote numerous Gothic novels in the manner of Ann Radcliffe, including <span class="italic">The Romance of the Cavern</span> (1792), <span class="italic">Cinthelia</span> (1797), and <span class="italic">The Three Spaniards</span> (1800). The latter two works were translated into French for sale in Paris. He also published a volume of poetry in 1801, and <span class="italic">The Battle of Waterloo: a Poem</span> in 1815. His <span class="italic">Adventures of Timothy Thoughtless</span> (1813) is a children's book. More notable was a satirical novel, <span class="italic">The Vagabond</span> (1799). A. D. Harvey regards this &#145;picaresque masterpiece&#146;, with Godwin as its target, as the best of the anti-reform novels, exhibiting &#145;the anxious repudiation of the new ideals about woman's role&#146;  (Harvey, 292). George Walker died on 8 February 1847 and was buried in Highgate cemetery.<br>
<br>J. R. MacDonald<span class="italic">, rev. </span> John D. Haigh </div>
