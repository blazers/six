<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Honorius <span class="st"> [Henricus, Heinricus]</span> Augustodunensis</span>  (<em>d.</em> <em>c.</em>1140), <span class="occ">scholar and author</span>, was long believed to have come from Autun in Burgundy, in consequence of the name by which he was generally known in Latin: Honorius Augustodunensis. However, it is now impossible to associate Honorius with Autun. The only direct and contemporary biographical information about Honorius appears in the final chapter of his own <span class="italic">De luminaribus ecclesiae</span>, published <i>c</i>.1133. This describes Honorius as priest and <i>scholasticus</i> of the church <i>Augustodunensis</i>, assigns his floruit to the reign of the Emperor Heinrich V of Germany (<i>r</i>. 1106&#150;25), and lists twenty-two of his writings in this order: <span class="italic">Elucidarius</span>, <span class="italic">Sigillum</span>, <span class="italic">Inevitabile</span>, <span class="italic">Speculum ecclesiae</span>, <span class="italic">Offendiculum</span>, <span class="italic">Summa totius</span>, <span class="italic">Gemma animae</span>, <span class="italic">Sacramentarium</span>, <span class="italic">Neocosmum</span>, <span class="italic">Eucharistion</span>, <span class="italic">Cognitio vitae</span>, <span class="italic">Imago mundi</span>, <span class="italic">Summa gloria</span>, <span class="italic">Scala coeli</span>, <span class="italic">De anima et de Deo</span>, <span class="italic">Expositio Psalterii</span>, <span class="italic">Cantica Canticorum</span>, <span class="italic">Evangelia</span>, <span class="italic">Clavis physicae</span>, <span class="italic">Refectio mentium</span>, <span class="italic">Pabulum vitae</span>, <span class="italic">De luminaribus ecclesiae</span>. All but the <span class="italic">Evangelia</span>, <span class="italic">Refectio mentium</span>, and <span class="italic">Pabulum vitae</span> have survived. Another ten may, through manuscript attribution and inner associations, be added to the list, together with many, as yet unedited, fragments and <i>quaestiones</i>. Over 260 manuscript copies of Honorius's works remain from the twelfth century alone, the majority from religious houses sympathetic to the reforming ideals of the monasteries of Gorze and Fruttuaria.<br>
<br>Honorius was possibly of south German or Savoyard origin, and arguably a kinsman of Archbishop Anselm of Canterbury (<i>d</i>. 1109). He spent his formative years (<i>c</i>.1096&#150;1110) as a reforming canon in England, many of them in the archbishop's immediate circle. He was an ardent supporter of Anselm's battle for papal reform against the Anglo-Norman kings. At, or shortly after, the archbishop's death Honorius left England for Regensburg, in Bavaria. The puzzling name Augustodunensis may be a toponym, derived from his later career (<i>c</i>.1110&#150;1133) as canon <i>scholasticus</i>, or teacher in the school, of the Alte Kapelle in Regensburg. He remained an advocate of the papal cause, and earned the favour of Bishop Cuno of Regensburg (1126&#150;32).<br>
<br>The immediate influence of Anselm's teaching is especially marked in the first three of the works listed in the <span class="italic">De luminaribus</span>, above all in the most popular of these, the catechetical <span class="italic">Elucidarius</span>. Honorius's teaching career in Regensburg allowed him to continue to communicate Anselm's views, notably through the <span class="italic">Offendiculum</span> and the <span class="italic">Cognitio vitae</span>. Honorius was thus one of the main vehicles whereby Anselm's writings and reforming sentiments gained early currency, both in England and in Europe. In England, the dioceses of Canterbury, Rochester, Worcester, and Winchester showed an especially early interest in Honorius's writings. He may have spent time in each one, perhaps leaving Canterbury for the other southern dioceses during Anselm's exiles, between 1097 and 1100, and between 1103 and 1107. Honorius's abandonment of England for Regensburg, and his preferment there, is probably connected with the betrothal in 1110 of Matilda, daughter of Henry I of England, to the Emperor Heinrich V of Germany (<i>d</i>. 1125). The betrothal is noted in an important early copy of Honorius's <span class="italic">Imago mundi</span>  (Cambridge, Corpus Christi College, MS 66). The first recension of this work is dated 1110, and was dedicated to one Henricus. The earliest surviving copy of the <span class="italic">Imago mundi</span>  (Bodl. Oxf., MS Rawl. B. 484) comes from Winchester, home of Archdeacon Henry, Princess Matilda's chaplain. This connection would account, too, for Honorius's associating his own floruit with the reign of the emperor.<br>
<br>Honorius's works may be divided by content into four main groups: polemical (primarily in support of the celibate priesthood), liturgical, cosmological, and exegetical. Many remain inadequately edited. A desire to extend the influence throughout the Christian church of reforming bishops, especially monastic bishops such as Anselm or Cuno of Regensburg, pervades his immense literary output and is perhaps the fundamental reason for it.<br>
<br>The name Honorius is an unusual one for the period. This fact, together with the interest in the works of John Scottus (Eriugena) manifest in many of his writings, and the dedication of certain of these to Christian, Irish abbot (<i>c</i>.1133&#150;1152) of the Schottenkloster of St Jakob of Regensburg, have been used to support the idea that Honorius was himself an Irishman. This idea has little substance. The name Honorius appears rather to have been adopted, perhaps in honour of Honorius, archbishop of Canterbury (627&#150;52). Honorius's original name was probably He[i]nricus, a name that occurs not merely in early manuscripts of his works, but also in a twelfth-century gift of books containing, out of a collection of some forty-seven treatises, twenty-two known to be by him. This same donation, together with surviving Lambach codices of certain of the works on the list, connects He[i]nricus with the abbey of Lambach. In many other early manuscript copies of Honorius's writings their author is described as <i>inclusus</i> or <i>solitarius</i>. This combined evidence suggests that He[i]nricus, or Honorius, entered a Benedictine abbey late in life (possibly in 1133)&#151;perhaps Lambach or St Jakob of Regensburg, home of such other famous solitary monks, or monks <i>inclusi</i>, as Marianus of Regensburg. The precise date of his death is unknown, but is likely to have been about 1140.<br>
<br>Valerie I. J. Flint </div>
