<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Green,  Benjamin</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1813, <em>d.</em> 1858), <span class="occ">architect</span>, was baptized on 15 February 1813 in Horsley, Northumberland, the younger son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/37484/', "John Green (1787&#150;1852)")</script> of Newcastle upon Tyne, architect and civil engineer, and his wife, Jane Stobart. A pupil of Augustus Charles Pugin, in the mid-1830s he became a partner of his father. Thereafter they practised together until the latter's death in 1852, and it is not always possible to distinguish the work of one from that of the other. The somewhat undistinguished Gothic-style churches in Northumberland and Durham, which formed a substantial element in the firm's output, for example, are in general simply ascribed to &#145;John and Benjamin Green&#146;, and the same applies to their best-known work, the monument to John Lambton, first earl of Durham, at Penshaw Hill, co. Durham (1844), in the form of a Greek Doric temple. However, the normal pattern appears to have been that the father concentrated on the civil engineering works and the son on the more purely architectural.<br>
<br>The Greens were active as railway architects, and the stations on the main line between Newcastle and Berwick upon Tweed, commissioned by George Hudson in 1846&#151;&#145;Fine, handsome buildings &hellip; more like the villas raised by retired tradesmen than residences for railway officers&#146;  (Welford, 2.330)&#151;are said to have been by Benjamin Green. More important, the Theatre Royal (1836&#150;7) and the Grey Column (1837&#150;8) in Newcastle, stately neo-classical structures which were key elements in the heroic replanning of the city centre by Richard Grainger, were evidently also his conceptions, and suggest that he might have become a more significant figure in his own right had he not died relatively young, surviving his father by a mere six years.<br>
<br>In contrast to his father, Green was &#145;an artistic, dashing sort of a fellow&#146; whose style was &#145;ornamental, florid and costly&#146;  (Welford, 2.330). He died in a mental home at Dinsdale Park, co. Durham, on 14 November 1858.<br>
<br>Peter Leach<span class="italic">, rev. </span>
