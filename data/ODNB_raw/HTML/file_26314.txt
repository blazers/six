<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Starke,  Mariana</span>  (1762&#150;1838), <span class="occ">traveller and writer</span>, was born in the last week of September 1762 and baptized at Epsom, Surrey, on 23 October; she was the eldest daughter of Richard Starke (1719&#150;1794), of Hylands House, Epsom, and his wife, Mary (1734/5&#150;1816), daughter of Isaac Hughes of Banstead, Surrey. Her father, born in Madras, was an officer of the East India Company and was deputy-governor of Fort St David, Madras, from 1752 to 1756, when he returned to England. Her younger brother, Richard Isaac Starke (<i>b</i>. 1768), acquired through marriage Laugharne Castle, Carmarthenshire; her sister Louise died in Nice in 1791.<br>
<br>Brought up at Hylands House, Mariana Starke wrote two plays on themes of Anglo-Indian life, <span class="italic">The Sword of Peace</span> (1789) and <span class="italic">The Widow of Malabar</span> (1791), but there is no evidence that she ever visited India. A third play, <span class="italic">The Tournament</span> (1800), was in imitation of an earlier German one, and her first, &#145;The British Orphan&#146;, was unpublished. The Indian plays are known to have been performed briefly. She is also thought to have been the author of <span class="italic">The Poor Soldier</span> (2nd edn, 1789), an account in verse of Charles Short, American loyalist of South Carolina.<br>
<br>Mariana Starke spent seven years in Italy, from 1792 to 1798, attending a consumptive relative; her father died in Pisa in 1794 and was buried in Leghorn. This residence and her considerable linguistic abilities that prompted her next publications, particularly the travel guides on which her fame was later to rest. <span class="italic">Letters from Italy</span> (2 vols., 1800) reached a second edition and was translated into German. She moved to Exmouth, Devon, before the publication in 1811 of <span class="italic">The Beauties of Carlo Maria Maggi</span>, but revisited Italy in 1817&#150;19 and published <span class="italic">Travels on the Continent</span> (1820) and <span class="italic">Information and Directions for Travellers on the Continent</span> (1824), which went to several editions and translations before being enlarged and republished as <span class="italic">Travels in Europe</span> (1832). The various editions and versions enlarged the geographical scope of their predecessors, and brought up to date the very detailed practical advice on accommodation, expenses, food, and itineraries she gave to travellers. Pirated editions appeared as the fame of the books spread. Her publisher, John Murray, realized the success of the genre and used the format as the basis of his famous guides from 1836 onwards. The books convey the impression of the immense complexity of travel in the early nineteenth century, the huge quantities of traps which travellers&#151;particularly those who suffered ill health&#151;felt obliged to haul about with them, the dangers and discomforts (real or imagined) which they faced, and the benefits they hoped to derive. Mariana Starke died, aged seventy-six, at Milan on a journey from Naples to England at the end of April 1838.<br>
<br>Elizabeth Baigent </div>
