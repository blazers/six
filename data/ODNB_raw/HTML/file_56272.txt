<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Swaine,  Ann Sykes</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1821, <em>d.</em> 1883), <span class="occ">suffragist and philanthropist</span>, was baptized at Birstall, Yorkshire, on 15 September 1821, the eldest of the four daughters of Edward Swaine and his wife, Martha Sykes. She was a spinster, and lived in York for the last twenty-eight years of her life, nursing her ageing father until her own death at the age of sixty-one. A Unitarian, Swaine had a strong interest in social and educational questions, and was one of the first women in York to become involved in the women's suffrage movement. She supported the national campaigns for married women's right to hold property and for the extension of university education to women. Active in local education, Swaine was honorary secretary of the Yorkshire Ladies' Educational Association and a member of the Ladies' Council of Education of Yorkshire. She was also involved in Sunday school and cookery school work in York.<br>
<br>Swaine published a small number of texts, including a translation, <span class="italic">History of the Doctrine of the Deity of Jesus Christ</span> (1870), of the work by the French theologian Albert R&eacute;ville, which ran to three editions. A year before her death she published a collection of short biographies for the Sunday School Association, entitled <span class="italic">Remarkable Women as Examples for Girls</span>. The volume included biographical sketches of Caroline Herschel, Harriet Martineau, Mary Carpenter, and Charlotte Bront&euml;, among others. Ann Sykes Swaine was an active, clear-thinking woman who divided her time between philanthropy, feminism, publishing, translation, and domestic and familial duties. The <span class="italic">Englishwoman's Review</span> (1883) noted that her writings were characterized by her own common sense and pragmatism. She died unexpectedly from heart disease at 1 The Crescent, Micklegate, her home in York, on 21 June 1883, survived by her father.<br>
<br>Heloise Brown </div>
