<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Dickons <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Poole]</span>,  Martha Frances Caroline <span class="st"> [Maria; </span></span> <span class="st"> <em>known as </em>Caroline Poole]</span>  (<em>c.</em>1774&#150;1833), <span class="occ">singer</span>, is said to have been born in London about 1774&#151;perhaps a few years earlier&#151;the daughter of William Poole (1737/8&#150;1812). A precocious musician, she sang and played the harpsichord at an Oxford concert in 1785 and studied at Bath with Venanzio Rauzzini, in whose opera <span class="italic">La vestale</span> she made her London stage d&eacute;but at the King's Theatre on 1 May 1787. She was known professionally as Caroline Poole in her early career. In the summer of 1787 she began a series of engagements at Vauxhall, and in Lent 1790 sang in the Covent Garden oratorios, as she was to do almost annually through the 1790s. In the same year she started appearing in the Salomon concerts, for one of which (1792) Haydn wrote an aria for her (now lost). A new departure was her engagement at Covent Garden as an actress and singer at &pound;8 a week; her d&eacute;but as Ophelia on 9 October 1793 was noted more for her singing in the mad scenes than for her acting, though her Polly in <span class="italic">The Beggar's Opera</span>, three days later, was said to be delightful. In 1793&#150;5 she appeared in many comic operas and patriotic plays; after an unexplained hiatus she again acted and sang at Covent Garden in 1798&#150;9. In the summers she performed in Edinburgh (1794) and Ireland (1795&#150;6). She retired to marry, on 7 August 1800, a Liverpool merchant, Peter Dickons, but his trading losses led her to resume her professional career in 1807. The marriage ended in separation in 1810.<br>
<br>As Mrs Dickons she reappeared at Covent Garden on 20 October 1807 in Arne's <span class="italic">Artaxerxes</span>. Between then and 1815 she also performed at the Lyceum, in Drury Lane oratorios, and at the Italian Opera (as the Countess in the first London performance of Mozart's <span class="italic">Le nozze di Figaro</span>, 18 June 1812). A foray to the Th&eacute;&acirc;tre Italien in Paris in Peter Winter's <span class="italic">Il ratto di Proserpina</span> (7 December 1816) failed. Engagements in Italy in 1816&#150;17, later described in England as successful, remain undocumented. She is said to have been elected to the Istituto Filarmonico at Venice&#151;a short-lived academy that ended in 1816. If she was the Maria Castiglioni who sang at La Fenice, Venice, in 1816 and 1817, she was demoted in the latter year to secondary parts. She returned to London and reappeared at Covent Garden in Henry Bishop's perversions of Rossini's <span class="italic">Il barbiere di Siviglia</span> (13 October 1818) and of <span class="italic">Le nozze di Figaro</span> (6 March 1819, again as the Countess). She then retired. She died of cancer at her house in Regent Street on 4 May 1833. Her voice at its best was &#145;powerful and mellifluous&#146;, and she possessed &#145;a sensible and impressive intonation and a highly polished taste&#146;.<br>
<br>W. B. Squire<span class="italic">, rev. </span> John Rosselli </div>
