<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Clerk <span class="st"> [Clarke]</span>,  Josiah <span class="st"> [Josias]</span></span>  (<em>bap. </em>1639?, <em>d.</em> 1714), <span class="occ">physician</span>, may have been the son of Josias Clerk baptized at Romford, Essex, on 4 January 1639. He was educated at Bishop's Stortford, Hertfordshire, and was admitted as a pensioner of Peterhouse, Cambridge, on 1 May 1656, and took the two degrees in medicine, MB in 1661, MD on 3 July 1666. He was admitted a candidate of the College of Physicians on 26 June 1671, a fellow on 29 July 1675, and was appointed censor in 1677 and 1692. On the death of Sir Thomas Witherley he was named elect on 16 April 1694, delivered the Harveian oration in 1708, was consiliarius in 1707, 1709, 1710, 1711, and 1712, and was elected to the presidentship, void by the death of Dr Edward Browne, on 13 September 1708, being re-elected at the general election of officers on the 30th of the same month. Clerk &#145;being indisposed by many bodyly infirmityes, and also aged&#146;, was unable to act; he accordingly resigned on 18 December, and Charles Goodall was appointed on 23 December 1708. Clerk had been chosen treasurer on 16 April 1708, and retained that office as long as he lived. Clerk died at his house in Fenchurch Street, London, in the autumn of 1714, in the seventy-fifth year of his age. In the annals of the college cited by Munk the date of Clerk's death is given as 8 December, which is erroneous. His will  (TNA: PRO, PROB 11/542/188) was proved on 14 October. He desired &#145;to be decently, tho' very privately, buried by night in the vault in St. Olave Hart Street Church, where my honoured mother and my children lye, if it may be done with conveniency&#146;. He was survived by his wife, Abigail, and their daughter, Elizabeth, who was married to Richard Wilshaw.<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Michael Bevan </div>
