<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Beverley,  Peter</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1566&#150;1568), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was a member of Staple Inn in 1566. He may be the person of that name who matriculated pensioner from St John's College, Cambridge, in Michaelmas term 1553, graduated BA from Clare College in 1556&#150;7, and became a fellow of Clare on 16 December 1556. His <span class="italic">Historie of Ariodanto and Jenevra</span> was entered in the Stationers' register to Henry Wekes, 1565&#150;6. Its dedication to the &#145;Worshipfull M. Peter Reade&#146; is dated &#145;from my chamber at Staple Inne. The first day of August&#146;, presumably 1566. Reade, a Norwich man, left Beverley (his godson) an annuity of &pound;6 on his death in December 1568. This Norwich connection makes it unlikely that Beverley was a Yorkshireman by birth, as Prouty suggested.<br>
<br>
<span class="italic">Ariodanto and Jenevra</span> is the earliest English translation from Ariosto's <span class="italic">Orlando Furioso</span>, although it is so free with its original that to call it a translation is misleading. In competent but artless fourteeners Beverley elaborates the tale of Ariodante and Ginevra from <span class="italic">Orlando Furioso</span>, cantos 4.51&#150;6.16, which relates how Polinesso, the wicked duke of Albany, tricks the Italian Ariodante into believing that his love, the Scottish princess Ginevra, is unfaithful. Polinesso disguises Ginevra's maid as the princess, and kisses her in view of Ariodante and his brother. This episode from Ariosto provides the chief source for the trick played by Don John on Claudio in <span class="italic">Much Ado about Nothing</span>, although it is unlikely that Shakespeare came to it through Beverley's version. Beverley transforms his original (which he nowhere mentions) from a fabliauesque novella chiefly related by Ginevra's maid Dalinda, into a tale of <i>fine amour</i> which owes much to Chaucer's <span class="italic">Knight's Tale</span>. The complex sequence in which the events of the tale are related in Ariosto is replaced by simple chronological order. Beverley's Polinesso first steals a diamond engagement ring from Jenevra in order to trick Ariodanto into believing that she is unfaithful (an episode with no counterpart in Ariosto), and then resorts to disguising Dalinda, as he does in the original. Beverley adds lengthy courtly complaints and set-piece descriptions of times of day to each stage of the narrative. The work was reprinted by Prouty in 1950. Beverley's only other known work is a commendatory poem for Geoffrey Fenton's <span class="italic">Tragical Discourses</span> (1567).<br>
<br>Colin Burrow </div>
