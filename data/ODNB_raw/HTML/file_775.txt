<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ashton,  Peter</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1548), <span class="occ">translator</span>, may be the same person as the prebendary of Lincoln (1542&#150;8); if so, he was the son of Peter Ashton, of Old Weston, Huntingdonshire, and studied at Cambridge (his college is not known), where he graduated BA in 1516 and MA in 1519. He was fellow of Christ's College, 1523&#150;8; rector of Tillingham, Essex, 1540&#150;48; and rector of Houghton, Huntingdonshire, 1537&#150;48; &#145;apparently the same man&#146;  (Venn, <span class="italic">Alum. Cant.</span>) was licensed to practise medicine in 1533, and became a fellow of the College of Physicians in 1542, and its censor in 1543.<br>
<br>Ashton translated into English, in 1546, the popular contemporary work on Turkish history by the Italian Paulus Jovius 
    (Paolo Giovio; <i>c</i>.1486&#150;1552)
  . Giovio's <span class="italic">Comentario de le cose de' Turchi</span> (1532) had already gone through a number of continental editions (German translation, 1538; Spanish, 1547); Ashton worked not from the Italian but from the Latin translation by Franciscus Niger. <span class="italic">A Shorte Treatise upon the Turkes Chronicles</span> covers the origins of Turkish power, the lives of all Turkish sultans, and the &#145;araye and discipline of the Turkish warfare&#146;. The subject is highly relevant, as verses on the title-page remind us:<blockquote>
<div>Wake up now, Christiens out of your slumbre.<br>
</div>
<div>Of the Turkes to recover your long lost glory,<br>
</div>
<div>Fear not theyr strength, theyr power, ne numbre,<br>
</div>
<div>Sith ryght, and not myght, atchyveth the victory.<br>
</div>
</blockquote>Further verses overleaf compare the Turks to Hannibal; the dedicatory letter to Sir Rafe Sadler opens with Plato, and mentions many other classical parallels. Ashton chooses &#145;playn and famylier&#146; English, rather than Chaucer's old-fashioned words, or Latinate &#145;inkhorn termes&#146;. His style is straightforward, sometimes flat, but generally clear and effective. Marginal notes aid the reader. So Ashton marks &#145;A digression&#146;  (fol. ciii) before Solyman assaults Belgrade: his opponent Leweys, son of Vladislaus, is &#145;a younge man not all of the wyttyest, ne yet (because he was young) of any experience&#146;. Earlier we read of &#145;lightning&#146; Bayazet, who &#145;used such spedines&#146; (&#145;Hyldrin&#146;, that is Yildirim, in Turkish; fol. xii), captured and held in a cage by Tamberlayne, as in Marlowe's play. The &#145;Janizars, most strong and puyssant fotemen&#146; are described in detail, among other troops: we find they &#145;starche [their] cappes so hard with glewe&#146;  (fol. cxxii). Overall, Ashton's translation forms a useful introduction to the manners and ruthless history of the leading Mediterranean power of his age. He died in 1548.<br>
<br>D. K. Money </div>
