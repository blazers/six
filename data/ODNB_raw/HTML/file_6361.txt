<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Cory,  Isaac Preston</span>  (1801/2&#150;1842), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was the son and heir of Robert Cory FSA, an attorney at Great Yarmouth and mayor of the town in 1815. His father's library of topographical, architectural, and miscellaneous works was sold on the latter's death in 1840. Cory was educated at the grammar schools at Saffron Walden and Norwich before matriculating at Clare College, Cambridge, in 1820. Five of his brothers were subsequently educated at Cambridge. In 1821 he migrated to Gonville and Caius College, where he was elected to a scholarship in 1822. Graduating BA in 1824 (as thirteenth wrangler) and MA in 1827, he was elected in 1824 to a fellowship of Caius, which he held until his death. He was Hebrew lecturer at Caius from 1839 to 1841. His chief academic work was a compilation of <span class="italic">Ancient fragments of the Phoenician, Chaldean, Egyptian, Tyrian, Carthaginian, Indian, Persian, and other writers</span> (2nd edn, 1832; rev. edn, 1876). In 1832 he was called to the bar at Lincoln's Inn, and subsequently published a <span class="italic">Practical Treatise on Accounts</span> (1839), which described methods of bookkeeping and discrepancies between the law and reality of commercial practice. It was reprinted in 1980. He died, unmarried, at Blundeston, Suffolk, on 1 April 1842 aged forty.<br>
<br>M. C. Curthoys </div>
