<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Cooling <span class="st"> [Colinge]</span>,  Richard</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1697), <span class="occ">government official</span>, was from a family apparently of Shropshire origins, and with several members in government service. Wood was of the opinion that he &#145;was originally, as it seems, of All Souls' College&#146;  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span>: <span class="italic">Fasti</span>, 2.285&#150;6). He was to claim that he had undertaken &#145;hazardous services&#146; towards the restoration of Charles II, and had assisted in preparations for the subsequent coronation  (<span class="italic">CSP dom.</span>, <i>1677&#150;8</i>, 120). More certainly, by 5 July 1660 he was acting as secretary to the lord chamberlain, the second earl of Manchester, and was one of the persons incorporated MA at Oxford with Manchester, on 8 September 1665. He continued to act as secretary to successive lord chamberlains until April 1697, while building up a collection of other offices. Thus he was appointed a commissioner for licensing hackney coaches in 1670 and acted as clerk of the robes and wardrobes from 26 September 1680 and, having vacated this post by March 1685, again from 1689. Perhaps a clerk-in-extraordinary to the privy council from 1679, he was sworn a clerk-in-ordinary on 21 February 1689. Pepys was acquainted with him, and regaled by him with court gossip. His attitude to office seems appropriately expressed in his reported comments that &#145;his horse was a Bribe, and his boots a bribe, and &hellip; he was made up of bribes, as an Oxford scholar is set out with other men's goods when he goes out of town&#146;  (Pepys, 8.369); a colleague remarked, at his death (17 June 1697), that he &#145;had the reputation of a &#147;griping&#148; officer to little purpose&#146; leaving little in property and hefty debts  (<span class="italic">CSP dom.</span>, <i>1697</i>, 203). He bequeathed to his wife (&#145;now liveing&#146;; TNA: PRO, PROB 11/440), Mary (<i>n&eacute;e</i> Cullum), son Charles, and daughters Mary and Anne (he appears to have had at least one other son, Richard) little more than the residue of leases on some small properties, mostly in Shropshire, and of his house in Suffolk Street, London.<br>
<br>R. M. Armstrong </div>
