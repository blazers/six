<div class="para">
<span class="headword">D'Ouvilly,  George Gerbier</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1638&#150;1661), <span class="occ">playwright and translator</span>, was the eldest son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/10562/', "Sir Balthazar Gerbier (1592&#150;1663/1667)")</script>, art agent, miniature painter, and architect, and his wife, Deborah Kip (<i>b</i>. 1601). Balthazar Gerbier later styled himself Baron d'Ouvilly. Descended from a Huguenot family which had sought refuge in establishing the French church in Middelburg, Zeeland, after the St Bartholomew's day massacre, George showed early educational promise and, while still a youth, &#145;had all the exercises fitt for a gentleman&#146;, possessing the ability to speak &#145;eight several languages prompt&#146; (<span class="italic">Manifestation</span>, 3&#150;4; Sainsbury, 318). By 1638, however, D'Ouvilly had enlisted as a captain in the regiment of William, Lord Craven, who was financing attempts to restore the Palatinate, held by Ferdinand II, to the former king and queen of Bohemia. In 1637&#150;8 campaigns were fought in the Low Countries, and it is possible that D'Ouvilly was taken prisoner there with Craven and Elizabeth of Bohemia's son, Prince Rupert. D'Ouvilly's military links may have been instrumental in securing a commission from 1662 for his father to design and supervise the rebuilding of Craven's house at Hampstead Marshall, Berkshire.<br>
<br>D'Ouvilly appears to have been resident in England throughout the period of the protectorate and on 18 March 1651 was ordered by the Committee for Examinations to offer securities that he would not &#145;do anything to the prejudice of the commonwealth, and to appear before the Council on summons&#146;  (<span class="italic">CSP dom.</span>, <i>1651</i>, 94).<br>
<br>D'Ouvilly's literary works differed in genre, reputation, and success. In 1657 his tragi-comedy <span class="italic">The False Favourite Disgrac'd. And the Reward of Virtue</span> was printed in London, but remained unacted. Set in Florence, the plot revolves around a series of misunderstandings which are ultimately resolved in the closing scene when concord, hierarchy, and true vision are re-established within the court. D'Ouvilly's linguistic talents, however, proved to be more apparent in his translation of a selection of French biographies by Andr&eacute; Thevet, cosmographer to Henri III. Introduced under the title, &#145;Prospographia, or, Some select Portraitures and Lives of Antient and Modern Illustrious Personages &hellip; newly translated into English by some Learned and Eminent Persons; and generally by Geo: Gerbier, alias D'Ouvilly&#146;, the passages form the third part of the stationer William Lee's edition of North's <span class="italic">Plutarch</span> (1657).<br>
<br>D'Ouvilly's final work, <span class="italic">Il trionfo d'Inghilterra overo racconto et relatione delle solennit&agrave; fatte &amp; osservate nella &hellip; incoronatione &hellip; di Carlo Secondo &hellip; nel terzo giorno di Maggio, 1661, insieme con la descrittione degl' archi trionfali &hellip; e altre &hellip; dimostrationi d'allegrezze &hellip; nella &hellip; Citt&agrave; di Londra &hellip; anco la superba cavalcata fatto &hellip; il giorno innanzi &hellip; Il tutto transportato nella lingua italiana, per il Capitan Giorgio Gerbieri D'Ouvilly</span>, was printed in Venice in 1661.<br>
<br>Elizabeth Haresnape </div>
