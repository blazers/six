<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hoare,  Prince</span>  (1755&#150;1834), <span class="occ">playwright and artist</span>, was born at Bath, the younger son and fourth of the five children of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/13388/', "William Hoare RA (1707/8&#150;1792)")</script> and his wife, Elizabeth, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Barker (<i>d</i>. 1793). He was educated by Mr Hele, first master of the grammar school at Bath, and instructed in art by his father. In 1772 he gained a Society of Arts premium, and in that year moved to London to study at the Royal Academy. In 1776 he visited Rome, and there studied under Mengs, together with Fuseli and Northcote. On his return to England in 1780 he painted for some time, exhibiting at the Royal Academy in 1781 and 1782. His exhibited work included a classical picture called <span class="italic">Alceste</span>, and a portrait of Sir T. Lawrence when a child. He ceased to exhibit after 1785.<br>
<br>In 1788 Hoare took a voyage for his health to Lisbon, returning in June to London. During his absence his first play, a tragedy, <span class="italic">Such Things Were</span>, was acted at Bath on 1 January 1788, and afterwards (as <span class="italic">Julia, or, Such Things Were</span>) at Drury Lane on 2 May 1796, for the benefit of Sarah Siddons. His best-known production, <span class="italic">No Song, No Supper</span>, a farce, with music by Storace, was first acted at Drury Lane on 16 April 1790, and often subsequently. Hoare's other productions include: <span class="italic">The Cave of Trophonius</span>, a musical farce performed at Drury Lane on 3 May 1791; <span class="italic">Dido</span>, an opera staged at the Haymarket, on 23 May 1792; <span class="italic">The Prize</span>, a musical farce first performed at the Haymarket on 11 March 1793, and often subsequently; <span class="italic">The Three and the Deuce</span>, a comic drama played at the Haymarket on 2 September 1795; <span class="italic">Mahmoud</span>, an opera performed at Drury Lane on 30 April 1796; and <span class="italic">The Italian Villagers</span>, staged at Covent Garden on 25 April 1797.<br>
<br>In 1799 Hoare was appointed honorary foreign secretary to the Royal Academy, and in this capacity he published <span class="italic">Extracts from a Correspondence with the Academies of Vienna and St Petersbourg</span> (1802), <span class="italic">Academic Correspondence</span> (1804), and <span class="italic">Academic Annals of Painting</span> (1805; 2nd edn, 1809). He was a fellow of the Society of Antiquaries, and of the Royal Society of Literature, to which he bequeathed his library. He also produced several other volumes, including <span class="italic">An Inquiry into the &hellip; Art of Design in England</span> (1806), <span class="italic">Epochs of the Arts</span> (1813), and <span class="italic">Memoirs of Granville Sharp</span> (1820). He edited a collection of essays (some his own work) entitled <span class="italic">The Artist</span> (2 vols., 1809&#150;10). Prince Hoare died at Brighton on 22 December 1834.<br>
<br>W. W. Wroth<span class="italic">, rev. </span> M. Clare Loughlin-Chow </div>
