<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Walker,  William</span>  (1623&#150;1684), <span class="occ">schoolmaster and author</span>, was born in Lincoln to unknown parents. Educated at Lincoln School under John Clarke, in 1640 he matriculated from Trinity College, Cambridge, graduating BA early in 1644. He was an usher at Louth grammar school, Lincolnshire, by the year 1645&#150;6 and headmaster of that school from 1651 to 1657. His first wife, Elizabeth, was buried at Louth on 16 January 1650 and by 1661 he had remarried; Walker's second wife, whose name is unknown, died in 1677. In 1666 he was awarded the degree of BD <i>per literas regias</i>. From 1657 to 1663 he was rector of Welton-le-Wold, and from 1661 until his death rector of Colsterworth (and an acquaintance of Sir Isaac Newton). From 1671 to his death he was master of Grantham grammar school.<br>
<br>Noted as an author of textbooks dealing with grammar, his best-known work, <span class="italic">A Treatise of English Particles</span> (1655), was the first systematic treatment of English particles for students of Latin. <span class="italic">Some Improvements to the Art of Teaching</span> (1669) and his revision of Lily's &#145;authorized&#146; grammar, <span class="italic">The Royal Grammar</span> (1670), also stress the use of English in teaching Latin grammar. His final work, <span class="italic">English Examples of the Latine Syntaxis</span> (1683), includes a portrait of the author on the frontispiece. Walker was a diffident man and a poor public speaker. His defence of the Church of England, made in <span class="italic">A Modest Plea for Infants Baptism</span> (1677) and <span class="italic">The Doctrine of Baptisms</span> (1678), aimed to pacify rather than provoke.<br>
<br>By the time of his second wife's death, Walker was himself suffering from gout, stone, and other illnesses. When he made his will on 31 July 1684 he had two sons, William (<i>b</i>. 1661/2) and Thomas, two married daughters, and another daughter, Margaret. He died, probably at Colsterworth, on 1 August 1684 aged sixty-one, and was buried in the parish church. His epitaph in the church, &#145;Heic jacent Gulielmi Walkeri Particulae&#146; (&#145;Here lie William Walker's particles&#146;), was subsequently repeated in collections of curious epitaphs. Walker's will was proved on 15 September by his executor, son-in-law, and successor as rector of Colsterworth, George Parish.<br>
<br>W. R. Meyer </div>
