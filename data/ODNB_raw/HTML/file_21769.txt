<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Peend <span class="st"> [de la Pend]</span>,  Thomas</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1565&#150;1566), <span class="occ">translator and poet</span>, whose parentage is unknown, may have been related to Stephen de la Pyend of the Inner Temple, London, and Depden, Suffolk. He was educated at Oxford  (according to Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span>, 1.430) and was a London barrister with a &#145;chamber over agaynst Sergeants Inne in Chancery Lane&#146;  (Peend, <span class="italic">Fable</span>, 2). Peend is best known for <span class="italic">The Pleasant Fable of Hermaphroditus and Salmacis</span>, a translation of part of Ovid's <span class="italic">Metamorphoses</span> (IV.287&#150;388), written in rhyming couplets in alternating lines of iambic octameter and hexameter. In the dedicatory epistle to Nicholas Sentleger of Eastwell, Kent, Peend dates the work to 1564 and claims to have translated additional sections of the <span class="italic">Metamorphoses</span>, but divulges that he will publish only this one tale because he has been pre-empted by another translator, doubtless a reference to Arthur Golding, whose English rendition of the <span class="italic">Metamorphoses</span> appeared in 1565. It is, however, unlikely that Peend executed his translation in 1564, and hence before reading Golding's, because  (according to Taylor) the numerous parallels between the translations suggest that Peend knew Golding's work. Peend's text concludes with a glossary (in prose) of characters mentioned in the text so &#145;that the unlearned myght the better understande&#146;, from which we can infer his intended audience  (Peend, <span class="italic">Fable</span>, 27). To educate his readers further, the fable is followed by a moralizing poem which casts Salmacis as &#145;eche vyce that moveth one to ill&#146;  (ibid., 16). This interpretation leads into the final part of the composition&#151;a catalogue of evil, passionate women&#151;which forms part of the anti-feminist side of the sixteenth-century debate on women. All the examples are drawn from classical sources, except for the stories of Romeo and Juliet  (ibid., 22), Aleran and Adelasie  (ibid., 21&#150;2), and Gysmond and Guistardes  (ibid., 22). This last allusion attests to Peend's knowledge of William Walter's text of the same name, dated 1532, and the others to his familiarity with Matteo Bandello's remarkably popular and influential <span class="italic">Novelle</span>. Indeed, Peend's second composition, <span class="italic">The Moste Notable Historie of John Lord Mandosse</span>, is a translation of one of Bandello's stories. Although Peend claims a Spanish source for <span class="italic">Mandosse</span>, he actually used Pierre Boaistuau's French translation (1559) of Bandello. Bandello and Boaistuau both allege a Spanish author for the tale (although Boaistuau's Spaniard purportedly wrote in Latin), and it is likely that Peend did the same to designate proximity to the original, thus lending more authority to his narrative (Bandello, 425; Boaistuau, 169). <span class="italic">Mandosse</span> is dedicated to Sir Thomas Kemp, and is written in rhyming couplets in alternating lines of iambic hexameter and heptameter. Peend is also the author of a short poem prefixed to John Studley's <span class="italic">Agamemnon</span> (1566). Nothing is known of his later life.<br>
<br>Joyce Boro </div>
