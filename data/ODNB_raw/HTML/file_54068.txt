<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Rathborne,  Aaron</span>  (<em>b. </em>1571/2), <span class="occ">land surveyor and author</span>, was possibly trained by the London mathematician and teacher John Goodwyn, but his origins are obscure. From 1605 he was surveying estates in Yorkshire, Cumberland, Durham, and Lincolnshire, and he was one of those employed in the Jacobean surveys of crown lands. He surveyed land in Suffolk in 1613.<br>
<br>Rathborne was lodging with Roger Burgis against Salisbury House Gate in the Strand, London, when he published <span class="italic">The Surveyor in Foure Bookes</span> (1616). This influential textbook addressed the practical everyday needs of surveyors more realistically than had been done hitherto. Rathborne placed surveying firmly among the mathematical sciences and the first two books of <span class="italic">The Surveyor</span> dealt with the elements of geometry, especially with those relevant to surveying. The third book described surveying instruments and their application. Rathborne was particularly concerned with the need to maintain professional standards and pointed out common errors in the use of the plane table (a popular surveying instrument), and suggested ways of overcoming them. He introduced a &#145;decimal chain&#146;&#151;a predecessor to the one devised by Edmund Gunter&#151;in which each perch was subdivided into 100 parts, and discussed its employment in land measurement using geometrical methods. The title page to <span class="italic">The Surveyor</span> promoted the use of the altazimuth theodolite (an instrument that enabled simultaneous measurement of horizontal and vertical angles) but Rathborne treated it dismissively in the text, as a complicated instrument already well described. Instead, he recommended his azimuth theodolite or &#145;peractor&#146; (an instrument for measuring horizontal angles) and a circumferentor (a type of surveying compass) for measuring large areas. The final book of <span class="italic">The Surveyor</span> followed earlier texts in discussing legal and manorial issues.<br>
<br>Two years after this publication Rathborne was granted a patent to make a map of the cities of London and Westminster, as well as maps of York, Bristol, Norwich, Canterbury, Bath, Oxford, Cambridge, and Windsor. The co-patentee was Roger Bruges (probably his landlord of 1616). Rathborne was consulted about estate business in Galtres Forest in the North Riding of Yorkshire in 1622. Neither his later activities nor his date of death are known.<br>
<br>Sarah Bendall </div>
