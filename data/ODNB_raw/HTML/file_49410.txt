<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ecgburh <span class="st"> [Ecgburg]</span></span>  (<em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>717), <span class="occ">Benedictine nun</span>, is known from her one extant letter, sent to the West Saxon missionary Boniface in Germany between 716 and 718 and preserved in his collected correspondence. &#145;No day unrolls nor night slips by without some recollection of your teaching. Therefore believe me, as God is my witness, I embrace you with the noblest kind of love&#146;  (Tangl, no. 13). Although Ecgburh describes herself as &#145;the least&#146; of Boniface's pupils, her educational attainment is revealed by her allusions to Virgil and Jerome. She laments that her sister Wehtburh is in prison in Rome, and declares that Boniface has been her spiritual brother since the cruel death of her blood brother Oshere. Her letter includes a postscript by a certain Ealdbeorht, who was later to add a similar postscript to a letter by Tyccea, probably the abbot of that name who (as abbot probably of Glastonbury) in 757 attested a charter of &AElig;thelbald of Mercia, probably in favour of the monastery of Malmesbury. This western orientation supports the suggestion that Ecgburh's brother Oshere was <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/20896/', "Oshere, king of the Hwicce")</script> [<i>see under </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/52342/', "Hwicce, kings of the (act. c.670&#150;c.780)")</script>], the alleged founder of the see of Worcester in 679, who had probably died (or retired) in the 690s or soon after, and certainly by 709. Ecgburh may be the same person as the abbess of Gloucester called Eadburh in later sources, according to which she succeeded Abbess Cyneburh (<i>d</i>. after 704), the sister of King Osric (Oshere's predecessor), and was the widow of Wulfhere, king of Mercia (<i>d</i>. 674/5). This Ecgburh was buried at St Peter's, Gloucester. The names Ecgburh (or Ecgburg) and Eadburh (or Eadburg) were distinct, but the latter appears as a corruption of the former, as in the case of Ecgburg (miswritten Eadburg), sister of &AElig;lfwald, king of the East Angles (<i>d</i>. 749). (The identification of the East Anglian Ecgburg with Boniface's correspondent is unlikely.)<br>
<br>Patrick Sims-Williams </div>
