<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Donn&aacute;n</span> <span class="st"> [St Donn&aacute;n]</span>  (<em>d.</em> 617), <span class="occ">martyr</span>, is earliest reliably attested in the annals of Tigernach and the annals of Ulster. In their annal for the year 617, these record: &#145;the burning of Donn&aacute;n of Eigg on 17 April with 150 martyrs&#146; (<span class="italic">Annals of Tigernach</span>, s.a. 616; <span class="italic">Ann. Ulster</span>, s.a. 617; Adomn&aacute;n, trans. Sharpe, 369). Although its date of composition is uncertain, probably the earliest fuller account of Donn&aacute;n's death appears in Latin in the twelfth-century <span class="roman">Book of Leinster</span>. This relates how a rich woman who lived on Eigg had ill feeling towards Donn&aacute;n and the monastic community that he had established on the island, and persuaded bandits to kill him. Arriving on the eve of Easter, the bandits found the community chanting psalms in the oratory, &#145;and they could not kill them there&#146;. Donn&aacute;n, however, instructed his monks to move to the refectory, &#145;since as long as we remain where we have done our all to please God, we cannot die, but where we have served the body, we may pay the price of the body&#146;  (Sharpe, 370). According to this version, fifty-four companions were killed along with Donn&aacute;n. The ninth-century martyrology of Tallaght puts the number at fifty-two. The account in the <span class="roman">Book of Leinster</span> also appears in the scholia of the earliest glossed copies of the ninth-century <span class="italic">F&eacute;lire &Oacute;engusso</span>. The scholia in two late medieval copies of that work tell in Irish that Donn&aacute;n wanted Columba (<i>d</i>. 597) to be his soul-friend (<i>anmchara</i>), but Columba refused. He is supposed to have shown a preference for the white martyrdom of ascetic self-denial, rather than the red martyrdom of death, saying: &#145;I will be soul-friend only to people of white martyrdom; that is, I will not be your soul-friend because you and all your community with you will suffer red martyrdom&#146;  (Sharpe, 370).<br>
<br>Donn&aacute;n's foundation on Eigg was probably located at or near the existing ruin of the church of Kildonnan in the south-east corner of the island. The name Kildonnan (or Kildonan) was also given to a church in Sutherland, indicating some small dissemination of his cult in Scotland.<br>
<br>Marios Costambeys </div>
