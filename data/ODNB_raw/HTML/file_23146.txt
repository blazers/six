<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Rasbotham,  Dorning</span>  (1730&#150;1791), <span class="occ">antiquary and playwright</span>, was born at Manchester and baptized on 10 May 1730 at Cross Street Presbyterian Chapel, Manchester. The son of Peter Rasbotham and his wife, Hannah, daughter of John Dorning, of Birch House, Farnworth, in the parish of Dean, Lancashire, he was educated at the Manchester grammar school. On 24 July 1754 he married Sarah, eldest daughter of James Bayley, of Withington, near Manchester, granddaughter of Samuel Peploe, bishop of Chester. They had five children; a son, the Revd Dorning Rasbotham, became a fellow of Manchester collegiate church.<br>
<br>Rasbotham was chairman of the quarter sessions at Manchester for twenty-five years, and high sheriff of Lancashire in 1769. He made extensive collections for a history of Lancashire, and his manuscripts, partly written in Byrom's shorthand, proved of great use to Edward Baines when compiling his history of Lancashire. In 1774 Rasbotham wrote <span class="italic">Codrus, a Tragedy</span>, in five acts and in verse, which although refused by two London managers was successfully performed at Manchester in that year. He published it anonymously to test the public's reaction to this verdict. It was produced again at Manchester in 1778 for the benefit of Younger the actor, when Kemble, Lewis, and Mrs Siddons took part in the performance. In 1782 he printed <span class="italic">Verses originally intended to have been spoken at the breaking-up of the free grammar school in Manchester</span>. He is also thought to have written <span class="italic">A Dissuasive from Popular Rioting Directed Against Mechanical Manufacturing Improvements</span> (1779). Rasbotham died on 7 November 1791 and was buried at the parish church of Dean, where a mural tablet was placed to his memory, with an inscription by Thomas Barnes DD.<br>
<br>C. W. Sutton<span class="italic">, rev. </span> J. A. Marchand </div>
