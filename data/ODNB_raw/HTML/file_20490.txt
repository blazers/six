<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ochs,  John Ralph</span>  (1704&#150;1788), <span class="occ">medallist</span>, was the son of <a name="cosubject_20490"></a><span class="headword">Johann Rudolph Ochs</span>  (1673&#150;1749/50), <span class="occ">gem-engraver and sealcutter</span>, from Bern, Switzerland. Johann Rudolph Ochs the elder worked for a time at the Royal Mint as assistant. F&uuml;ssli  (p. 474) described him as being &#145;appreciated as one of the great modern artists&#146;. Johann Rudolph Ochs went back to Switzerland for a period, but returned to Britain in 1719 where he died in 1749 or 1750.<br>
<br>Probably taught by his father, John Ralph Ochs was employed at the mint between <i>c</i>.1727 and 1787. He was promoted to become third assistant engraver in 1757. He was still third engraver in 1768 when he was paid an annual salary of &pound;80. As second engraver at the mint in 1779 Ochs was offered the position of first engraver after Richard Yeo's death that year. He declined owing to his advanced years and by 1786 he was incapable of working at all. In 1787 he retired at the age of eighty-three, having worked at the mint for more than fifty years. Hocking attributes the maundy money of George III (1763&#150;86) to Ochs. Although he would, as chief engraver, have been entitled to an annual pension of &pound;200, his position as assistant engraver would have paid him only &pound;80 yearly. Lewis Pingo, the then chief engraver, proposed to give Ochs a higher pension in acknowledgement of his long working life at the mint, officially permitting him an annual pension of &pound;120 and 6 guineas.<br>
<br>On Ochs's death in 1788 his widow, Frances, was appointed office sweeper at the mint. Challis considers her salary of &pound;10 yearly to have been generous, since she was elderly and infirm and not able to perform her official duties. As the widow of a mint engraver she continued to receive a pension until her death in 1806.<br>
<br>W. W. Wroth<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Lorna Colberg Goldsmith </div>

<h2>Wealth at death &nbsp;</h2>
<p> approx. &pound;150 (at time of writing will); wife to inherit all, or, if she predeceased him, Charles Wyatt and wife of Prescott Street, Goodman's Fields, Middlesex, to receive &pound;10 each and easy chair; Thomas Dalrymple (who lived with Ochs) to receive &pound;10 and liquor case; C. Johnson, Swann Street, to receive 5 guineas, and to invest &pound;100 and give interest to C. Wyatt the younger until he was twenty-five (or his brother John if C.
