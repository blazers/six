<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Charlton,  Mary</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1794&#150;1824), <span class="occ">writer and translator</span>, is principally remembered as being the author of several successful novels at the close of the eighteenth and beginning of the nineteenth centuries. Little else is known about her life. Her first novel, <span class="italic">The Parisian, or, Anecdotes of Distinguished Characters</span>, set partly during the French Revolution, was published in 1794 at William Lane's Minerva Press, the most notorious publisher of popular fiction of its day. By the end of 1798 Charlton had produced three more novels for the Minerva&#151;<span class="italic">Andronica, or, The Fugitive Bride</span> (1797), <span class="italic">Phedora, or, The Forest of Minski</span>, and <span class="italic">Ammorvin and Zallida</span> (both 1798)&#151;and Lane gratefully placed her sixth on his list of &#145;particular and favourite Authors&#146; in his prospectus. Charlton has much in common with her fellow Minerva best-sellers. All of Lane's top authors worked in the Gothic mode, providing their readers with tales of domestic persecution in quasi-exotic settings, but both contemporary and modern critics have professed to find Charlton's descriptions superior to those of her competitors.<br>
<br>Charlton was also typical in that, even with Europe at war, her work crossed national borders. Like most of the successful Minerva novelists, Charlton was translated into French, her next two novels, <span class="italic">Rosella, or, Modern Occurrences</span> (1799), a satire on novel-reading and her best work, and <span class="italic">The Pirate of Naples</span> (1801), being published in Paris in 1801. The following year Charlton published <span class="italic">The Reprobate</span>, a translation from the German of August Lafontaine. Two of her fellow Minerva best-sellers, Eliza Parsons and Elizabeth Meeke, also translated Lafontaine, but it was Charlton's version which provided the basis for Thomas Dibdin's successful comedy, <span class="italic">Guilty or Not Guilty</span>, first acted at the Theatre Royal, Haymarket, in May 1804. She went on to publish three further translations, <span class="italic">The Philosophic Kidnapper</span> (&#145;altered from the French&#146;, 1803), <span class="italic">The Rake and the Misanthrope</span> (also from Lafontaine, 1804), and <span class="italic">The Homicide, a Novel, Taken from the Comedie di Goldoni</span> (1805, reissued as <span class="italic">Rosaura di Viralva</span> in 1813). In 1815 Charlton compiled a volume of <span class="italic">Pathetic Poetry for Youth</span> and in 1824 she published her final works, <span class="italic">Grandeur and Meanness, or, Domestic Persecution</span> and <span class="italic">Past Events: an Historical Novel, of the Eighteenth Century</span>.<br>
<br>M. O. Grenby </div>
