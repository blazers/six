<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Collings,  Samuel</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1793?), <span class="occ">painter and caricaturist</span>, exhibited regularly at the Royal Academy between 1784 and 1789. Paintings accepted were <span class="italic">Children in the Wood</span>, 1784; <span class="italic">The Chamber of Genius</span>, from which an engraving was made, 1785; <span class="italic">The Triumph of Sensibility</span>, 1786; <span class="italic">The Convict: a Portrait</span>, 1787; <span class="italic">Chalk Pit Near Lewisham</span>, 1788, and <span class="italic">Frost on the Thames, Sketched on the Spot</span>, 1789. The last was sold at Christies on 11 March 1960, and is now at Yale University, as is the self-portrait offered in the same sale. These are the only known oil paintings by the caricaturist. A signed watercolour titled <span class="italic">Elijah Going up to Heaven</span> is in the British Museum (1881&#150;11&#150;12&#150;174). Other paintings that formed the subject of engravings are a pair in the style of William Hogarth's <span class="italic">The Rake's Progress</span>, titled <span class="italic">The Heir Disinherited</span> and <span class="italic">The Disinherited Heir</span>, aquatinted by F. Jukes, and portraits of George III and Lord Thurlow.<br>
<br>Collings appears to have abandoned painting to concentrate on his work as a caricaturist during the last few years of his life. His earliest dated pieces in this vein appear to be the folding plates published in the <span class="italic">Wit's Magazine</span> in 1784. Of the seven signed plates, four were engraved by William Blake. Another engraved by Smith, <span class="italic">Gilpin Going Farther than he Intended</span>, is probably the earliest illustration of Cowper's famous character. Collings's best-known caricatures are the twenty designs reproduced as etchings by his friend Thomas Rowlandson, published anonymously in two parts under the title <span class="italic">Picturesque Beauties of Boswell, Designed and Etched by Two Capital Artists</span> (1786), reprinted in the mid-nineteenth century as the work of Rowlandson. Six of Collings's drawings for the series are in the Victoria and Albert Museum (Dyce bequest, 754&#150;9), including two unused designs. The figures portrayed are similar to those by Rowlandson himself, but in Collings's other work his style is closer to that of Hogarth and the other early caricaturists. The two friends also collaborated to produce a similar satire on Goethe's <span class="italic">Sorrows of Werther</span>. Other periodicals in which Collings's caricatures appear are the <span class="italic">Carlton House Magazine</span>; <span class="italic">Attick Miscellany</span>; and <span class="italic">The Bon-Ton Magazine, or, Microscope of Fashion and Folly</span>. Caricatures published under the pseudonym Annibal Scratch have also been attributed to him. He is thought to have died at an early age. He was described by Henry Angelo as &#145;a great tavern goer &hellip; [whose] fate was lamented, he being found dead on the steps of an hotel&#146;  (Angelo, 1.133) in Soho.<br>
<br>Michael Heseltine </div>
