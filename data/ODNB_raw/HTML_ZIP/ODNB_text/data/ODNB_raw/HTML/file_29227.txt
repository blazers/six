<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Whitaker,  Tobias</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1601?, <em>d.</em> 1664), <span class="occ">physician</span>, may have been the Tobias Whitaker, son of Francis Whitaker, who was baptized on 1 May 1601 at St Dunstan and All Saints, Stepney, Middlesex. In the preface to his first published work, <span class="italic">A Discourse of Water</span> (1634), he describes himself as &#145;Doctor of Physicke, of Norwich&#146;, but moved to London some time before 1638. In that year he brought out his principal work, <span class="italic">The tree of humane life, or the blood of the grape, proving the possibilitie of maintaining humane life from infancy to extreame old age without sickness, by the use of wine</span>, which was republished several times and translated into Latin (Frankfurt, 1655; The Hague, 1660, 1663). The purpose of this work, as stated in the introduction to the 1654 edition, was as &#145;an addition to our medicinal faculty, and more exactly to declare, how, and after what manner the juice of the Grape may be conducible to humane bodyes, both for Aliment and Medicament&#146;. Whitaker seems to have encountered some criticism of his recommendation of wine as a universal prophylactic and remedy, for he declares that his book is &#145;for the acquaintance of more reasonable creatures&#146; rather than the &#145;Tred of Dunghill-Cocks&#146; who oppose him. The work describes wine in terms of temperature and dryness, and parallels symptoms of various illnesses to these two conditions before indicating which can be used to treat each ailment, and concludes rousingly that &#145;other Herculian braines&#146; should take wine &#145;plentifull in substance and measure, by which every sense shall be refreshed, every capacity filled, and every intellect really delighted&#146;.<br>
<br>In September 1660 Whitaker was appointed physician-in-ordinary to the royal household with a salary of &pound;50 per year, although the position had first been offered to him by Charles II when in exile in 1649. In 1661 Whitaker published <span class="italic">An elenchus of opinions concerning the cure of the small pox. Together with problematical questions concerning the French pest</span>, prefixed to which was a portrait engraved by John Chantrey. Whitaker died in 1664. His widow received his pay, in arrears, in 1668.<br>
<br>Both Wood and Munk ascribe the writing of <span class="italic">The Tree of Humane Life</span> to William Whitaker, admitted a candidate of the College of Physicians in 1654, who died in the parish of St Clement Danes in January 1671.<br>
<br>Jo Payne </div>
