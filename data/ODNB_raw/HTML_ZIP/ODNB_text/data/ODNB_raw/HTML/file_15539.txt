<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Killigrew,  Thomas</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1694, <em>d.</em> 1719), <span class="occ">playwright</span>, was baptized on 23 February 1694, the second son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/15532/', "Charles Killigrew (1655&#150;1724/5)")</script>, theatre manager, and Jemima, niece of Richard Bokenham, mercer, of London, and the grandson of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/15538/', "Thomas Killigrew the elder (1612&#150;1683)")</script>, with whose son Thomas (1657&#150;1674) he has sometimes been confused. He was probably the author of <span class="italic">Chit-Chat</span>, a comedy, first performed at the Theatre Royal, Drury Lane, on 14 February 1719, and published in two separate editions in the same year. Its strong cast included Barton Booth as Worthy, Robert Wilks as Bellamar, Colley Cibber as Alamode, and Anne Oldfield as Florinda. Described in the prologue as the author's &#145;first Coup d'Essay&#146;, <span class="italic">Chit-Chat</span> proved one of the most popular plays of the 1718&#150;19 season. It was given eleven performances at Drury Lane between its premi&egrave;re and 19 March, and another two at Richmond on 6 and 20 June, the former, &#145;by his Royal Highness's Command&#146;  (Avery, 542), celebrating the opening of William Pinkethman's new theatre. <span class="italic">Chit-Chat</span> was also a financial success: in addition to two author benefits and a sum of 150 guineas presented him by the prince and the princess of Wales, Killigrew secured the patronage of John Campbell, second duke of Argyll, &#145;whose interest was so powerfully supported, that it was said the profits of his play amounted to above a thousand pounds&#146;, according to Thomas Whincop  (Nicoll, 18). In letters addressed to Booth and Steele, the critic John Dennis expressed his indignation at the success of such trivia as <span class="italic">Chit-Chat</span> and other comedies, when his own tragedy <span class="italic">The Invader of his Country</span> had never even reached the stage. Killigrew contributed &#145;The Fable of Aumilius and the Statue of Venus&#146; to <span class="italic">Miscellanea aurea, or, The Golden Medley</span>, a collection of &#145;epistolary essays in prose and verse&#146;  (1720). He was buried at Kensington on 21 July 1719.<br>
<br>J. P. Vander Motten </div>
