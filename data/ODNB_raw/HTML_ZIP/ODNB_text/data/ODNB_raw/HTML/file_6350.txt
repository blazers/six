<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Gorrie,  Archibald</span>  (1778&#150;1857), <span class="occ">agriculturist</span>, was born on 15 May 1778 at Logiealmond, Perthshire. He was educated at the local school, and from 1797 to 1807 he held various gardening posts; for a time he was in charge of the hothouse at the Leith Walk nursery in Edinburgh. From 1807 until his death he was manager of the estate of Annat, Kilspindie, near Errol, Perthshire, farming also on his own account at Shanry. He was responsible for introducing into gardens the pansy, known in the wild as &#145;love-in-idleness&#146;, and he raised new fruits, including the Annat Park apricot, the Annat scarlet apple, and the Annat beurr&eacute; pear. He introduced the Perthshire red potato, and also cultivated grasses such as wood millet grass (<i>Milium effusum</i>).<br>
<br>In his early years Gorrie was associated with George Don, who published <span class="italic">A System of Gardening and Botany</span>, founded on Philip Miller's <span class="italic">Gardener's Dictionary</span>. Gorrie contributed to many agricultural and horticultural magazines, including the <span class="italic">Gardener's Magazine</span>, and wrote monthly agricultural reports for the <span class="italic">Perthshire Courier</span> and the <span class="italic">Dundee Advertiser</span>. His paper, &#145;Preventing the depradations of the turnip fly&#146;, was presented to the Caledonian Horticultural Society in 1811.<br>
<br>On 6 November 1809 Gorrie married Euphemia Moyes. They had two sons and two daughters. He was an elder of the Free Church of Scotland and was chosen as a representative at its general assembly. Gorrie died at Annat Cottage, near Errol, on 21 July 1857, and was buried nearby in the churchyard at Rait, Perthshire. His wife survived him.<br>
<br>Anne Pimlott Baker </div>
