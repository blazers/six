<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Linnecar,  Richard</span>  (1722&#150;1800), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was born at Wakefield and was for some time postmaster there. In 1763 he was elected one of the coroners for the West Riding of Yorkshire. For many years he was a prominent freemason. Linnecar was initiated into the order at Gibraltar in 1743 and then later held the office of master of the lodge of unanimity at Wakefield for twenty-six years. In 1787 his portrait was painted for the lodge by Singleton. It was later engraved by T. Barrow and reproduced on a masonic banner that remains at the Wakefield lodge.<br>
<br>Linnecar published by subscription in 1789 <span class="italic">Miscellaneous Works</span>, containing two comedies, <span class="italic">The Lucky Escape</span> and <span class="italic">The Plotting Wives</span>, the latter of which was performed at York on 6 February 1769; a tragedy, <span class="italic">The Generous Moor</span>, &#145;founded upon a true story&#146; which Linnecar apparently heard in Gibraltar  (Linnecar, 105); some prose <span class="italic">Strictures on Freemasonry</span> and numerous songs and other trifles in verse. The work was dedicated to John Berkenhout and the author's other &#145;generous subscribers&#146;  (ibid., unpaginated dedication). Linnecar's substantial list of subscribers induced his unenthusiastic anonymous reviewer in the <span class="italic">Critical Review</span> &#145;to suppose that Mr. Linnecar has some other claim on public favour than his literary abilities&#146;  (1792, 229).<br>
<br>Linnecar died while holding an inquest at Swillington, Yorkshire, on 14 March 1800, aged seventy-eight  (<span class="italic">GM</span>, 1800, 391).<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Jeffrey Herrle </div>
