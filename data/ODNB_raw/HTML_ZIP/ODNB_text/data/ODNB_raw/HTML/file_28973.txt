<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Wehnert,  Edward Henry</span>  (1813&#150;1868), <span class="occ">watercolour painter</span>, was baptized on 14 February 1813 at St Anne's Church, Soho, London, the son of Henry Wehnert, a German tailor, and his wife, Susanna; he had at least two brothers. In 1825 he was sent to Germany to study in G&ouml;ttingen, and in 1829 he returned to England, where he studied drawing from classical sculptures in the British Museum, London, and did a large oil painting, <span class="italic">The Death of Hippolytus</span>, which was exhibited at the Society of British Artists in Suffolk Street in 1832. From 1832 to 1834 he studied art in Paris, and then lived and worked in Jersey for three years before returning permanently to London in 1837, when he became a member of the New Society of Painters in Water Colours. From then on he mainly painted historical genre scenes, including <span class="italic">Luther Reading his Sermon to some Friends</span>, <span class="italic">The Death of Wycliffe</span>, <span class="italic">Caxton Examining the First Proof Sheet from his Press</span>, and <span class="italic">The Prisoner of Gisors</span>. He exhibited three pictures at the Royal Academy between 1839 and 1863, including <span class="italic">The Elopement&#151;Eve of St Agnes</span> (exh. 1852) and <span class="italic">The Death of Jean Goujon</span> (exh. 1863). He took part in the Westminster Hall cartoon competition in 1845, and the chalk cartoon of <span class="italic">The Triumph of Justice</span>, submitted by him, is in the Victoria and Albert Museum in London, which also has his <span class="italic">George Fox Preaching in a Tavern</span> (1865).<br>
<br>Wehnert's large works, Germanic in style, did not sell well, and he was more successful as a book illustrator, doing the drawings for editions of Keats's <span class="italic">Eve of St Agnes</span> (1856), Coleridge's <span class="italic">Ancient Mariner</span> (1857), Hans Christian Andersen's <span class="italic">Eventyr</span> (as <span class="italic">Fairy Tales</span>, 1861), and Edgar Allan Poe's <span class="italic">Poetical Works</span> (1865). He died, unmarried, at his home, 9 Fortress Terrace, Kentish Town, London, on 15 September 1868, aged fifty-five. A memorial exhibition was held at the Institute of Painters in Water Colours (the former New Watercolour Society) in 1869.<br>
<br>F. M. O'Donoghue<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Anne Pimlott Baker </div>
