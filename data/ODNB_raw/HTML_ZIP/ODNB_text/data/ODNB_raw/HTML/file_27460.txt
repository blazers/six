<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Timbs,  John</span>  (1801&#150;1875), <span class="occ">author</span>, was born on 17 August 1801 at Clerkenwell, London, and was educated at a private school at Hemel Hempstead. He was apprenticed to a printer and druggist at Dorking, and while there began to write, his first contributions appearing in the <span class="italic">Monthly Magazine</span> in 1820. About that year he came to London, and was for some time amanuensis to Sir Richard Phillips, publisher of the magazine. From that time he contributed to a large number of London publications, but chiefly to the <span class="italic">Mirror of Literature</span>, which he edited from 1827 to 1838; <span class="italic">The Harlequin: a Journal of Drama</span>, which appeared between 11 May and 16 July 1829, and which was stopped by the commissioners of stamps insisting that it should be stamped as a newspaper; the <span class="italic">Literary World</span>, which he edited during 1839 and 1840; and the <span class="italic">Illustrated London News</span>, of which he was sub-editor under Dr Charles Mackay from 1842 to 1858. He was also the originator and editor of the <span class="italic">Year Book of Science and Art</span>, begun in 1839 after he left the <span class="italic">Mirror</span>.<br>
<br>Timbs's works, which run to more than 150 volumes (some appearing under the pseudonym Horace Welby), are compilations of interesting facts gathered from every conceivable quarter, and relating to the most varied subjects. They include, on subjects of domestic interest, <span class="italic">Family Manual</span> (1831), <span class="italic">Domestic Life in England</span> (1835), and <span class="italic">Pleasant Half-Hours for the Family Circle</span> (1872), and, on scientific subjects, <span class="italic">Popular Zoology</span> (1834), <span class="italic">Stories of Inventors and Discoverers</span> (1859), <span class="italic">Curiosities of Science</span> (1860), and <span class="italic">Wonderful Inventions: from the Mariner's Compass to the Electric Telegraph Cable</span> (1867). He also wrote on artistic and cultural matters works such as <span class="italic">Painting Popularly Explained</span> (jointly with Thomas John Gulick) (1859) and <span class="italic">Manual for Art Students and Visitors to the Exhibitions</span> (1862). Through his connection with <span class="italic">The Harlequin</span> he has been identified as the likely compiler (under the pseudonym Harold Foote) of the <span class="italic">Companion to the Theatre and Manual of British Drama</span> (1829), which contains much valuable information on London theatres of the period. On contemporary city life his works included <span class="italic">Curiosities of London</span> (1855), <span class="italic">Club Life of London with Anecdotes</span> (1865), <span class="italic">Romance of London: Strange Stories, Scenes, and Persons</span> (1865), and <span class="italic">London and Westminster, City and Suburb</span> (1867). He also published on subjects of biographical and historical interest, including <span class="italic">Schooldays of Eminent Men</span> (1858), <span class="italic">Columbus</span> (1863), <span class="italic">Curiosities of History</span> (1859), <span class="italic">Anecdote Biography</span> (1859&#150;60), <span class="italic">Anecdote Lives of Wits and Humourists</span> (1862), <span class="italic">Ancestral Stories and Traditions of Great Families</span> (1869), and <span class="italic">Abbeys, Castles and Ancient Halls of England and Wales</span> (1869). He also edited <span class="italic">Manuals of Utility</span> (1847), the <span class="italic">Percy Anecdotes</span> (1869&#150;70), and <span class="italic">Pepys's Memoirs</span> (1871). In recognition of his antiquarian labours he was elected a fellow of the Society of Antiquaries in 1854. He died in considerable poverty in London on 6 March 1875.<br>
<br>J. R. MacDonald<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Nilanjana Banerji </div>
