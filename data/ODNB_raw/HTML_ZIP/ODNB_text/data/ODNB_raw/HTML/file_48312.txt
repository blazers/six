<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ste Maure,  Beno&icirc;t  de</span>  (<em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>1160&#150;<em>c.</em>1180), <span class="occ">historian and poet</span>, may have been a monk at the Benedictine abbey of Marmoutier. Virtually nothing is known about his life, except that he is more likely to have come from the town of Ste Maure in the Touraine region than from Ste Maure in Champagne. Two long narrative poems in octosyllabic rhymed couplets have been identified as his: the first, the <span class="italic">Roman de Troie</span> (<i>c</i>.1160), with certainty, and the second, the <span class="italic">Chronique des ducs de Normandie</span> (<i>c</i>.1174&#150;1180), with certainty by a majority of scholars, but with hesitation (or not at all) by others. The identification of the Beneeiz de Sainte More of the <span class="italic">Roman de Troie</span> with the Beneeit of the <span class="italic">Chronique</span> is based upon linguistic and stylistic evidence, and not on biographical information in the <span class="italic">Chronique</span>, or on extratextual evidence (of which there is almost none). Although he was a contemporary of Wace who also wrote a poem containing a myth of Trojan descent, the <span class="italic">Roman de Brut</span> (1155), and a chronicle of the Norman dukes and Anglo-Norman kings, the <span class="italic">Roman de Rou</span> (<i>c</i>.1160&#150;1174), Beno&icirc;t de Ste Maure was much less forthcoming about his personal circumstances than the Norman poet; in that respect he was more typical of the majority of contemporary authors about whose lives very little is known.<br>
<br>Beno&icirc;t de Ste Maure's practice of translation was also typical of contemporary practice: he considered the 30,316-line <span class="italic">Roman de Troie</span> a translation, though it is a lengthy adaptation of two short Latin prose works, the <span class="italic">De excidio Troiae historia</span> of Dares the Phrygian, and the <span class="italic">Ephemeridos belli Troiani</span> of Dictys of Crete, pseudo-histories dating from the fourth to the sixth centuries <span class="smallcap">AD</span>. One of the <i>romans d'antiquit&eacute;</i> (French narratives based on classical authors), the <span class="italic">Roman de Troie</span> traces the legendary history of Troy from the Argonauts to the death of Ulysses upon his return home from the siege. In the <span class="italic">Roman de Troie</span> where Hector is the main hero rather than Achilles and the story of Troilus and Cressida (or as Beno&icirc;t calls her, Briseida) is thought to appear for the first time, antiquity is depicted under the guise of twelfth-century feudalism, and the element of love intrigue is considerable. The <span class="italic">Roman de Troie</span>, often thought to have been dedicated to Eleanor of Aquitaine based on Beno&icirc;t's reference to a &#145;riche dame de riche rei&#146;, was extremely popular, widely translated and imitated. According to the scholars who accept the single identity of the author of the <span class="italic">Troie</span> and of the <span class="italic">Chronique</span>, it was probably the popularity of the <span class="italic">Troie</span> that brought the poet to Henry Plantagenet's attention.<br>
<br>Beno&icirc;t de Ste Maure's chronicle of the Norman dukes, the 44,544-line <span class="italic">Chronique des ducs de Normandie</span>, was, however, less popular than the <span class="italic">Roman de Troie</span>. According to Wace, Henry II commissioned &#145;Maistre Beneeit&#146; to write a history of the Norman dukes, leading Wace to break off the <span class="italic">Roman de Rou</span> before bringing the narrative up to the reign of Henry II. Although Beno&icirc;t's chronicle of the Normans is more diffuse and less lucid than Wace's, and is considered by historians to be less reliable, possibly owing to the large amount of panegyric inserted for the benefit of the royal house, notable parallels exist between the <span class="italic">Rou</span> and the <span class="italic">Chronique</span> as regards circumstances of composition. Like Wace, Beno&icirc;t de Ste Maure was at this point in his career writing for Henry's court. He also used the Norman histories of Dudo of St Quentin, William of Jumi&egrave;ges, and William of Poitiers as his primary sources; passages in the <span class="italic">Chronique</span> suggest that Beno&icirc;t may have had Wace's <span class="italic">Rou</span> at hand as well. Again like Wace he did not complete his chronicle of the Norman dukes, ending with the death of Henry II's grandfather, Henry I, in 1135; towards the end of the work Beno&icirc;t also expresses reservations about his patron's possible displeasure with the work, due perhaps to slow progress. Beno&icirc;t de Ste Maure devoted less energy than Wace to the military aspects of the Normans' rise to power, and concentrated more on demonstrating the necessary interaction of chivalric with clerical values and interests. He is thereby thought to have made a significant contribution to the medieval theory of the three orders of social organization.<br>
<br>Jean Blacker </div>
