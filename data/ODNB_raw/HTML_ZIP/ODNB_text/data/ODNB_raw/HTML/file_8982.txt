<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Evans,  Thomas</span>  (1742&#150;1784), <span class="occ">bookseller</span>, left no record of his parents or origin. He was apprenticed in London in 1757 for &pound;40 to Charles Marsh, whose shop was registered as in St Martin-in-the-Fields (but later in Round Court in the Strand and in Charing Cross). A handwritten note on a copy of Marsh's poem <span class="italic">The Library</span>  (1766; Nichols, 3.647) suggests that Evans carried on at Marsh's shop beyond his apprenticeship, perhaps even under Marsh's successor Samuel Leacroft (1767), until he set up his own shop at 50 Strand, near the York Buildings, in 1774.<br>
<br>Evans was a &#145;literary&#146; bookseller who, as Nichols says, &#145;favoured the world with elegant editions of complete collections of the works of some very eminent poets&#146;  (Nichols, 6.435). Among these multi-volume editions (some of which Evans personally edited) were the works of Beaumont and Fletcher, George Villiers, Matthew Prior, Richard Savage, Oliver Goldsmith (the first collected edition), and Fran&ccedil;ois Rabelais. Other notable publications included Shakespeare's <span class="italic">Poems</span>, Evans's own four volumes of <span class="italic">Old Ballads, Historical and Narrative</span>, Nicholson's <span class="italic">The English, Scotch, and Irish Historical Libraries</span>, Caradoc of Lhancarvan's <span class="italic">History of Wales</span>, and a revised edition of Francis Peck's <span class="italic">Desiderata curiosa</span>. The dedicatees of some of these works reflect Evans's circle of friends and acquaintances: Sir Joshua Reynolds, Richard B. Sheridan, Samuel Foote, and David Garrick. Pieces of his correspondence with Garrick are extant. Evans's engaging conversation and lively humour delighted fellow booksellers who met weekly at the Grecian Coffee House, a club that included Thomas Davies, Lockyer Davis, James Dodsley, Thomas Longman, Thomas Payne, Thomas Cadell, Robert Baldwin, and John Nichols, with most of whom Evans issued joint publications.<br>
<br>Evans died at 50 Strand, London, on 30 April 1784, and his closest friend, Cadell, served as the executor of his will (witnessed by Isaac Reed and William Fell) and was appointed guardian to <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/8977/', "Robert Harding Evans")</script>, the only son of Evans and his wife, Ann, who survived him. Robert was only six years old at the time of his father's death, and in 1792 Cadell apprenticed the young Evans to Thomas Payne at Mew's Gate for &pound;120. About 1804 Robert Evans took over the bookselling business of James Edwards in Pall Mall but then abandoned it about 1812 to become a celebrated auctioneer of private libraries. In a laudatory two-column obituary, Nichols says nothing of the cause of Evans's early death  (<span class="italic">GM</span>, 1st ser., 54/1, 396). Thomas Evans should not be confused with a contemporary bookseller of the same name, the one Goldsmith caned and whose bookshop was variously located at 20 and 54 Paternoster Row and 429 Strand.<br>
<br>James E. Tierney </div>
