<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Atkinson,  Thomas</span>  (1799&#150;1833), <span class="occ">bookseller and writer</span>, was born on 18 November 1799 in Glasgow, the son of Thomas Atkinson and his wife, Agnes, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Millar. After an apprenticeship with a Mr Turnbull, bookseller, he became co-partner in a publishing business with David Robertson, later king's publisher for Glasgow. The partners also conducted a brisk bookselling business. The assertion that Daniel Macmillan, the publisher, in his youth worked as a shopman for Atkinson is apocryphal. Early in his career Atkinson published <span class="italic">The Sextuple Alliance</span>, a series of poems on Napoleon Bonaparte, favourably commented upon by Sir Walter Scott. From 2 December 1826 to 29 December 1827 Atkinson was co-editor, with Solomon Saveal, of <span class="italic">The Ant</span>, published by Robertson and Atkinson, at first twice monthly and then weekly, and circulated in Glasgow, Edinburgh, and London. It carried original poems and essays, narrative, correspondence, and local literary intelligence. Atkinson's <span class="italic">A Sketch of the Origin and Progress of the Literary and Commercial Society of Glasgow</span> was printed for private circulation in 1831. That same year, his firm began publication of a Scottish annual, <span class="italic">The Chameleon</span>, which lasted for three issues and was composed of folklore, fiction, songs, poetry, and music.<br>
<br>After the <span class="roman">Reform Act</span> was passed in 1832 Atkinson was invited to stand as &#145;a candidate in the liberal interest&#146;  (<span class="italic">DNB</span>) for Stirling, but was not returned. After the election he was diagnosed as suffering from tuberculosis and embarked on a voyage to Barbados for his health. Before departure he purchased an oaken coffin to be shipped with his baggage in case of need. He died on 10 October 1833, and was buried at sea, using his coffin. Atkinson bequeathed a sum to be added to a fund to erect a building in Glasgow to house scientific projects. A monument to his memory was erected in the Glasgow necropolis.<br>
<br>Rosemary T. Van Arsdel </div>
