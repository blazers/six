<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Kennedy,  John</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1626&#150;1629), <span class="occ">poet</span>, a Scot, published two small volumes at Edinburgh in the early part of the seventeenth century. His first work, printed in 1626, was a love tale interspersed with songs and relations in different metres and entitled <span class="italic">The history of Calanthrop and Lucilla, conspicuously demonstrating the various mutabilities of fortune in their loves, with every several circumstance of joyes and crosses, fortunate exploites and hazardous adventures, which either of them sustained before they could attaine the prosperous event of their wished aimes</span>. The dedication to Sir Donald Mackay, from June 1628 Lord Reay of Stranever, reveals that this was the author's first production: &#145;the first perspicuous invention of my stirile braine&#146;. Kennedy confesses to being &#145;meanly acquainted&#146; with Mackay and seeks to mitigate his audacity with allusions to his addressee's learning, virtue, and generosity, and to the poet's own &#145;dutie-bound obligation&#146;. Commendatory verses in Latin were provided by Walter Bellenden and Robert Fairley, and in English by Patrick Mackenzie.<blockquote>
<div>But view this pamphlet, and thy wilt shall finde<br>
</div>
<div>Wise precepts and instructions for thy minde.<br>
</div>
<div>Sweet peace of thought, the secret joy of heart,<br>
</div>
<div>Chaste modest love, void of all vitious airt.<br>
</div>
</blockquote>It was reprinted at London in 1631 as <span class="italic">The Ladies' Delight</span>. Both editions are very rare, and references to copies can be found in the Huth library catalogue of 1880 and in the catalogue of Heber's collection of early English poetry.<br>
<br>Kennedy also wrote <span class="italic">A theological epitome or divine compend, apparently manifesting Gods great love and mercie towards man</span> (1629). This short verse exploration of <span class="roman">Old Testament</span> narratives dwells on aspects of divine justice and mercy. Kennedy's closing appeal is for unity of purpose:<blockquote>
<div>What obligation then have we,<br>
</div>
<div>Towards our gracious Lord?<br>
</div>
<div>Who hath from hell thus set us free,<br>
</div>
<div>O then in one accord!<br>
</div>
<div>In faith, and love with hearts contreit,<br>
</div>
<div>Let us our spirits raise,<br>
</div>
<div>And yeeld to Father, Sonne, and Spreit,<br>
</div>
<div>Thanks, Honour, Glorie, praise.<br>
</div>
</blockquote>A copy, believed to be unique, is in the British Library.<br>
<br>Thomas Seccombe<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Elizabeth Haresnape </div>
