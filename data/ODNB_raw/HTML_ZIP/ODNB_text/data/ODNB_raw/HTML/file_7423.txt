<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Degge, Sir  Simon</span>  (1612?&#150;1703), <span class="occ">judge</span>, was probably born on 5 January 1612, the eldest son of Thomas Degge (<i>d</i>. 1628) of Strangsall, Uttoxeter, Staffordshire, and Dorothy, daughter of George Crichlow of Wolscote, Staffordshire. He was admitted to the Inner Temple in 1639, but the civil war interrupted his studies. As a royalist he was present at the siege of Lichfield in 1643 and was captured at Stafford. Eventually he received a licence from the Stafford county committee to reside at home in Callowhill, Kingston. He compounded for delinquency in November 1645. Degge had married Jane (1609/10&#150;1652), daughter of Thomas Orrell, but following her death in July 1652, on 7 December 1652 he married Alice Oldfield (1614&#150;1696), relict of James Trollope (<i>d</i>. 1649), of Spalding, Lincolnshire.<br>
<br>Degge resumed his legal studies and was called to the bar on 24 November 1653. He practised as a civilian lawyer in Doctors' Commons. In 1660 he became puisne judge of Carmarthen circuit and two years later a justice of the Welsh marches. Both places he retained until 1674. In 1661 he became recorder of Derby and in 1662 steward of the court of the manor of Peverel. He was elected a bencher of the Inner Temple on 7 November 1668, but he was &#145;disbenched&#146; in 1674 for refusing to read at the next Lent vacation. He was knighted on 2 March 1670. Degge served as high sheriff of Derbyshire in 1674&#150;5 and the following year published <span class="italic">The Parson's Counsellor, with Law of Tithes or Tithing</span>, a leading textbook on the subject for many years. Degge was a committed tory and during the reign of James II freely consented to the &#145;three questions&#146; posed by the king's agents. This led to some royal support for a parliamentary candidature in 1688, but it did not lead to Degge having any truck with dissenters as he refused to let Derby's dissenters meet despite the declaration of indulgence.<br>
<br>In his later years Degge seems to have pursued his antiquarian interests, providing William Woolley with information for his <span class="italic">History of Derbyshire</span>. Degge's &#145;Observations upon the possessions of monastery-lands in Staffordshire&#146; was included in Sampson Erdeswick's <span class="italic">A Survey of Staffordshire</span> (1717). Degge died on 10 February 1703, having recently made his will in which he declared his wish to be buried in the &#145;little chapel I lately built adjoining to the parish church of Kingston&#146;.<br>
<br>Stuart Handley </div>
