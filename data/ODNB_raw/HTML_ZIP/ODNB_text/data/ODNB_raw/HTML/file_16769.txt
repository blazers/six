<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Liston,  Henry</span>  (1771&#150;1836), <span class="occ">inventor of a musical instrument and writer on music</span>, the eldest son of Robert Liston, minister of Aberdour, Fife, was born on 30 June 1771. He matriculated at Edinburgh University in 1787 and read physics and mathematics before studying for the church. In 1793 he became minister of the parish of Ecclesmachan, Linlithgowshire, where he remained for the rest of his life. He married Margaret Ireland, the daughter of David Ireland, town clerk of Culross, on 21 October 1793. There were two sons of their marriage: <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/16772/', "Robert Liston")</script> and David Liston, professor of oriental languages at Edinburgh. Henry Liston died suddenly at Merchiston Hall, Falkirk, on 24 February 1836.<br>
<br>Liston's abilities in mechanics and music led to his invention of the euharmonic organ, an acknowledged adaptation of a similar instrument by Dr Robert Smith of Trinity College, Cambridge. In Liston's instrument, built by Flight and Robson, each note of the chromatic scale was provided with a further pipe tuned to its enharmonic equivalent. Designed to give perfect intonation in each of the major and minor keys, an &#145;original scale&#146; (C&#150;C&#9839;&#150;D&#150;E[flat]&#150;E&#150; F&#150;F&#9839;&#150;G&#150;G&#9839;&#150;A&#150;B[flat]&#150;B) was played from the manual keys, but the pipes of the &#145;euharmonic scale&#146; (B&#9839;&#150;C&#9839;&#150;D&#9839;&#150;E&#9839;&#150;F&#9839;&#150;A&#9839;&#150; D[flat]&#150;F[flat]&#150;G[flat]&#150;B[flat]&#150;C[flat]) were made playable as required through five pedals, three &#145;acute&#146; and two &#145;grave&#146;, which acted on the sharp and flat pitches respectively. Although complicated to operate, it was approved by Samuel Wesley, who compared it favourably with similar inventions by Hawkes and Loeschman when it was exhibited in London in 1811. In his <span class="italic">Essay on Perfect Intonation</span> (1812), Liston described his instrument and related it to the theory of acoustics and tonal music. He wrote the article &#145;Music&#146; in the <span class="italic">Edinburgh Encyclopaedia</span>, and translated the sixth book of Caesar's <span class="italic">Gallic Wars</span> for use in schools. Liston also invented an improved form of plough, which was used in his district.<br>
<br>Christopher Kent </div>
