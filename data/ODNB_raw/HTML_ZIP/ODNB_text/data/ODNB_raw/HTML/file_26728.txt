<span class="headword">Wortley, Lady  Emmeline Charlotte Elizabeth  Stuart-</span> <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Lady Emmeline Charlotte Elizabeth Manners]</span>  (1806&#150;1855), <span class="occ">poet and travel writer</span>, was born on 2 May 1806, the second daughter of John Henry Manners, fifth duke of Rutland (1778&#150;1857), and Lady Elizabeth Howard (<i>d</i>. 1825), fifth daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/13899/', "Frederick Howard, fifth earl of Carlisle (1748&#150;1825)")</script>. On 17 February 1831 Lady Emmeline married the Hon. Charles James Stuart-Wortley (1802&#150;1844), second son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/26731/', "James Archibald Stuart-Wortley, first Baron Wharncliffe (1776&#150;1845)")</script>; they had three children: Archibald Henry Plantagenet (1832&#150;1890), Adelbert William John (<i>d</i>. 1844), and Victoria Alexandrina [<i>see </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/38619/', "Welby, Victoria Alexandrina Maria Louisa")</script>], who married Sir William Earle Welby-Gregory on 4 July 1863.<br>
<br>Lady Emmeline's earliest poems appeared in <span class="italic">Blackwood's Magazine</span> and in <span class="italic">Poems</span> (1833), and for the next eleven years she published a volume of verse annually. Some poems were written as a result of her travels, for example <span class="italic">Travelling Sketches in Rhyme</span> (1835), <span class="italic">Impressions of Italy, and other Poems</span> (1837), and <span class="italic">Sonnets</span> (1839), written chiefly during a tour through Holland, Germany, Italy, Turkey, and Hungary (1839). In 1837 and 1840 Lady Emmeline edited the popular annual <span class="italic">The Keepsake</span>, for which she wrote many poems. Among the contributors was Tennyson, who in <span class="italic">The Keepsake</span> for 1837 published his poem &#145;St Agnes&#146; (afterwards republished under the title of &#145;St Agnes' Eve&#146; in the volume of 1842). Lady Emmeline's associates also included the countess of Blessington, Theodore Hook, Richard Monckton Milnes, Caroline Norton, and Mary Shelley.<br>
<br>From the time of Lady Emmeline's European wedding journey, she became an avid traveller, at first touring for enjoyment, but with her itineraries becoming &#145;increasingly punishing&#146;  (Robinson, 122) after the death of her husband and youngest son in 1844. In 1849&#150;50 she visited America with her daughter and published <span class="italic">Travels in the United States</span> (1851) and <span class="italic">Sketches of Travel in America</span> (1853). During this trip, she and the twelve-year-old Victoria did not confine themselves to the eastern United States, but made their way to Mexico, across Panama, and into Peru. Victoria also wrote about these travels: <span class="italic">A Young Traveller's Journal of a Tour in North and South America During the Year 1850</span> was published in 1852. Lady Emmeline's last published book of travel, <span class="italic">A Visit to Portugal and Madeira</span>, appeared in 1854, although an account of a journey to Spain, <span class="italic">The Sweet South</span>, was printed posthumously for private circulation in 1856. Although Lady Emmeline was a fairly prolific poet and travel writer the quality of her work is not generally of a high standard.<br>
<br>While riding in the neighbourhood of Jerusalem on 1 May 1855 Lady Emmeline was kicked by a mule and fractured her leg. She was not in good health at the time, yet persisted in journeying without a guide from Beirut to Aleppo, and returned by an unfrequented road across Lebanon. Afflicted with dysentery and sunstroke, she died at Beirut on 30 October 1855.<br>
<br>Elizabeth Lee<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Gill Gregory </div>
