<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Walsh,  Antoine Vincent</span>, Jacobite first Earl Walsh  (<em>bap. </em>1703, <em>d.</em> 1763), <span class="occ">Jacobite naval officer</span>, was baptized on 22 January 1703 <span class="smallcap">NS</span> at St Malo in France, the third son of Philip Walsh (<i>bap</i>. 1666, <i>d</i>. 1708), a Waterford merchant who settled at St Malo about 1685, and his wife, Anne Whyte (<i>d</i>. 1727). On 10 January 1741 <span class="smallcap">NS</span> he married Marie, daughter of Luke O'Shiell of Nantes, and they had two sons, including Antoine Jean Baptiste (1745&#150;1798), and a daughter, Marie. After serving in the French navy Walsh became a merchant and made a fortune from the Nantes slave trade. He took a leading role in the Irish &eacute;migr&eacute; community's planning and financing of Charles Edward Stuart's expedition to Scotland, which resulted in the 1745 Jacobite rising, and was &#145;a key figure throughout the naval side of the '45&#146;  (Gibson, 11). In their correspondence Walsh adopted the pseudonym Monsieur Le Grand and the young prince was Mr Douglas; they also had face-to-face discussions at Navarre in April 1745.<br>
<br>Accompanied by Walsh, Charles Edward left St Nazaire on 3 July 1745 <span class="smallcap">NS</span> in one of Walsh's vessels, the <i>Du Teillay</i> (18 guns, Captain Claude Durb&eacute;). On 20 July <span class="smallcap">NS</span> the warship <i>Elisabeth</i> (64 guns), hired from the French navy as a privateer to accompany them, encountered the British <i>Lyon</i> (58 guns) off the Lizard; the <i>Elisabeth</i> was badly damaged and had to return to port, and the <i>Du Teillay</i> continued alone, without the arms and Irish volunteers on the warship, landing Charles Edward at Lochailort in Loch-nan-Uamh in South Morar on 5 August <span class="smallcap">NS</span>, and departing on 17 August <span class="smallcap">NS</span>.<br>
<br>On his return to France, Walsh urged Louis XV to send assistance to Charles Edward, and was put in charge of assembling coastal craft in the channel ports for an invasion of England. When the invasion plans were abandoned he sent two of his own Nantes privateers, the <i>Mars</i> (36 guns) and the <i>Bellone</i> (32 guns), loaded with arms and 852,000 livres, to bring the prince home if necessary; but they were attacked by three British ships in Loch-nan-Uamh after landing the supplies on 3 May 1746 <span class="smallcap">NS</span>, and nobody could stay to search for Charles Edward.<br>
<br>After the battle of Culloden, Walsh sent detailed proposals for rescuing the prince to Maurepas, the minister of marine, some of which advice was acted upon, but following the failure of the rising he had fallen out of favour with the French court. He was, however, ennobled by Louis XV in December 1753, having been created Earl Walsh by James III (James Francis Edward Stuart) in October 1745, and remained a trusted friend of Charles Edward, acting as his intermediary with Earl Marischal in their dispute in 1754. He died at Cap Fran&ccedil;ais, San Domingo (Haiti), on 2 March 1763. On his death the Jacobite peerage passed to his second and only surviving son, Antoine Jean Baptiste.<br>
<br>Randolph Cock </div>
