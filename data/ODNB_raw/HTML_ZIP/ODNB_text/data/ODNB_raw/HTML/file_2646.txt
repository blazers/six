<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Blicke, Sir  Charles</span>  (1745&#150;1815), <span class="occ">surgeon</span>, received his medical education at St Bartholomew's Hospital, London, where he was elected assistant surgeon in 1779; he succeeded Percivall Pott as surgeon 17 July 1787. Blicke was one of the court of assistants at Surgeons' Hall. In 1803 he became master of the Royal College of Surgeons; he was knighted in the same year.<br>
<br>In 1772, while living in Old Jewry, Blicke published his only work, <span class="italic">An essay on the biliou or yellow fever of Jamaica, collected from the manuscript of a late surgeon</span>. In the preface Blicke states that he has abridged the original work and simplified its style. The essayist, whose name is not preserved, advocates the treatment of the fever by bleeding, purging, warm baths, fresh air, and acid drinks. Some twenty authors are quoted to little purpose, and the only interesting contents of the composition are a few lines on the sufferings of the Carthagena expedition, in which the original writer had served, and the mention of the fact that the water of the Bristol hot wells was exported to Jamaica. Whatever he may have cut out, Blicke certainly added nothing. The essay was translated into Italian.<br>
<br>In 1779 Blicke, then living in Mildred Court, received John Abernethy as his apprentice in surgery.<blockquote>The tone in which he [Abernethy] usually spoke of Sir Charles's practice did not convey a very favourable idea of the nature of the impression which it had left on him. In relating a case he would say: &#145;Sir Charles was at his house in the country, where he was always on the look out for patients.&#146; (Macilwain, 42)</blockquote>Blicke died on 30 December 1815 in Bedford Place, Russell Square, London.<br>
<br>Norman Moore<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Michael Bevan </div>
