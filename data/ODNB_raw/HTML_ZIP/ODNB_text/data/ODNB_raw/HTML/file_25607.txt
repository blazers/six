<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Simson,  Thomas</span>  (1696&#150;1764), <span class="occ">physician</span>, was born at Kirktonhall in the parish of West Kilbride, Ayrshire, the third son of John Simson (<i>d</i>. 1731), a merchant, of Kirktonhall, and his wife, Agnes, daughter of Patrick Simson, minister of Renfrew. The mathematician <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/25606/', "Robert Simson")</script> was his brother, and the theologian John Simson was his uncle.<br>
<br>In 1721 James Brydges, duke of Chandos, established a medical professorship at the University of St Andrews, and on 10 January 1722 Simson, who had been awarded an MD by Glasgow University in 1720, was admitted as its first incumbent. He was an able and efficient professor who, unusually for the time, taught in the vernacular.<br>
<br>About 1724 Simson married a daughter of Sir John Preston of Prestonhall, Fife, who was deprived of his estates in 1715. They had four sons and two daughters; their son James was professor of medicine at St Andrews in succession to his father from 1764 to 1770.<br>
<br>In 1744 Simson was elected an honorary fellow of the Royal College of Physicians of Edinburgh, and in 1756 he produced <span class="italic">Regulations for the Degree in Medicine</span> at St Andrews, but his recommendations were not acted upon. He held the chair of medicine until his death in St Andrews on 30 April 1764. Simson was the author of: <span class="italic">De re medica</span> (1726); <span class="italic">De erroribus circa materiam medicam</span> (1726); <span class="italic">System of the Womb</span> (1729); and <span class="italic">Enquiry on the Vital and Animal Actions</span> (1752).<br>
<br>E. I. Carlyle<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Kaye Bagshaw </div>
