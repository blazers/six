<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Sinclair,  George</span>  (1786&#150;1834), <span class="occ">gardener and writer on horticulture</span>, was born at Mellerstain in Berwickshire into a Scottish family which had long been devoted to gardening. His father, George Sinclair (1750&#150;1833), gardener to the Hon. G. Baillie of Jerviswood, was in his earlier years considered to be one of the best horticulturists in the south of Scotland, and his uncle was superintendent of the grounds, gardens, and farms at Bonnington, near Lanark. Sinclair was himself originally in the service of the Gordon family, and became, on the duke's marriage with Lady Georgiana Gordon in 1803, gardener to John Russell, sixth duke of Bedford, at Woburn Abbey.<br>
<br>On instructions from the duke, and under the direction of Sir Humphry Davy, Sinclair conducted an extensive series of experiments on the nutritive qualities of various types of animal fodder. The results were published in 1816 in <span class="italic">Hortus gramineus Woburnensis</span>, an expensive folio volume containing dried specimens of the grasses. These were replaced by plates in cheaper editions published in 1825, 1826, and 1829, and in a German translation of 1826. The nutritional value of the grasses described in <span class="italic">Hortus</span> was assessed through comparison of their water-soluble constituents, the theory being that these formed the bulk of the feeding material. As a comparative guide the technique did have some value.<br>
<br>After having superintended the gardens at Woburn Abbey for seventeen years, Sinclair left the service of the duke and entered into partnership about 1824 with Cormack &amp; Son, nurserymen and seedsmen of New Cross. On 26 March 1824 he became a fellow of the Linnean Society, and he was also a fellow of the Horticultural Society and of other botanical organizations. He remained a partner of the firm of seedsmen for some nine or ten years, until his death at New Cross Nursery, Deptford, on 13 March 1834. In addition to producing the <span class="italic">Hortus gramineus</span> and a subsequent <span class="italic">Hortus ericaeus Woburnensis</span> (1825), Sinclair edited the <span class="italic">Hortus Cantabrigiensis</span> of James Donn, the <span class="italic">Essay on Weeds</span> of Benjamin Holdich (1825), and a <span class="italic">Treatise on Useful and Ornamental Planting</span>, published by the Society for the Diffusion of Useful Knowledge.<br>
<br>Ernest Clarke<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Alexander Goldbloom </div>
