<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Stark,  William</span>  (1740&#150;1770), <span class="occ">physician</span>, born in Birmingham in July 1740, was the eldest son of Thomas Stark, a merchant of Manchester. He studied philosophy at Glasgow University, where he graduated MA in 1758, and then proceeded to Edinburgh, where he became friends with William Cullen. He moved to London in 1765, and concentrated on the study of medicine, entering as a pupil at St George's Hospital. He studied anatomy under John Hunter (1728&#150;1773), and undertook experiments on blood and other animal fluids. On 2 September 1766 he graduated MD at Leiden, publishing his thesis, <span class="italic">Specimen medicum inaugurale septem historias et dissectiones dysentericorum exhibens</span> (1766).<br>
<br>In June 1769 Stark began a series of experiments on diet, in which he was greatly encouraged by Sir John Pringle. The keenness with which Stark tried these experiments on himself ruined his health, and he died on 23 February 1770. <span class="italic">The works of the late William Stark &hellip; consisting of clinical and anatomical observations, with experiments dietetical and statical</span> (1788) were edited by his friend James Carmichael Smyth. Stark recorded his diet, together with any weight loss or gain and his mood. This final experiment involved a diet of honey and flour, which he lived on for several days, until his death.<br>
<br>E. I. Carlyle<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Kaye Bagshaw </div>
