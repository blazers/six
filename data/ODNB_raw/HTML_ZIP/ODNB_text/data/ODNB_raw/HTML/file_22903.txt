<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Purnell,  Thomas</span> <span class="st"> [<em>pseud. </em>Q]</span>  (1834&#150;1889), <span class="occ">theatre critic and writer</span>, was born in Tenby, Pembrokeshire, the son of Robert Purnell and his wife, Ann, and was baptized on 13 March 1834 at Narberth, Pembrokeshire. He matriculated at Trinity College, Dublin, in 1852 and afterwards went to London and began a career in journalism. In 1862 he was, on the recommendation of Thomas Duffus Hardy, appointed assistant secretary and librarian of the Archaeological Institute of Great Britain and Ireland, and retained the post until 1866. In 1870&#150;71 he contributed to <span class="italic">The Athenaeum</span>, under the signature Q, a series of severe and incisive dramatic criticisms to which Charles Reade and Tom Taylor published aggrieved replies.<br>
<br>Purnell was popular in literary society and founded a club known as the Decemviri, of which A. C. Swinburne, J. A. M. Whistler, R. E. Francillon, and Joseph Knight were members. He also came to know Giuseppe Mazzini, to whom he introduced Swinburne and others. In 1871 he edited Lamb's <span class="italic">Correspondence and Works</span> and organized the Charles Lamb centenary dinner. Purnell's collected <span class="italic">Athenaeum</span> theatre reviews were reprinted in <span class="italic">Dramatists of the Present Day</span> (1871). He also wrote literary essays and <span class="italic">The Lady Drusilla: a Psychological Romance</span> (1886), and edited (1868) Dr John Herd's <span class="italic">Historia quatuor regum Angliae</span> for the Roxburghe Club.<br>
<br>After a long illness Thomas Purnell died on 17 December 1889 at Lloyd Square, Pentonville, London, where his sister kept house for him.<br>
<br>Megan A. Stephan </div>
