<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Davis,  Henry Edwards</span>  (1756&#150;1784), <span class="occ">historian</span>, was the son of John Davis of Windsor. He was born on 11 July 1756, and educated at Ealing. On 17 May 1774 he entered Balliol College, Oxford, and graduated BA early in 1778. On 2 May 1778 he published <span class="italic">An Examination of the Fifteenth and Sixteenth Chapters of Mr Gibbon's &#145;History&#146;</span>, in which the historian's alleged plagiarisms and other misdemeanours with evidence were set out at length. Edward Gibbon affected to despise Davis, concluding in his <span class="italic">Memoirs</span> that &#145;victory over such antagonists was a sufficient humiliation&#146;  (<span class="italic">Miscellaneous Works</span>, 1.153). Nevertheless, although Davis made some embarrassing blunders when censuring Gibbon's use of classical authors, he was altogether more acute and sure-footed when analysing Gibbon's debts to moderns such as Middleton and Beausobre. In his <span class="italic">Vindication</span> (1779) Gibbon concentrated on Davis's weaker side and largely passed over his shrewder perceptions. It was a legitimate enough procedure, but it perhaps explains the complicated tone (half chastened, half defiant) of Davis's <span class="italic">Reply to Mr Gibbon's &#145;Vindication&#146;</span> (1779). Gibbon states that Davis was rewarded for the attack by a &#145;royal pension&#146;. He took priest's orders in 1780, and became fellow and tutor of Balliol College. His health broke down and he died, after a lingering illness, on 10 February 1784. His friends praised &#145;the cheerfulness and vivacity of his conversation [and] the warmth and benevolence of his heart&#146;  (Chalmers, 11.325); he is said to have been very amiable, poetical, and patient under suffering.<br>
<br>Leslie Stephen<span class="italic">, rev. </span> David Womersley </div>
