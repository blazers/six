<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lochore,  Robert</span>  (1762&#150;1852), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was born at Strathaven, Lanarkshire, on 7 July 1762. He became a shoemaker at the age of thirteen, and ultimately he conducted a successful business of his own in Glasgow. On 7 June 1786 he married in Paisley Isobel Browning, a native of Ayrshire. Lochore's local interests and his literary tastes brought him into contact with Robert Burns, who became a close friend. About 1797 he published an elegiac pastoral on Burns's death entitled <span class="italic">Patie and Ralph</span>; in later life he often told of having seen Burns reproved on the cutty-stool by the Revd Auld. Generous and philanthropic, Lochore founded the Glasgow Annuity Society and assisted other institutions for the public good.<br>
<br>Lochore wrote verses throughout his life, and he composed in his eighty-eighth year his spirited &#145;Last Speech of the Auld Brig of Glasgow on being Condemned to be Taken Down&#146;. In 1795&#150;6 he published <span class="italic">Willie's Vision</span> and <span class="italic">The Foppish Taylor</span>, and about 1815 he published anonymously <span class="italic">Tales in Rhyme and Minor Pieces, in the Scottish Dialect</span>. His song, &#145;Noo, Jenny, lass, my bonny bird&#146;, has been attributed to Burns. He used the vernacular dexterously, and his poems are valuable illustrations of Scottish life and character. For a time, about 1817, he edited the <span class="italic">Kilmarnock Mirror</span> for his son. Lochore died at Glasgow on 27 April 1852, leaving unpublished an autobiography and various Scottish tales and poems.<br>
<br>T. W. Bayne<span class="italic">, rev. </span> James How </div>
