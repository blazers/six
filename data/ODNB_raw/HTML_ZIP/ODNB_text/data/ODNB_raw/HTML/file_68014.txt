<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Tasburgh <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Kitson]</span>,  Dorothy</span> <span class="st"> [<em>other married name </em>Dorothy Pakington, Lady Pakington]</span>  (1531&#150;1577), <span class="occ">landowner</span>, was born in London, one of four daughters of the wealthy London mercer and sheriff <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/15833/', "Sir Thomas Kitson (1485&#150;1540)")</script> and his second wife, Margaret Donnington of Stoke Newington, Middlesex. In November 1540 Dorothy's widowed mother married Sir Richard Long (<i>d</i>. 1546), a gentleman of the privy chamber, and in 1548 she took as her third husband John Bourchier, second earl of Bath. On 20 September 1546, only days before her stepfather's death, Dorothy was granted a dispensation to marry Sir Thomas Pakington (<i>c</i>.1530&#150;1571) of Hampton, Worcestershire, son of the evangelical London merchant Robert Pakington (who was murdered in 1536). Thomas and Dorothy had three sons and three daughters; their eldest son, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/21145/', "Sir John Pakington (1549&#150;1625)")</script>, known as Lusty Pakington, was a courtier and favourite of Elizabeth I. Following Thomas Pakington's death in June 1571, Dorothy remained a widow for at least a year before taking as her second husband the young Buckinghamshire lawyer and future exchequer teller Thomas Tasburgh (<i>c</i>.1554&#150;1602) of Hawridge.<br>
<br>Dorothy's celebrity derives from her role as a parliamentary patron. Shortly before their marriage Dorothy's first husband, Thomas Pakington, had inherited the lordship of Aylesbury in Buckinghamshire from Chief Justice John Baldwin, his maternal grandfather and guardian, and in 1554 Aylesbury was incorporated as a borough under the control of the Pakington family, who usually returned their friends as MPs. As a widow Dorothy Pakington exercised the full powers of lordship herself, including returning the writ for the 1572 parliament. On 4 May 1572 she nominated Thomas Lichfield and George Burden as the borough's two MPs; Lichfield, a family friend, was to marry Dorothy's eldest daughter, Margaret, the following year. Although Lady Pakington's 1572 return was not unique, returns of MPs by widows were extremely rare in early modern England. They presented the authorities with a dilemma, for in 1586 the privy council expressed concern at a woman nominating burgesses, yet at the same time there was a powerful reluctance to restrict any property rights, even when exercised by widows. With her husband's consent, Dorothy made her will on 30 April 1577, requesting burial in the parish church at Hawridge, Buckinghamshire. The will, which has a protestant preamble, was proved on 21 June 1577.<br>
<br>P. R. N. Carter </div>
