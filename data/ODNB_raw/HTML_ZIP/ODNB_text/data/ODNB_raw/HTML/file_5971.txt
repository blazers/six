<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Collyer <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Mitchell]</span>,  Mary</span>  (1716/17&#150;1762), <span class="occ">translator and novelist</span>, is of unknown parentage, nor has her birthplace been identified. Although her work demonstrates a good knowledge of languages and literature, there is no record of her early life or education, with the exception of one comment by Elizabeth Carter, saying she had been &#145;a little acquainted with her before she was married&#146;  (<span class="italic">Letters</span>, 1.151). She married <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/5969/', "Joseph Collyer the elder (1714/15&#150;1776)")</script>, a writer and bookseller who had a shop in Plough Court and a house in Islington; their son, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/5970/', "Joseph Collyer the younger")</script> [<i>see under </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/65008/', "Boydell, John, engravers")</script>], born in 1748, engraved illustrations for one edition of her extremely popular <span class="italic">Death of Abel</span> (1761), a translation from Salomon Gessner. In dedicating this work to the queen, Collyer states: &#145;It is in order to contribute to the support and education of my children, I have taken up the pen&#146;  (Gessner, sig. A2). There is evidence, however, for only the one child, Joseph.<br>
<br>Collyer published <span class="italic">The Virtuous Orphan</span>, her translation and completion of Marivaux's <span class="italic">La vie de Marianne</span>, in instalments in the <span class="italic">London Evening-Post</span> during 1742, and in a single volume in 1743. McBurney and Shugrue describe this as &#145;the most popular translation during the eighteenth century, the period of Marivaux's greatest influence upon the English novel&#146;  (<span class="italic">Virtuous Orphan</span>, vi). <span class="italic">Felicia to Charlotte</span> (2 vols., 1744&#150;9), Collyer's original novel, in which she introduces a hero who is both a man of feeling and a man of reason, was also successful.<br>
<br>Collyer also published <span class="italic">Memoirs of the Countess de Bressol &hellip; from the French</span> (2 vols., 1743) and <span class="italic">The Christmas Box</span> (1748&#150;9). Her husband completed her final work, a translation of Klopstock's <span class="italic">Messiah</span> (2 vols., 1763); and in a preface, he states that his wife's death followed &#145;a lingering illness occasion'd by the agitations of mind she suffer'd in writing&#146; <span class="italic">Death of Abel</span> and the first part of <span class="italic">The Messiah</span>. She died on 31 December 1762 and was buried at St Mary's, Islington, on 5 January 1763.<br>
<br>Joyce Fullard </div>
