<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Paxton,  Peter</span>  (<em>d.</em> <em>c.</em>1711), <span class="occ">physician and writer on politics</span>, was admitted to the degree of MD <i>per literas regias</i> at Pembroke College, Cambridge, in 1687. His name does not appear in the admission-book of Pembroke College, and he may have come from Oxford for an <i>ad eundem</i> degree. In 1704 he was known to be living in Beaufort Street, London. Apart from his writings, nothing more is known of Paxton's life. In 1701 he published <span class="italic">An Essay Concerning the Body of Man</span>, which traced all diseases to the fluids in the body. This was followed by <span class="italic">The Grounds of Physick Examined</span> in 1703. Paxton's next three works were on political topics. <span class="italic">Civil Polity</span> appeared in 1703, <span class="italic">A Discourse Concerning the Nature, Advantage, and Improvement of Trade</span> in 1704, and <span class="italic">A Scheme for Union between England and Scotland</span> in 1705. Paxton went on to produce two more medical works: <span class="italic">A Directory Physico-Medical</span> (1707) and <span class="italic">Specimen physico-medicum</span>, which was published posthumously in 1711.<br>
<br>W. A. Shaw<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Michael Bevan </div>
