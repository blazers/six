<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Pentland,  Joseph Barclay</span>  (1797&#150;1873), <span class="occ">naturalist and traveller</span>, was born in Ballybofey, co. Donegal, Ireland, on 17 January 1797. Nothing is known of his early life save that he was educated at the Royal School, Armagh, and at the University of Paris. He became associated with Cuvier, vividly reporting day-to-day events in the Jardin des Plantes in a long series of letters to William Buckland and making percipient observations on the osteology of fossil marine reptiles (ichthyosaurs and plesiosaurs), which were to be drawn upon in W. D. Conybeare's descriptions. In addition to his work for Cuvier, Pentland studied fossil bones from India and Australia and collected them in Italy, an extinct pygmy hippopotamus from Sicily being named <span class="italic">Hippopotamus pentlandi</span> after him.<br>
<br>Appointed secretary to the British consul-generalship in Lima (1826&#150;8), Pentland undertook extensive explorations in newly independent Bolivia. Using the most up-to-date barometers, he determined the heights of many Andean peaks, including Gualtieri (22,000 ft), Arequipa (later El Misti, 18,300 ft), Illimani (21,300 ft), and Chirquibamba (21,000 ft); the measurements, though since corrected, were of the right order of magnitude. Pentland recognized that most of these mountains were volcanoes, but also discovered Palaeozoic fossils at high altitudes (14,000 to 17,000 ft). He examined the antiquities of Cuzco and visited Lake Titicaca, perceiving that the Rio Desaguadero, hitherto thought to flow into the lake, was in fact its outlet. His <span class="italic">Report on Bolivia</span> to the Foreign Office, though the first substantial account of that country's geography, was not published until 1974, edited by J. V. Fifer  (<span class="italic">Camden Miscellany</span>, 25).<br>
<br>After returning to Europe and preparing the posthumous catalogue of Cuvier's collection, Pentland was appointed consul-general in La Paz to the short-lived Peru&#150;Bolivia Confederation (1836&#150;9). He undertook further explorations, preparing a first map of Lake Titicaca (close to which he found fossil &#145;mastodon&#146; bones) and disinterring human skulls from nearby tombs. He collected many plant and animal specimens, at least three plant species and a bird (the Andean tinamou) being consequently named after him.<br>
<br>Following his second return to Europe, Pentland remained interested in South America, supporting the work of explorers John Bowring and Hugh Weddell, encouraging the growing of Andean plants in Kew Gardens and the Jardin des Plantes and, in particular, being instrumental in arranging for the introduction of <i>Cinchona</i>&#151;the quinine plant&#151;to the West Indies. He helped Mary Somerville in the preparation for publication of maps of Peru and Bolivia for her <span class="italic">Physical Geography</span> (1848). A hitherto undescribed nickel&#150;iron mineral he had obtained from Craigmure, Argyll, came to be named &#145;pentlandite&#146;.<br>
<br>Pentland never married. From 1845 he made Rome his home, becoming so well acquainted with the antiquities of Italy that he edited for John Murray three editions of <span class="italic">A Handbook of Rome and its Environs</span> (1860, 1871, and 1872); <span class="italic">A Handbook for Travellers in Southern Italy</span> (6th edn, 1868); and <span class="italic">A Handbook for Travellers in Northern Italy</span> (11th edn, 1869). During two visits by the prince of Wales to Rome, Pentland acted as his cicerone. He died at his home, 3 Motcomb Street, Belgrave Square, London, on 12 July 1873 and was buried in Brompton cemetery.<br>
<br>William A. S. Sarjeant </div>
