<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Kirke,  John</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1629&#150;1643), <span class="occ">actor and playwright</span>, first appears in a patent for the travelling Red Bull company at Reading in 1629. In 1634 he was a member of the same company in London (now playing at the Fortune) and the following year he became a groom of the chamber as a member of Prince Charles's men, again at the Red Bull. Kirke and his wife, Elizabeth, lived in the parish of St James's, Clerkenwell, Middlesex, where they had three children between 1633 and 1643 (Marie, Parry, and John), all of whom died in infancy.<br>
<br>Kirke was author of a popular tragicomedy, <span class="italic">The Seven Champions of Christendome</span>, licensed for the press on 13 July 1638 at the same time as <span class="italic">The Life and Death of Jack Straw and Watt Tyler by John Kirke</span>, of which nothing is known. <span class="italic">The Seven Champions</span> was printed the same year, with a title-page declaring that it had been &#145;Acted at the Cocke-pit, and at the Red-Bull in St. Johns Streete, with a generall liking&#146;. The dedication is addressed to the author's &#145;much respected and worthy friend, Master John Waite&#146;, possibly the travelling actor of the same name. The play is based on Richard Johnson's very popular chivalric romance of the same name, also showing the influence of Shakespeare's <span class="italic">Tempest</span> and <span class="italic">Winter's Tale</span>. In June 1642 Kirke paid master of the revels Sir Henry Herbert for two plays, one of which Herbert burnt &#145;for the ribaldry and offense that was in it&#146;, but he was not necessarily the author  (Lawrence, 591). Kirke was author of the dedication to Sir Kenelm Digby prefixed to Shirley's <span class="italic">Martyred Soldier</span> (1638) and he may have been the subject of Thomas Jordan's <span class="italic">Epitaph on my Worthy Friend, Mr. John Kirk</span>, first printed in 1643.<br>
<br>David Kathman </div>
