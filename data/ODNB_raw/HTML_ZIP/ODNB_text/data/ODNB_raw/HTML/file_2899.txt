<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Boothby,  Hill</span>  (1708&#150;1756), <span class="occ">friend of Samuel Johnson</span>, was born on 27 October 1708 at Ashbourne, Derbyshire, the only daughter of Brooke Boothby (<i>d</i>. 1727) of Ashbourne Hall, Derbyshire, and his second wife, Elizabeth Fitzherbert, daughter of John Fitzherbert of Somersall-Herbert, Derbyshire. A woman of considerable ability, she made the acquaintance of Samuel Johnson in 1739 when he stayed in Ashbourne with John Taylor. She became reacquainted with Johnson in 1753, a year after his wife died. In March 1753 Miss Boothby's close friend Mary Meynell, wife of William Fitzherbert, died. As her executor Miss Boothby undertook the management of Fitzherbert's household and six children. On Easter Sunday 1753 Johnson wrote in his diary that he would &#145;try on Monday to seek a new wife&#146;. Scholars believe the woman he had in mind was almost certainly Hill Boothby, but her duties in the Fitzherbert household prevented any serious thoughts of marriage. They did, however, correspond regularly. Johnson addresses her as &#145;my sweet angel&#146; and &#145;dearest dearest madam&#146;, and writes, &#145;You know Des Cartes's argument, &#147;I think therefore I am&#148; It is as good a consequence &hellip; &#147;I am alive therefore I love Miss Boothby&#148;&#146;  (<span class="italic">Letters of Samuel Johnson</span>, 1.118). When she was near death Johnson wrote: &#145;I love you and honour you, and am very unwilling to lose you&#146;. In the same letter he included a remedy for &#145;indigestion and lubricity of the bowels&#146;  (ibid., 1.120&#150;21), dried orange peel taken with wine, thus disclosing the mystery of the orange peel, which Boswell asked for in vain  (Boswell, <span class="italic">Life</span>, 1 April 1775). When Miss Boothby died on 16 January 1756 Johnson &#145;was almost distracted with his grief&#146;  (Piozzi, 114). Her letters (thirty-three are extant) were collected and published by Richard Wright, of Lichfield, in 1805, in <span class="italic">An Account of the Life of Dr. Samuel Johnson</span>.<br>
<br>Kathryn M. Burton </div>
