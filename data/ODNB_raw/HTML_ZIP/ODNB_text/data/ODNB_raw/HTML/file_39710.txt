<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Jorden,  William</span>  (1685&#150;1739), <span class="occ">college teacher</span>, was born and baptized on 19 November 1685 at Newland, Gloucestershire; his mother, Catherine Jorden (<i>d</i>. 1685), died in childbirth. His father, the Revd Humphrey Jorden (<i>b</i>. 1651/2, <i>d</i>. in or after 1715), lecturer of Newland, was of a Staffordshire family. Jorden matriculated from Corpus Christi College, Oxford, on 28 March 1702, but migrated to his father's old college, Pembroke, where he was founder's kin, and graduated BA in 1705, before proceeding MA in 1708, and BD in 1728. A fellow of the college, he served as bursar, chaplain, and vicegerent, but he is now remembered only as Samuel Johnson's tutor. Johnson disparaged Jorden's scholarship and absented himself from lectures; it was to Jorden that Johnson admitted he had been sliding in Christ Church meadow instead of attending his lectures. Johnson none the less loved and respected the man. &#145;Whenever (said he) a young man becomes Jorden's pupil, he becomes his son&#146;  (Boswell, <span class="italic">Life</span>, 1.61). Johnson's first published poem, a Latin version of Pope's <span class="italic">Messiah</span>, was written as an exercise for Jorden in the Christmas vacation of 1728.<br>
<br>On 14 November 1729 Jorden was presented to the rectory of Standon, Staffordshire, by William Vyse (1709&#150;1770), his pupil and Johnson's friend. He resigned Standon on 18 October 1733, having been instituted to the vicarage of nearby Seighford on 1 December 1731. He died, unmarried, at Seighford in January 1739 and was buried there on 17 January that year.<br>
<br>James Sambrook </div>
