<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Arundell,  Thomas</span>, first Baron Arundell of Wardour  (<em>c.</em>1560&#150;1639), <span class="occ">army officer</span>, was the son and heir of Sir Matthew Arundell (1532&times;4&#150;1598) of Wardour Castle, Wiltshire, and his wife, Margaret, daughter of Sir Henry Willoughby of Wollaton, Nottinghamshire, and his wife, Anne. His father inherited extensive former monastic lands, serving as high sheriff, custos-rotulorum, and deputy lieutenant of Dorset. His mother was a courtier to Elizabeth I.<br>
<br>Imprisoned in 1580 for his Catholic zeal, Arundell returned to favour donating &pound;100 towards defeating the Armada. On 18 June 1585 at St Andrew's, Holborn, he married Mary (<i>c</i>.1567&#150;1607), daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/30072/', "Henry Wriothesley, second earl of Southampton")</script>, and his wife, Mary, daughter of Anthony Browne, first Viscount Montague. Elizabeth I allegedly recommended him to the emperor, and in 1595 Sir Robert Cecil arranged for him to fight the Turks in Hungary. On 7 September 1595 he stormed the breach at Gran, replacing the Turkish crescent with the imperial eagle. Known as &#145;the Valiant&#146;, he was created an imperial count by Rudolf II on 14 December 1595. Elizabeth disapproved of his imperial title, and on his return committed Arundell to the Fleet prison, commenting &#145;I would not have a sheep branded with another man's mark&#146;  (Morey, 129).<br>
<br>James I created Arundell Baron Arundell of Wardour on 4 May 1605 and appointed him colonel of the English regiment serving the archduke in Spanish Flanders, but Arundell disobeyed royal orders and sailed to Flanders in September 1605. Named by Guy Fawkes under torture, he was suspected of complicity in the Gunpowder Plot. On 1 July 1608, at St Andrew's, Holborn, he married Anne Philipson (<i>d</i>. 1637), daughter of Miles Philipson of Crook, Westmorland, and his wife, Barbara, sister of Francis Sandys of Conishead, Lancashire. During the 1630s he engaged in acrimonious religious disputes with the bishop of Durham. After attempting to sell Wardour to the king in 1637 he died there on 7 November 1639, and was buried at Tisbury, Wiltshire.<br>
<br>Arundell's eldest son was <a name="cosubject_727"></a><span class="headword">Thomas Arundell</span>, second Baron Arundell of Wardour  (<em>c.</em>1586&#150;1643), <span class="occ">royalist army officer</span>. On 11 May 1607 he married Lady Blanche Somerset (1583/4&#150;1649) [<i>see </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/714/', "Arundell, Lady Blanche")</script>], the sixth daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/26005/', "Edward Somerset, fourth earl of Worcester")</script>, and his wife, Elizabeth, fourth daughter of Francis Hastings, second earl of Huntingdon. A committed royalist (the House of Commons issued orders for his arrest in November 1641), Thomas Arundell was described as &#145;a great Defender of the Romish Faith&#146;  (Newman, 6). His wife became famous for her spirited defence of Wardour Castle against the parliamentarians during his absence in May 1643. Arundell was mortally wounded at the battle of Stratton on 16 May 1643. He died in Oxford on 19 May and was buried at Tisbury. He was succeeded by his eldest son, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/716/', "Henry Arundell, third Baron Arundell of Wardour")</script>.<br>
<br>Andrew J. Hopper </div>
