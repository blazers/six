<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Thompson,  William</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1712, <em>d.</em> <em>c.</em>1766), <span class="occ">poet</span>, baptized at Brough in Westmorland on 1 January 1712, was the second son of Francis Thompson (1665&#150;1735), vicar of Brough, and his wife, Isabel (<i>d</i>. 1737), the widow of Joseph Fisher, archdeacon of Carlisle. William was educated at Appleby and then matriculated from Queen's College, Oxford, on 26 March 1731, graduating BA in 1735 and MA on 26 February 1739. He was elected a fellow of his college, and succeeded to the rectory of Hampton Poyle with South Weston in Oxfordshire.<br>
<br>While still an undergraduate, in 1734, Thompson wrote &#145;Stella, sive, Amores, tres libri&#146; and two years later &#145;Six Pastorals&#146;, but considered neither production worthy of publication. In 1745, while at Hampton Poyle, he published <span class="italic">Sickness, a Poem</span>, in which he paid a tribute to the memory of Pope and Swift, both recently dead.<br>
<br>In 1751 Thompson was an unsuccessful candidate for the Oxford professorship of poetry against William Hawkins (1722&#150;1801), and in the same year published <span class="italic">Gondibert and Bertha</span>, a tragedy, the subject of which was taken from Davenant's poem <span class="italic">Gondibert</span>. On the presentation to the University of the Pomfret statues, in 1756, he wrote <span class="italic">Gratitude</span>, a poem in honour of the donor, Henrietta Louisa Fermor, countess dowager of Pomfret.<br>
<br>In 1758 Thompson published <span class="italic">Poems on Several Occasions</span> in which he demonstrated his enthusiasm for the &#145;ancient English poets, in whose history and writings he was critically skilled&#146;  (Chalmers, 5). A noted imitator of Spenser, his poems &#145;Hymn to May&#146;, &#145;Nativity&#146;, and <span class="italic">Sickness</span> were once highly esteemed. He died about 1766, probably at South Weston, and his library was sold by Thomas Davies (1712?&#150;1785) in 1768.<br>
<br>Thompson also superintended an edition of Joseph Hall's <span class="italic">Virgidemiarum</span> (1753), and at his death he left manuscript notes and observations on William Browne's <span class="italic">Works</span>, which were revised and published by Thomas Davies in his edition of Browne's <span class="italic">Works</span> (1772). Earlier biographical studies have confused William Thompson with Anthony Thompson, dean of Raphoe, who died on 9 October 1756  (H. Cotton, <span class="italic">Fasti ecclesiae Hibernicae</span>, 1860, 5.265).<br>
<br>E. I. Carlyle<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Jeffrey Herrle </div>
