<div class="para">
<span class="headword">O'Meara,  Kathleen</span> <span class="st"> [<em>pseud. </em>Grace Ramsay]</span>  (1839&#150;1888), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was born in Dublin, the eldest daughter of Dennis O'Meara of Tipperary. Shortly after her birth her parents moved to Paris and she did not return to Ireland again. Her mother (<i>d</i>. 1887) received a pension under the second empire because she was directly related to Barry Edward O'Meara, Napoleon's physician in his last years.<br>
<br>O'Meara wrote for a period under the pseudonym of Grace Ramsay. Her English publishers suggested the <i>nom de plume</i> believing, as she noted, that &#145;the Irish and Catholic ring of my real name would indispose the liberal British public&#146;  (<span class="italic">Irish Monthly</span>, 531). She wrote stridently Catholic books and her first published novel was <span class="italic">A Woman's Trials</span> (1867), in which the central incident is the conversion of an English girl to Catholicism. <span class="italic">The Battle of Connemara</span> (1878), set in Paris and the west of Ireland, features a similar theme. Lady Peggy Blake, an Englishwoman, marries an Irish protestant landlord and settles in Connemara. So inspired is she by her tenants' faith and their acceptance of their lot, that she converts to Catholicism. Other novels include <span class="italic">Are you my Wife?</span> (1878), <span class="italic">The Old House in Picardy</span> (1887), and <span class="italic">Narka, a Story of Russian Life</span> (1888).<br>
<br>O'Meara also wrote a number of biographies such as <span class="italic">Life and Times of Thomas Grant, First Bishop of Southwark</span> (1874), <span class="italic">Frederick Ozanam, Professor at the Sorbonne, his Life and Works</span> (1876), <span class="italic">One of God's Heroines: a Biographical Sketch of Mother Mary Teresa Kelly</span> (1878), and <span class="italic">Madame Mohl, her Salon, and her Friends</span> (1885). Her short stories appeared in the <span class="italic">Irish Monthly</span>, <span class="italic">Harper's New Monthly Magazine</span>, the <span class="italic">Atlantic Monthly</span>, the <span class="italic">Catholic World</span>, and <span class="italic">The Tablet</span>. She appears to have travelled widely in Europe and visited America, and was Paris correspondent for <span class="italic">The Tablet</span> for many years. She never married. Kathleen O'Meara died of pneumonia at her home, 15 rue Washington, Paris, on 10 November 1888. Her last work, <span class="italic">The Venerable John Baptiste Vianney, Cur&eacute; d'Ars</span>, was published posthumously in 1891.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Maria Luddy </div>
