<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ulf Fenisc</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1066), <span class="occ">magnate</span>, was a major landholder in England in 1066. According to <span class="roman">Domesday Book</span> he was a king's thegn with land in nine counties that rendered &pound;394 annually; only two other laymen below the rank of earl had more valuable estates. Most of his property was in Lincolnshire (&pound;190), Yorkshire (&pound;88), and Nottinghamshire (&pound;49), counties in which he had jurisdictional rights; but in Nottinghamshire he had the exceptional privilege of retaining the third penny of royal revenues normally reserved for the earl. It is therefore remarkable that he rarely, if ever, attested charters of Edward the Confessor.<br>
<br>There is little doubt that the byname Fenisc, recorded in several <span class="roman">Domesday</span> entries, meant &#145;from [the Danish island of] Fyn&#146;. It is not known when he came to England or how he acquired his property. The church at Aldborough in Yorkshire, one of his most valuable properties, has a mid-eleventh-century inscription &#145;Ulf had this church built for himself and for the soul of Gunvara&#146;, probably his wife  (Okasha, 147). He is not named as one of those who rebelled against William the Conqueror, but a few years after the conquest most of his property was held by Gilbert of Ghent.<br>
<br>P. H. Sawyer </div>
