<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Pickering,  Ellen</span>  (1802&#150;1843), <span class="occ">novelist</span>, was born on 24 March 1802 and baptized at Lyndhurst, Hampshire, on 21 August 1802, the eighth and youngest daughter of the ten surviving children of Isaac Pickering (<i>d</i>. 1829) of Foxlease House, Lyndhurst, and his wife, Katharine (1758/9&#150;1855), daughter of Ferdinando Collins of Betterton House, East Lockinge, Berkshire. Isaac Pickering owned sugar plantations and enslaved people (numbering 400 when he made his will in 1826) in Tortola on the Virgin Islands and Trinidad, but they were mortgaged in exchange for an annuity. He sold the Foxlease property in 1827, moving the family to Bath. Ellen Pickering commenced novel-writing about 1825 with a view to a livelihood. She wrote rapidly, acquired some popularity, and earned, it is said, &pound;100 per annum. Her stylized tales of society manners and romance, with occasional injections of history, after the fashion of Sir Walter Scott, received qualified approval from contemporary reviewers. The <span class="italic">London Spectator</span>, for example, noted, &#145;We are inclined to place Miss Pickering at the very head of the Circulating Library School, with a much better literature and a chaster taste than the rest of her class&#146;  (Allibone, <span class="italic">Dict.</span>), while in his satiric &#145;Hints to authors&#146; in <span class="italic">Blackwood's Magazine</span> James White described her as above the run-of-the-mill romancers. Her work included <span class="italic">The Marriage of the Favourite</span> (1826), <span class="italic">The Heiress</span> (1833), <span class="italic">The Merchant's Daughter</span> (1836), <span class="italic">The Quiet Husband</span> (1840), <span class="italic">The Secret Foe: an Historical Novel</span> (1841), and <span class="italic">Friend or Foe</span> (1843). Most of her novels were republished in the United States.<br>
<br>The most successful of Pickering's books, however, was <span class="italic">Nan Darrell</span>, published in 1839 at the height of the fashion for Gypsy tales (see Hannah Maria Jones's Gypsy trilogy of 1835&#150;40). The heroine is a crazy Gypsy, described in a &#145;postscript&#146; as drawn from life, but bearing an obvious resemblance to Scott's Gypsy Meg Merrilies, as well as to other examples in his novels of mad female outcasts. Other editions appeared in 1846, 1853, 1862, and 1865. Pickering died at her home, 13 Prior Park Buildings, Bath, on 25 November 1843, of scarlet fever. She did not live to finish her last novel, <span class="italic">The Grandfather</span>, which was completed by Elizabeth Youatt, and published in 1844.<br>
<br>Elizabeth Lee<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Kathryn Sutherland </div>
