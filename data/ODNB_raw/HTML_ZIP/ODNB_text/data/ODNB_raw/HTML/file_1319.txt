<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Bansley,  Charles</span>  (<em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>1550), <span class="occ">poet</span>, is unknown except as the author of <span class="italic">A Treatyse Shewing and Declaring the Pryde and Abuse of Women now a Dayes</span>, a caustic and often bawdy rhyming satire attacking the love of dress in women. It begins with the line &#145;Bo pepe what have I spyed!&#146; and concludes with a benediction of Edward VI. There can be no doubt of Bansley's religious opinions. The poem (written in fifty-nine four-line stances) castigates Catholic Rome as much as women's dress. Bansley even blames that city as the source of the feminine love for light raiment, declaring that:<blockquote>
<div>From Rome, from Rome, thys carkered pryde,<br>
</div>
<div>from Rome it came doubtles:<br>
</div>
<div>Away for shame wyth soch filthy baggage,<br>
</div>
<div>As smels of papery and develyshnes!<br>
</div>
</blockquote>The poem was printed by Thomas Raynold, (<i>c</i>.1550), which places it within a year of William Thomas's <span class="italic">An Argument wherein the Apparaile of Women is both Reproved and Defended</span> (1551), which had defended women against a vicious attack on female attire. Within the poem Bansley also refers to <span class="italic">The Scole House of Women</span>, a tract published by Edward Gosynhill in 1560, but probably circulating in manuscript before that date.<br>
<br>James Mew<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Cathy Shrank </div>
