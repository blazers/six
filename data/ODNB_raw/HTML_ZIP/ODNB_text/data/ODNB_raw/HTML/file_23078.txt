<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ramsay,  Charles Aloysius</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1677&#150;1680), <span class="occ">stenographer and translator</span>, was descended from a younger branch of the noble Scottish family of Ramsay. He was thought to be related to Andrew Michael Ramsay and has been stated to be the son of Carl Ramsay (1617&#150;1669), a municipal official of Elbing, Prussia, and author of travelogues and works on Polish and Russian history, which have been left in manuscript. Charles Aloysius Ramsay, who was living at Frankfurt in 1677 and at Paris in 1680, is identified in his own works as a &#145;Scottish gentleman&#146;. A student of chemistry, medicine, and Latin, Ramsay became a wandering scholar and &#145;stenography's knight errant&#146; (&#145;<i>Stenografiens vandrande riddare</i>&#146;; Melin, 1.344) on the European continent.<br>
<br>Ramsay's shorthand system is based on the popular <span class="italic">Tachy-Graphia</span> of Thomas Shelton. Ramsay may first have seen Shelton's system in its anonymous Latin translation (<span class="italic">Tachy-Graphia</span>, 1660) intended for a European market. Ramsay's adaptation of Shelton's system was disseminated on the continent in German (<span class="italic">Tacheographia, oder, Geschwinde Schreib-Kunst</span>, Frankfurt, 1678; translated presumably by Ramsay from a Latin original); in Latin (<span class="italic">Tacheographia</span>, Frankfurt, 1681); and in a French translation by the unidentified &#145;Sieur A. D. G.&#146; (<span class="italic">Tach&eacute;ographie, ou, L'art d'&eacute;crire aussi vite qu'on parle</span>, Paris, 1681, with accompanying Latin text). All had subsequent editions; claims that earlier editions existed, however, have not been verified. In its French translation Ramsay's system had great influence and was published at least four times between 1683 and 1693 (from 1690 with the title <span class="italic">Nouvelle m&eacute;thode</span>). A copy of the 1690 <span class="italic">Nouvelle m&eacute;thode</span> was in the library of Samuel Pepys, who himself used Shelton's shorthand (cf. <span class="italic">Catalogue of the Pepys Library at Magdalene College, Cambridge</span>, 1978, 1.119). In 1678 Ramsay translated into Latin as <span class="italic">Utiles observationes</span> (two parts, part 2 with the title <span class="italic">Observationes chymicae</span>) a German work by chemist and alchemist Johann Kunckel, dedicated to the Royal Society.<br>
<br>Ramsay's was not the first shorthand system published in Europe. The Abb&eacute; Jacques Cossard had published a French system in 1651, but few copies were printed and it had little circulation. Likewise, a German system has been attributed to Daniel Georg Morhof (1666). Shelton's system was known in Europe at this time and was discussed by Gaspard Schott in <span class="italic">Technica curiosa</span> (1664). A year after his German system was published, Ramsay was accused by Johann Christoph Mieth (<span class="italic">Die curiose Schreibe-Kunst</span>, 1679) of plagiarizing Shelton's alphabet. Ramsay's German alphabet is indeed virtually identical to that of Shelton; two characters differ and two are dropped. His French alphabet, however, differs in eight characters, four being omitted. The differences among his German, Latin, and French alphabets suggest that Ramsay attempted to adapt his system to the linguistic requirements of each language and undermines the idea that he was a mere plagiarist. The question of plagiarism aside, the influence of the &#145;Ramsay literature&#146;  (M. Levy, <span class="italic">The History of Short-Hand Writing</span>, 1862, 58) was strong in Europe for a century after its first publication and was recognized in England. In 1736 Philip Gibbs remarked that the French &#145;have got a Short-hand Book, which was published a good while ago&#146;, citing the full title of Ramsay's <span class="italic">Tach&eacute;ographie</span> though not giving his name  (<span class="italic">An Historical Account of Compendious and Swift Writing</span>, 56).<br>
