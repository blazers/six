<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Herbert,  Henry John George</span>, third earl of Carnarvon  (1800&#150;1849), <span class="occ">travel writer</span>, born on 8 June 1800 in Grosvenor Square, London, was the eldest son of Henry George Herbert (1772&#150;1833), the second earl, and Elizabeth Kitty (1772&#150;1813), daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/65/', "Colonel John Dyke Acland")</script> of Pixton, Somerset. He was at first known as Viscount Porchester, was educated at Eton College, and matriculated from Christ Church, Oxford, on 22 October 1817, but did not take a degree. A love of adventure led him from an early age to spend much time abroad. He travelled in north Africa, and subsequently for many years in Portugal and in Spain, where he was imprisoned by the Christinos for displaying active sympathy with the Carlists. An intelligent observer and an excellent linguist, he was attracted by Spanish history and literature, and in 1825 published <span class="italic">The Moor</span>, a poem in six cantos, and in 1828 <span class="italic">Don Pedro, King of Castile</span>, a tragedy, which was successfully produced at Drury Lane during his absence abroad, on 10 March 1828, when Macready and Ellen Tree filled the chief parts. On returning home he published the results of his observations in <span class="italic">The Last Days of the Portuguese Constitution</span> (1830), and in <span class="italic">Portugal and Galicia</span> (1830).<br>
<br>Portchester married, on 4 August 1830, Henrietta Anne (1804&#150;1876), the eldest daughter of Lord Henry Molyneux Howard. They had five children: <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/13035/', "Henry Howard Molyneux Herbert, fourth earl (1831&#150;1890)")</script>; Alan Percy Harty Molyneux Herbert MD (1836&#150;1907); <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/33828/', "Auberon Edward William Molyneux Herbert (1838&#150;1906)")</script>, Eveline (<i>d</i>. 1906), who married Isaac Newton Wallop, fifth earl of Portsmouth; and Gwendoline Ondine. In 1831 he was elected MP for Wootton Basset, Wiltshire, and spoke eloquently against the <span class="roman">Reform Bill</span> which abolished his constituency. On 16 April 1833 he succeeded his father as third earl and continued his opposition to Liberal measures, including municipal reform, in the House of Lords.<br>
<br>In 1839 Carnarvon travelled through Greece, then suffering from the effects of war and civil disturbances&#151;his notes of the tour were published in 1869 by his son Henry under the title <span class="italic">Reminiscences of Athens and the Morea</span>. Between 1839 and 1842 Carnarvon undertook restoration of the family seat, Highclere, Hampshire, on a very elaborate scale. He engaged Sir Charles Barry who encased the existing classical mansion in an Elizabethan fa&ccedil;ade, based loosely on Woollaton, but with Gothic influences. The exuberant style reflected Carnarvon's liking for the exotic, which he had indulged in his travels, and for the theatrical&#151;and his dislike for styles he thought whiggish. He was jealous of his rights and in 1844 established in the courts his claims to free-warren over the manors of Highclere and Burghclere, giving him the exclusive right to kill game on those estates; but in private life he was kind and unassuming.<br>
<br>Carnarvon's health was never very good, and he died at Pusey House, Berkshire, the home of his brother-in-law, Philip Pusey MP, on 10 December 1849. His funeral chapel was by Thomas Allom, in Highclere Park, north of the house.<br>
<br>[Anon.]<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Elizabeth Baigent </div>
