<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ogilvy,  James</span>, sixth earl of Findlater and third earl of Seafield  (<em>c.</em>1714&#150;1770), <span class="occ">agriculturist</span>, was the only son in the family of one son and two daughters of James, fifth earl of Findlater and second earl of Seafield (<i>c</i>.1689&#150;1764), and his first wife, Lady Elizabeth Hay, second daughter of Thomas, seventh earl of Kinnoull. Before succeeding his father in 1764 he was known as Lord Deskford, and while travelling abroad he made the acquaintance of Horace Walpole, who, in a letter to General Conway on 23 April 1740, wrote of him, &#145;There are few young people have so good an understanding&#146;, but who also referred to his &#145;solemn Scotchery&#146; as not a &#145;little formidable&#146;  (<span class="italic">Letters</span>, 1.46). On 9 June 1749 Ogilvy married Lady Mary (1720&#150;1795), second daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/19627/', "John Murray, first duke of Atholl")</script>. They had two sons, one of whom died in 1763. The other, James, who died in 1811, was the last earl of the Ogilvy line.<br>
<br>Ogilvy became interested in encouraging industry and agriculture early in his career. In 1752 he opened a large bleachfield in the parish of Deskford, and he started a factory in Cullen for the manufacture of linen and damask. From 1754 to 1761 he was one of the commissioners of customs for Scotland, and in 1765 he was appointed one of the lords of police. He was also a trustee for the improvement of fisheries and manufactures, and for the management of the annexed estates in Scotland. By his example and encouragement he did much to promote advanced methods of agriculture in Banffshire. He introduced turnip husbandry, and granted long leases to his tenants on condition that within a certain period they should enclose their lands and adopt certain improved methods of cropping. To prevent damage to young plantations on his estate, he agreed to give certain of his tenants, on the termination of their leases, every third tree, or its value in money.<br>
<br>Ogilvy died at Cullen House, Banff, on 3 November 1770.<br>
<br>T. F. Henderson<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Anne Pimlott Baker </div>
