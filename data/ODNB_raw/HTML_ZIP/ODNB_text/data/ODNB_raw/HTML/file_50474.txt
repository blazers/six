<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Rose,  Giles</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1664&#150;1681), <span class="occ">master cook and translator</span>, is known only from his printed works, which give no information about his family. In 1679 Richard Bentley and M. Magnes published his translation of Boaistuau's <span class="italic">Th&eacute;&acirc;tre du monde</span>. Rose described this as &#145;an Ancient Book, which it was my Fortune to meet with in my Travels&#146;  (A2<i>v</i>), and referred to &#145;my own inabilities (as being no Scholar)&#146;  (A6<i>r</i>), and &#145;the obscure Calling &hellip; wherein I have always been occupied&#146;  (A6<i>v</i>), in his dedication to Sir Winston Churchill (1620&#150;1688). His calling was, according to the title-page of his next book, that of &#145;one of the Master Cooks in His Majesties Kitchen&#146;. Churchill, as one of the clerks of the board of green cloth, was Rose's superior in the royal household, but they must have been on good terms; Rose had received a presentation copy of Churchill's <i>magnum opus</i>, entitled <span class="italic">Divi Britannici</span> (1675). Bentley then invited him to translate the anonymous <span class="italic">&Eacute;cole parfaite des officiers de bouche</span>, a book of instructions for servants in great houses, as <span class="italic">A Perfect School of Instructions for the Officers of the Mouth</span> (1682). Rose's appointment in the royal kitchens appears by this time to have been nominal: his dedication (to another clerk of the board of green cloth, Sir Stephen Fox) is dated from a private residence in the country, Langley Park, and in it he complains twice of having &#145;but too much time to spare&#146;  (A2<i>r</i>), and refers to &#145;the distance of my imploy from the Press&#146;  (A6<i>r</i>). In 1699 the term catalogue for Easter listed &#145;The Art and Mystery of good Housekeeping, or, A frugal Way to live well &hellip; The Second Edition &hellip; By G. Rose, late one of the Master Cooks to His Majesty&#146;  (Arber, 128). This appears to have been Rose's last work; it may even have been a misleadingly entitled posthumous edition of his book of 1682.<br>
<br>John Considine </div>
