<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Sutton,  Thomas</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1274&#150;1300), <span class="occ">Dominican friar and theologian</span>, was ordained deacon on 20 September 1274 at Blyth in the York diocese. He may have been a fellow of Merton College, Oxford, <i>c</i>.1270, and was certainly a member of the Dominican convent in Oxford, perhaps by <i>c</i>.1282. He had a long career in the university, graduating bachelor and then, <i>c</i>.1284, doctor of theology as well as master <i>c</i>.1290. He was licensed to hear confessions in the Lincoln diocese on 11 October 1300, and it is likely that he is the Thomas Sutton who preached in Oxford on 23 November 1292, and on 1 March and 24 May 1293. He was perhaps still living in 1315, but the date of his death and the place of his burial are not known.<br>
<br>Sutton was one of the foremost early exponents and defenders at the University of Oxford of the teaching of Thomas Aquinas, the Dominican philosopher and theologian. Aquinas had incorporated into his philosophy many Aristotelian features, such as the notion that human knowledge starts from sense perception rather than with a divine illumination of the human mind in which &#145;seminal reasons&#146; are implanted. Also controversial at Oxford at this time was the question whether man, being both body and soul, has both a corporeal and a spiritual form: Aquinas taught that in every corporeal being there is one substantial form. In 1277 Robert Kilwardby (<i>d</i>. 1279), the archbishop of Canterbury, himself a Dominican friar and former master at Oxford University, condemned a range of Aristotelian theses as well as Aquinas's novel doctrine of the unicity of forms. This condemnation was renewed in 1284 by Archbishop John Pecham (<i>d</i>. 1292), a Franciscan friar and former master at Oxford who also excommunicated an English Dominican master, Richard Knapwell, in 1286. At this time and in this climate Thomas Sutton emerged as a keen student and follower of Thomas Aquinas and as a notable thinker among the group of English Dominican Thomists who flourished in the last quarter of the thirteenth century. The group included Robert Winchelsey (<i>d</i>. 1313), Simon of Faversham, and Robert Orford. Thomas Sutton also participated in the quarrels between the Friars Preacher and the Friars Minor over the issue of poverty.<br>
<br>Sutton's writings are difficult to identify and to date, and some have in the past been confused with writings of Thomas Aquinas. He apparently wrote a continuation of the unfinished commentary of Aquinas on Aristotle's <span class="italic">Perihermeneias</span>, as well as a continuation of Aquinas's commentary on Aristotle's <span class="italic">De generatione et corruptione</span>. Another confusion is possibly with a Thomas the Englishman, whose <span class="italic">Liber propugnatorius</span> (<i>c</i>.1311) contains an attack upon Duns Scotus (<i>d</i>. 1308), the Franciscan master, who taught at Oxford during the 1290s. Thomas Sutton wrote <span class="italic">De esse et essentia</span> (in support of Aquinas, and against Henri de Gand) and probably also <span class="italic">Quaestiones super sententias</span>. He wrote <span class="italic">Quodlibeta</span> (between 1291 and 1301), and treatises opposing the doctrine of the plurality of forms.<br>
<br>David Luscombe<span class="italic">, rev. </span>
