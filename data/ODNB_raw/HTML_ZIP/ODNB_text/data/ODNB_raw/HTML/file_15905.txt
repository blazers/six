<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lackenheath <span class="st"> [Lakingheth]</span>,  John</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1381), <span class="occ">Benedictine monk</span>, derived his name from Lakenheath, a few miles north-west of Bury St Edmunds. Having been appointed keeper of the barony there, Lackenheath found that the archives of the abbey were still in a state of disorder as a result of the sack of the abbey by the townsfolk of Bury in 1327. He reorganized those archives and, <i>c</i>.1380, compiled an elaborate register  (now BL, Harley MS 743) which provided a finding aid for the records. He is also recorded in court records as making decisions on seigneurial dues payable to the abbey. His activities on behalf of his house made him very unpopular, and during the rising of 1381 the Suffolk rebels, at the instigation of the Bury townsfolk, demanded that Lackenheath be surrendered to them. In order to prevent the destruction of the monastery, Lackenheath gave himself up to the rebels and on 15 July was beheaded in the market place, where his head was set upon the pillory.<br>
<br>Another <span class="occ">Benedictine monk</span> and near namesake, <a name="cosubject_61822"></a><span class="headword">John Lakyngheth</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1396), was a member of the order at Westminster, holding a number of offices there, most notably as treasurer of the abbey at various times between 1363 and 1392. In 1386 Richard II unsuccessfully sought to secure his election as abbot of Westminster. Lakyngheth has been proposed as the author of the Westminster chronicle  (in Cambridge, Corpus Christi College, MS 197A), which ends in the year of his death and includes a vivid account of the Salisbury parliament of April&#150;May 1384 which was attended by him. However, it seems unlikely, from the description of the attempt to make him abbot, that he was responsible for this chronicle. As Barbara Harvey has observed, &#145;It hardly seems likely that the author who chose to record this episode&#151;according to the evidence of the rest of his chronicle a self-effacing man&#151;was himself the abbot <i>manqu&eacute;</i>&#146;  (<span class="italic">Westminster Chronicle</span>, xl).<br>
<br>Andrew Prescott </div>
