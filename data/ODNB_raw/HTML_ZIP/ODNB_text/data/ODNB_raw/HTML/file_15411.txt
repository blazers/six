<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Kenny,  William Stopford</span>  (1787/8&#150;1867), <span class="occ">schoolmaster and educational writer</span>, was born in 1787 or 1788. On 1 January 1812 he married, at St Pancras, London, Eliza Adams. For many years he kept a classical school at 5 Fitzroy Street, Fitzroy Square, London, later moving to Richmond, Surrey, where his son, William D. Kenny, ran a boarding-school. In 1856 Kenny stated that he had been a teacher for forty years. An accomplished chess player, his first published works were <span class="italic">Practical Chess Grammar</span> (1817), <span class="italic">Practical Chess Exercises</span> (1818), and <span class="italic">Analysis of the Game of Chess</span> (1819), a translation of Danican Philidor's work. He was later known for his schoolbooks. His most popular work, <span class="italic">Why and because: being a collection of familiar questions and answers on subjects relating to air, water, light and fire</span> (4th edn, 1830; 18th edn, 1854), translated from a French original, set out in an appealing way answers to children's questions about nature. His <span class="italic">Grammatical Omnibus</span> (8th edn, 1853) identified and corrected &#145;improprieties frequent in writing and conversation&#146;. Latterly he was described as a teacher of languages, and he published French and Italian phrase books (1854&#150;6). <span class="italic">Kenny's School Geography</span> (1856) was a catalogue of facts to be learned by heart, with questions and exercises. He died on 16 November 1867 at 2 York Villas, Richmond.<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> M. C. Curthoys </div>
