<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Dod,  Henry</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1583?&#150;1620), <span class="occ">poet and merchant</span>, was of the old family of Dod, or Doddes, the son of Peter Dod of Smithes Pentrey, Broxton, in Cheshire, and Anne, daughter of Hugh Carrington of Over, in Cheshire. He versified nine psalms &#145;into English meter fitting the common tunes&#146;, and at the request of certain friends, as he writes, he published them in 1603 as <span class="italic">Certaine Psalmes of David in Meter</span>. The undertaking was sanctioned by James I, and the impression was quickly sold. Afterwards, at the request of some of the puritan clergy, Dod undertook a metrical recast of the entire psalter, published as <span class="italic">Al the Psalmes of David, with Certaine Songes and Canticles</span>. It is dedicated to John Brewen (Bruen), John Dod of Tussingham, and John Dod of Broxon, all of Cheshire. It has no name of author, printer, or place. It is dated 1620, and the initials H. D. are appended to its &#145;Address to the Christian Reader&#146;. It was perhaps printed abroad, and Wither, who is scornful of the quality of the verse, was possibly right when he said it was condemned in England by authority to the fire. With it Dod printed his metrical version of the act of parliament for ordering a Gunpowder Plot thanksgiving service.<br>
<br>Dod is described as a &#145;mercer&#146;  (<span class="italic">Pedigrees</span>) and Wither calls him &#145;Dod the silkman&#146;. He is unlikely to have been the Henry Dod who was incumbent of Felpham, Sussex, in 1630: the record of matriculation of this particular Dod into Corpus Christi College, Oxford, gives his year of birth as approximately 1592. He may very likely be the &#145;H. D.&#146; for whom Gregory Seaton printed <span class="italic">A Treatise of Faith and Workes</span> in 1583. Nothing is known of his death; he had four sons.<br>
<br>Jennett Humphreys<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Christopher Burlinson </div>
