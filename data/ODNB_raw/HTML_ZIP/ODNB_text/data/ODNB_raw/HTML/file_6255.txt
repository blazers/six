<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Cop <span class="st"> [Cope]</span>,  Michel</span>  (<em>c.</em>1501&#150;1566), <span class="occ">religious author</span>, mistakenly known as Michael Cope, was conjectured without conviction by Anthony Wood, who describes him as &#145;a zealous Calvinist of Geneva&#146;  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span>, 1.192), to have been a member of the Oxfordshire family of Cope of Hanwell. The undoubted fact that two of his works, both of them religious and evangelical in character, were published in English in the mid-sixteenth century subsequently led to the understandable assumption that Cop must have been one of the colony of English protestants who sought refuge in Calvin's city during the reign of Mary Tudor. In fact Cop was the second of three sons of the distinguished physician Guillaume Cop, a native of Basel who migrated to France, where he obtained the prestigious position of king's physician. Two of Cop's sons, Michel and Nicolas, were soon drawn into the humanist&#150;evangelical circle which flourished in the capital in the early 1530s. In 1534 Nicolas Cop, who had obtained the prestigious position of rector of the University of Paris, attained notoriety as the preacher of the famous sermon which, by injudicious support for views propounded by the reformers, caused a conservative backlash which engulfed many of the Paris evangelicals. Many were forced to flee, including Calvin and both the brothers Cop. Michel Cop repaired first to Basel, before moving, in 1545, to Geneva, where he was swiftly promoted to the ministry. He served as a minister, and a firm supporter of Calvin, until his death in 1566. An accomplished and urbane author, in 1556 he published his <span class="italic">Exposition sur les proverbes de Salomon</span>, and the following year his master work, <span class="italic">Le livre de l'Eccl&eacute;siaste, autrement dit le Prescheur</span>, swiftly also published in English translation as <span class="italic">A Faithful and Familiar Exposition of Ecclesiastes</span> (1557). The <span class="italic">Salomon</span> also enjoyed an English life, the printer Luke Harrison being licensed to print <span class="italic">An Exposition upon the Fyrste Chap. of the Proverbis of Salomon by Mygchell Coope</span> in 1564. In Geneva, Cop married one Ayma Waremberg; he was created a <i>bourgeois</i> of the city on 25 October 1554. He died on 18 September 1566.<br>
<br>Andrew Pettegree </div>
