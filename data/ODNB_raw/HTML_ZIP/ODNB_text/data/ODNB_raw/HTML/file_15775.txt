<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Knowles,  Richard Brinsley</span>  (1820&#150;1882), <span class="occ">journalist</span>, son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/15770/', "James Sheridan Knowles (1784&#150;1862)")</script>, dramatist and Baptist preacher, and Maria Charteris (<i>d</i>. 1841), actress, was born at Glasgow on 17 January 1820. From 1838 to 1841 he was a clerk in the registrar-general's office, Somerset House, London. He was admitted as a student of the Middle Temple on 14 November 1839 and called to the bar on 26 May 1843. Having inherited his father's love of the theatre, on 19 November 1845 he produced at the Haymarket Theatre a comedy entitled <span class="italic">The Maiden Aunt</span>, which had a run of thirty nights. In 1845&#150;6 he edited the humorous magazines <span class="italic">Joe Miller the Younger</span> and <span class="italic">Mephystopheles</span>. On 25 October 1845 he married Eliza Mary, youngest child of Peter and Elizabeth Crowley of Dublin, and sister of Nicholas Joseph Crowley (1819&#150;1857), painter.<br>
<br>In 1849 Knowles became a Roman Catholic and was appointed editor of the <span class="italic">Catholic Standard</span>, subsequently purchased by Henry Wilberforce and renamed the <span class="italic">Weekly Register</span>. From 1853 to 1855 he edited the <span class="italic">Illustrated London Magazine</span>, a series of five volumes. He was a leading writer on <span class="italic">The Standard</span> from 1857 to 1860, but the anti-Catholic prejudice of the proprietors led to his dismissal. John Sherren Brewer, who was then editing the paper, at once resigned in protest. Knowles was editor of the <span class="italic">London Review</span>, but in later years his chief engagement was on the <span class="italic">Morning Post</span>, until ill health forced his resignation.<br>
<br>Turning to historical research, Knowles edited the <span class="italic">Chronicles of John of Oxenedes</span> from a manuscript in the duke of Newcastle's collection; this was published in 1859 in the <span class="roman">Rolls Series</span>. In 1871 he was appointed to the inspectorate of the Royal Commission on Historical Manuscripts, and catalogued many valuable collections, mainly belonging to Roman Catholic families. Among these were the papers of the marquess of Bute, the earl of Denbigh, the earl of Ashburnham, and Colonel John Towneley. His <span class="italic">Life of James Sheridan Knowles</span>, a limited edition privately printed in 1872, besides being a work of filial piety, vividly evokes the theatrical milieu of the early nineteenth century. Knowles died suddenly at his home, 29 North Bank, Regent's Park, London, on 28 January 1882, survived by his wife. His son Richard Brinsley Sheridan Knowles (1848&#150;<i>c</i>. 1930), educated at Ratcliffe College, became a civil servant and occasional contributor to literary reviews. His three-volume novel <span class="italic">Glencoonoge</span> (1891) is an attractive Irish romance set in Bantry Bay.<br>
<br>G. C. Boase<span class="italic">, rev. </span> G. Martin Murphy </div>
