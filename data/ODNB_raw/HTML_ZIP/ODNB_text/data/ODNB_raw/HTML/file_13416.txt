<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hodder,  James</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1659&#150;1673), <span class="occ">arithmetician</span>, also taught writing, shorthand, and accounting at his school &#145;next dore to the Sunne&#146; in Tokenhouse Yard, Lothbury, City of London. He is first known as author of <span class="italic">The Penman's Recreation</span>, published about 1660 (2nd edn, 1673), which has a preface by him dated 1659. It was &#145;invented and written&#146; by Hodder, and the thirty plates, each bearing his name, were &#145;engraven&#146; by Edward Cocker. The dedication is to Sir Walter Earle.<br>
<br>In 1661 Hodder issued his more successful work, <span class="italic">Arithmetick, or, That Necessary Art Made most Easie</span>. The publisher was Thomas Rooks, who published for Cocker also, and it seems likely that the two teachers were friends. In the ninth edition (1671) Rooks inserted a preface, saying &#145;in this bad time of trade in Books, in less than ten months, I sold of them 1550&#146;. After the tenth edition, of 1672, the next known is the twelfth (1678), edited by Henry Mose (or Moss), described as &#145;late servant and successor to&#146; Hodder. A page of verse signed &#145;S. Hodder&#146;, presumably a close relation, heaps praise on Mose, concluding remarkably &#145;Posterity shall make Account, It owes to Hodder's Memory, but Much more, to Mose&#146;. Mose's editions ended with the twenty-sixth, of 1720, followed by a final one edited by W. Hume (1739).<br>
<br>The <span class="italic">Arithmetick</span> has been described as &#145;the most general book of the kind ever published&#146;  (Granger, 377). Cocker's more famous <span class="italic">Arithmetick</span> (1678) was very similar; De Morgan suggested that if Hodder's work &#145;had given the new mode of division [without crossings-out], it must have stood in the place of Cocker&#146;  (De Morgan, 46).<br>
<br>The great fire of 1666 forced Hodder to move to Bromley by Bow, where he set up a boarding-school. While there, in 1668, he published <span class="italic">Hodder's Decimal Arithmetick</span>, with a third and final edition in 1672. In 1671 he was able to return to Lothbury, apparently to the same address, and was still there in 1673. S. Hodder's verse demonstrates that Hodder was dead by 1678. Another apprentice of his, besides Mose, might have been the penman and arithmetician Eleazar Wigan, whom Samuel Pepys described as &#145;pupil to Mr. Hodder&#146;  (Heal, 115).<br>
<br>Ruth Wallis </div>
