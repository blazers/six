<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Tomkins,  William</span>  (<em>c.</em>1732&#150;1792), <span class="occ">landscape painter</span>, was first recorded on 7 July 1757 as the father of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/27508/', "Charles Tomkins (1757&#150;1823)")</script> [<i>see under </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/27514/', "Tomkins, Peltro William")</script>], topographical painter and engraver, and on 15 October 1759, on the baptism of his second son, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/27514/', "Peltro William Tomkins (1759&#150;1840)")</script>, also an engraver and draughtsman. The date of his marriage to their mother, Susanna Callard, is not known.<br>
<br>Tomkins first exhibited at the Free Society of Artists in 1761 and was awarded the second premium for a landscape by the Society of Arts in 1763. His reputation rests on his paintings in the manner of Claude (a number of whose works he copied) of gentlemen's seats, mainly in the west and north of England. He was promoted and patronized by Edward Walter of Stalbridge, Dorset, and his other patrons included the Parkers of Saltram House, Devon. Seven works by Tomkins remain in the Saltram collection, including five views of the environs of the house. He was paid &pound;10 10<i>s</i>. for two pictures in 1785 but his work at Saltram began earlier, as accounts also record payments of &pound;20 in 1778 for cleaning pictures and &pound;31 10<i>s</i>. in June 1779 for &#145;Tomkins to pay Zucchi&#146;  (<span class="italic">The Saltram Collection</span>, 67). James Duff, second Earl Fife, was another important patron, for whom Tomkins painted a group of twelve landscapes. A number of his works were engraved for William Angus's and William Watt's sets of views of seats of the nobility and gentry, published in 1787 and 1779&#150;86 respectively.<br>
<br>Tomkins exhibited at the Free Society of Artists (1761&#150;4), at the Incorporated Society of Artists (1764&#150;8), and at the Royal Academy (1769&#150;90). He was elected an associate of the Royal Academy in 1771. He died at his house in Queen Anne Street, London, on 1 January 1792.<br>
<br>Deborah Graham-Vernon </div>
