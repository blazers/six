<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Dixon,  John</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1574&#150;1597), <span class="occ">annotator of Faerie Queene</span>, was the eldest of the three sons of Humphry Dixon (<i>fl</i>. 1538&#150;1555) of Hilden Manor, near Tonbridge, Kent, and his wife, Elizabeth (<i>fl</i>. 1540&#150;1555), a daughter of the Stace family of Hallenden. Dixon was born into a landowning family, originating in Scotland but established at least since his grandfather's time in Kent. He may, like his younger brother William, have attended Tonbridge School and gone on to university (it is just possible that he is the same &#145;John Dixon&#146; who matriculated at Jesus College, Cambridge, in 1573). He married Joan Launce of Stanstead, Kent, and had two sons, Henry and William&#151;the first born about 1574. John Dixon inherited Hilden Manor from his father and was able to pass it on to his elder son.<br>
<br>Dixon is of significance today solely as the presumed author of the copious annotations to be found in a copy of Edmund Spenser's <span class="italic">Faerie Queene</span> (1590) now in the possession of the Stansted Park Foundation in Hampshire. The volume has the name &#145;John Dixon&#146; written on its title-page, but the specific connection to the resident of Hilden can be established only through the book's provenance. The annotations show John Dixon to have been of nationalist and puritan sympathies&#151;a man very familiar with the <span class="roman">Bible</span> but not, apparently, with Spenser's Latin or Italian models. Dixon interprets Book I in the context of the prophecies of <span class="roman">Revelation</span>. In addition, he links episodes in the poem to recent history (sometimes making use of a basic letter-substitution code). The Redcrosse Knight is, for example, identified as the earl of Leicester, while his betrothal to Una is (with no apparent sense of contradiction) later paralleled with the accession of Elizabeth. Although Dixon displays a sound grasp of Spenser's didactic intentions (for instance glossing the reconciliation of Guyon and Britomart as &#145;unitie between Temperance and Chastitie&#146;; <span class="smallcap">III</span>.i.12) his understanding of plot is often less secure. In this, as in other ways, his priorities sometimes appear the reverse of those of modern readers. The annotations are the only trace which remains of Dixon's character. It is possible to determine that he wrote them in 1597. We do not know his date of death, but a document showing that his son had inherited Hilden Manor by 1642 does give a late date by which that event had occurred  (Almack).<br>
<br>Bart van Es </div>
