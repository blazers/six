<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Keary,  Eliza Harriett</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1827, <em>d.</em> 1918), <span class="occ">poet and children's writer</span>, was born in Bilton rectory, near Wetherby, Yorkshire, and baptized at Sculcoates, Yorkshire, on 22 April 1827, one of the six surviving children of the five sons and three daughters born to William Keary (<i>d</i>. 1856), rector of the parish, and his wife, Lucy (<i>d</i>. 1869), daughter of Plumer Hall, of Bilton Hall. Her father originally hailed from Clough in co. Galway, Ireland, and emigrated to England after a loss of property. Eliza Keary lived in Nunnington, North Riding, and from 1845 to 1848 in Clifton, near Bristol. In her lifetime she was known chiefly for her collaborations with her older sister, novelist <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/15216/', "Anna Maria (Annie) Keary (1825&#150;1879)")</script>. Together they published popular children's literature such as <span class="italic">The Heroes of Asgard</span> (1857), <span class="italic">Early Egyptian History for the Young</span> (1861), and <span class="italic">Little Wanderlin and other Fairy Tales</span> (1865). In 1882 Eliza Keary published a <span class="italic">Memoir of Annie Keary</span> and in 1883 she edited Annie Keary's letters. The memoir suggests that Eliza accompanied her sister on sojourns to Egypt and P&eacute;gomas, near Cannes, to do research and care for the chronically ill Annie. For a time the two sisters were also involved in running a home for unemployed young female servants in Bessborough Gardens, Pimlico.<br>
<br>In the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries Eliza Keary's poetry has received increasing critical attention from feminist scholars and cultural historians interested in Victorian women poets. Her collection <span class="italic">Little Seal-Skin and other Poems</span> (1874) contains work in a multitude of forms ranging from the sonnet to verse drama to dramatic monologue. Isobel Armstrong and Joseph Bristow consider her breadth the mark of an iconoclast; they write: &#145;Most unusual for a woman writer of this period, many of the poems in <span class="italic">Little Seal-Skin</span> approach free verse. Both rhyme and metrical scheme are unorthodox&#146;. Keary's three volumes of <span class="roman">Bible</span> selections, <span class="italic">Rays of Light: a Textbook</span> (1884), <span class="italic">The River of God</span> (1884), and <span class="italic">A Casket of Pearls</span> (1884), and poems such as &#145;Christine and Mary: a Correspondence&#146; from <span class="italic">Little Seal-Skin</span> have rightly marked her religious fervour; however, the bulk of her work for adults and children addresses a wider range of subject matter. Her literature for children includes <span class="italic">The Magic Valley, or, Patient Antoine</span> (1877), <span class="italic">At Home Again</span> (1886), and <span class="italic">Pets and Playmates</span> (1887). <span class="italic">Enchanted Tulips and other Verses for Children</span>, a collaboration by Eliza, Annie, and their niece Maud Keary, who also served as editor, was published in 1914. In 1901 Eliza Keary edited <span class="italic">The Francis Letters by Sir Phillip Francis and other Members of the Family</span> with Beata Francis; her nephew Charles Francis Keary (1848&#150;1917), also an author, provided a note to the edition. Keary died at Orwell Lodge, Grafton Road, Torquay, Devon, on 29 August 1918, aged ninety-one.<br>
<br>Jodi Lustig </div>
