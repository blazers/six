<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Cochrane, Sir  James</span>  (1798&#150;1883), <span class="occ">judge in Gibraltar</span>, the fourth son of Thomas Cochrane, speaker of the house of assembly of Nova Scotia, was born in Nova Scotia. He was admitted as a student in 1818, and was called to the bar at the Inner Temple on 6 February 1829. In the same year he married Theresa (<i>d</i>. 1873), the daughter of Colonel William Haly. They had at least one son, Thomas, who later became rector of Stapleford Abbotts, Essex.<br>
<br>In 1837 Cochrane was appointed attorney-general at Gibraltar, where he remained for the rest of his life. In 1841 he was made chief justice, a post he held for thirty-six years. He was knighted at St James's Palace on 12 March 1845. He resigned in 1877, four years after his wife's death. According to a speech made by General Lord Napier of Magdala, governor of the fortress, at the resignation, Cochrane had &#145;eminently maintained the high character of the bench&#146;. His clarity of judgment, wisdom, and character had, according to Napier, &#145;commanded the respect of all classes of the community&#146;. The general drew particular attention to Cochrane's &#145;firmness and perfect fairness&#146;, to his achievements with working-class people, and to his success in helping &#145;to dispel from the city of Gibraltar the crime of using the knife, which was unfortunately once so prevalent&#146;. Cochrane died at Glenrocky, his home in Gibraltar, on 24 June 1883.<br>
<br>H. M. Stephens<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Lynn Milne </div>
