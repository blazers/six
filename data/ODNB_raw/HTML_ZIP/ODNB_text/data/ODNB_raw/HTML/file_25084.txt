<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Sench&aacute;n Torp&eacute;ist</span>  (<em>fl. </em>6th&#150;7th cent.), <span class="occ">Gaelic poet</span>, is not commemorated by a contemporary annalistic reference. However, he is associated with Guaire Aidne (<i>d</i>. 663), U&iacute; Fhiachrach king of Connacht, and many historians have imagined that he might have had genuine links with that king's court. Furthermore, it has been argued that an early Leinster genealogical poem, ascribed to him, may be his work. The evidence for his career is otherwise non-existent.<br>
<br>Despite this, Sench&aacute;n is an important literary figure, alleged to have been chief poet of Ireland. He appears as the main protagonist in the various stories associated with the finding of the Irish epic, <span class="italic">T&aacute;in b&oacute; Cuailnge</span> (&#145;cattle-Raid of Cuailnge&#146;). The earlier, possibly originally ninth-century, versions describe how Sench&aacute;n, on his own initiative or on the insistence of Guaire, calls the poets of Ireland together to recite the <span class="italic">T&aacute;in</span>. The poets only know scraps, however, for the <span class="italic">T&aacute;in</span> has been exchanged for knowledge of the <span class="italic">Culmen</span>, an Irish name for the <span class="italic">Etymologiae</span> of Isidore of Seville (570&#150;636). In one version Sench&aacute;n's son, Muirgein, makes an incantation at the grave of the dead pagan hero Fergus mac R&oacute;ig. Fergus duly appears and recites the <span class="italic">T&aacute;in</span>, giving an eye-witness account. Another version describes how Sench&aacute;n fasts against the dead saints, Brendan of Clonfert (<i>d</i>. 577?) and Ciar&aacute;n of Clonmacnoise (<i>d</i>. 549?). Ciar&aacute;n advises him, through a clerical proxy, to visit Fergus's grave and recite a poem there. Sench&aacute;n does this, with the same result as in the first account.<br>
<br>These stories proved popular and by the eleventh century had been considerably expanded to incorporate the relationship of Guaire to his uterine brother, the saintly hermit Marb&aacute;n. The poet&#150;collector Sench&aacute;n was transformed into the leader of a <i>tromd&aacute;m</i>, &#145;a burdensome company&#146;, of poets, who make inroads into Guaire's legendary hospitality. Marb&aacute;n's revenge is to impel Sench&aacute;n to seek the <span class="italic">T&aacute;in</span> in reparation for his behaviour. This narrative reached its height with the late Middle Irish tale, <span class="italic">Tromd&aacute;m Guaire</span>. Sench&aacute;n's retinue is described in loving detail and Marb&aacute;n's role is expanded, as is Sench&aacute;n's quest for the <span class="italic">T&aacute;in</span>, which takes him to Scotland before he returns to Ireland.<br>
<br>Sench&aacute;n's trip to Scotland in <span class="italic">Tromd&aacute;m Guaire</span> may have been inspired by an anecdote related in the late ninth- or early tenth-century &#145;Cormac's glossary&#146; under the word <i>pr&uacute;ll</i>. Here, Sench&aacute;n visits the Isle of Man and, in the company of the spirit of poetry, brings back to Ireland a female poet, belonging to a minor branch of the M&uacute;scraige living among the U&iacute; Fhidgeinte (modern co. Limerick).<br>
<br>However, Sench&aacute;n's other literary associations are with Guaire. In an anecdote, &#145;Mac D&aacute; Cherda and Cuimm&iacute;ne Fota&#146;, which may date from the ninth century, the holy fool Mac D&aacute; Cherda (<i>d</i>. 645?) proves himself to be the true chief poet of Ireland. Furthermore, Cuimm&iacute;ne Fota (<i>d</i>. 661?), the saint and abbot of Clonfert, defeats Sench&aacute;n in a poetic duel. The former's gospel lessons overcome Sench&aacute;n's poetic compositions.<br>
<br>This, less than complimentary, view of Sench&aacute;n seems to dominate the ninth-century <span class="italic">Sc&eacute;la Cano meic Gartn&aacute;in</span>. It is Sench&aacute;n's jealousy of Guaire's hospitality to the young hero Cano mac Gartn&aacute;in (<i>d</i>. 688?) which causes Cano to depart Guaire's court. But Sench&aacute;n's behaviour is not completely reprehensible. An episode in <span class="italic">Sc&eacute;la Cano</span> describes how Sench&aacute;n sends two poems to the high-king, Diarmait, son of &Aacute;ed Sl&aacute;ine (<i>d</i>. 665/668). He is insulted by the king, but, showing a forbearance much at odds with his picture in <span class="italic">Tromd&aacute;m Guaire</span>, does not satirize him. He sends two more poems and is well rewarded by Diarmait.<br>
<br>The evidence for Sench&aacute;n's association with the <span class="italic">T&aacute;in</span> is unhistorical and far too flimsy to treat seriously. The same can be said for the claim that Sench&aacute;n compiled the earliest stratum of the genealogies. Clearly, the historical figure of Sench&aacute;n attracted a series of legends. In these, Sench&aacute;n is a representative of the poets. The narratives in which he appears emphasize the value of poets as tale-tellers or criticize the economic burden they place on the nobility.<br>
<br>Elva Johnston </div>
