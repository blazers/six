<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Seagrave <span class="st"> [Segrave]</span>,  Gilbert</span>  (<em>c.</em>1260&#150;1312), <span class="occ">theologian</span>, was probably a member of the Leicestershire baronial family of Seagrave, and if so was possibly a younger son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/25039/', "Nicholas of Seagrave, first Lord Seagrave (1238?&#150;1295)")</script>. He must have read arts at Oxford from about 1280, and was admitted rector of Harlaxton, Lincolnshire, on 20 November 1282. While studying and teaching at Oxford he was maintained by additional benefices, which he was dispensed to hold on 3 February 1291: he became a canon of Lincoln, with the prebend of Milton Ecclesia, on 8 February 1297; sacrist of the chapel of St Mary and the Holy Angels, York, on 2 August 1303 (but resigned it under royal pressure in 1304); and archdeacon of Oxford on 10 December 1303. By then he had probably left Oxford and was in the service of Thomas Corbridge, archbishop of York (<i>d</i>. 1304), as his seneschal (1303) and proctor in parliament (1301). He went overseas, perhaps to represent the crown at the coronation of Pope Clement V, in October 1305; he was certainly at the papal curia at the Ch&acirc;teau St Symphorien d'Ozon near Lyons in 1312, where he died and was probably buried.<br>
<br>Seagrave's theological <span class="italic">quaestiones</span> and quodlibets were said by Leland to be preserved in most Oxford libraries. If so, there is no trace of them now, and they were even unknown to Bale. All that remains of his work is a disputed question with its magisterial determination, and a response to the question of another master, the Cistercian Thomas Kirkeby, in the first quire of the Worcester collection of disputed questions  (Worcester Cathedral, MS Q.99). In these debates, which probably took place about 1297, he was associated with Richard Clive, Thomas Sutton, and William of Macclesfield, three luminaries of the brilliant Oxford generation of theologians who included Duns Scotus (<i>d</i>. 1308). Seagrave's question, whether the emanation of the Word in divine beings should precede the distinction of ideas, is a characteristically abstract topic of its time.<br>
<br>Jeremy Catto </div>
