<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Armstrong <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Cobbe]</span>,  Lucie</span>  (1851&#150;1907), <span class="occ">musician and writer on etiquette</span>, was the daughter of Major Charles Henry Cobbe and granddaughter of General George Cobbe (<i>b</i>. 1782), who was descended on the paternal side from Charles Cobbe (1687&#150;1765), archbishop of Dublin (1742&#150;3). She was the second cousin of Frances Power Cobbe (1822&#150;1904). As a young woman she was a gifted pianist; she studied at the Royal Conservatory of Music and composed the hymn &#145;Oh, for the wings of a dove&#146;, which was performed at Westminster Abbey and placed on the regular list of hymns approved for performance. An injury to her hands, from over-practising, turned her talents to writing. On 21 November 1885 she married John Childe Heaton Armstrong but was widowed, childless, within the year.<br>
<br>Circumstances dictated that Lucie Armstrong earn her living, so she turned to journalism. For many years she wrote the etiquette column for the <span class="italic">Lady's Pictorial</span> and the &#145;Turnovers&#146; column for <span class="italic">The Globe</span>, and did interviews for <span class="italic">The Sketch</span> and the <span class="italic">Pall Mall Budget</span>. Using the pseudonym Zingara (Italian for Gypsy), she wrote a &#145;Lady's Letter&#146;, which appeared weekly in a number of provincial journals, and did a series of letters on etiquette for <span class="italic">Hearth and Home</span>. She also published in <span class="italic">Womanhood</span> and was the etiquette columnist for the <span class="italic">Woman's Signal</span> from 1895 to 1899.<br>
<br>Mrs Armstrong's first book was a group of stories for children, <span class="italic">Doll Stories</span> (n.d.), followed by a series of etiquette books: <span class="italic">Good Form</span> (1889); <span class="italic">The Etiquette of Party-Giving</span> (1893); <span class="italic">Letters to a Bride</span> (1896); <span class="italic">Etiquette in Entertaining</span> (1903), followed by a second, posthumous edition (1908); and <span class="italic">Etiquette-Up-To-Date</span>, published posthumously (1908). She was known to be gay in spirits, yet earnest in thought; it was said that &#145;Politeness in her is not insincerity, graciousness is not weakness, etiquette is not formality&#146;  (<span class="italic">Woman's Signal</span>). She died on 2 May 1907 at Camberwell House Asylum, London.<br>
<br>Rosemary T. Van Arsdel </div>
