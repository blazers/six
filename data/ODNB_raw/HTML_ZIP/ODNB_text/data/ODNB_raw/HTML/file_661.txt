<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Armstrong,  John</span>  (1771&#150;1797), <span class="occ">journalist and poet</span>, was born in Leith on 7 June 1771, the son of James Armstrong, a barber of that town, and his wife, Mary Lautie. After attending Leith grammar school and the high school in Edinburgh, he entered Edinburgh University, where he graduated MA on 3 September 1792. In 1789 he published <span class="italic">Juvenile poems, with remarks on poetry, and a dissertation on the best means of punishing and preventing crimes</span>. These poems, if stilted in style and hackneyed in sentiment, are characterized by general good taste and some artistic finish. Although the <span class="italic">New Catalogue of Living English Authors</span> (1799) later lamented their publication, it obtained for him the honour of being invited to compose the lyrics used in connection with the ceremony of laying the foundation-stone of the Edinburgh University buildings.<br>
<br>While tutor in a family in Edinburgh, Armstrong pursued the theological studies necessary to qualify him to become a preacher in the Church of Scotland, but in 1790 he moved to London, where he obtained employment on one of the daily papers at a small weekly salary. In 1790 he published, under the pseudonym Albert, <span class="italic">Confidential Letters from the Sorrows of Werter</span>; and again as Albert in 1791 he published a collection of poems under the title <span class="italic">Sonnets from Shakespeare</span>. His literary prospects continued gradually to improve, and he was in receipt of a considerable income, when his health began suddenly to give way. He retired to Leith, where he died on 21 July 1797.<br>
<br>T. F. Henderson<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Grant P. Cerny </div>
