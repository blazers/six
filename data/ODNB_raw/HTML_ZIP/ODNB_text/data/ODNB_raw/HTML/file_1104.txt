<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Baird,  William</span>  (1803&#150;1872), <span class="occ">zoologist and physician</span>, was born at Eccles, Berwickshire, on 8 January 1803, a younger son of the Revd James Baird (<i>d</i>. 1814), then minister of Eccles, and his wife, Sarah. His older brother was the evangelical preacher <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/1103/', "John Baird (1799&#150;1861)")</script>. He was educated at the high school, Edinburgh, and studied medicine at Edinburgh, Dublin, and Paris. In 1823, having already visited the West Indies and South America, Baird became a surgeon with the East India Company. While in that company's service he visited India and China five times, availing himself of every possibility to pursue his interest in natural history.<br>
<br>Baird was a zoologist of considerable ability, and communicated several papers to the Zoological and Linnean societies. In 1829, following his return to Britain, he helped to establish the Berwickshire Naturalists' Club, which served to extend the pursuit of natural science. After some years in private medical practice, in 1841 he accepted an appointment in the zoological department of the British Museum. There he had charge of the molluscan collection and, though he published little on the group, his curatorial efforts succeeded in making the collection available for study by others.<br>
<br>In 1850 Baird's important work on the natural history of British entomostraca was published by the Ray Society, and in 1858 he published his <span class="italic">Cyclopaedia of the Natural Sciences</span>. In 1862 he brought out a memoir of his brother, John, who died in the previous year. Baird was a contributor to a number of journals, including Loudon's <span class="italic">Magazine of Natural History</span>, <span class="italic">The Zoologist</span>, and the <span class="italic">Proceedings</span> of the Zoological Society, and he was elected FRS in 1867. He died on 27 January 1872 at his home, 38 Burlington Road, Westbourne Park, London, and was buried in Kensal Green cemetery. His wife, Mary Baird, survived him.<br>
<br>R. E. Thompson<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Peter Osborne </div>
