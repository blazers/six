<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Balatine,  Alan</span>  (<em>supp. </em><em>fl. </em>1520&#150;1560), <span class="occ">supposed historian</span>, appears in Thomas Dempster's <span class="italic">Historia ecclesiastica gentis Scotorum</span> as one of the distinguished writers of the Scots. According to Dempster, Balatine was born in Scotland but spent most of his life as a writer and teacher in Germany, where he died some time after 1560. Dempster claims that Balatine was the author of at least three works, &#145;De astrolabio&#146;, &#145;De terra mensura&#146;, and &#145;Chronicon universalis&#146;. The last of these may have been used as a source by Edward Hall in his <span class="italic">Chronicle</span> of 1542, as he lists one &#145;Balantyne&#146; among his &#145;Englishe Writers&#146;  (Hall, 4), &#145;Englishe&#146; referring to the language of the text rather than the nationality of the writer. However, it is probable that this Balantyne is in fact <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/2045/', "John Bellenden")</script>, whose translation of Hector Boece's <span class="italic">Scotorum historia</span> was printed about 1540, and which seems to have been widely available in England. Other writers offer slightly different interpretations: John Pits, in his <span class="italic">Relationum historicarum de rebus Anglicis</span> (1619) claims Balatine (without a first name) as English, while Thomas Tanner (<span class="italic">Bibliotheca Britannico-Hibernica</span>, 1748) simply repeats what his predecessors have said. There is, therefore, no conclusive proof that Balatine had an existence independent of that of John Bellenden, though it is possible that his works, which would give some proof, simply have not survived.<br>
<br>Nicola Royan </div>
