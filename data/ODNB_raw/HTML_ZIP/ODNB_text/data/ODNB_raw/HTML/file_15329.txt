<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Kemp,  John</span>  (1665&#150;1717), <span class="occ">antiquary</span>, details of whose origins and upbringing are unknown, was possessed of private means, and resided in the parish of St Martin-in-the-Fields, Westminster. He owned a fine museum of antiquities, chiefly formed by the French educational writer Jean Gailhard, who was governor to George, first Baron Carteret (1667&#150;1695). Gailhard sold it to Lord Carteret for an annuity of &pound;200, and Kemp subsequently purchased it after Carteret's death in 1695. Kemp was elected a fellow of the Royal Society on 20 March 1712 and died, unmarried, on 19 September 1717. By his will he directed that the museum (with books) should be offered to Robert Harley, earl of Oxford, or his son for &pound;2000. However, the proposal was declined. The lexicographer Robert Ainsworth drew up an elaborate account of Kemp's antiquities, <span class="italic">Monumenta vetustatis Kempiana, ex vetustis scriptoribus illustrata, eosque vicissim illustrantia</span> (2 pts, 1719&#150;20). John Ward, professor of rhetoric at Gresham College, provided Ainsworth with descriptions of the statues and lares, with the discourse <span class="italic">De vasis et lucernis, de amuletis, de annulis et fibulis</span>, and with the <span class="italic">Commentarius de Asse et partibus ejus</span>, printed in 1719.<br>
<br>Kemp's collection was eventually sold by auction at The Phoenix tavern in Pall Mall, Westminster, on 23&#150;5 and 27 March 1721, in 293 lots, for &pound;1090 8<i>s</i>. 6<i>d</i>. Six ancient inscriptions, bought by the antiquary Richard Rawlinson, form part of his collection at the Bodleian Library, Oxford, and appear in Richard Chandler's <span class="italic">Marmora Oxoniensia</span> (1763).<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Philip Carter </div>
