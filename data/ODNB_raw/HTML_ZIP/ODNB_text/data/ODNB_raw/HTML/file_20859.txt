<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Osbald</span>  (<em>d.</em> 799), <span class="occ">king of Northumbria</span>, may be identical with the ealdorman (<i>dux</i>) of that name who in 780 (the date 779 given by the <span class="roman">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</span> is probably incorrect) gathered an army with another ealdorman called &AElig;thelheard and burned King &AElig;lfwald I's patrician (<i>patricius</i>) Bearn at an unidentified place called &#145;Seletune&#146;. The burning happened on 24 December, so presumably Bearn was incinerated in his house while celebrating Christmas. At some time after 793, Alcuin wrote to King &AElig;thelred I (<i>d</i>. 796) and Osbald, now designated as his patrician, and warned them against following those kings and princes who &#145;perished because of their injustices, rapines, and uncleanliness of life&#146;  (D&uuml;mmler, no. 18). In 796, following the murder of &AElig;thelred I, certain nobles (presumably those responsible for the murder) made Osbald king, but after only twenty-seven days he was &#145;deprived of the society of the royal household and of the princes&#146;  (Symeon of Durham, <span class="italic">Opera</span>, 2.57) and forced into flight, first to Lindisfarne and then, in company with some of the monks, by sea to Pictland. Following his deposition, perhaps in the same year but possibly in 798, Alcuin again wrote to him reminding him that more than two years previously he had sworn a vow to give up secular life and enter religion and urging him to fulfil that vow. Apparently he did so, for he died an abbot in 799, when he was buried in St Peter's Minster, York. Osbald's origins are unknown, but his family had clearly been ruthless contenders for royal power, since Alcuin urges him to &#145;think how much blood of kings, princes and people has been shed through you and your family&#146; (D&uuml;mmler, no. 109; <span class="italic">English Historical Documents</span>, 1, no. 200).<br>
<br>David Rollason </div>
