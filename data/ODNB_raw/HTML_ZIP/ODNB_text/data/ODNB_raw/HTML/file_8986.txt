<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Evans,  Thomas Simpson</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1777, <em>d.</em> 1818), <span class="occ">mathematician</span>, was baptized at Llangattock juxta Caerleon, Monmouthshire, on 12 August 1777, the eldest son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/8973/', "Lewis Evans (1755&#150;1827)")</script>, the curate of Ashbury, and his wife, Ann Norman. He was named after Thomas Simpson, the mathematician. On 12 February 1796 he was appointed assistant to Nevil Maskelyne at the Royal Observatory, Greenwich. However, shortly after marrying Deborah Mascall, governess to Maskelyne's daughter Margaret and daughter of John Mascall of Ashford, Kent, on 7 June 1798, he left the Royal Observatory. He took charge of a private observatory at Blackheath belonging to William Larkins, formerly accountant-general to the East India Company at Bengal. Some time after the death of Larkins on 24 April 1800, he was appointed mathematical master under his father at the Royal Military Academy, Woolwich. There he continued until 1810, when he accepted the mastership of the mathematical school at New Charlton, near Woolwich. He left this position in 1813 to become master of mathematics at Christ's Hospital, London. He appears to have held the degree of LLD and to have been a fellow of the Linnean Society. He and his wife had five children: Thomas Simpson Evans (1798&#150;1880), vicar of St Leonard, Shoreditch; Aspasia Evans (1799&#150;1876); Herbert Norman Evans MD (1802&#150;1877), a great book collector; Arthur Benoni Evans (<i>d</i>. 1838); and Lewis Evans (1815&#150;1869), headmaster of Sandbach Free Grammar School, Cheshire.<br>
<br>Evans died in London on 28 October 1818, aged forty-one. He left a completed translation of Antonio Cagnoli's <span class="italic">Trigonometria piana e sferica</span> besides other translations from foreign scientific works and a vast collection of unfinished papers in several branches of science. He also contributed various articles to the <span class="italic">Philosophical Magazine</span>. Apart from these he wrote a comprehensive account of the Royal Observatory, its instruments, and the role of the assistant, and brief biographies of the astronomers royal, all of which appeared in John Evans's <span class="italic">Juvenile Tourist</span> (3rd edn, 1810). His library was considered one of the most valuable collections of mathematical and philosophical works in the kingdom.<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> H. K. Higton </div>
