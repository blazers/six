<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Davy,  Charles</span>  (1722/3&#150;1797), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman and writer on music</span>, was born in Norwich, the son of Charles Davy of Hatton Garden, London; his mother may have been Sarah Davy. He was educated at Mr Haslehurst's school at Market Rasen, Lincolnshire, Mr Pigge's school at Watton, Hertfordshire, and Mr Brett's school at Scarning in Norfolk. On 3 May 1739 he matriculated aged sixteen at Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge, where he graduated BA (1742) and MA (1748). In 1764 he was instituted to the rectory of Topcroft, Norfolk, to the rectory of Benacre, Suffolk, two years later, and to that of Onehouse in the same county in 1776.<br>
<br>In 1772 Davy published his <span class="italic">Conjectural Observations on the Origin and Progress of Alphabetical Writing</span> followed by <span class="italic">Letters Addressed Chiefly to a Young Gentleman</span> (1787), which included a discussion of Ptolemy's doctrine on music. This work came after his and Christopher Smear's proposed, but unpublished, &#145;Essay upon the principles and powers of vocal and instrumental music&#146;.<br>
<br>Davy was married to Mary, daughter of Thomas Sheppard; they had two sons, Charles and Frederick. Charles became a fellow of Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge, vicar of Wickham Market, Suffolk (1803), rector of Barking and of Combs, in the same county (1818), and died on 7 March 1836 aged seventy-nine. With Frederick he published a translation of M. T. Bourrit's <span class="italic">A Relation of a Journey to the Glaciers in the Dutchy of Savoy</span> (1775). The elder Charles Davy died at Onehouse on 8 April 1797 and was buried in the chancel of the parish church.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Philip Carter </div>
