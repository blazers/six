<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Shipton,  William</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1640?, <em>d.</em> in or after 1672), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was probably the William Shipton, son of Thomas Shipton of Lythe, Yorkshire, baptized on 2 August 1640 at Whitby. A Richard Shipton, son of Thomas, had been baptized there three years before. William Shipton was admitted pensioner at Clare College, Cambridge, on 28 April 1657. He matriculated that year. On 6 April 1659 he migrated to Magdalene College, Cambridge, and graduated BA in 1660 and MA in 1664.<br>
<br>In 1659 Shipton published a collection of poetry and prose under the title of <span class="italic">Dia: a Poem</span>. The introductory portion extends to thirty pages, comprising a dedication &#145;to the Truly Noble Edward Trotter Esquire&#146;, and commendatory verses by &#145;Jo. Cooke, Gent., Aulae Clar.&#146;, and by Richard Shipton (possibly the poet's brother). Besides a series of poems in praise of his mistress Dia, the volume contains elegies on Thomas Shipton (who was drowned) and on Lord Sheffield; poems on gunpowder treason and on Robert Wilson (a noted musician); and a prose essay entitled &#145;Cupid made to see and love made lovely&#146;. His poems are full of extravagant and complex metaphors, and his prose is even more fantastic.<br>
<br>Shipton was ordained a priest on 27 May 1661, and was appointed vicar of Escrick, Yorkshire, in 1663 (retaining the appointment until 1672) and vicar of Lythe in 1664. One of his sermons was published in 1668 under the title <span class="italic">The Mystery of Afflictions</span>.<br>
<br>E. I. Carlyle<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Joanna Moody </div>
