<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Cooper,  Elizabeth</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1865&#150;1874), <span class="occ">historian</span>, made her d&eacute;but as a writer in 1865 with the publication of <span class="italic">A Popular History of America</span>. Her subsequent works were biographies. In 1866 she published <span class="italic">The Life and Letters of Lady Arbella Stuart</span> (2 vols.), a laudatory portrait of an altruistic protestant heroine destroyed by court intrigues. It was based on original documents, some of which belonged to Sir Thomas Phillipps, the famous bibliophile. A pause in publication ensued, probably because Cooper became ill around 1869 (in her dedication to her next work she described herself as &#145;the hapless victim of a neglected and torturing malady&#146;). In 1874 she published <span class="italic">The Life of Thomas Wentworth, Earl of Stafford</span>, the first scholarly biography of the seventeenth-century statesman. According to the reviewer for <span class="italic">The Spectator</span>, the work showed that she had &#145;industry and care, but wants the grasp of imagination which is needed for her difficult subject&#146;. He concluded, however, that the work had &#145;real merits&#146;, praising in particular her account of Wentworth's government of Ireland  (<span class="italic">Spectator</span>, 471&#150;2). Very little else is known of Cooper's life. She appears to have lived mainly in or near London, and was unmarried at the time of her publications.<br>
<br>Rosemary Mitchell </div>
