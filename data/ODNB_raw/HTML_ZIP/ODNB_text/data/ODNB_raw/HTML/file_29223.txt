<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Whitaker,  John</span>  (1776?&#150;1847), <span class="occ">composer</span>, was a teacher of music, and organist at the church of St Clement, Eastcheap, in London. He also became a partner in the music-publishing firm of Button and Whitaker at St Paul's Churchyard in the City. In 1818 Whitaker collected and published two volumes of sacred music for four voices entitled <span class="italic">The Seraph</span>, which included many original pieces. However, he made his name chiefly through the composition of numerous popular dramatic pieces and occasional songs introduced in musical plays at the principal London theatres between 1807 and 1825. He wrote music with William Reeve for <span class="italic">Who's to Have Her?</span> (1813) and joined Henry Bishop in contributing some songs to a stage version of Scott's <span class="italic">Guy Mannering</span> (1816), including a number that were favourites in their day: &#145;Oh, slumber, my darling&#146;, &#145;Dog Tray&#146;, and &#145;O say not woman's heart is bought&#146;. He also composed the music for several pantomines, including <span class="italic">The Weird Sisters, or, The Thane and the Throne</span>, based on Shakespeare's <span class="italic">Macbeth</span>. This was produced at Sadler's Wells Theatre on Easter Monday, 12 April 1819, and contained the then famous clown's song &#145;Hot Codlins&#146;, written for Joseph Grimaldi. Other songs that were popular were &#145;Fly away, dove&#146;, sung by Miss Cawse on her d&eacute;but in <span class="italic">The Hebrew Family</span>, &#145;Go, rover, go&#146;, &#145;Remember me&#146;, &#145;The Little Farmer's Daughter&#146;, and &#145;The Lily that Blooms&#146;. Comic songs, such as &#145;Darby Kelly&#146; and &#145;Paddy Carey&#146;, adapted from Irish airs, helped to broaden Whitaker's appeal. Additional works include the glee &#145;Winds, Gently Whisper&#146;, some anthems, music for English versions of the odes of Anacreon and of Aesop's fables, and <span class="italic">Twelve Pedal Exercises</span> for organ. Whitaker died at his home, Thavies Inn, Holborn, London, on 4 December 1847.<br>
<br>David J. Golby </div>
