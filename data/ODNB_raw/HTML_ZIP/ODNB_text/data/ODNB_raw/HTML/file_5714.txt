<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Clowes,  Butler</span>  (<em>d.</em> <em>c.</em>1788), <span class="occ">engraver and print seller</span>, lived, and kept a print shop, in London at 18 Gutter Lane, Cheapside, from about 1760 until his death (his address has been recorded on prints by James Watson and others). The business was subsequently carried on by a John Clowes, presumably his son, at the same address. Clowes engraved a number of small-scale, modest mezzotint portraits after his own designs, which usually show the sitter half-length and enclosed in an oval or roundel. These include a self-portrait in a cap of 1771, and portraits of John Glas, founder of the Sandemanian sect, of the jeweller Thomas Liddiard, of a Mr Atwood, writing master to Christ's Hospital, and of other &#145;middling&#146; folk drawn from his family, acquaintance, or general social milieu.<br>
<br>Larger, more ambitious mezzotints by Clowes consist of theatrical scenes and fancy pictures. His best-known theatrical subject is probably the portrait of the playwright Charles Dibdin, <span class="italic">Mr Dibdin in the Character of Mungo, in the Celebrated Opera of The Padlock</span>, after his own design, published in 1769 and later retouched and republished by Carington Bowles. Clowes's fancy pictures include a Chardinesque <span class="italic">School Boy</span> at a table with writing instruments, after William Elmer of Dublin, and a female figure on a terrace engaged in <span class="italic">Domestick Employment, Starching</span> of 1769, probably after, or at least reminiscent of, the works of Henry Morland. A large mezzotint after Colson of a man firing a miniature cannon, entitled <span class="italic">The Engineer</span>, is described in a pencil note on the print as &#145;very scarce&#146;; an impression is held in the British Museum. At the Free Society of Artists, between 1768 and 1773, Clowes exhibited a total of sixteen mezzotints of a largely comical or theatrical content, such as <span class="italic">The Hen-Pecked Husband</span> and <span class="italic">The Dying Usurer, or, The Wife the Physician</span>, after William Dawes (both 1768), and <span class="italic">The Female Bruisers</span>, after John Collet (1771), as well as portraits (1771, 1772, 1773), a print after Heemskerck (1772), and a scene from rural life (1769).<br>
<br>According to a contemporary trade directory, Mortimer's <span class="italic">Universal Director</span> of 1763, before his activity as a mezzotint engraver from the late 1760s, Clowes seems to have been known primarily as an &#145;engraver of heraldry, ornaments &amp;c&#146;, and the production of furniture designs and engraved ornament continued to occupy him at least until the late 1770s. He etched the majority (forty-one of fifty-three plates) of untitled designs for carved furniture by Thomas Johnson (1758); this collection was later reissued by Robert Sayer under the misleading title <span class="italic">One Hundred and Fifty New Designs</span> (1761). The influence of Johnson's vivid, spiky rococo style is very obvious in a set of nine heraldic bookplates designed and etched by Clowes about 1760 for the dukes of Beaufort, Bolton, Cleveland, Grafton, Leeds, Norfolk, Richmond, St Albans, and Somerset. In 1762 Clowes etched nine plates for carvers' pieces for the third edition of Chippendale's <span class="italic">The Gentleman and Cabinet-Maker's Director</span>, and a decade later he produced, after his own designs, a set of eight plates with six monograms (AA-ZA) each. In a complete stylistic departure (together with a J. A. Clowes), he engraved a set of twenty small-scale designs, moderately neo-classical and relatively simple, for chimney-pieces after the sculptor Richard W. Westmacott (<i>d</i>. 1808), which were published in 1777.<br>
<br>Clowes married Susanna Matthews on 7 January 1753. He died about 1788. A copy after his 1771 self-portrait shows &#145;a man past the prime of life, with a round, jovial, and doubtless rubicund countenance&#146;  (<span class="italic">DNB</span>). Henry Bromley's unfavourable account of Clowes's work, published soon after the latter's death, influenced the majority of nineteenth-century critics&#151;with the notable exception of John Chaloner Smith&#151;who considered Clowes's mezzotints amateurish and incompetent; his engraved ornament (and thus his brilliant etching technique and his versatility) were ignored.<br>
<br>Anne Puetz </div>
