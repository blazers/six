<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Philipps,  Thomas</span>  (1774&#150;1841), <span class="occ">singer</span>, was born in London. He came from a Monmouthshire family. His first appearance was on 10 May 1796 at Covent Garden Theatre, when he played Philippo in Samuel Arnold's <span class="italic">The Castle of Andalusia</span>; his tenor voice was pronounced by critics to be tolerable in tone. After receiving some lessons from Arnold he improved rapidly, and in 1801 he was engaged at the Crow Street Theatre in Dublin. Back in London, on 26 June 1809 he appeared at the English Opera House in <span class="italic">Up All Night</span>. In the same theatre in 1811 he took part in Henry Bishop's <span class="italic">The Maniac</span>, and other pieces&#151;<span class="italic">The Peasant Boy</span>, <span class="italic">Plots</span>, and <span class="italic">M.P.</span> A tour in America is said to have enriched him by &pound;7000. In 1826 he was regarded by Michael Kelly as the best acting singer on the English stage. Philipps retired early from the stage, taught singing, and composed ballads. He was a professional member of the Catch Club in 1828. He died at Northwich, Cheshire, on 27 October 1841 as a result of falling from a moving train.<br>
<br>Philipps published <span class="italic">Elementary Principles and Practices for Singing</span> (1826), compiled <span class="italic">The Mentor's Harp, a Collection of Moral Ballads</span> by T. H. Bayly, and produced many songs and ballads, the best-known of which was the three-part &#145;Crows in a Cornfield&#146;, written about 1830. He also arranged Thomas Linley's <span class="italic">The Duenna</span>.<br>
<br>L. M. Middleton<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Anne Pimlott Baker </div>
