<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Odingsells,  Gabriel</span>  (1690?&#150;1734), <span class="occ">playwright</span>, was probably born in the parish of St Dionis Backchurch, London, the third child of Gabriel Odingsells (<i>bap</i>. 1653) and Mary Love (1660?&#150;1690). He was admitted to Pembroke College, Oxford, matriculating on 23 April 1707, but left without taking a degree. He took residence in London, with the aim of making a living as a dramatic writer. His first piece, <span class="italic">The Bath Unmask'd</span>, was first performed at Lincoln's Inn Fields on 27 February 1725 and ran for a week. The play, which has characters with such names as Sir Captious Whiffle and Pander, was described as &#145;an indifferent comedy&#151;he meant by the title to imply that he had described the humours of Bath&#146;  (Genest, <span class="italic">Eng. stage</span>, 3.168).<br>
<br>At the end of the same year, on 8 December, a second dramatic piece by Odingsells, <span class="italic">The Capricious Lovers</span>, was first performed at Lincoln's Inn Fields. It contains Mock-Youth, a colonel who is &#145;twice frightened by the ghost of a lawyer who had forged a will in his favour&#146;. Genest comments:<blockquote>this is a poor comedy by Odingsells, there is however a considerable degree of humour in the character of Mrs Mince-Mode, who (as the author expresses it) grows sick at the sight of a man, and refines upon the significancy of phrases till she resolves common conversation into obscenity. (Genest, <span class="italic">Eng. stage</span>, 3.178)</blockquote>Odingsells's final work, <span class="italic">The Bays' Opera</span>, was produced at Drury Lane for three nights from 30 March 1730. It concerns the threat to Tragedo, the lawful heir to the empire of wit, by a usurper, one Cantato. The work is prefaced by an essay &#145;concerning that way of writing&#146;  (Mottley, 271). Genest's verdict was that it was &#145;very dull&#151;the design is much better than the execution&#146;  (Genest, <span class="italic">Eng. stage</span>, 3.258).<br>
<br>Odingsells became mentally unstable, and took his own life by hanging on 10 February 1734 at his house in Thatched Court, Westminster. A publication appeared posthumously in 1742: <span class="italic">Monumental inscriptions, or, A curious collection of near five hundred of the most remarkable epitaphs &hellip; collected by the late ingenious Gabriel Odinsells</span> [<i>sic</i>].<br>
<br>William R. Jones </div>
