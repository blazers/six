<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Evans,  John</span> <span class="st"> [<em>pseuds. </em>I. D. Ffraid, Adda Jones]</span>  (1814&#150;1875), <span class="occ">Welsh scholar and businessman</span>, was born at T&#375; Mawr, Llansanffraid Glan Conwy, on 23 July 1814, the son of David and Grace Evans. His education consisted of two periods, at Abergele in 1824 and at Wrexham in 1830. He started work in his uncle's shop when he was eleven, and by the age of sixteen he had written <span class="italic">Hanes yr Iddewon</span> (1831). He then edited <span class="italic">Difyrwch bechgyn Glanau Conway</span> (1835), a volume of poetry which included some of his own work. On 9 November 1836 he married Ann (1817&#150;1850), daughter of Thomas Williams, a grocer of Llansanffraid. They went on to have six children.<br>
<br>Evans continued his study, and his effort as a lexicographer is found in <span class="italic">Geiriadur Saesneg&#150;Cymraeg</span> (1847), which included a glossary of herbs. His first wife died in 1850, and in 1853 he married Hannah, who survived him. From January 1869 to December 1874 Evans contributed a series of 483 satirical letters, published as &#145;Llythyrau Adda Jones&#146; in <span class="italic">Baner ac Amserau Cymru</span>. His lasting contribution, however, is his translation of <span class="italic">Paradise Lost</span> (<span class="italic">Coll Gwynfa</span>, 1865), although he also translated <span class="italic">The Life of Dick Turpin</span> (<span class="italic">Bywyd Turpin Leidr</span>, 1835) and Young's &#145;Night Thoughts&#146;  (NL Wales, MS 4757), which remain unpublished.<br>
<br>Evans combined his literary work with a thriving business career, eventually taking over his uncle's shop and running four other businesses&#151;a nail factory, a sawmill, a watermill, and another shop. Ordained in the Calvinistic Methodist church in 1853, he never accepted a call, but preached regularly, and also campaigned for the Liberation Society and for temperance, and was an enthusiastic attender of eisteddfodau. His bardic name was given credence when he was accepted into the order of bards at the national eisteddfod held at Denbigh, on 7&#150;10 August 1860. He was presented with a national testimonial in 1869. John Evans died of pneumonia at Llansanffraid Glan Conwy on 4 March 1875, and his remains were interred in the burial-ground of his native parish on 10 March.<br>
<br>D. Ben Rees </div>
