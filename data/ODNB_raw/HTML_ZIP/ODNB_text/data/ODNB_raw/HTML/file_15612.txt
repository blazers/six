<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Kinglake,  Robert</span>  (1765&#150;1842), <span class="occ">physician</span>, graduated MD at G&ouml;ttingen, and also studied at Edinburgh. After practising for some years as a surgeon at Chipping Norton, Oxfordshire, he moved to Somerset, first to Chilton upon Polden and then in 1802 to Taunton. At Taunton he frequently attended public meetings and made a number of speeches in support of the first <span class="roman">Reform Bill</span>. He was a member of the Royal Medical Society of Edinburgh, the Physical Society of G&ouml;ttingen, and other learned societies. He married Joanna, daughter of Anthony Apperlay, of Herefordshire. They had at least two sons.<br>
<br>Kinglake attracted considerable attention through his writings on gout, in which he advocated the cooling treatment. His first papers on the subject appeared in 1801 and 1803 in the <span class="italic">Medical and Physical Journal</span> (nos. 33 and 48). His views were opposed by William Wadd, W. Perry, John Hunt, J. King, and others. He replied to his antagonists in: <span class="italic">A Dissertation on Gout</span> (1804, with appendix), and in a <span class="italic">Reply to Mr Edlin's Two Cases of Gout</span> (1804). He also published in 1820 his &#145;Observations on the medical effects of digitalis&#146; in the <span class="italic">Medical and Physical Journal</span>. In Robert Macnish's <span class="italic">Anatomy of Drunkenness</span> (1827) there is a short article by the author on Kinglake's experiment with ether.<br>
<br>Kinglake died on 26 September 1842 at West Monkton rectory, near Taunton, the home of his son, the Revd W. C. Kinglake.<br>
<br>[Anon.]<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Claire L. Nutt </div>
