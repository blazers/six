<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hewlett,  Ebenezer</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1737&#150;1747), <span class="occ">religious writer</span>, lived at the New Pales in Sun Street, without Bishopsgate, London, and was for a time employed by the East India Company. Between 1737 and 1747 he wrote and published approximately twenty theological tracts, the doctrines of which, he says, brought him &#145;only poverty, disgrace, and loss of friends&#146;. Hewlett's pamphlets, which often attacked trinitarian orthodoxy, include <span class="italic">A Harmony in Christianity</span>, a response to the deist Thomas Chubb's <span class="italic">True Gospel of Jesus Christ</span> (1738); <span class="italic">The Deist Turned Christian</span> (1740); and <span class="italic">Miracles Real Evidence</span> (1741), which was written against Chubb's <span class="italic">Discourse on Miracles</span> (1741). He published answers to the works of many theologians, most notably Edward Elwall, the nonconformist James Foster, Samuel Clarke, and Caleb Fleming, and did not refrain from joining in debates over Baptist and Methodist doctrines.<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Adam Jacob Levin </div>
