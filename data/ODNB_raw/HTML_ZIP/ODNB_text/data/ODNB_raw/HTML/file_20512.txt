<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Cathal mac Conchobair</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1010), <span class="occ">king of Connacht</span>, was a member of the S&iacute;l Muiredaig branch of U&iacute; Bri&uacute;in A&iacute;. He was son of the Conchobar from whom the U&iacute; Chonchobair of Connacht, who ruled as kings of that province for much of the rest of the middle ages, take their name; though, as Conchobar's son, he was not <i>ua</i> (or <i>&Oacute;</i>) Conchobair (that is, grandson of Conchobar), but <i>mac</i> Conchobair. When Cathal's father, Conchobar son of Tadc, died in 973, the kingship of Connacht was assumed by a certain Cathal son of Tadc, a first cousin once removed according to an eighteenth-century king-list  (O'Flaherty, 133). However, he was killed by the Cen&eacute;l n&Eacute;ogain (line 5768 of the <span class="roman">Book of Leinster</span> says after a reign of three days) in the battle of C&eacute;is Corainn. Cathal himself then presumably succeeded to the kingship, although nothing whatever is known of him until 1001, when he and the reigning high-king, M&aacute;el Sechnaill mac Domnaill of the southern U&iacute; N&eacute;ill, built a causeway across the Shannon at Athlone. M&aacute;el Sechnaill invaded Connacht at least three times during Cathal's reign, in 985, 992, and 998, but only formally received the hostages of Connacht in 998, when they were handed to him by Brian B&oacute;ruma, king of Munster. In 1002, when Brian assumed the high-kingship, he himself is recorded as receiving the hostages of Connacht. Brian married Cathal's daughter, Dubchoblaig, who predeceased him in 1009, apparently childless. Cathal himself died in the following year. The annals say that he died in penitence, perhaps at Clonmacnoise, since the late king-list states that his death came two years after taking orders (<i>a dhul a nord crabhaidh</i>). He was succeeded as king of Connacht by his son, Tadc in Eich Gil.<br>
<br>Se&aacute;n Duffy </div>
