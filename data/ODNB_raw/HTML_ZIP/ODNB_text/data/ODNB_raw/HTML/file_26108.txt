<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Spence,  Elizabeth Isabella</span>  (1768&#150;1832), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was born on 12 January 1768 at Dunkeld, Perthshire, the only child of Dr James Spence (<i>d</i>. 1786), physician at Dunkeld, and his wife, Elizabeth (<i>d</i>. 1777), youngest daughter of George Fordyce, provost of Aberdeen (<i>d</i>. 1733), and sister of James Fordyce (1720&#150;1796). Losing her parents early, Elizabeth Spence went to live in London with an uncle and aunt, and was by their death left destitute of relatives. She had already begun writing as a pastime, and now carried it on as a livelihood, producing works of fiction and travel narratives. Her first novel, published in 1799, was <span class="italic">Helen Sinclair</span>. Her books of travel include <span class="italic">Summer Excursions through Part of England and Wales</span>, published in 1809, and <span class="italic">Sketches of the Present Manners, Custom, and Scenery of Scotland</span>, of which the second edition bears the date 1811. The latter work was ridiculed in <span class="italic">Blackwood's Magazine</span> (3, 1818, 428&#150;38) in an article entitled &#145;Miss Spence and the Bagman&#146;.<br>
<br>Among Elizabeth Spence's friends were Lady Anne Barnard, Elizabeth Ogilvy Benger, the Porters, Laetitia Elizabeth Landon, and Sir Humphry Davy. Her other works include <span class="italic">Nobility of the Heart</span> (1804), <span class="italic">The Wedding Day</span> (1807), <span class="italic">The Curate and his Daughter: a Cornish Tale</span> (1813), <span class="italic">The Spanish Guitar</span> (1815), <span class="italic">Letters from the North Highlands</span> (1816), <span class="italic">A Traveller's Tale of Last Century</span> (1819), and <span class="italic">How to be Rid of a Wife</span> (1823). Elizabeth Isabella Spence died, unmarried, at Chelsea on 27 July 1832 of a paralytic stroke.<br>
<br>Elizabeth Lee<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Rebecca Mills </div>
