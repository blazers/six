<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Rastrick,  William</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1697, <em>d.</em> 1752), <span class="occ">Presbyterian preacher</span>, was baptized on 14 March 1697 at Spalding, Lincolnshire, a son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/23152/', "John Rastrick (1650&#150;1727)")</script>, then a nonconformist minister in Rotherham, and his third wife, Elizabeth Horn (<i>d</i>. 1740). He was probably educated by his father, on whose death he succeeded as minister to King's Lynn's divided Presbyterian congregation, some of whom were combatively hyper-Calvinist. By disposition retiring, he eschewed ordination, although this meant exchanging, at communion times, with the minister at Wisbech, 13 miles away. Eventually the hyper-Calvinists seceded from his congregation, reportedly in 1744, to form an independent body that about 1777, after his death, became a Particular Baptist church, with William Richards (1749&#150;1818) as minister. Richards, who arrived in Lynn in 1776, when William Rastrick's immediate successor, Anthony Mayhew, was in post, was friendly with Mayhew's own successor and son-in-law, William Warner, and portrays Rastrick sympathetically in his <span class="italic">History of Lynn</span> (1812). His character, says Richards, was virtuous, his interests scholarly; and because of his local pre-eminence in knowledge, &#145;especially the mathematics&#146;, &#145;his superior skill and judgement would accordingly be resorted to on such difficult occasions as required extraordinary scientific expertness or accuracy&#146;  (W. Richards, 2.1067).<br>
<br>By continuing a preacher, Rastrick had fulfilled the conditions of his father's complex will and so inherited John Rastrick's mathematical instruments, telescopes, and library. He sent the Royal Society's vice-president, Martin Folkes, who had local connections, his Latin report of displays during 1723&#150;7 of the aurora borealis; this, &#145;Registrum observationum de lumine boreali per quadriennium&#146;, appeared in the <span class="italic">Philosophical Transactions</span>, 34 (1727), 255&#150;6. Rastrick's plan of Lynn, <span class="italic">Ichnographia burgi perantiqui Lennae regis in agro Norfolciensi accurate delineata</span>, published in 1725 and dedicated to Sir Robert Walpole, Lynn's MP, is impressive and important. It includes his prospect of Lynn from the west and his drawings of Henry Bell's custom house (1685) and market cross (1710). &#145;Index eorum theologorum aliorumque &hellip; alphabetico ordine ac secundum gradus suos dispositus&#146;, Rastrick's manuscript index of ejected ministers of 1662, used material from Edmund Calamy's <span class="italic">Account</span> of them, Calamy's <span class="italic">Continuation of the Account</span>, and from the <i>valor ecclesiasticus</i> (1535), and was employed in <span class="italic">The Nonconformist's Memorial</span> of Samuel Palmer, who had access to the copy Rastrick had presented to Calamy. A holograph of the &#145;Index&#146;, dated 1734, is in Lynn's central library.<br>
<br>Apparently never married, Rastrick lived in his father's house in Spinner Lane. He died in early August 1752, and was buried on 9 August in Lynn's St Nicholas's Chapel.<br>
<br>J. A. Oddy </div>
