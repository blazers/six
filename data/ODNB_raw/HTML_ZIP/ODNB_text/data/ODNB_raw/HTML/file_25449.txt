<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Sherwood <span class="st"> [Shirwood]</span>,  William  of</span>  (<em>d.</em> in or before 1272), <span class="occ">logician and ecclesiastic</span>, was presumably born in Nottinghamshire. The details of his early career are not known, though it is likely that he studied at Oxford, and he most probably wrote his logical treatises in the late 1230s and 1240s. Some authorities claim that he taught at the University of Paris, but there is no positive evidence for this. He was certainly a master by 27 January 1249, when &#145;magister Willielmus de Shirwood&#146; was one of the witnesses to the foundation deed of the nunnery of Marham in Norfolk; and he was equally certainly at Oxford in 1252, when &#145;Magister Wilhelmus de Skirwodde&#146; was one of the northerners who swore to the terms of peace agreed by the northern and Irish scholars (who had recently been involved in violent conflict) at Oxford. He became treasurer of Lincoln no earlier than December 1254, after the death of Peter Chaceporc; and the first surviving document mentioning him as treasurer is dated between December 1254 and November 1258. In 1258 he was granted a papal dispensation to hold a benefice with cure of souls in addition to his post as treasurer; and he was still treasurer in 1263, when he played a prominent role in the settlement of a dispute between the cathedral and the city of Lincoln. A document dated October 1266 refers to him as rector of the prebendal church of Aylesbury, Buckinghamshire, but he ceased to be rector before November 1267. His name also appears in the list of rectors of Attleborough in Norfolk under Henry III, and a reference there to the man who became rector at his death suggests that Sherwood must have died by 1272. If it is assumed that he remained treasurer of Lincoln until his death, this conjecture is supported by the fact that Richard of Battle became treasurer some time between 1269 and 1272.<br>
<br>The most important source of Sherwood's works is a Paris manuscript  (Biblioth&egrave;que Nationale, MS Lat. 16617), whose first part contains several works written in an English hand. The first item is an <span class="italic">Introductiones in logicam</span> which is explicitly attributed to Sherwood  (ibid., fols. 1&#150;23<i>r</i>), one of several general outlines of logic that survive from the thirteenth century. These include the well-known <span class="italic">Tractatus</span> (or <span class="italic">Summulae logicales</span>) of Petrus Hispanus, and the slightly later <span class="italic">Summulae dialectices</span> of Roger Bacon. Like Petrus Hispanus, Sherwood covers both the basic topics of Aristotle's logic with the exception of the <span class="italic">Posterior Analytics</span>, and the newly developed logic of terms or supposition theory. There is no evidence that either man knew the other's work, and similarities derive from the fact that they drew on the same tradition. A tract on supposition theory is found in whole or in part in two other manuscripts (Venice, Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana, MS Z.L.302, fols. 161<i>r</i>&#150;162<i>v</i>; Worcester Cathedral, MS Q.13, fols. 58<i>r</i>b&#150;59<i>v</i>b), and its use in teaching is attested by the existence of anonymous <span class="italic">Dubitationes</span>  (Worcester Cathedral, MS Q.13, fols. 59<i>v</i>b&#150;63<i>v</i>) dating from the 1290s. The <span class="italic">Introductiones</span> are followed by a work on <span class="italic">Syncategoremata</span>  (Paris, Biblioth&egrave;que Nationale, MS Lat. 16617, fols. 23<i>r</i>&#150;46<i>r</i>)&#151;that is, special logical terms, such as &#145;whole&#146;, &#145;only&#146;, &#145;necessarily&#146;. This too is explicitly attributed to Sherwood, as it is in the only other manuscript copy  (Bodl. Oxf., MS Digby 55, fols. 205<i>r</i>a&#150;224<i>r</i>b). Next there are two treatises on <i>insolubilia</i>, or semantic paradoxes  (Paris, Biblioth&egrave;que Nationale, MS Lat. 16617, fols. 46<i>v</i>&#150;50<i>v</i> and 50<i>v</i>&#150;54<i>v</i>) of which the first has been tentatively assigned to Sherwood by its editor, though there is no firm evidence. Finally there is a treatise on <i>obligationes</i>, or the rules governing what one is obliged to accept or reject in a certain kind of logical disputation  (Paris, Biblioth&egrave;que Nationale, MS Lat. 16617, fols. 54<i>v</i>&#150;62<i>v</i>), followed by a treatise called <span class="italic">Petitiones contrariorum</span>, on solutions to sophismata, or logical puzzle-cases  (ibid., fols. 62<i>v</i>&#150;64<i>v</i>). There is no particular reason to think that the last work is by Sherwood, but the <span class="italic">De obligationibus</span>, which exists in three other manuscripts (Erfurt, Wissenschaftliche Allgemeine Bibliothek, Amploniana MS Q 259, fols. 209<i>r</i>&#150;214<i>v</i>; Paris, Biblioth&egrave;que Nationale, MS Lat. 16130, fols. 110<i>v</i>&#150;114<i>r</i>; Venice, Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana, MS Z.L.302 (X 204), fols. 151<i>r</i>a&#150;155<i>v</i>a), has caused some controversy. The Paris manuscript attributes it to Magister W, but two other manuscripts attribute it to Walter Burley, and the Venice manuscript attributes it to Burley in the heading and to Sherwood in the <i>explicit</i>. There seems no way to settle the issue of authorship at this point. Three theological works were attributed to Sherwood by Leland in the sixteenth century, but even if they were by Sherwood, they have not survived.<br>
<br>Evidence of Sherwood's importance is provided by Roger Bacon. In his <span class="italic">Opus tertium</span> of 1267 Bacon wrote that Sherwood was &#145;much wiser than Albert [the Great]; for in <i>philosophia communis</i> no one is greater than he&#146;  (<span class="italic">Rogeri Bacon opera</span>, 14); and in his <span class="italic">Compendium studii philosophiae</span> of 1271 Bacon linked Sherwood with Robert Grosseteste (<i>d</i>. 1253) and other wise men of the recent past.<br>
<br>E. J. Ashworth </div>
