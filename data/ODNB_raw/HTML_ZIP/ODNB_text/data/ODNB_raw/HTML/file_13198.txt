<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hibbs,  Richard</span>  (1812?&#150;1886), <span class="occ">writer on poverty</span>, came from Hampshire, and studied at St John's College, Cambridge (of which he was scholar), matriculating in 1837 and proceeding BA in 1841 and MA in 1844. He took orders in 1842 and was curate of Bishop Hatfield, Hertfordshire (1841&#150;3), and of Corton, near Lowestoft (1843&#150;8); teacher and preacher at Lowestoft (1848&#150;52); curate of St Paul's, Covent Garden (1852); and assistant minister of St John's Chapel, Edinburgh (1852&#150;4). His connection with this last terminated somewhat suddenly. A bitter controversy with the incumbent led him to establish the New Church of England Chapel, St Vincent Street, where he ministered for some years and of which he published an account (1858). He was subsequently British chaplain at Lisbon, Rotterdam, and Utrecht. Hibbs's chief work, founded on personal investigation, is <span class="italic">Prussia and the poor, or, Observations upon the systematised relief of the poor at Elberfeld in contrast with that of England</span> (1876; 4th edn, 1883). Besides separate sermons, he also published works on Scottish church controversies and baptism, and <span class="italic">God's Plea for the Poor</span> (1851). He died at his home, 13 St Lawrence Road, North Kensington, London, on 26 March 1886, his wife, Fanny, surviving him.<br>
<br>Francis Watt<span class="italic">, rev. </span> H. C. G. Matthew </div>
