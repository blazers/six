<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Loveday,  Robert</span>  (1620/21&#150;1656), <span class="occ">translator</span>, was a scion of the Loveday family seated at Chediston, Suffolk, but his exact parentage is unclear. He went to school at Metfield, Suffolk, was admitted as a pensioner at Peterhouse, Cambridge, on 20 December 1636, aged fifteen, and matriculated in the following year. Loveday did not complete his studies because of the civil war, and became a secretary in the Clinton family; in this capacity he travelled extensively throughout England, spending time at the Clintons' seat, Tattershall Castle, Lincolnshire, and at the Clares' residence, Thurland House, Nottinghamshire (a county Loveday disliked).<br>
<br>Having become proficient in French and Italian under the instruction of another member of the Clinton household, Loveday translated into English the first three parts of La Calpren&egrave;de's <span class="italic">Cl&eacute;op&acirc;tre</span> under the title <span class="italic">Hymen's pr&aelig;ludia, or, Love's master-piece</span>; these appeared respectively in 1652, 1654, and 1655, and were reprinted many times. Despite the erroneous attribution of other parts of the romance to Loveday's hand in some later editions, it was only with the collaboration of John Coles that part 4 was completed and published in 1656. Loveday was an agreeable writer, and his translation is accurate and idiomatic. After Robert Loveday's death his brother Anthony edited a selection from his correspondence under the title <span class="italic">Loveday's Letters, Domestick and Forrein</span> (published 1659, reprinted several times), at the beginning of which is a portrait of the author by Faithorne. The work includes some verses by Loveday that intimate a facility in metre. A hard-working and kind man, Loveday was also sickly and died, probably of a consumptive disease, in 1656. As he has been plausibly but contentiously identified with the patient in Sir Thomas Browne's <span class="italic">A Letter to a Friend</span> (published 1690), his death may have taken placed in mid-May 1656.<br>
<br>Ross Kennedy </div>
