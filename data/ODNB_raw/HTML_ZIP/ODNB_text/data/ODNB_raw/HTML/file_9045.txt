<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Eyton,  Stephen</span>  (<em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>1320), <span class="occ">Augustinian canon and supposed chronicler</span>, is known only from the writings of John Leland and later antiquaries, who attribute to him a <i>floruit</i> in the reign of Edward II. He is described as having been a canon of the Augustinian priory of Warter, a few miles east of York, on the edge of the Yorkshire downs, and it is possible that he took his name from the village of Etton, east of Warter and north-west of Beverley. Leland records Eyton as the author of a history of the reign of Edward II, a copy of which he saw at Fountains Abbey. To this John Bale adds that Eyton's work, of which he claims to have seen a copy in the library of All Souls College, Oxford, had the incipit &#145;Post mortem mundo deflendam&#146;. However, this incipit is strikingly similar to that of the account of Edward II's reign in Thomas Walsingham's <span class="italic">Historia Anglicana</span>, which begins with the words &#145;Post mortem toti mundo deflendam Edwardi primi inclyti regis&#146;. It is therefore possible either that the text Leland saw was in fact that of part of Walsingham's chronicle, and that Eyton was only its later scribe or owner, or that Bale, coming upon the relevant section of Walsingham's chronicle in a version detached from the rest, assumed that this was identical with the account of Edward II's reign noted by Leland at Fountains, and attributed Walsingham's work to Eyton in error. There is nothing inherently improbable about a canon of Warter composing a historical text: the writing of chronicles flourished among the Augustinians in the fourteenth century. But no evidence survives that might now shed light on either the quality or, indeed, the existence of the work attributed to Eyton.<br>
<br>Henry Summerson </div>
