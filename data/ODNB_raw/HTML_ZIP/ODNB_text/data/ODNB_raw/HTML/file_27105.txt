<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Telfair,  Charles</span>  (1778&#150;1833), <span class="occ">naturalist</span>, was born at Belfast and arrived in Mauritius as a naval surgeon on one of the British blockading ships in 1810. He settled there, practised as a surgeon, and served as government secretary at Bourbon (R&eacute;union), and private secretary to Sir Robert Farquhar at Mauritius. He also held the posts of guardian of vacant estates and secretary to the vice-admiralty court, and became something of a sugar &#145;baron&#146;, introducing the first horizontal roller mill at the Bel-Ombre factory in 1819. He became a correspondent of Sir William Jackson Hooker, sent plants to Kew, became honorary supervisor of the botanical garden at Pamplemousses, Mauritius, 1826&#150;9, and established a botanical garden at R&eacute;union. He also collected bones of an extinct bird, the solitaire, from Rodriguez, which he forwarded to the Zoological Society, London, and to the Andersonian Museum, Glasgow. From 1829 to 1833 Telfair was president of the Soci&eacute;t&eacute; d'Histoire Naturelle at Port Louis, a society which he co-founded. In 1830 he published <span class="italic">Some account of the state of slavery at Mauritius since the British occupation in 1810, in refutation of anonymous charges &hellip; against government and that colony</span>.<br>
<br>Telfair was married to Annabella Chamberlain. He died at Port Louis, Mauritius, on 14 July 1833 following a short but painful illness, and was buried in the cemetery there. Hooker commemorated him by the African genus <i>Telfairia</i> in the cucumber family. His wife, who died at Port Louis on 23 May 1832, also communicated specimens of Mauritius algae to W. J. Hooker and drawings to <span class="italic">Curtis's Botanical Magazine</span> (1826&#150;30).<br>
<br>G. S. Boulger<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Andrew Grout </div>
