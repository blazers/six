<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Pilkington,  Gilbert</span>  (<em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>1450), <span class="occ">scribe</span>, was once thought to be the author of the mid-fifteenth-century English burlesque poem <span class="italic">The Tournament of Tottenham</span>, a comic description of a &#145;tournament&#146; in which a group of rustics compete for the hand of Tyb, the Reeve's daughter, which survives in two manuscripts  (CUL, MS Ff.5.48, and BL, Harley MS 5396). He seems in fact to have been simply the scribe of the copy now in Cambridge University Library, where his name, Gilbertus Pylkyngton, is appended to another text. The oldest catalogue of the library's manuscripts ascribed all the poems in the volume to him. However his spurious fame as author of the <span class="italic">Tournament</span> is due to the scholar William Bedwell, vicar of Tottenham in the early seventeenth century, who printed the Cambridge text together with a survey of Tottenham in 1631, describing it on the title-page as &#145;written long since in verse by Mr. Gilbert Pilkington, at that time, as some have thought, Parson of the Parish&#146;. There is no evidence for the latter claim either. But Bedwell's text was reprinted by other antiquaries in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries before Pilkington was stripped both of his poem and of his parish.<br>
<br>Douglas Gray </div>
