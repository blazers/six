<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Stenhouse,  William</span>  (1773&#150;1827), <span class="occ">musical antiquary</span>, was a native of Roxburghshire. He became an accountant in Edinburgh, where, in 1806, he published <span class="italic">Tables of Simple Interest and of Commission, Brokerage, or Exchange</span>. He died in Edinburgh on 10 November 1827 and was buried in St Cuthbert's churchyard.<br>
<br>Stenhouse was an antiquary with strong musical leanings. He is best known for his notes in the 1839 (Edinburgh) reprint of James Johnson's <span class="italic">The Scots Musical Museum</span>, which he edited. These notes, invaluable though inaccurate in many particulars, have been extensively quoted by biographers of Robert Burns and by editors of Scottish songs. They amount to more than five hundred pages, and were reprinted, with additions, in David Laing's 1853 edition of the <span class="italic">Musical Museum</span>. Much of the criticism of Stenhouse's work (especially that of William Chappell in his <span class="italic">Popular Music of Olden Time</span>, 1855&#150;9) is thoroughly misplaced. Stenhouse broke new ground as well as contributing much from his own musical background in the borders. On the other hand, James Hogg, writing to Blackwoods (the publishers of his <span class="italic">Jacobite Relics</span>) on 20 November 1820, complained &#145;I care not for the running copies of the relics. Stenhouse has spoiled all by altering the tunes&#146;  (A. L. Strout, <span class="italic">The Life and Letters of James Hogg</span>, 1946).<br>
<br>J. C. Hadden<span class="italic">, rev. </span> John Purser </div>
