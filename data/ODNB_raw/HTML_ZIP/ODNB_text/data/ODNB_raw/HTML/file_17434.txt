<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Macdonald,  Hugh</span>  (1817&#150;1860), <span class="occ">journalist</span>, born in Bridgeton, Glasgow, on 4 April 1817, was apprenticed, after a scanty education at a night school, to a block-printer. He subsequently kept a provision shop in Bridgeton, and ultimately returned to his trade in Paisley. He began to write verse in the <span class="italic">Glasgow Citizen</span>, to which he also contributed a series of letters defending Robert Burns from an attack by George Gilfillan. In 1849 he gave up his trade and joined the staff of the <span class="italic">Glasgow Citizen</span>, contributing to it, and to the <span class="italic">Glasgow Times</span>, the series of descriptive papers subsequently collected under the titles of <span class="italic">Rambles Round Glasgow</span> (1856), and <span class="italic">Days at the Coast</span> (1874). In 1855 he joined the <span class="italic">Glasgow Sentinel</span>, soon afterwards became editor of the <span class="italic">Glasgow Times</span>, and in 1858 literary editor of the <span class="italic">Morning Journal</span>, a post that he held until his death on 16 March 1860. In 1883 a rustic stone fount, with a medallion bust of Macdonald, was erected to his memory on the site of &#145;the bonnie wee well&#146;, which is the subject of one of his songs. All his literary work shows an intense love for nature, but his prose is better than his verse. His poetical works were published, with a memoir, in 1863.<br>
<br>J. C. Hadden<span class="italic">, rev. </span> H. C. G. Matthew </div>
