<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Sheridan <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Callander]</span>,  Caroline Henrietta</span>  (1779&#150;1851), <span class="occ">novelist</span>, was the second daughter of Colonel James Callander (afterwards <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/4507/', "Sir James Campbell")</script>, 1745&#150;1832) and his third wife, Lady Elizabeth Helena (<i>d</i>. 1796), youngest daughter of Alexander Macdonnell, fifth earl of Antrim. Caroline Callander, one of the beauties of her day, was married on 21 June 1805 to <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/25372/', "Thomas (Tom) Sheridan (1775&#150;1817)")</script>, the son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/25367/', "Richard Brinsley Sheridan")</script>, and was mother of &#145;the three graces&#146;: <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/20339/', "Caroline Elizabeth Sarah Norton")</script>; <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/25366/', "Helen Selina Hay, countess of Gifford")</script>; and Jane Georgiana, duchess of Somerset. They also had four sons, three of whom predeceased their mother.<br>
<br>In 1813 Caroline Sheridan accompanied her husband to the Cape of Good Hope, where, while serving the office of colonial treasurer, he died of consumption on 12 September 1817. She received a small pension, and rooms in Hampton Court Palace were given to her by the prince regent. She published three novels: <span class="italic">Carwell, or, Crime and Sorrow</span> (1830), which was designed to expose the inequitable sentences pronounced upon those who had been guilty of forgery; <span class="italic">Aims and Ends</span> (1833); and <span class="italic">Oonagh Lynch</span> (1833). <span class="italic">Carwell</span> was also published in a French edition. She died on 9 June 1851, at 39 Grosvenor Place, London, the house of her daughter Lady Dufferin.<br>
<br>W. F. Rae<span class="italic">, rev. </span> K. D. Reynolds </div>
