<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Spelman,  Clement</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1598, <em>d.</em> 1679), <span class="occ">judge</span>, was baptized on 4 October 1598 at Sedgeford, Norfolk, the fourth and youngest son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/26104/', "Sir Henry Spelman (1563/4&#150;1641)")</script>, and his wife, Eleanor (<i>d</i>. 1620), daughter and coheir of John L'Estrange of Sedgeford. <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/26106/', "Sir John Spelman (1594&#150;1643)")</script> was his eldest brother. Spelman was admitted to Gray's Inn on 20 March 1614, and to Queens' College, Cambridge, on 16 September 1616. He was called to the bar in 1624. On 16 July 1628 he married Mary, daughter and coheir of Francis Mason of St Leonard's, Shoreditch. They had two sons and two daughters.<br>
<br>Spelman performed in a masque on 24 February 1635 at the Middle Temple. On 22 August 1638 he was named a member of the commission charged with inquiring into the breach of the statute of 31 Eliz. that required every new cottage to have 4 acres of land attached, and also into usury. He became an ancient of Gray's Inn on 4 May 1638.<br>
<br>Spelman wrote a preface to the 1646 Oxford edition of his father's <span class="italic">De non temerandis ecclesiis</span>, and in 1647 he published anonymously <span class="italic">Reasons why we should admit the king to a personall treaty in parliament, and not treat by commissioners</span>. In 1648 he wrote <span class="italic">A letter, to the assembly of divines at Westminster: shewing the conversion of church-lands to lay-uses, to be condemned by Luther, Calvin, Knox, and the whole assemblies of Scotch divines</span>. Either Spelman or a namesake was named on a list of sequestrated delinquents on 24 April 1648.<br>
<br>Spelman was elected a bencher of Gray's Inn on 6 July 1660. He may have been the author of <span class="italic">A Character of the Oliverians</span> (1660). He was appointed cursitor baron of the exchequer on 9 March 1663. Anthony Wood reported that Spelman had died in Fleet Street after Whitsuntide 1679. He had certainly died by 30 May 1679, when his successor was appointed. Spelman was buried in St Dunstan's Church, Fleet Street. He should not be confused with his second cousin and namesake, Clement, the recorder of Nottingham, whose will was proved in 1680.<br>
<br>William Carr<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Stuart Handley </div>
