<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Barff,  Samuel</span>  (1793?&#150;1880), <span class="occ">philhellene and banker</span>, was probably born in England. In 1816 he established himself in Zante, Greece, where he became an important merchant and banker. He died there on 23 September 1880. He was probably married.<br>
<br>Barff's part in the struggle for Greek independence is evidenced in the papers of contemporary philhellenes such as Church and Blaquiere. Byron wrote to him early in 1824 after witnessing his untiring activities in the cause and his generous support of Englishmen then in difficulties in Greece. Barff offered his house on Zante to Byron should ill health compel the latter to leave Mesolonghi. Barff took part in negotiating the first western loans, especially in 1824 and 1825. When Greek rivalries made it uncertain to whom the funds should be given the money was frozen on Zante in Barff's care. He was foremost in urging his friend Richard Church (then in England) to accept the office of generalissimo of the Greek forces.<br>
<br>After the establishment of the Greek kingdom Barff joined George Finlay in supporting the project for setting up a bank in Greece and pressed for careful definitions of banking law. Throughout his life Barff kept himself informed on Greek political activities and pressed for governmental reform. For more than fifty years Barff, Hancock &amp; Co. continued to provide indispensable banking services as Finlay's travel accounts and letter books show.<br>
<br>J. M. Hussey </div>
