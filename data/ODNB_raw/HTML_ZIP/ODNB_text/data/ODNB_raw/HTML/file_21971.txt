<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Perkins,  Joseph</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1657), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was baptized on 6 May 1657 at Slimbridge, Gloucestershire, the son of George Perkins. He matriculated from Oriel College, Oxford, on 16 July 1675, and graduated BA in 1679. After this his career must be reconstructed from his works.<br>
<br>Perkins became a chaplain in the navy, and sailed to the Mediterranean in the <i>Norfolk</i> under Admiral Edward Russell (afterwards earl of Orford). He was cashiered for having, it was alleged, brought a false accusation of theft against a naval officer, and his response was a series of publications in 1697. <span class="italic">Capellanus regiae cuiusdam navis</span> (1697) set out the terms of his grievances in verse, and he also published several Latin elegies, including the one he had written in 1694 for the death of Admiral Russell from illness, the occasion for which had been spoiled when Russell unexpectedly recovered. At the same time he published three texts with links to Tunbridge Wells: an open letter remonstrating with the church authorities, &#145;who denied him the use of the pulpit there&#146;, a poem composed extempore on a musical contest at Tunbridge Wells on 9 July 1697, and a Latin poem celebrating the ladies who accompanied Queen Anne on her visit there in the same year.<br>
<br>In the succeeding years Perkins printed various further sermons and elegies. In 1707 another burst of publications included a collection of poetry: <span class="italic">The Poet's Fancy, in a Love-Letter to Galatea, or any other Fair Lady, in English and Latin</span>. He also printed a sermon, on the title-page of which it is claimed that Perkins &#145;heard the voice of an angel or spirit speaking to him two several times, in one night&#146;.<br>
<br>Perkins's last known work is an elegy on the death of Thomas Ken, published in Bristol in 1711. He styled himself the White Poet and the Latin Laureate, but seems to have been largely unsuccessful in his quests for patronage.<br>
<br>Matthew Steggle </div>
