<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Smythies,  Harriette Maria Gordon</span>  (1813?&#150;1883), <span class="occ">poet and novelist</span>, was born in Margate, Kent, the daughter of Edward Lesmoin Gordon and his wife, Jane, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Halliday. No further information on Smythies's parents or early life has been traced. On 1 March 1842 she married the Revd William Yorick Smythies, a Church of England clergyman who was unable to maintain a steady income and who engaged the family in financially crippling litigation. The couple had five children before Harriette Maria Smythies left her husband about 1860 and moved with her children to London.<br>
<br>Harriette Maria Smythies struggled to support her family, and published two long poems and at least twenty-two novels under a number of variations on her married name. Some of her work was serialized in the <span class="italic">London Journal</span> and <span class="italic">Cassell's Family Magazine</span>. Between 1857 and 1860 she wrote advice columns for the <span class="italic">Ladies' Treasury</span>. While her work has often been dismissed for its melodrama and didacticism, it is notable for its keen satire and sensitive renderings of the economic and sexual dangers besetting young and working women.<br>
<br>Harriette Maria Smythies published her first work, a long poem entitled <span class="italic">The Bride of Sienna</span>, in 1835. In 1838 she published her first novel, <span class="italic">Fitzherbert, or, Lovers and Fortune Hunters</span>, and she continued to publish about one novel a year during the next three decades. Her most popular works were <span class="italic">Cousin Geoffrey</span> (1840) and <span class="italic">The Jilt</span> (1844).<br>
<br>By the mid 1870s all but one of Harriette Maria Smythies's children had died. Her last novel was published in 1880, and she herself died on 15 August 1883 at her home, 24 Brunswick Square, London.<br>
<br>Clare Cotugno </div>
