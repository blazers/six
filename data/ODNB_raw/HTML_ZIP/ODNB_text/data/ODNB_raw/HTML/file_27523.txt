<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Tomlins, Sir  Thomas Edlyne</span>  (1762&#150;1841), <span class="occ">legal writer</span>, was born in London on 4 January 1762, the eldest son of Thomas Tomlins (<i>d</i>. 1815), solicitor and clerk to the Company of Painter&#150;Stainers. <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/27521/', "Elizabeth Sophia Tomlins (1763&#150;1828)")</script> was his sister. The family was descended from the Tomlins of the neighbourhood of Ledbury in Herefordshire and of Hereford. Thomas Edlyne was admitted a scholar at St Paul's School on 21 September 1769. He matriculated from Queen's College, Oxford, on 27 October 1778, and was called to the bar by the society of the Inner Temple in Hilary term 1783.<br>
<br>For some years Tomlins was editor of the <span class="italic">St James's Chronicle</span>, a daily newspaper, and on 30 May 1801 he was appointed counsel to the chief secretary for Ireland. In the same year he became parliamentary counsel to the chancellor of the exchequer for Ireland, a post which he retained until the union of the British and Irish treasuries in 1816. He was knighted at Wanstead House on 29 June 1814, on the recommendation of the duke of Wellington. In 1818 he was appointed assistant counsel to the Treasury. In Hilary term 1823 he was elected a bencher of the Inner Temple, and in 1827 he filled the office of treasurer to the society. In January 1831, on the whigs' coming into office, he retired from his post in the Treasury. He died a widower on 1 July 1841 at St Mary Castlegate, York.<br>
<br>Tomlins was a prolific writer. Among other works, he wrote <span class="italic">A Familiar Explanation of the Law of Wills and Codicils</span> (1785), a clear account of the subject for the layman, which reached a seventh edition in 1819. His new edition of Giles Jacob's <span class="italic">Law Dictionary</span> (1797) was equally successful, and he produced further editions, under his own name, until 1838.<br>
<br>Sir Thomas's nephew, <a name="cosubject_27524"></a><span class="headword">Thomas Edlyne Tomlins</span>  (1803&#150;1875), <span class="occ">legal writer and antiquary</span>, was born on 26 September 1803, the son of Sir Thomas's brother Alfred Tomlins, a clerk in the Irish exchequer office, Paradise Row, Lambeth. He entered St Paul's School on 6 February 1811, and was admitted to practice in London as an attorney in the Michaelmas term of 1827.<br>
<br>Tomlins's legal publications included a popular law dictionary (1838) and an edition of Sir Thomas Littleton's <span class="italic">Treatise of Tenures</span> (1841). As an antiquary he translated the <span class="italic">Chronicles</span> of Jocelin of Brakelond (1844) and published an account of the environs of Islington (1844). He died in Islington, Middlesex, on 17 May 1875.<br>
<br>E. I. Carlyle<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Jonathan Harris </div>
