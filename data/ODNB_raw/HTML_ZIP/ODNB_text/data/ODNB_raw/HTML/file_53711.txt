<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Savage family</span>  (<em>per. </em>1333&#150;1519), <span class="occ">landowners and administrators</span>, styled in the late middle ages lords of Lecale, in co. Down, came from an old Anglo-Norman family of Ulster. The William Savage who witnessed a charter of John de Courcy, first lord of Ulster, was probably related to the twelfth-century Cheshire landholders of that name. Courcy's charter establishing Black Abbey on the Ards peninsula (<i>c</i>.1183) mentions the nearby castle of Ardkeen, later recorded as <i>caput</i> of the Savage family's estates. After the assassination of William de Burgh, third earl of Ulster, in 1333 the influential post of seneschal of the liberty of Ulster passed from the guilty Mandevilles to <a name="cosubject_53709"></a><span class="headword">Sir Robert Savage</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1360)<span class="occ"></span>, who earned a legendary reputation among the Anglo-Irish for his defence of the neglected colony against Irish attacks. Later the office of seneschal was held by <a name="cosubject_53710"></a><span class="headword">Edmund Savage</span>  (<em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>1385&#150;<em>c.</em>1398)<span class="occ"></span>, named with dislike and fear in letters of Irish chiefs to Richard II. From <i>c</i>.1452 <a name="cosubject_53713"></a><span class="headword">Janico <span class="st"> [Seinic&iacute;n]</span> Savage</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1468)<span class="occ"></span>, styled &#145;captain of his nation&#146;, was seneschal. He instigated a dramatic appeal for support to Edward IV about 1467, comparing the Ulster colonists to the children of Israel in bondage to the pharaoh. The annals of Ulster (1469&#150;70) equate the title of seneschal with that of lord of Lecale, the last remnant of English Ulster, by stating that the great &Oacute; N&eacute;ill, &Eacute;inr&iacute; son of Eoghan, assisted Patrick White to capture <a name="cosubject_53712"></a><span class="headword">Patrick Savage</span>  (<em>d.</em> in or after 1481)<span class="occ"></span>, expel his family, and seize the lordship of Lecale, but that the Savages took back Lecale and the seneschalcy from Patrick White the following year. Patrick Savage was still seneschal in 1481, but in that year he was blinded and exiled. On 2 August 1482 <a name="cosubject_53711"></a><span class="headword">Roland Savage</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1519)<span class="occ"></span> was appointed seneschal by the Dublin government. However, an anonymous tract of about 1515 lists Roland as &#145;one of the English great rebels&#146; living outside Dublin's control. The ninth earl of Kildare expelled him from the lordship of Lecale, substituting the Prior M&aacute;g Aonghusa. Roland died soon after, of grief, the annals suggest, leaving an attenuated patrimony to his son Edmund, who was inaugurated chief in his place.<br>
<br>Katharine Simms </div>
