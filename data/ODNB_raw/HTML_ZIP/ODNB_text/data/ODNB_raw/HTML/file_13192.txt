<span class="headword">Hibbart <span class="st"> [Hibbert]</span>,  William</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1725, <em>d.</em> 1808), <span class="occ">printmaker</span>, the son of James Hibbart, was born in Bath and baptized there on 29 November 1725. He practised in the city for over fifty years, etching and engraving book illustrations, maps, plans, and bookplates. He was apprenticed to the Bath engraver Jacob Skinner in 1738, but was greatly influenced by the presence of Thomas Worlidge in the city between 1744 and 1766; his small etched portraits imitate Worlidge's drypoint technique. The majority of these portraits appear as frontispieces, typical examples being his two portraits of Richard Nash (&#145;Beau Nash&#146;) for the 1762 and 1763 editions of the <span class="italic">New Bath Guide</span>. An etching, <span class="italic">The Knights of the Baythe, or, The One Headed Corporation</span>, satirizing Ralph Allen's role within the Bath council, dated 1763 and fancifully signed William O'Gaarth, is attributed to Hibbart. A handsome brass commemorative plaque signed by him and dated 1764 is still affixed to the wall of the Pump Room in Bath, next to the Tompion clock. His maps are generally confidently executed, especially his etching of Thomas Thorpe's <span class="italic">A Map of 5 Miles Round the City of Bath</span> of 1773. Not all of Hibbart's work was so successful: the amateur artist Coplestone Warre Bampfylde greatly disliked Hibbart's etchings after his illustrations for the poet Christopher Anstey's <span class="italic">An Election Ball</span> and was not placated by Anstey's assurance that &#145;Mr Hoare [the Bath portrait painter William Hoare] has taken a great deal of pains and has corrected many parts of them &hellip;&#146;  (Som., ARS, DD/GC 103).<br>
<br>Hibbart married Elizabeth Hibbart (1724&#150;1797), who was probably a cousin, in 1748. A trade card designed by Bartolozzi for &#145;Hibbert, Engraver&#146; of 8 Bridge Street, Bath, and engraved by J. Hibbert must have been for his son John (1768&#150;1848), who invariably spelled his name Hibbert rather than Hibbart and took over his father's business; a copy of the card is now in the Banks collection in the British Museum. Another son, Charles (1765&#150;1819), also a printmaker, travelled to Rome with the painter Thomas Barker in 1790; he was later convicted of forging banknotes and was hanged at Ilchester in 1819. Hibbart died on 13 July 1808 in Bath.<br>
<br>Susan Sloman </div>
