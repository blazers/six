<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hodson,  William</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1617&#150;1640), <span class="occ">poet and religious writer</span>, was born in London, the son of John Hodson (<i>d</i>. 1628), citizen. He followed his lawyer brother John up to Peterhouse, Cambridge, as a pensioner, matriculating a decade after his elder sibling on 3 May 1617. He graduated BA in 1621 and MA in 1624. Thereafter he seems to have lived at Tottenham in Middlesex; he was resident there in 1628 when he acted as executor for his father's will. The only known portrait of Hodson is an engraving by the royalist William Marshall (published as the frontispiece to his <span class="italic">Credo resurrectionem carnis</span> of 1636), in which he appears as a blond-haired cavalier gentleman in a fine satin suit with a wide lace collar. Marshall illustrated all three of Hodson's theological tracts.<br>
<br>At the death of James VI and I, when &#145;each poetaster blubbers forth a verse&#146;, Hodson joined them, producing a ninety-five-line poem, printed between heavy mourning bands, entitled <span class="italic">The plurisie of sorrow, let blood in the eye-veine, or, The muses teares for the death of our late soveraigne</span>  (1625; quotation at sig. A*3<i>r</i>, line 50). In this unlikely classical poem he drew on Pliny's <span class="italic">Natural History</span> for a conceit based on the death of the queen bee. To this poem the young graduate appended twelve lines commending the late king's scholarship and a thirty-six-line verse welcoming Charles as monarch, &#145;Illustrissimo regi&#146;. The little volume closes with three pages of distichs on the mottoes engraved on the new coinage.<br>
<br>As a gentleman scholar Hodson wrote a tract on the eleventh article of the Nicene creed, <span class="italic">Credo resurrectionem carnis</span>, which ran to a second, expanded, edition in 1636, after the first, which had been entered for publication on 20 April 1633, was destroyed &#145;by some deficiencie, suffred in the printer's stocks&#146;  (<span class="italic">Credo</span>, sig. A3<i>v</i>). Two other theological tracts from his pen&#151;<span class="italic">The Holy Sinner</span> (1639), based on <span class="roman">Luke</span> 7, and <span class="italic">The Divine Cosmographer</span> (1640), a tract on <span class="roman">Psalm 8</span>&#151;are the serious writing of a pious layman living in the environs of London, grounded in the theology of James VI and I whom he saluted as &#145;of our nobles the most skilful in Divinitie&#146;  (<span class="italic">Divine Cosmographer</span>, 9), who kept some contact with his Cambridge contemporaries; John Cosin, Thomas Bainbridge, and Ralph Brownrigg are among those who licensed both works for publication.<br>
<br>It is not known when after 1640 Hodson died.<br>
<br>Nicholas W. S. Cranfield </div>
