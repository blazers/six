<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Vernon, Sir  George</span>  (<em>c.</em>1578&#150;1639), <span class="occ">judge</span>, was the son of Sir Thomas Vernon of Haslington, Cheshire, and his wife, Dorothy, daughter of William Egerton of Betteley. Admitted to the Inner Temple from Clement's Inn in 1594, Vernon was called to the bar in 1603 and to the bench in 1619, and as autumn reader in 1621 read on the <span class="roman">Statute of Westminster II</span>, c. 5, &#145;de advocationibus&#146;. Created serjeant-at-law and knighted in 1627, Vernon served as baron of the exchequer from 1627 and justice of the common pleas from 1631 until his death in 1639.<br>
<br>Vernon came from a wealthy and well-established Cheshire family. He married twice, first Alice, daughter of Sir George Booth, from whom he acquired Haslington Hall. His second marriage, to Sir George Corbet's daughter Jane, brought them a daughter, Muriel, who married Henry Vernon of Sudbury in Derbyshire. At the time of his death Vernon had built up extensive land holdings around Haslington and Church Coppenhall. Vernon's &#145;slandering&#146;  (<span class="italic">CSP dom.</span>, <i>1627</i>, 437) of the exchequer at Chester in litigation against Sir Randle Crew led to his imprisonment by Star Chamber about 1612. Vernon served in the parliament of 1626, where he was nominated to three committees examining acts to resolve oath taking and property issues.<br>
<br>Under James I the sale of the coif by the king's ministers had become an &#145;open scandal&#146;  (Baker, 110), exacerbated, in the reign of Charles I, by the influence of the duke of Buckingham over judicial appointments. Vernon was created serjeant-at-law on 4 July 1627, with the public patronage of Sir Thomas Coventry and Sir Robert Heath, and baron of exchequer on 13 November. Sir Richard Hutton, who was concerned by the non-consultation of judges before Vernon's call, was unsure whether to believe if Vernon had, as &#145;generalment reported&#146;, paid up to &pound;1500 to the countess of Denbigh for his promotion to the exchequer, but hoped he would &#145;prove un honest man&#146;  (CUL, Add. MS 6863, fols. 40<i>v</i>&#150;41). Upon appointment to a judgeship in the court of common pleas on 8 May 1631, Vernon remarked that he had found the king's &#145;favor far above his deserts&#146;  (<span class="italic">Diary of Sir Richard Hutton</span>, 86)&#151;but it appears that he took his legal duties seriously; Croke later described him as &#145;a man of great reading in Statute and Common Law, and of extraordinary memory&#146;  (Leach, 566).<br>
<br>After his creation as serjeant Vernon challenged the integrity of the provincial council at Chester, and also at York. In 1627 attempts by Vernon to remove litigation between himself and Sir Randolph Crew from the determination of Roger Downes, vice-chamberlain of Chester, were rejected by fellow judges. In 1633 Vernon clashed with Sir Thomas Wentworth while presiding over the northern assizes, leading Wentworth to complain that the dignity of the council at York had been slighted, but Vernon continued to ride the northern circuit until the year of his death.<br>
<br>Generally Vernon was a supporter of crown policy. As a judge he enforced the oath of allegiance, serving on the high commission in 1633. Offering brief opinion in <i>Hampden's case</i> (1637&#150;8) on grounds of poor health, Vernon supported the king's right to ship money &#145;notwithstanding any act of parliament&#146;, suggesting &#145;that a statute derogatory from the prerogative doth not bind the king; and the king may dispense with any law in cases of necessity&#146;  (<span class="italic">State trials</span>, 3.1125)&#151;comments which drew his judicial reputation into obloquy. Vernon died on 16 or 17 December 1639, and was buried on 18 December in Temple Church. His death prevented his prosecution by parliament, but he was fined posthumously for his actions.<br>
<br>D. X. Powell </div>
