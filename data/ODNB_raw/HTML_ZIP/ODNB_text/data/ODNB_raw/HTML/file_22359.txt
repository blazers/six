<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Platt,  Thomas Pell</span>  (1798&#150;1852), <span class="occ">biblical translator</span>, was born in London, the son of Thomas Platt. After school in Little Dunham, Norfolk, he was admitted to Trinity College, Cambridge, as pensioner, on 25 November 1816. He was elected scholar on 3 April 1818, Davies scholar in 1819 (the same year in which he was president of the union), and Tyrwhitt Hebrew scholar in 1821. He graduated BA in 1820, became a fellow in 1820, and proceeded MA in 1823.<br>
<br>While at Cambridge Platt became involved with the British and Foreign Bible Society, and was for several years its librarian. In 1823 he published a catalogue of Ethiopic biblical manuscripts held in the Royal Library of Paris and in the British and Foreign Bible Society library. Afterwards he also collated and edited the Ethiopic versions of the <span class="roman">New Testament</span> for the society. The publication did not aim to offer new interpretations but &#145;simply to give the Abyssinians the Scriptures in as good a form of their ancient version as could be conveniently done&#146;. Platt did, however, make a few interpretative notes on the readings in the gospels which particularly struck him, and was prepared to defend his methods when the publications of the British and Foreign Bible Society were attacked in an article in a <span class="italic">Quarterly Review</span> of 1827. In 1829 he prepared an edition of the Syriac gospels, and in 1844 edited an Amharic version of the <span class="roman">Bible</span>, using the translation of Abba Rukh for the <span class="roman">Old Testament</span>, and that of Abu Rumi Habessinus for the <span class="roman">New</span>. Platt was one of the earliest members of the Royal Asiatic Society (founded in 1823), and for many years served on its oriental translation committee. He was also a fellow of the Society of Antiquaries.<br>
<br>Platt came under the influence of the Tractarian movement and described his conversion from evangelicalism in a <span class="italic">Letter to Dr. Pusey</span> of 1840. Even after his change of heart, however, he resisted &#145;mystical and spiritual interpretations to the prophecies of the <span class="roman">Old Testament</span>&#146; in vogue among some Tractarians. Platt lived for many years at Child's Hill, Hampstead, but died at Dulwich Hill, Surrey, on 31 October 1852, leaving an only son, Francis Thomas Platt.<br>
<br>G. Le G. Norgate<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Gerald Law </div>
