<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Mac Craith,  Sea&aacute;n  mac Ruaidhr&iacute;</span>  (<em>fl. </em>mid-14th cent.), <span class="occ">historian</span>, was a member of the Mac Craith family of hereditary bardic poets, which appears in the sixteenth-century <span class="italic">Fiants</span> of Queen Elizabeth I as settled near Pallas Green in east co. Limerick and Clogheen in south co. Tipperary. The death of &#145;the son of Mac Raith, the <i>file</i>&#146; (or learned poet), is recorded in the annals of Inisfallen as early as 1097. The annals of the four masters, under the year 1098, add that he was &#145;arch-poet of Munster&#146;. The next representative of the line to appear in the annals was Ruaidhr&iacute; Mac Craith (<i>d</i>. 1343), described by the four masters as <i>ollamh le d&aacute;n</i> or &#145;master-poet&#146; to the southern half of Ireland. This Ruaidhr&iacute; is depicted in the elaborate historical narrative <span class="italic">Caithr&eacute;im Thoirdhealbhaigh</span> (&#145;The triumphs of Toirdhealbhach&#146;), which celebrates Toirdelbach &Oacute; Briain (<i>d</i>. 1306), as lamenting the slain warriors after the battle of Corcomrua Abbey, which took place in 1317 between rival factions within the &Oacute; Briain dynasty. The <span class="italic">Caithr&eacute;im</span> (which survives in copies at the Royal Irish Academy and Trinity College, Dublin) takes as its main themes the power struggle within the U&iacute; Briain from the mid-thirteenth century, promoting the cause of the Clann Toirdelbaig over that of the Clann Briain Ruaid, and the relationship of the U&iacute; Briain with the Anglo-Norman Clares, whom it vilifies. According to the heading in a copy of <span class="italic">Caithr&eacute;im Thoirdhealbhaigh</span> which was written in 1721 by the co. Clare scribe Aindrias Mac Cruit&iacute;n, the tract was composed by Sea&aacute;n mac Ruaidhr&iacute; Mac Craith in 1459. However, literary and historical critics since T. J. Westropp in 1904 have agreed that on internal evidence the tract was composed in the mid-fourteenth century, most probably for the chief Diarmait &Oacute; Briain (<i>d</i>. 1364), the 1459 date being either a misreading by Mac Cruit&iacute;n, or applying to an intermediary copy of the original text. A mid-fourteenth-century date for the tract's composition increases the likelihood that the author, Sea&aacute;n, was a son of the poet Ruaidhr&iacute; who is twice mentioned in the tract itself.<br>
<br>Other Mac Craith poets who flourished in the fourteenth century were Murchadh gan Crios (&#145;the Beltless&#146;), who addressed a poem to Sir William Bermingham (<i>d</i>. 1332?), Maolmhuire Bacach (&#145;the Lame&#146;), who eulogized the chiefs Muircheartach &Oacute; Briain (<i>d</i>. 1343) and Aodh Reamhar &Oacute; N&eacute;ill (<i>d</i>. 1364), and Eoghan an t-&Oacute;rth&oacute;ir (&#145;the Gilder&#146;) who composed an ode to Art Mac Murchadha Caomh&aacute;nach (<i>d</i>. 1417), paramount chief of Leinster. The family continued to supply professional court poets to the aristocracy of Munster and Leinster up to the early seventeenth century, one of the later members, Eoghan, son of Donnchadh Maol (&#145;the Bald&#146;) Mac Craith, also being the author of a number of religious poems.<br>
<br>Katharine Simms </div>
