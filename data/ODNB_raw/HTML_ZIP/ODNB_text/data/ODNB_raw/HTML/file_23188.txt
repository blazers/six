<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Rawlinson,  Christopher</span>  (1677&#150;1733), <span class="occ">antiquary</span>, was born at Springfield, Essex, on 13 June 1677, the second son of Curwen Rawlinson (1641&#150;1689), landowner and MP, of Carke Hall in Cartmel, Lancashire, and his wife, Elizabeth (<i>d</i>. 1691), daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/18941/', "Nicholas Monck")</script>, bishop of Hereford. He matriculated from Queen's College, Oxford, on 14 June 1695; there he was one of the group of Anglo-Saxon scholars who worked with Edward Thwaites, fellow of the college. He published in 1698 an edition of King Alfred's translation of Boethius, <span class="italic">De consolationis philosophi&aelig;</span>, from a transcript made by Francis Junius (Bodl. Oxf., MS Junius 12); this was printed at Oxford University Press with the Junian types. Thwaites dedicated his <span class="italic">Grammatica Anglo-Saxonica ex Hickesiano thesauro excerpta</span> to Rawlinson in 1711.<br>
<br>Rawlinson, having inherited his father's estates, died unmarried and intestate in Holborn Row, London, on 8 January 1733; he was buried in the abbey church of St Albans, Hertfordshire, on 31 January. His landed estates passed to the children of his father's sisters Anne and Katherine. The furniture of Carke Hall was sold by auction and his manuscripts were disposed of in bundles and bought for pence by the villagers. Rawlinson had made valuable collections for the history of Lancashire, Westmorland, and Cumberland, none of which appear to have survived. Sir Daniel Fleming had however copied extracts from the portion relating to Westmorland, and these extracts, in the collection of manuscripts at Rydal Hall, were used by Joseph Nicolson and R. Burn in their <span class="italic">History and Antiquities of the Counties of Westmorland and Cumberland</span> (1777).<br>
<br>W. W. Wroth<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Mary Clapinson </div>
