<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Martin <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Eccleston]</span>,  Dorcas</span>, Lady Martin  (1536/7&#150;1599), <span class="occ">translator and bookseller</span>, was probably the daughter of John Eccleston, grocer, of Cheapside and Tottenham, who may have moved to London from Lancashire (on her monument, Dorcas is described as the daughter of John Eccleston of Lancaster, gentleman), and his wife, Margery (<i>d</i>. 1571). She married, probably after 1552, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/18205/', "Sir Richard Martin (1533/4&#150;1617)")</script>, master of the mint and twice lord mayor of London, and they had five sons and one daughter. She is depicted with her husband on a silver medal of 1562, which gives her age as twenty-five. White identifies Martin as an active participant in a radical puritan circle. Martin's role in the admonition crisis appears in Archbishop Grindal's letter to Archbishop Parker which refers to her as &#145;the stationer for all the first impressions&#146; of Thomas Cartwright's unlawfully published <span class="italic">Replie to an Answere</span> (1573)  (Nicholson, 347&#150;8). James Rowbotham dedicated <span class="italic">The Perfect Pathway to Salvation</span> (1590?) to her. Martin's translation of a French text, <span class="italic">An Instruction for Christians</span>, appears in the second lamp of Thomas Bentley's <span class="italic">Monument of Matrones</span> (1582) where Bentley introduces her as a &#145;godlie matrone and gentlewoman&#146;  (p. 221). Her translation includes prayers, psalm verses, and, notably, a catechism in which a mother questions a child.<br>
<br>Martin died on 1 September 1599, and was buried the following night in the south chancel of All Hallows, Tottenham.<br>
<br>Elaine V. Beilin </div>
