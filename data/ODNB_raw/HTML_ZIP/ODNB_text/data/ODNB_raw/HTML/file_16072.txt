<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Laroche <span class="st"> [Laroch]</span>,  James <span class="st"> [Jemmy]</span></span>  (<em>c.</em>1688&#150;1710?), <span class="occ">singer</span>, although active in London was possibly of French origin or birth. He first came to notice as a little boy of about seven in late December 1695 in George Granville's <span class="italic">The She-Gallants</span> at the Lincoln's Inn Fields Theatre singing &#145;So well Corinna likes the joy&#146; to music by John Eccles. He may well have been &#145;the Boy&#146; who was &#145;sometimes cited in printed songs in 1696&#146;  (Highfill, Burnim &amp; Langhans, <span class="italic">BDA</span>, 155). He sang in <span class="italic">The City Bride</span> in March of that year, and from 14 November &#145;Jemmy Laroche&#146; appeared as Cupid in Peter Anthony Motteux's <span class="italic">The Loves of Mars and Venus</span>, a musical entr'acte to Edward Ravenscroft's <span class="italic">The Anatomist</span> with music by John Eccles and Gottfried Finger. Appearances as the Child of Hercules in <span class="italic">Hercules</span> (June 1697) and as a Savoyard in <span class="italic">Europe's Revels</span> (4 November 1697) followed, in the latter of which (according to Highfill, Burnim &amp; Langhans, <span class="italic">BDA</span>) he sang &#145;The Raree-Show&#146;. This was the source of his subsequent fame and popularity. Laroche's raree-show was given in London and the provinces about 1710. The claim that the &#145;Raree show&#146;, as depicted in a print in the Harvard Theatre Collection, was played at the theatre in Little Lincoln's Inn Fields in April 1713 is doubtful, as the new theatre was not opened until December 1714.<br>
<br>There appears to be no record of Laroche's activities after 1710. He may have been the James &#145;La Roach&#146; buried at St Paul's, Covent Garden, on 4 July 1710  (Highfill, Burnim &amp; Langhans, <span class="italic">BDA</span>).<br>
<br>David J. Golby </div>
