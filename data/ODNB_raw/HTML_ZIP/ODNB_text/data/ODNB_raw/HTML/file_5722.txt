<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Clubbe,  William</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1745, <em>d.</em> 1814), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman and poet</span>, was the seventh son of the <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/5721/', "Revd John Clubbe (c.1703&#150;1773)")</script>, rector of Whatfield, Suffolk, and his wife, Susannah, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Beeston. He was baptized at Whatfield on 16 April 1745, and attended schools at Lavenham and at Hackney, Middlesex. He matriculated in 1762 at Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge, where he was a scholar from 1762 to 1768 and graduated LLB in 1769. In the same year he was instituted to the rectory of Flowton, and in the following year to the vicarage of Brandeston, both in Suffolk. He married Mary, daughter of the Revd William Henchman of Brandeston; they did not have any children.<br>
<br>In addition to performing his pastoral duties, Clubbe also composed poetry, and his works include: <span class="italic">The Emigrants</span> (1793), <span class="italic">Six Satires of Horace</span> (1795), and <span class="italic">The Epistle of Horace to the Pisos on the Art of Poetry; Translated into English Verse</span> (1797), as well as other miscellaneous translations from the classics, and translations of English works into Latin. He also produced a miscellany entitled <span class="italic">The omnium; containing the journal of a late three days tour in France; &hellip; and other miscellaneous pieces, in prose and verse</span> (1798), and published two works on education, <span class="italic">A Plain Discourse on the Subject of National Education</span> (1812) and <span class="italic">A Plain Discourse on the &hellip; Establishment of a Sunday School</span> (1812).<br>
<br>Clubbe continued to live at Brandeston until the death of his wife in 1808, when he moved to the home of his youngest brother, Nathaniel, an attorney at Framlingham. Clubbe died on 16 October 1814 at Framlingham and was buried in the churchyard at Brandeston.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Rebecca Mills </div>
