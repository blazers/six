<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Copinger,  William</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1416), <span class="occ">clerk and supposed theologian</span>, was a member of a family settled at Buxhall, Suffolk, where he was himself buried. His will is dated 20 January 1412, and was proved on 2 March 1416. Bale attributes to Copinger, whom he describes as <i>doctor</i>, a treatise <span class="italic">De vitiis et virtutibus</span>, with incipit &#145;Vani sunt omnes homines in quibus non est scientia dei&#146;, and a <i>sacramentale</i>, both of which he found in a manuscript at Balliol College, Oxford. In this he was followed by Pits, who dubs Copinger a <i>magister insignis</i>, and by Tanner. The volume Bale saw was Balliol College, Oxford, MS 83, which contains an abbreviation of <span class="italic">De vitiis et virtutibus</span> by the mid-thirteenth-century French Dominican Guillaume Peyraut, and also the <i>sacramentale</i> of Guillaume de Mont Lauzun. The colophon of the former is signed Toppynger (or perhaps Toppyng), who, however, was clearly the transcriber, not the author, of the work in question, and whose name Bale misread, replacing its capital letter &#145;T&#146; with a &#145;C&#146;. Bale also endowed his inadvertent creation with a first name, William, perhaps through a confusion with the William Copinger who was successively a scholar of Winchester College and New College, Oxford, and who proceeded BCL in 1542.<br>
<br>R. L. Poole<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Henry Summerson </div>
