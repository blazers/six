<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Conington <span class="st"> [Conyngton]</span>,  Richard</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1330), <span class="occ">Franciscan friar and theologian</span>, is of unknown origins. He is first mentioned in July 1300 when, along with John Duns Scotus, Robert Cowton, and nineteen other Oxford Franciscans, he was presented (unsuccessfully) to Bishop John Dalderby of Lincoln by the Franciscan provincial minister, Hugh of Hartlepool, for licence to hear confessions; only eight licences were granted. As an advanced student in theology he participated in disputations at Oxford in or shortly after 1300. He lectured on the <span class="italic">Sentences</span> at Oxford as a bachelor of theology <i>c</i>.1302&#150;3, and was the Franciscan regent master (DTh) at Oxford <i>c</i>.1306. He subsequently lectured on scripture as Franciscan regent master at Cambridge. His lost commentary on the penitential psalms, whose opening words, according to Bale, were &#145;Saepius dum sedebam solitarius&#146;, was probably a result of his teaching as master at Oxford or Cambridge. In 1310 he was elected provincial minister for the Franciscan order in England, a position he apparently held until 1316. He died at Cambridge in 1330 and was buried there in the Franciscan convent.<br>
<br>Although a contemporary and co-religious of Duns Scotus, Conington often favoured the positions of theologians of the previous academic generation, particularly the views of Henri de Gand, who died in 1293. John Baconthorpe, a Carmelite friar and younger contemporary, as well as an anonymous author in Vatican City, Biblioteca Apostolica Vaticana, MS Vat. lat. 869, described Conington as a disciple (<i>discipulus</i>) of Henri, which R. L. Poole interpreted to mean Conington had studied under Henri at Paris, but which Doucet read as implying only that Conington followed in Henri's intellectual footsteps. It is likely that Conington was among the select group of Franciscans sent to Paris for part of their initial theological training, which would have been in the early 1290s, but his training would have come primarily through Franciscan masters. Several of Conington's quodlibetal questions and disputed questions have survived from his teaching at Oxford or Cambridge.<br>
<br>While he was in attendance at the Council of Vienne (1311&#150;12) as provincial minister, Conington wrote a treatise on apostolic poverty, <span class="italic">Beatus qui intelligit</span> (ed. Heysse, 1930), which he followed in 1322 with a second treatise, <span class="italic">Responsiones ad conclusiones domini papae</span> (ed. Douie, 1931), which was critical of the views Pope John XXII expressed on this issue in the bull <span class="italic">Ad conditorem canonum</span> of the same year. He is also credited by Leland and Bale with a defence, <span class="italic">De Christi Dominio</span>, of John XXII against the attacks of William Ockham, and by Leland with a commentary on the <span class="italic">Quadragesimale</span> of Pope Gregory the Great. Conington was widely cited by contemporaries who, in addition to Baconthorpe, included William Alnwick, Robert Walsingham, William of Nottingham, Peter Thomas, and John Reading. Contemporary citations from his lost commentary on the <span class="italic">Sentences</span> may eventually help identify it.<br>
<br>W. J. Courtenay </div>
