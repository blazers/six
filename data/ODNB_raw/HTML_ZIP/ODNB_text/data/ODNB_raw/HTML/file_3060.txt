<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Bowes,  Thomas</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1579&#150;1589), <span class="occ">translator</span>, was educated at Clare College, Cambridge, where he graduated BA in 1579&#150;80 and MA in 1583. Bowes translated the first two volumes of Pierre de la Primaudaye's <span class="italic">Academie Fran&ccedil;oise</span> (1577 and 1583): the first volume, dedicated to Master John Barne, was issued in 1586; the second, printed in 1594 (entered in the Stationers' register in 1589), is dedicated to Sir John Puckering. The contents of the books are accurately summarized by their full titles: <span class="italic">The French academie, wherin is discoursed the institution of maners, and whatsoever els concerneth the good and happie life of all estates and callings, by preceptes of doctrine, and examples of the lives of ancient sages and famous men</span> and <span class="italic">The second part of the French academie, wherein, as it were by a naturall historie of the bodie and soule of man, the creation, matter, composition, forme, nature, profite and use of all the partes of the frame of man are handled, with the naturall causes of all affections, vertues and vices, and chiefly the nature, powers, workes and immortalitie of the soule</span>. While the second volume is almost exclusively concerned with the powers and nature of the body and the soul, the first deals with a much wider range of subjects. It contains discussions of the vices and virtues; counsel and counsellors; forms of government; the education of princes and children; death; and various facets of duty, including one's duties to God, to one's community, fellows, and family, as well as the specific duties of princes, magistrates, and soldiers. This moral and philosophical encyclopaedia, written as a dialogue, is a storehouse of exempla, quotations, proverbs, and maxims. Both volumes went through several editions. The <span class="italic">Academie</span> was pilfered by many subsequent readers and writers, such as Robert Greene and the author of <span class="italic">Boke his Surfeyt</span>, as cited by Jane Anger  (Magnusson, 311&#150;14). Bowes was a devout puritan and condemned literature as dangerous and evil: he denounced plays, mummings, and masks  (1.202), and the press is generally rebuked for printing &#145;lewde and wanton bookes &hellip; Termed Stage-playes and Theaters &hellip; [and] the Legend of lies, Huon of Burdeaux, King Arthur, with the rest of that rabble&#146; because they are &#145;the soveraigne place of Satan&#146;, responsible for &#145;the upholding of Atheisme&#146; and possessing &#145;the force to mainteine Popery in the dayes of ignorance&#146;  (vol. 2, &#145;Epist. to reader&#146;). The date and circumstances of Bowes's death are unknown; it is possible that he was the Thomas Bowes, gentleman, of East Bergholt, Suffolk, whose will was proved in 1598.<br>
<br>Joyce Boro </div>
