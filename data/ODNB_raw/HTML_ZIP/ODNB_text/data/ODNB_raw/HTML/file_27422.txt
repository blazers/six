<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Thynne,  John Alexander</span>, fourth marquess of Bath  (1831&#150;1896), <span class="occ">diplomatist and politician</span>, born in Westminster on 1 March 1831, was the eldest son of Henry Frederick Thynne, third marquess of Bath (<i>d</i>. 1837), and Harriet, daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/1380/', "Alexander Baring")</script>. He succeeded his father on 24 June 1837. Bath was educated at Eton College and matriculated from Christ Church, Oxford, on 31 May 1849. He soon began to take an active part in county business, being appointed a deputy lieutenant of Somerset in 1853, and of Wiltshire in 1860. He was also active in the volunteers and yeomanry. In 1889 he was appointed lord lieutenant of Wiltshire and chairman of the county council.<br>
<br>In May 1858 Bath was sent to Lisbon as ambassador-extraordinary and plenipotentiary, when he received from Pedro V the order of the Tower and Sword. Nine years later, in July 1867, when ambassador-extraordinary on a special mission at Vienna, he received from the emperor Franz Josef the grand cross of the order of Leopold of Austria. A keen Anglo-Catholic and a violent enemy of the <span class="roman">Public Worship Regulation Act</span> of 1874, Bath, though in general conservative in politics, played an active role in 1876&#150;8 in the campaign against the &#145;Bulgarian atrocities&#146; and Disraeli's handling of them. Longleat, Bath's house, became a centre for meetings and consultations attended by Gladstone and others. In 1880 he published <span class="italic">Observations on Bulgarian Affairs</span>. He was appointed trustee of the National Portrait Gallery in 1874, and of the British Museum in 1883. He was a member of the Academy of Belgrade in 1884. He also served on the Historical Manuscripts Commission.<br>
<br>Bath married, in August 1861, Frances Isabella Catherine Vesey (<i>d</i>. 31 Oct 1915), eldest daughter of Thomas Vesey, third Viscount de Vesci. They had three sons and three daughters. He died on 20 April 1896 at Venice and was buried at Longbridge Deverill, Wiltshire. His first son, Thomas Henry, succeeded to the title.<br>
<br>G. Le G. Norgate<span class="italic">, rev. </span> H. C. G. Matthew </div>
