<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Knowles <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Morris]</span>,  Mary</span>  (1733&#150;1807), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was born on 5 May 1733, the eldest daughter of Moses and Mary Morris of Rugeley, Staffordshire. Her talent for needlework was brought to the attention of Queen Charlotte, for whom she was commissioned to produce worsted portraits of George III and the young princess, Charlotte. Mary married Thomas Knowles (<i>d</i>. 1784), physician and author of <span class="italic">Tentamen medicum</span> (1772). The couple, who had one son, George, travelled abroad and were received at The Hague and at Versailles. A brilliant conversationalist, Mary Knowles was on intimate terms with Samuel Johnson and said of Johnson's reading that &#145;he tore the heart out of a book&#146;  (Boswell, 942). About 1776 she wrote <span class="italic">Compendium of a Controversy on Water-Baptism</span>, in which she engaged with a Revd Mr Rand of Coventry. &#145;A Poetic Correspondence&#146;, between herself and a Captain Morris, was printed in the <span class="italic">British Friend</span> (April 1848); others of her verses appeared as small tracts without dates. James Boswell recorded her talents but declined to accept as authentic her account of a &#145;Dialogue between Dr Johnson and Mrs Knowles&#146; (regarding the conversion to Quakerism of Jane Harry), which Mrs Knowles forwarded to Boswell while he was engaged on his biography of Johnson. After Anna Seward, who was present at the interview, confirmed the authenticity of the account, Mary Knowles published it in the <span class="italic">Gentleman's Magazine</span> (June 1791) and it later appeared separately in 1799. Thomas Knowles died on 16 November 1784 at Lombard Street, London. Mary died, also in London, on 3 February 1807.<br>
<br>Charlotte Fell-Smith<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Philip Carter </div>
