<div class="para">
<span class="headword">O'Neill,  Hugh</span>  (1784&#150;1824), <span class="occ">architectural draughtsman</span>, was born in Bloomsbury, London, on 20 April 1784, the son of an architect who designed a portion of Portland Place. Nothing is known of his early training but he won a silver palette from the Society of Artists in 1803, and exhibited at the Royal Academy from 1800 to 1804. From 1813 he taught drawing in Oxford and later worked in Edinburgh, Bath, and Bristol, where he settled in 1821. He was a prolific draughtsman, and made over 500 drawings of Bristol alone. Generally he retained his carefully finished originals and disposed only of copies. Many of his drawings were published; for example, his sketches of the ruins of Christ Church, Oxford, after the fire of 3 March 1809, were engraved by W. Crotch and published in Oxford in 1809. Skelton engraved a number of O'Neill's drawings of Oxford and its surroundings for the second volume of his <span class="italic">Oxonia antiqua restaurata</span> (1823) and O'Neill's work also appears in Skelton's <span class="italic">Antiquities of Bristol</span> (1820, 1826). Drawings of St Peter's Church and of Balliol, Magdalen, Exeter, and All Souls colleges (engraved by J. Basire and J. S. Storer) were published in the <span class="italic">Oxford Almanack</span> for 1809, 1810, 1812, 1813, and 1828. O'Neill also made an interesting collection of fossils, minerals, and other curiosities.<br>
<br>O'Neill died in poverty, in Princes Street, Bristol, where he was living, on 7 April 1824. He is represented in the collections of the British Museum, the Victoria and Albert Museum, the Victoria Art Gallery in Bath, the Whitworth Art Gallery in Manchester, and Reading Art Gallery.<br>
<br>M. A. Goodall </div>
