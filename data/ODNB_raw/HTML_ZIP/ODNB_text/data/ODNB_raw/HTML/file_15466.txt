<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Kerr,  Robert</span>  (1757&#150;1813), <span class="occ">scientific writer and translator</span>, was born on 20 October 1757 at Drumsheugh, Edinburgh, the son of James Kerr (<i>d</i>. 1768), jeweller and goldsmith and MP for Edinburgh city from 1747 to 1754, and his second wife, Elizabeth (1713/14&#150;1799), daughter of Lord Charles Kerr of Cramond, director of chancery in Scotland. Kerr was educated at Edinburgh high school and at the University of Edinburgh and became surgeon to Edinburgh Foundling Hospital, but relinquished a successful medical career to manage a paper mill at Ayton, Berwickshire, which eventually proved a failure. He married at St Cuthbert's Church, Edinburgh, on 27 June 1778, Julia Wardrope. They raised two sons and two daughters. Kerr returned to Edinburgh about 1810.<br>
<br>In later life Kerr occupied himself with historical writing and with translating a broad range of scientific works. His <span class="italic">Elements of Chemistry</span> (1790), drawing on the text of A. L. Lavoisier, and his <span class="italic">Animal Kingdom, or, Zoological System of Linnaeus</span> (1792), a translation of part one of Linnaeus's <span class="italic">Systema naturae</span>, gained him election as fellow of the Royal Society of Edinburgh in 1805. Kerr's works were largely derivative, and his translations were often only extracts or parts of the originals. His <span class="italic">Essay of the Theory of the Earth</span> (1813) was criticized in the <span class="italic">Edinburgh Review</span> for its misleading title; it was in fact the introductory essay to Georges Cuvier's four-volume <span class="italic">Recherches sur les ossements fossiles</span>, researches on the fossil bones found in a specific geological setting. Kerr's biographical <span class="italic">Memoirs of the Life, Writings and Correspondence of the Late Mr William Smellie</span> (1811) related to the Edinburgh printer, naturalist, and antiquary, who died in 1795, not his medical namesake. He also compiled the first ten of the eighteen volumes of <span class="italic">A General History and Collection of Voyages and Travels</span> (1811&#150;24). He died at Edinburgh on 11 October 1813 and was buried in Greyfriars churchyard. His son David Wardrope Kerr, a medical student, died in 1815 aged nineteen; he was survived by another son, then a captain in the navy, and two married daughters.<br>
<br>Thomas Seccombe<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Anita McConnell </div>
