<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Cin&aacute;ed ua hArtac&aacute;in</span> <span class="st"> [Cineth O'Hartagain]</span>  (<em>d.</em> 975), <span class="occ">poet</span>, is referred to in the notices of his death as &#145;chief poet&#146; (<i>pr&iacute;m&eacute;ices</i>) of the northern half of Ireland (for example, in the annals of Tigernach), or of Ireland as a whole (for example, in the annals of Ulster). A marginal note in the annals of Ulster adds that he belonged to &#145;the race of Cernach Sotal&#146;; that is, to the southern branch of the U&iacute; N&eacute;ill dynasty of S&iacute;l n&Aacute;eda Sl&aacute;ine, rulers of the eastern midlands. Support for this statement is perhaps provided by Cin&aacute;ed himself, who praises Cernach's descendants in his poem on the Hill of Skreen. There are no other known details of his life, except for the year of his death, 975.<br>
<br>Cin&aacute;ed's most ambitious poem is &#145;Fianna b&aacute;tar i nEmain&#146; (edited by W. Stokes), a catalogue of the graves of famous warriors. His other compositions deal with the lore of places in or near S&iacute;l n&Aacute;eda Sl&aacute;ine territory: those whose attributions to him are most generally accepted are &#145;A chloch thall for elaid uair&#146;, on a stone at Monasterboice (Louth; edited by K. Meyer), &#145;Achall ar aicce Temair&#146;, on the Hill of Skreen (Meath), &#145;&Aacute;n sin, a maig Meic ind &Oacute;c&#146;, on the Neolithic tumuli of the Boyne valley, &#145;D&eacute;iccid ferta n&iacute;thaig N&eacute;ill&#146;, on a burial mound near Tara, and &#145;&Eacute;tar &eacute;tan ri d&iacute;lind&#146;, on the Hill of Howth (Dublin) (all edited by E. Gwynn); and &#145;In chloch fors' t&aacute;t mo d&iacute; sh&aacute;il&#146;, on the oracular stone of F&aacute;l at Tara (edited by R. Macalister).<br>
<br>&#145;&Aacute;n sin&#146; concludes with praise of one Congalach, presumably the king of Ireland Congalach mac M&aacute;ele Mithig 
    (Congalach Cnogba; <i>d</i>. 956)
  ; it may well be that several of Cin&aacute;ed's other poems, dealing with sites in the immediate vicinity of Tara, were also composed under Congalach's patronage. This association with Congalach is interesting in both religious and dynastic terms. Twice in his writings Cin&aacute;ed invokes St Colum Cille (St Columba), at one point calling him the best of the U&iacute; N&eacute;ill, and there is other evidence of devotion to Columba in his family at this time; Congalach, however, seems to have been at odds with the monastic federation centred at Kells which looked back to Columba as its founder. Congalach was also a member of the northern branch of S&iacute;l n&Aacute;eda Sl&aacute;ine, the perennial and generally successful rivals of the group to which Cin&aacute;ed appears to have belonged.<br>
<br>Several manuscripts of &#145;Achall ar aicce Temair&#146; conclude with a quatrain acknowledging receipt of a horse as payment for the poem from &#145;Amla&iacute;b of populous Dublin&#146;, who &#145;has taken the kingship in Howth&#146;. This is the viking king of Dublin, Olaf Cuar&aacute;n (Olaf Sihtricson), who was Congalach's ally for several years but subsequently party to the ambush in which the latter lost his life. Cin&aacute;ed's poem on Howth may likewise have been composed for Olaf; perhaps also &#145;A chloch thall&#146;, since Monasterboice seems at one point to have been in Olaf's hands.<br>
<br>John Carey </div>
