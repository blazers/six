<span class="headword">Dick <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Mackenzie]</span>,  Anne</span>, Lady Dick  (<em>d.</em> 1741), <span class="occ">eccentric</span>, was the daughter of a Scottish law lord, Sir James Mackenzie, Lord Royston (1671&#150;1744), third son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/17580/', "George Mackenzie, first earl of Cromarty")</script>, and his wife, Elizabeth. Neither the date of Anne's birth nor the date of her marriage to William Cunyngham (<i>d</i>. 1746) are known. Cunyngham, who adopted the name of Dick, became Sir William Dick of Prestonfield, bt, on the death of his maternal grandfather in 1728. The marriage did not produce any children. Lady Dick's eccentric behaviour, which included walking through the streets of Edinburgh with her maid, both dressed in boys' clothing, made her notorious in the eyes of her contemporaries. She was also known as a writer of coarse lampoons and epigrams in verse, three specimens of which are reprinted in C. Kirkpatrick Sharpe's <span class="italic">Book of Ballads</span> (1823). Her verses seem to have earned the disapproval of her friends, who wished her talents could be put to more seemly use. Lady Dick died in 1741. Her husband, who survived her until 1746, was succeeded in his baronetcy by his brother, the physician Sir Alexander Dick.<br>
<br>Jennett Humphreys<span class="italic">, rev. </span> David Turner </div>
