<div class="para">
<span class="headword">&AElig;lfstan of Boscombe</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1043&#150;1065), <span class="occ">nobleman</span>, is identified by <span class="roman">Domesday Book</span> as a substantial landholder, with an estate of some 237 hides of land, scattered over eight shires, making him the fifth richest of the recorded pre-conquest thegns below the rank of earl. Most of this land was probably his inheritance, but the charter whereby King Edward gave him Sevington, Wiltshire, is still extant  (<span class="italic">AS chart.</span>, S 999). Clearly he was an important magnate; and he is probably the &AElig;lfstan who attests royal charters between 1043 and 1065, latterly as <i>princeps</i>, an appellation suggesting high rank. His largest concentration of land (just over 70 hides) was in Wiltshire; this included two minor estates held as a tenant of the abbot of Malmesbury and the bishop of Sherborne respectively.<br>
<br>&AElig;lfstan also had a respectable holding (just over 50 hides) in Bedfordshire and Hertfordshire. In these shires he is described as a thegn of King Edward, which suggests that he was a royal servant. If he is identical with &AElig;lfstan <i>wicgerefa</i> (&#145;portreeve&#146;) who, between 1053 and 1066, attests a private grant of land in Bedfordshire to St Albans  (<span class="italic">AS chart.</span>, S 1235), he may have been reeve of Bedford; another St Albans memorandum is attested by Godwine, portreeve of Oxford  (<span class="italic">AS chart.</span>, S 1425). He may also be identical with the &AElig;lfstan the Staller, who attests a Kentish document of <i>c</i>.1045  (<span class="italic">AS chart.</span>, S 1471), for stallers are sometimes found well outside their own areas as agents for the king's interests. The name is not uncommon, however, and unless &AElig;lfstan is given the distinctive toponym of his estate at Boscombe, Wiltshire, his identity cannot be established with certainty without other indications.<br>
<br>&AElig;lfstan may have died in or before 1066, for he attests no documents of the Conqueror's reign. The bulk of his estate, with some inconsiderable exceptions, passed first to Ralph de Limesey, who was perhaps sheriff of Hampshire early in William I's reign, and thence to the 1086 holder, William, count of Eu.<br>
<br>Ann Williams </div>
