<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Falconer,  Alexander</span>, Lord Falconer of Halkertoun  (1593/4&#150;1671), <span class="occ">judge</span>, was the eldest son of Sir Alexander Falconer of Halkertoun (or Halkerton), Kincardineshire (<i>d</i>. 1645/6), and Agnes (<i>d</i>. 1634), daughter of Sir David Carnegie of Colluthie and Kinnaird and his second wife, Euphame Wemyss, and sister of David Carnegie, first earl of Southesk (1574/5&#150;1658). He married, about April 1619, Anne, daughter of John, Lord Lindsay of the Byres (<i>d</i>. 1609), and Anne, daughter of Lawrence Oliphant, master of Oliphant; they had two sons and a daughter. He and his wife separated in 1627 by agreement approved by the privy council.<br>
<br>Falconer was appointed a lord of session on 9 July 1639, at the beginning of a turbulent period in the court's history, taking the judicial title of Lord Halkertoun. According to Fountainhall he gained office by paying his predecessor, Lord Woodhall, 7000 merks to stand down. He was reappointed <i>ad vitam aut culpam</i> on 13 November 1641, but was removed from the session in the purge of 1649 for &#145;malignancy&#146; in supporting the engagement. He was reappointed at the Restoration and remained in office until his death. Falconer was commissioner to the convention of estates in 1643&#150;4, and commissioner to parliament in 1644&#150;6, representing Kincardineshire. He was also a commissioner for the loan and tax in 1643; commissioner of treasury in 1645; on the committee of estates in 1645, 1646, and 1648; and on the committee of war for Kincardineshire in 1643, 1644, 1646, and 1648. He was a commissioner of supply for Kincardineshire in 1656 and 1659. He was created Lord Falconer of Halkertoun on 20 December 1646 and a privy councillor in 1661. Falconer died in Edinburgh on 1 October 1671.<br>
<br>A brother, Sir David Falconer of Glenfarquhar, was also a member of the committee of war, and assented for Kincardineshire to the English parliament's tender of union in 1652, being one of twenty-one deputies elected to treat in London with the parliament. Another brother, Sir John (<i>d</i>. 1670), was master of the (Scottish) mint to Charles I and Charles II.<br>
<br>W. D. H. Sellar </div>
