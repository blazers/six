<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Kirwan,  Stephen</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1601), <span class="occ">Church of Ireland bishop of Clonfert</span>, was born in Galway and came from a prominent Anglo-Norman family. He was typical of the transitional generation of native Irish clergy in Elizabeth's reign who accepted appointments within the established (though hardly protestant) church, but whose own religious convictions were ambiguous, and whose children were Roman Catholic. He was educated at Oxford <i>c</i>.1566, and in Paris and Louvain, where, according to a hostile source, &#145;he both heard and said many a mass&#146;  (Cunningham, 19). During his studies he held the archdeaconry of Annaghdown and the rectory of Killmacryan (both Tuam). In 1572 he was nominated by the queen to the see of Kilmacduagh in the province of Tuam; letters patent for his consecration were issued on 13 April 1573. On 30 March 1582 he was nominated to Clonfert, and letters patent for his consecration were issued on 24 May. Active in local government in Connaught, he served as a justice and commissioner for the province, 1580&#150;1601.<br>
<br>The strongly protestant Sir Turlough O'Brien claimed <i>c</i>.1591 that though at the time of his appointment to Clonfert, Kirwan &#145;professed and swore to reformed disposition&#146;, he was in fact a lukewarm evangelist, more interested in the &#145;glutted security of corporate ease&#146; than preaching. Kirwan could, O'Brien thought, best be termed &#145;the English-Romish bishop O'Kirovan of Clonfert&#146;. Kirwan's eldest son, according to O'Brien, had run away to Spain and become an &#145;Antichristian rebel, a Spanish subject&#146;, while his other two sons, who remained in Ireland, were &#145;papish recusants&#146;  (Cunningham, 19). Kirwan died before 4 November 1601, when the see was said to be vacant following his death; at the time of his death he also held the deanery of Clonfert.<br>
<br>Alan Ford </div>
