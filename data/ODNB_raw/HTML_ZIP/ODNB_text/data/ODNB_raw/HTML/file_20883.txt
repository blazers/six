<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Osborne, Lord  Sydney Godolphin</span>  (1808&#150;1889), <span class="occ">philanthropist</span>, third son of Francis Godolphin Osborne, first Baron Godolphin (1777&#150;1850), and Elizabeth Charlotte (<i>d</i>. 1847), daughter of William Eden, first Baron Auckland, was born at Stapleford in Cambridgeshire on 5 February 1808. When in 1859 his elder brother, George Godolphin (1802&#150;1872), succeeded his cousin, Francis Godolphin D'Arcy Osborne, as eighth duke of Leeds, he obtained the rank of a duke's son.<br>
<br>Osborne was educated at Rugby School and from 1824 at Brasenose College, Oxford. He was awarded a BA in 1830, and, having taken holy orders, was appointed rector of Stoke Poges in Buckinghamshire in 1832. In 1841 he accepted the living of Durweston in Dorset, which was in the gift of Lord Portman, and he occupied that incumbency until 1875. Osborne married on 29 May 1834 Emily (<i>d</i>. 1875), daughter of Pascoe Grenfell of Taplow Court, Buckinghamshire, and was thus brother-in-law of Charles Kingsley (1819&#150;1875) and James Anthony Froude (1818&#150;1894). The couple had two sons and two daughters.<br>
<br>Osborne is best known for his series of &#145;lay sermons&#146; delivered from the pulpit of <span class="italic">The Times</span> under the signature S. G. O., which gained him something of a national reputation in his day. A militant and controversial philanthropist, in his published letters (1844&#150;84) he addressed matters as diverse as free trade, education, sanitation, women's rights, cattle plague, and cholera. Despite his relationship to Kingsley and his link, through him, to the Christian socialists, Osborne was essentially a tory paternalist, although he kept clear of party politics.<br>
<br>During the Crimean War, Osborne travelled to the East, made an unofficial inspection of the hospitals under Florence Nightingale's care, and published the results in <span class="italic">Scutari and its Hospitals</span> (1855). He was publicly thanked in parliament for his self-appointed task.<br>
<br>On the Irish question, in which he took a special interest after a visit to the west of Ireland during the famine of 1849, Osborne was a strong unionist, and in ecclesiastical matters he was an opponent of Tractarianism. His special cause was the plight of the agricultural labourer, of whom his knowledge was unrivalled, while his forecast of the villager's social and political emancipation and its results was remarkably acute. The last letters of Osborne's series addressed to <span class="italic">The Times</span> were on the subject of the Whitechapel murders. A selection from the letters was published, with a brief introduction by Arnold White (2 vols., 1888). Osborne's other writings included <span class="italic">Gleanings in the West of Ireland</span> (1850), <span class="italic">Lady Eva: her Last Days: a Tale</span> (1851), <span class="italic">Hints to the Charitable</span> (1856), <span class="italic">Hints for the Amelioration of the Moral Condition of a Village</span> (1856), and <span class="italic">Letters on the Education of Young Children</span> (1866). In 1875 Osborne resigned his benefice and retired to Lewes, Sussex, where he died at his home, Pelham House, on 9 May 1889.<br>
<br>Thomas Seccombe<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Mark Clement </div>
