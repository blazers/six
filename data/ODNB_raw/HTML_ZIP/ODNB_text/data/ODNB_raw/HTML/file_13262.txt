<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hildrop,  John</span>  (1682&#150;1756), <span class="occ">religious writer</span>, born on 30 December 1682 in Petersfield, Hampshire, was the son of William Hildrop of Petersfield. He matriculated on 25 November 1698 from St John's College, Oxford, and graduated BA on 7 July 1702, MA on 8 June 1705, and BD and DD on 9 June 1743. On 14 April 1703 he was presented with the mastership of the Royal Free Grammar School at Marlborough, Wiltshire, by Thomas Bruce, third earl of Elgin and second earl of Ailesbury. He published several works on the apocalypse between 1711 and 1713, and in 1722 <span class="italic">Reflections upon Reason</span> appeared, a satire on freethinking, attributed at first to Bishop Francis Gastrell, and examined by Thomas Morgan in <span class="italic">Enthusiasm in Distress</span> (1722).<br>
<br>Hildrop was appointed rector of Maulden, Bedfordshire, in 1733. He resigned the school mastership on 4 December 1733 and, three months later, he resigned the rectory. On 13 April 1734 he was instituted to the rectory of Wath, near Ripon, Yorkshire, on the presentation of his patron's son Charles Bruce, Lord Bruce, later fourth earl of Elgin and third earl of Ailesbury, to whom he was chaplain. Hildrop published&#151;alongside several less noteworthy tracts&#151;various fugitive essays of a satirical and polemical character, chiefly directed against deists, either anonymously or under the pseudonyms of Phileleutherus Britannicus and Timothy Hooker. Though of slight intrinsic value, they are written in an unusually nervous, yet easy and entertaining style. These include <span class="italic">An Essay for the Better Regulation and Improvement of Free-Thinking</span> (1739) and <span class="italic">The Contempt of the Clergy Considered</span> (1739); the latter argued for the liberation of the church from state control. His <span class="italic">Letter to a member of parliament containing a proposal &hellip; to revise, amend, or repeal &hellip; the ten commandments</span> is an amusing <i>jeu d'esprit</i>. On its first appearance in 1738 it was attributed to Swift, but it appears in Hildrop's <span class="italic">Miscellaneous Works</span> (1754), along with <span class="italic">A Modest Apology for the Ancient and Honourable Family of the Wrongheads</span> and <span class="italic">Some Memoirs of the Life of Simon Sallow</span>. In 1740 Hildrop became a regular contributor to the <span class="italic">Weekly Miscellany</span>. In 1741 his <span class="italic">An Essay on Honour</span> appeared and in 1742 he printed a work attempting to prove that animals' souls are degraded because of the fall of man, based on Guillaume Hyacinthe Bougeant's <span class="italic">Philosophical Amusement</span>. He also published a collection of sermons as <span class="italic">The Husbandman's Spiritual Companion</span> (1752). Hildrop died on 18 January 1756.<br>
<br>J. M. Rigg<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Adam Jacob Levin </div>
