<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Boyd,  Elizabeth</span> <span class="st"> [<em>pseud. </em>Louisa]</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1727&#150;1745), <span class="occ">poet and novelist</span>, apparently lived in London throughout her productive years. All that is known about her life or family comes from a few statements in her published works. In <span class="italic">The Snail</span> (1745), the only issue of her proposed periodical, she speaks of her &#145;deceased Father having long and zealously serv'd the <i>Stuart</i> family in a creditable Employ&#146;  (p. 15); and in an &#145;Advertisement&#146; added to <span class="italic">The Happy Unfortunate, or, The Female Page</span> (1732), she states she is publishing the novel:<blockquote>with a view of settling myself in a way of Trade; that may enable me to master those Exigencies of Fortune, which my long Illness hath for some Time past reduc'd me to suffer; That I may be capable of providing for my now ancient, indulgent Mother; whom Age, and the Charge of many Children hath render'd incapable of providing for herself. &hellip; I shall directly sell Paper, Pens, Ink &hellip; Almanacks, Plays, Pamphlets, and all Manner of Stationary Goods.</blockquote>The 353 subscriptions, among them a large number from the nobility, presumably enabled her to open the shop, possibly at her address in George Court, Princes Street.<br>
<br>The novel is set in Cyprus, in the tradition of Arcadian romance, with a convoluted plot that demonstrates the evil effects of arranged or loveless marriages. Amanda, Boyd's learned heroine, articulates the matrimonial ideal when she says:<blockquote>A Man must be settled enough to form a certain Judgement, of his own Affections and Inclinations, before in my Opinion he's qualified for either a Husband, a Lover, or a Friend, which three Persons ought to be always blended in one, if Happiness is propos'd in the Connubials. (2.215&#150;16)</blockquote>The novel was reissued as <span class="italic">The Female Page</span> in 1737.<br>
<br>In 1733 Boyd published <span class="italic">The Humorous Miscellany, or, Riddles for the Beaux</span>, a small collection of songs, riddles, and occasional verse. Her other poems include <span class="italic">Variety</span> (1727); <span class="italic">Verses most Humbly Inscrib'd to his Majesty King George IId on his Birth-Day</span> (1730); <span class="italic">The Happy North-Briton</span> (1737); and <span class="italic">Glory to the Highest, a Thanksgiving Poem on the Late Victory at Dettingen</span> (1743). <span class="italic">Admiral Haddock, or, The Progress of Spain</span> (1739 or 1740), is a long patriotic poem in which she reviews the defeat of the armada in 1588 and praises the naval actions of 1739. Her ballad-opera, <span class="italic">Don Sancho, or, The Students' Whim &hellip; with Minerva's Triumph, a Masque</span> (1739), is a strange mix of comedy, necromancy, and masque, set in the garden of an Oxford college, where Don Sancho conjures up assorted spirits, including the ghosts of Shakespeare and Dryden. The piece was never performed but was given a reading in the green room of Drury Lane Theatre. Although Boyd frequently mentions her ill health, neither the cause nor date of her death is known.<br>
<br>Joyce Fullard </div>
