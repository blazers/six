<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Wimborne,  Walter  of</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1261&#150;1266), <span class="occ">Franciscan friar and poet</span>, began his career at Wimborne Minster, Dorset, where he was first a schoolteacher and then joined the newly arrived Franciscan order. His up-to-date knowledge of Aristotle suggests that he may have attended lectures at Oxford, but he became Franciscan lector at Cambridge (between 1261 and 1266), and is finally associated with the friary at Norwich. A series of cumulative misunderstandings, beginning in the sixteenth century, led to the division of his works between two poets, of the thirteenth and fourteenth century respectively.<br>
<br>Walter's poems include satire (<span class="italic">De palpone</span>, <span class="italic">De symonia</span>, <span class="italic">De mundi vanitate</span>, <span class="italic">De mundi scelere</span>) and devotional works to the Virgin Mary (<span class="italic">Ave Virgo</span>, <span class="italic">Marie Carmina</span>). He employed several metres, all involving rhyme, and is notable for striking images and an exotic vocabulary, much of his own coining. He influenced, or was influenced by, John of Howden. The length of his poems caused some to be abbreviated, and passages from the <span class="italic">Marie Carmina</span> were adapted as hymns by Richard Ledred in the fourteenth century. His prose <span class="italic">Tractatus moralis super quatuor elementa</span> (of which only part survives) is an allegorical interpretation of the physical world. He had read widely in classical writings (especially the satirists and Solinus), patristic authors, medieval poets (Jean de Hauville, John of Howden), Marian devotees (Richard de St Laurent), and the new translations of Averroes and Aristotle. His greatest skill was in weaving these disparate strands into a highly affective and effective poetic style.<br>
<br>A. G. Rigg </div>
