<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Dow,  Robert</span>  (1553&#150;1588), <span class="occ">music copyist</span>, was the eldest of five sons born to Lettice Bull and Robert Dow (1523&#150;1612), citizen and merchant taylor of London. His father was collector of tonnage and poundage for the port of London. He may have attended Merchant Taylors' School before going up to Corpus Christi College, Oxford, where he was admitted BA on 14 January 1574, having supplicated for the degree on 12 October 1573. Although his name does not appear in the Corpus registers, Dow wrote Lord Burghley three Latin letters in 1573, &#145;Oxoniae in Collegio Corporis Christi&#146;. This correspondence sought to secure royal support for Dow's efforts to gain admittance to All Souls College as a student of civil law, but while this was not immediately forthcoming, he was eventually elected to a probationary fellowship there on 28 November 1575. At this time he was giving lessons in penmanship, and could count Robert Sidney among his pupils.<br>
<br>On 14 January 1577 Dow was admitted to All Souls as BA and student in laws. He supplicated for the degree of BCL on 29 March 1582 and was admitted on 26 April following. From 1585 to 1587 he was bursar of laws at All Souls, and in the latter year he contributed to <span class="italic">Exequiae Illustrissimi equitis</span>, a collection of verse in memory of Sir Philip Sidney; his elegy reveals that Dow had travelled to Poland and met King Stephen B&aacute;thory. Dow died intestate on 10 November 1588, presumably at All Souls. The inventory of his goods included a library of over 300 volumes, among them a set of music partbooks in his own fine italic hand which survives today as Oxford, Christ Church, MSS 984&#150;988. It contains instrumental pieces, songs, anthems, and motets, some of which bear annotations in Latin about their composers. Dated 1581, it is one of the most important sources of Elizabethan music, even if in the vocal pieces the scribe occasionally modernizes the composer's setting by altering notes and text underlay. Dow's music manuscripts are the major source of the compositions of Robert White. The content of his library may suggest recusant sympathies.<br>
<br>David Mateer </div>
