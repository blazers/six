<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Blakey,  Nicholas</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1758), <span class="occ">draughtsman and engraver</span>, is principally known for his book illustrations and for several large-scale scenes representing episodes from Britain's early history. He was born in Ireland and by 1747 was studying in Paris. Here, it can be assumed, he developed the late rococo style that can be seen throughout his work but is most notable in his composition of dancing nymphs in a rocaille cartouche. He worked principally for publishers, producing, and sometimes engraving, illustrations to embellish a wide variety of books. Although it is not clear whether he returned from Paris, he regularly collaborated with other noted book illustrators, including L. P. Boitard, Charles Grignion, Simon Fran&ccedil;ois Ravenet, and Louis G&eacute;rard Scotin, on British publications. This was the team behind the illustrations for Bernard Siegfried Albinus's anatomical publication <span class="italic">Tables of the Skeleton and Muscles of the Human Body</span> (1749), where Blakey's earliest published designs appear.<br>
<br>Between 1751 and 1753 Blakey worked closely with the painter Francis Hayman, producing frontispieces, very much in Hayman's distinctive style, for <span class="italic">The Works of Alexander Pope</span> (1751), Jonas Hanway's <span class="italic">An Historical Account of the British Trade over the Caspian Sea</span> (1753), and J. H. Merchant's <span class="italic">The Revolution in Persia</span> (1753)&#151;all of which are simply signed &#145;Blakey del.&#146;. Again produced with Hayman, Blakey's most complex works were designed for a subscription scheme launched by the publishers John and Paul Knapton and Richard Dodsley between 1749 and 1750. Eventually published as part of their <span class="italic">English History Delineated</span> (1750&#150;52), compositions such as <span class="italic">Alfred in the Isle of Athelney</span> reveal Blakey's abilities with expression and the human figure, as well as his sensitivity to the contemporary debates surrounding the need for a school of history painting in England. Blakey died in Paris on 20 November 1758, survived by his wife, Elizabeth, who died in 1770.<br>
<br>Lucy Peltz </div>
