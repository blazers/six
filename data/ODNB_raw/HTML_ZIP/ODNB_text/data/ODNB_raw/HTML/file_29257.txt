<div class="para">
<span class="headword">White,  John Meadows</span>  (1799/1800&#150;1863), <span class="occ">lawyer</span>, was born at Halesworth, Suffolk, the younger son of Robert Gostling White (<i>d</i>. 1828), a solicitor, and his second wife, Elizabeth Meadows (<i>d</i>. 1831); <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/29270/', "Robert Meadows White")</script>, Anglo-Saxonist, was his elder brother. On 17 September 1825 he married, at Halesworth, Anne, daughter of Robert Crabtree, an attorney there; they had many children. White was a partner of his father in Halesworth; he then moved to London, becoming partner of T. Barett in Great St Helen's Street. He was later senior partner of Borrett, White, and Borrett, in Lincoln's Inn.<br>
<br>White established a considerable reputation and practice as a parliamentary solicitor, specializing in poor-law, tithe, and copyhold business, on which subjects he published pamphlets, notably those of 1829, 1834, and 1835 on poor-law reform, and of 1841 on copyhold enfranchisement. From 1842 until his death he (acting for his firm) was solicitor to the ecclesiastical commission, in which office he was succeeded by his son, Robert Holmes White. He practised in London from 1835 until 1851 in Lincoln's Inn, and from 1851 in Whitehall Place. White died at Weymouth in Dorset on 19 March 1863.<br>
<br>H. C. G. Matthew </div>
