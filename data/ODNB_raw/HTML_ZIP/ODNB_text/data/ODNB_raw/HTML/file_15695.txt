<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Klitz,  Philip</span>  (1805&#150;1854), <span class="occ">composer</span>, was born on 7 January 1805 at Lymington, Hampshire, the eldest of the six sons of George Philip Klitz (1777&#150;1839), of Biebrich, Germany, a composer and drum-major in the Royal Flintshire militia, and his wife, Elizabeth Lane of Boldre (1775&#150;1838). All six sons became musicians.<br>
<br>Philip Klitz moved to Southampton about 1828, and in 1831 conducted Paganini's concert there. A brilliant pianist and violinist, he was also a popular lecturer and a successful proponent of the Hullah system of sight-singing. He became organist of St Lawrence and St Joseph's Church, Southampton, and from 1845 to his death was organist of All Saints' Church. On 20 June 1834 or 1835 he married Charlotte Lyte, half-sister of Henry F. Lyte, the hymn writer. They had one son, George, who also became a composer.<br>
<br>Among Klitz's compositions were <span class="italic">Songs of the mid-watch, the poetry by Captain Willes Johnson, the music composed for and dedicated to the British navy</span> (1838). These six songs were, by order of the Admiralty, reprinted in <span class="italic">Songs of Charles Dibdin, arranged by T. Dibdin</span> (1850). Klitz was one of the first to write songs for the concerts of &#145;black-face minstrels&#146;: both &#145;Miss Ginger&#146; and &#145;Dinah Dear&#146; (1847) became very popular. He was an active freemason, and his &#145;Faith, Hope, and Charity&#146; was performed at the Hampshire lodges. As well as his musical works, he also published <span class="italic">Sketches of life, character, and scenery in the New Forest: a series of tales, rural, domestic, legendary, and humorous</span> (1850). He died at 24 Portland Place, Southampton, on 13 January 1854.<br>
<br>G. C. Boase<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Anne Pimlott Baker </div>
