<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ashworth,  Henry</span>  (1785&#150;1811), <span class="occ">naval officer</span>, was born in London in December 1785. In November 1799 he entered the 38-gun frigate <i>Hussar</i>, under the immediate patronage of the first lieutenant, and four years later he was serving as midshipman on the same ship when she was lost on the Saintes, near Brest, on 8 February 1804. While a prisoner of war, Ashworth made several remarkable attempts to escape; and at last, having escaped from Bitche in December 1808, he succeeded in passing through Germany to Trieste, where he got on board the British frigate <i>L'Unit&eacute;</i>. On 31 October 1809 he was promoted lieutenant, and was on the <i>Centaur</i> (74 guns) off the coast of Spain when the French took Tarragona on 28 June 1811 and drove some of the panic-stricken inhabitants into the sea. Ashworth had command of one of the boats sent to rescue them and received a wound, of which he died a month later at Minorca on 25 July 1811.<br>
<br>Ashworth's curious adventures as a midshipman, in company with master's mate Donat Henchy O'Brien, were recounted in the <span class="italic">Naval Chronicle</span>, volumes 28&#150;31 and 33, and were apparently the original of an episode in Frederick Marryatt's novel <span class="italic">Peter Simple</span>.<br>
<br>J. K. Laughton<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Andrew Lambert </div>
