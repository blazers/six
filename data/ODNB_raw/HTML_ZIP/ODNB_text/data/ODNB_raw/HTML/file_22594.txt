<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Poste,  Beale</span>  (1793&#150;1871), <span class="occ">antiquary</span>, of an ancient Kentish family, was the second son of William Post (1738&#150;1806), one of the four common pleaders of the city of London. Born at Hayle Place, his father's seat near Maidstone, Kent, he was educated at Cheam School in Surrey and in 1811 matriculated from Trinity College, Cambridge, before migrating to Trinity Hall in July 1813. He then left the university to travel on the continent and in 1817 married Mary Jane (<i>d</i>. 1869), daughter of John Cousens of Westbourne, Sussex. He returned to Cambridge to graduate LLB in 1819. He was ordained in 1820 and was for some years curate of High Halden, and then of Milstead, both in Kent. At Milstead he devoted himself to the study of archaeology. He was one of the earliest members of the Archaeological Association, and many papers from his pen appeared in the association's <span class="italic">Journal</span>.<br>
<br>Poste's chief works, which dealt mainly with early British history, were a <span class="italic">History of the College of All Saints</span> (1847), <span class="italic">The Coins of Cunobeline and of the Ancient Britons</span> (1853), <span class="italic">Britannic Researches, or, New Facts and rectifications of Ancient British History</span> (1853), <span class="italic">Britannia antiqua</span> (1857), and <span class="italic">Celtic Inscriptions on Gaulish and British Coins</span> (1861). The Postes had three sons and four daughters. About 1851 they moved to Bydews Tovil, near Maidstone, where Poste died on 15 April 1871.<br>
<br>E. G. Hawke<span class="italic">, rev. </span> H. C. G. Matthew </div>
