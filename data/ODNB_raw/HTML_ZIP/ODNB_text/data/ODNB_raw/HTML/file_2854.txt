<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Bonnor,  Thomas</span>  (<em>c.</em>1743&#150;1807<span class="mult">x</span>12), <span class="occ">draughtsman and engraver</span>, was probably born in Gloucestershire. He was apprenticed in 1758 to Henry Roberts, engraver, for a 20 guinea premium. In 1763 he was awarded a premium by the Society of Arts for a landscape by an artist aged under twenty-one, and in the same year his drawing of the comedian Richard Yates as Launce was engraved and published by Roberts. Working chiefly for magazines, he designed and engraved a number of portraits of actors such as Ann Catley and Spranger Barry, in character, and he also designed illustrations to the works of Richardson, Smollett, and Fielding. In 1773 he exhibited drawings, including a design for the Gloucestershire Society, at the Society of Artists, together with a proof of a landscape after Adriaen Boudewijns and Pieter Bout. This was published later that year.<br>
<br>Bonnor, however, failed as an engraver&#150;publisher and in 1781 he was imprisoned in the king's bench prison as an insolvent debtor. Whether his work for Nash's <span class="italic">Collections for the History of Worcestershire</span> (1781&#150;2) had landed him in debt or helped to get him out of it, his later career was devoted chiefly to topographical illustration. He drew and engraved country houses, churches, and monuments for John Collinson's <span class="italic">The History and Antiquities of the County of Somerset</span> (1791), Ralph Bigland's <span class="italic">Historical, Monumental, and Genealogical Collections Relative to the County of Gloucester</span> (1791&#150;2), and Richard Polwhele's <span class="italic">The History of Devonshire</span> (1793&#150;1806). By 1799 he had published four numbers of a proposed <span class="italic">New Copperplate Perspective Itinerary</span>, containing views of Gloucester Cathedral and Goodrich Castle, for which he also wrote the text. He exhibited some drawings of architectural ruins at the Royal Academy in 1807, and he died between that date and 1812.<br>
<br>Timothy Clayton </div>
