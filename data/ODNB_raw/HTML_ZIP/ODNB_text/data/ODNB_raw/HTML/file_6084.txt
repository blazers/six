<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Connellan,  Owen</span>  (1800&#150;1869), <span class="occ">Irish scholar</span>, was born in co. Sligo, the son of a farmer who claimed descent from the chiefs of Bunnyconnellan in Mayo, and through them from Laoghaire MacNeill, king of Ireland. He studied Irish literature, and worked for more than twenty years as a scribe in the Royal Irish Academy, where he copied a great part of the large collections of Irish writings known as the books of <span class="roman">Lecan</span> and of <span class="roman">Ballymote</span>. After the visit of George IV to Ireland in 1821, Connellan was appointed Irish historiographer to the king, a post which he also held throughout the reign of William IV. In 1849, shortly after its establishment, Connellan was made professor of Irish at Queen's College, Cork, where he remained until his death in Dublin in 1869.<br>
<br>Connellan wrote a number of grammatical analyses of the gospels and a dissertation on the Irish language. His views on its evolution were somewhat eccentric; he admired Sir William Betham, whose <span class="italic">Etruria Celtica</span> he believed had proved that the Irish and Etruscan languages were identical. In 1844 he published a <span class="italic">Practical Grammar of the Irish Language</span>, which is of value as a record of the idiom and pronunciation of Irish in the north of Connaught. His edition of <span class="italic">The Annals of Ireland, Translated from the Original Irish of the Four Masters</span> (1846) was superseded by that of John O'Donovan (1860). His most important work was a translation of the <span class="italic">Imtheacht na tromdh&aacute;imhe, or, The Proceedings of the Great Bardic Institutions</span> (1860).<br>
<br>Norman Moore<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Marie-Louise Legg </div>
