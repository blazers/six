<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Wharton,  John</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1575&#150;1578), <span class="occ">religious writer and schoolmaster</span>, appears to have lived in London. He signed the preface to <span class="italic">A misticall devise of the spiritual and godly love betwene Christ the spouse, and the church or congregation</span> (1575), attributed on the title-page to Jud Smith. In Wharton's opinion, the work, in verse and adapted from the <span class="roman">Song of Solomon</span>, was far more edifying than &#145;olde bables, as I may terme them, or stale tales of Chaucer&#146;  (&#145;To the Christian reader&#146;). Henry Hickman was licensed in 1576 to print Wharton's &#145;novels&#146;, while on 26 July the same year his ballad &#145;Whartons follie&#146; was licensed to John Hunter. Wharton also issued <span class="italic">Whartons dreame: conteyninge an invective agaynst certaine abhominable caterpillers as userers, extorcioners, leasmongers and such others</span> (1578), describing himself on the title-page as a schoolmaster. He claimed that the work had been &#145;Perused and thought well of for the correcting of vice and terrifying of the wicked: by these following. John Fox, Robert Crowley, William Wager, Thomas Buckmaister, and others&#146;  (title-page verso). In a preface to Alexander Nowell, the dean of St Paul's, Wharton presented &#145;the little talent of my exile and slender learning&#146; and apologized for &#145;my rusticall and rude sentences&#146;. &#145;To the Christian reader&#146; complains of the city of London that &#145;there was never such avarice in Sodom, never such riotousness in Gomorrah, never such pride in Tyre&#146; and predicts &#145;a day of correction which will be most sharp and horrible&#146;; there then follow verses upon &#145;Wharton's dream&#146; by Buckmaister, William Vallans, Thomas Smith, and George Rogers. Nothing further is known of Wharton.<br>
<br>E. I. Carlyle<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Stephen Wright </div>
