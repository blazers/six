<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lablache,  Luigi</span>  (1795&#150;1858), <span class="occ">singer</span>, son of Nicolas Lablache (<i>d</i>. 1799), an &eacute;migr&eacute; Marseilles merchant, and his wife, Marie Francesca Bietagh (of Irish descent), was born at Naples on 6 December 1795. In 1806 he entered the Conservatorio della Piet&agrave; dei Turchini, the only survivor of the famous Naples music schools. His education, lasting until 1812, made him a thorough musician. In that year he won an engagement at the Teatro San Carlino, home of popular comic opera. In 1813 he married the singer Teresa Pinotti; they had thirteen children, several of them, notably <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/15836/', "Frederick Lablache")</script>, likewise singers.<br>
<br>After several years singing at Palermo (1816&#150;20), Lablache was by 1821, when he appeared at La Scala, a finished singer&#151;a bass-baritone with an even two-octave range from E to E and the ability to sing both traditional comic parts and the new virtuoso serious roles of Romantic opera. Through Domenico Barbaja, who from 1823 ran the leading opera houses of Milan, Naples, and Vienna, Lablache quickly became known in all three cities; in 1827 he took part in Beethoven's funeral. He showed his versatility by singing at various times all three lower parts in Rossini's <span class="italic">Il barbiere di Siviglia</span> as well as the florid, sinister part of Assur in his <span class="italic">Semiramide</span>.<br>
<br>From 1829 to 1851 in Paris, and from 1834 to 1855 in London, Lablache divided his career largely between the Italian opera houses of the two richest countries in the world. He still created roles in new operas: in Bellini's <span class="italic">I puritani</span> (Paris, 1835) as one of the &#145;<i>Puritani</i> quartet&#146; (with Grisi, Rubini, and Tamburini), long remembered as the finest of singers; in ten of Donizetti's operas, including <span class="italic">Don Pasquale</span>, his most famous comic performance (Paris, 1843); and in one by Verdi (<span class="italic">I masnadieri</span>, Her Majesty's, 1847). In London he taught singing to Princess (later Queen) Victoria. A late sensation was his Caliban in Hal&eacute;vy's <span class="italic">La tempesta</span> (Her Majesty's, 1850), and from 1852 to 1857 he sang each year at St Petersburg. In 1857 his health declined rapidly; he died in Naples on 23 January 1858, and was buried with his wife at Maisons-Laffitte, near Paris.<br>
<br>Lablache was a very big man, yet graceful in his movements; his voice was likewise huge but under firm control. In a long career he was universally admired as a matchless singing actor, to the critic Henry Chorley &#145;one of the greatest musicians and artists ever seen or heard&#146;.<br>
<br>John Rosselli </div>
