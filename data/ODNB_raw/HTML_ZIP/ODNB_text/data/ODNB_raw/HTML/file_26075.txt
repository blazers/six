<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Sowerby,  John Edward</span>  (1825&#150;1870), <span class="occ">artist and publisher</span>, was born in Lambeth on 17 January 1825, the eldest son of the botanist and publisher Charles Edward Sowerby (1795&#150;1842) and his wife, Judith Ainsley. A grandson of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/26073/', "James Sowerby (1757&#150;1822)")</script>, the natural history artist and publisher of <span class="italic">English Botany</span>, J. E. Sowerby inherited the family talent for botanical drawing.<br>
<br>Few details of J. E. Sowerby's early life and education are available, but it would appear that, like the children of each generation of that family, he was soon involved in the preparation of illustrations for its publications. The plates he produced at the age of sixteen for his father's <span class="italic">Illustrated Catalogue of British Plants</span> (1841) were his first signed work. On 10 February 1853 he married Elizabeth Dewhurst, the daughter of Roger Dewhurst of Preston, Lancashire.<br>
<br>J. E. Sowerby collaborated with the botanist Charles Johnson (1791&#150;1880) in 1855&#150;7, and his son Charles Pierpoint Johnson (<i>d</i>. 1893) from 1857 to 1862, to provide the illustrations for numerous British works for which they had written the text. These dealt with ferns (1855), fern allies (1856), poisonous plants (1856), grasses (1857&#150;61), wild flowers (1858&#150;60, 1863), and &#145;useful plants&#146; (1861&#150;2). He provided a supplement on &#145;Ferns, horsetails and club mosses&#146; for Johnson's <span class="italic">British Wild Flowers</span> (1863, 1894). Other illustrations were provided for the second (1850) and third (1863&#150;72) editions of <span class="italic">English Botany</span> published by J. T. I. Boswell Syme (1822&#150;1888). <span class="italic">An Illustrated Key to the Natural Orders of British Wild Flowers</span>, published by Van Voorst (1865), was his only independent work. He used miniature versions of drawings in <span class="italic">English Botany</span> as typical plants of the principal orders and borrowed descriptions from Babington's <span class="italic">Manual of British Botany</span>.<br>
<br>All J. E. Sowerby's drawings were on botanical subjects and through publishing economies had a similar multiple arrangement of figures. Among the works he was associated with were those on flowers by Mrs Lankester (1861, 1879) and J. E. Taylor (1878); on ferns&#151;T. W. Gissing (1862); rusts, smut, mildew, and mould&#151;M. C. Cooke (1865, 1878); and a flora of Essex by G. S. Stacey (1862). The extent of his botanical knowledge is not clear, but, surprisingly, not one of the authors acknowledged his contribution other than on the title-page.<br>
<br>Following a short illness, J. E. Sowerby died at his home, Stanley Villa, Poultney Road, Lavender Hill, Clapham, on 28 January 1870, aged forty-five. Shortly after his death an appeal was made through the <span class="italic">Gardeners' Chronicle</span> to provide funds for his family and to secure the admission of one of his six children to an infants' orphan asylum. His widow was later granted a civil-list pension in recognition of the scientific value of his work.<br>
<br>R. J. Cleevely </div>
