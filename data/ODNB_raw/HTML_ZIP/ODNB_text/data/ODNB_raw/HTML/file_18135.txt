<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Marshall,  Francis Albert</span>  (1840&#150;1889), <span class="occ">playwright</span>, was born at 41 Upper Grosvenor Street, London, on 18 November 1840, the fifth son of William Marshall (1796&#150;1872) of Patterdale Hall and Hallstead, Westmorland, and his wife, Georgina Christiana (<i>d</i>. 1866), seventh daughter of George Hibbert of Munden, Hertfordshire. His father was MP for Petersfield (1826&#150;30), for Carlisle (1835&#150;47), and for East Cumberland (1847&#150;65). Francis Marshall was educated at Harrow School, and matriculated from Exeter College, Oxford, on 14 June 1859, but did not take a degree. He was for some years a clerk in the Audit Office in Somerset House, but soon began contributing to newspapers and periodicals, and in 1868 resigned his appointment.<br>
<br>By this time, Marshall had already gained some reputation as a playwright, and afterwards became theatre critic for the <span class="italic">London Figaro</span>. His plays included farces, comic operas, comedies, and dramas, which were produced on the London stage between 1863 and 1887. His comedies <span class="italic">False Shame</span> (1872) and <span class="italic">Brighton</span> (1874) were among his best-known works. <span class="italic">Biohn</span>, a romantic opera in five acts, with music by Lauro Rossi (Queen's Theatre, 17 January 1877) featured his first wife, Imogene Fitzinman Marshall, as Elfrida, but was a failure. His first wife died on 19 February 1885. On 2 May 1885 he married <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/4926/', "Ada Cavendish (1839?&#150;1895)")</script>, a well-known actress.<br>
<br>With William Gorman Wills, Marshall produced <span class="italic">Cora</span>, a drama in three acts (Globe Theatre, 28 February 1877). For his friend Henry Irving he wrote two pieces: a drama in four acts, founded on the history of Robert Emmet, and a version of <span class="italic">Werner</span>, adapted for the stage. The latter was produced at the Lyceum Theatre on the occasion of the benefit given to Westland Marston by Henry Irving on 1 June 1887. Marshall's <span class="italic">Robert Emmet</span> was never produced.<br>
<br>During his last years Marshall edited, with assistance from many scholars, a new edition of the works of Shakespeare, called the <span class="roman">Henry Irving Edition</span>, to which Henry Irving contributed an introduction. Marshall died of cirrhosis of the liver and jaundice at his home, 8 Bloomsbury Square, London, on 28 December 1889.<br>
<br>G. C. Boase<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Megan A. Stephan </div>
