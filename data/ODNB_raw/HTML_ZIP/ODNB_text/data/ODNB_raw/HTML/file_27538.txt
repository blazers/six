<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Tonna,  Lewis Hippolytus Joseph</span>  (1812&#150;1857), <span class="occ">protestant controversialist</span>, was born on 3 September 1812 at Liverpool, where his father was vice-consul for Spain and consul for the kingdom of the Two Sicilies. His mother was the daughter of Major H. S. Blanckley, consul-general in the Balearic Islands. In 1828 he was at Corfu as a student when the death of his father left him to fend for himself. He found work as an interpreter, with the rating of &#145;acting schoolmaster&#146;, on board the <i>Hydra</i>, then in the Gulf of Patras. In January 1831 he was transferred to the <i>Rainbow</i> with Sir John Franklin, and in October 1833 to the <i>Britannia</i>, flagship of Vice-Admiral Sir Pulteney Malcolm. Tonna returned to England in 1835 and the following year obtained&#151;apparently through Malcolm's influence&#151;the post of assistant director and, from 1845 until his death, of secretary of the Royal United Service Institution. He acquired a mastery of many foreign languages, including French, Italian, and Greek, and was elected both fellow of the Society of Antiquaries and, in 1855, fellow of the Royal Geographical Society.<br>
<br>On 5 February 1841 Tonna married the widow of Captain George Phelan, Charlotte Elizabeth Browne (1790&#150;1846), the well-known writer of a wide range of popular protestant literature [<i>see </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/27537/', "Tonna, Charlotte Elizabeth")</script>]. Tonna shared her religious sentiments and he, too, was the author of numerous small books and pamphlets, almost all on religious and controversial subjects, written from an uncompromisingly ultra-protestant point of view. In 1848 he married Mary Anne, daughter of Charles Dibdin the younger, who survived him. There were no children of either marriage. He died in London on 2 April 1857.<br>
<br>J. K. Laughton<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Stephen Gregory </div>
