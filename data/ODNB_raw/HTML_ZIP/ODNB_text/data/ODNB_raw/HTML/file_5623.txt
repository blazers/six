<span class="headword">Maxwell, Sir  George  Clerk-</span> <span class="st"> [<em>formerly </em>George Clerk]</span>, fourth baronet  (1715&#150;1784), <span class="occ">agriculturist</span>, was born at Edinburgh in October 1715, the second son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/5617/', "Sir John Clerk of Penicuik, second baronet (1676&#150;1755)")</script>, and Janet, daughter of Sir John Inglis of Cramond. He was educated at the universities of Edinburgh and Leiden. From his father he received the lands of Drumcrieff in Annandale, and through his marriage to Dorothea Clerk-Maxwell, daughter of his uncle William and his wife, Agnes Maxwell, heiress of Middlebie, Dumfriesshire, he obtained the lands of Middlebie; he also adopted his wife's surname. They had five sons and four daughters.<br>
<br>Clerk-Maxwell was king's remembrancer in the exchequer from 1761, a commissioner of the customs in Scotland from 1763, and a trustee for improving fisheries and manufactures in Scotland. He worked hard to promote the agricultural and commercial interests of the country. At Dumfries he set up a linen factory and he initiated a variety of projects for the mining of lead and copper in the county. In 1755 he sent two letters concerning the usual method used to treat wool to the trustees for the improvement of the fisheries and manufactures of Scotland, and these were published by the board in 1756. He was also author of a paper on shallow ploughing, published by the Philosophical Society in Edinburgh (<span class="italic">Essays and Observations</span>, 3, 1774). A clever draughtsman, he etched a variety of views of Scotland. On the death of his elder brother in 1782, he succeeded to the baronetcy and estates of Penicuik. He died on 29 January 1784, and was succeeded in the baronetcy by his eldest son, John Clerk.<br>
<br>T. F. Henderson<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Anne Pimlott Baker </div>
