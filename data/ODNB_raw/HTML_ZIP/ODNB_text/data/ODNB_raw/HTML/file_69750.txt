<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Carter,  Samuel</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1641, <em>d.</em> 1713), <span class="occ">law reporter and legal writer</span>, was baptized at St Martin's, Birmingham, on 5 August 1641, the son of Thomas Carter and Margaret Ailesbury. He was admitted to Pembroke College, Cambridge, on 26 May 1657, graduating BA in 1660&#150;61. He was admitted to the Inner Temple in 1665 and called to the bar on 23 November 1673.<br>
<br>Carter's main activity appears to have been the research and publication of legal texts. In 1688 he published some law reports relating to the reign of Charles II. His next work appears to have been published in 1701: <span class="italic">Lex custumaria, or, A Treatise of Copy-Hold Estates</span>. In keeping with this kind of work, in 1706 he published <span class="italic">The Law of Executions</span>.<br>
<br>No doubt Carter's legal researches were facilitated by his appointment on 4 June 1709 as &#145;library keeper&#146; at the Inner Temple at a salary of &pound;20 p.a., his son, Thomas, providing the surety. In 1710 he published <span class="italic">Legal Provisions for the Poor, or, A Treatise of Common and Statute Laws Concerning the Poor</span>, which proved sufficiently popular to run to a fifth edition in 1725. Finally, he published <span class="italic">Lex vadiorum: the Law of Mortgages</span>, a second edition of which appeared in 1728.<br>
<br>Carter was buried on 8 March 1713 in the Temple Church. His will appears to have been proved by his sister Mary Lostin in October 1713.<br>
<br>Stuart Handley </div>
