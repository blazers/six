<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Booth,  Benjamin</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1746&#150;1789), <span class="occ">haberdasher and writer on bookkeeping</span>, originated in New England and was born before 1746, possibly the son of Benjamin and grandson of Ebenezer Booth (<i>d</i>. 1732), of Stratford, Massachusetts. He began work as a clerk in a New York store about 1759, eventually becoming chief clerk, when he first implemented his own bookkeeping system. He subsequently conducted his own haberdashery business for several years, until, apparently, the outbreak of the War of Independence brought it to a close.<br>
<br>Booth then moved to England, where he advertised that he had been in business in New York for thirty years. He endeavoured to publicize his system, which he considered superior to those in general use, by means of his book, <span class="italic">A complete system of bookkeeping &hellip; by an improved mode of double entry &hellip; [with] &hellip; a new method of stating factorage accounts, adapted particularly to the trade of the British colonies</span> published in London in 1789. The sample invoices shown in the book revealed both humour and literary interest, citing imaginary dealings with Lemuel Gulliver, Peter Pindar, and Tristram Shandy. John Ramsay McCulloch listed Booth's book in his <span class="italic">Literature of Political Economy</span> (1845), with the erroneous date of 1799  (McCulloch, 139).<br>
<br>No further details of Booth's life are known and he may have returned to America after the book was published. No record of his death has been found.<br>
<br>John Brunton </div>
