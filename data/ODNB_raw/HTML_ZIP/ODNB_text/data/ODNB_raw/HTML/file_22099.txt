<span class="headword">Thaun,  Philip  de</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1113<span class="mult">x</span>19&#150;1121<span class="mult">x</span>35), <span class="occ">Anglo-Norman poet</span>, was probably from the seigneurial family of Than, or Thaon, near Caen. He is the earliest known Anglo-Norman poet. It is possible that he moved from Normandy to England in the late eleventh century with his uncle Hunfrei de Thaun, chaplain to Yun, who was seneschal dapifer to Henry I, and is usually identified with Eudo or Odo Dapifer, who died on 29 February 1120. Hunfrei is named at the beginning of the <span class="italic">Cumpoz</span>, which Philip sent to him for comment and correction. The family de Thaun did not survive beyond the fifteenth century. P. Meyer disputed Philip's seigneurial background, and saw no reason to suppose that Philip was more than a simple cleric who, like Wace, travelled to England to make his services available to the court  (Meyer, 34.367).<br>
<br>Philip de Thaun's first work was the <span class="italic">Cumpoz</span>, or <span class="italic">Compuz</span> (&#145;Computus&#146;), called in Wright's edition <span class="italic">Liber de creaturis</span>. It was written between 1113 and 1119 as a clerics' aid to calculate the moveable feasts of the church calendar. Written in rhyming hexasyllabic couplets, and interspersed with edifying exhortations, its principal sources appear to have been Bede, William de Conches, and Chilperic of St Gall's <span class="italic">De compoto</span>. There are four manuscripts of the <span class="italic">Cumpoz</span> in England, and three in the Vatican Library.<br>
<br>Between 1121 and 1135 Philip de Thaun translated the first French version extant of the bestiary <span class="italic">Physiologus</span> (<span class="italic">Li bestiaire</span>) and dedicated it to his queen, Adeliza of Louvain, wife of Henry II. <span class="italic">Li bestiaire</span>, written in rhyming hexasyllabic couplets except for the final 303 octosyllabic lines (ll.2891&#150;3194), comprises a prologue, 35 chapters on animals, 3 on precious stones, and an epilogue. There are three extant manuscripts of <span class="italic">Li bestiaire</span>. Although Philip's manuscript source is unknown, he appears to have made few alterations to the general substance of the Latin bestiary tradition, with its descriptions of animal properties and its typological interpretation of the animal <i>exempla</i>. Presumably the ordering of the <i>exempla</i> and the addition of material from Isidore of Seville were done by his source. Philip was, however, responsible for the translation into French and for its (somewhat pedestrian) versification. While not a literary masterpiece, this very early French translation of the <span class="italic">Physiologus</span>, composed for the edification and enjoyment of the court, has become, by the primacy of its date of composition, an important source of information for the language of Anglo-Norman England.<br>
<br>Also attributed to Philip de Thaun, although its sole manuscript  (Paris, Biblioth&egrave;que Nationale, fonds fr. 25407) contains no dedication identifying him or a patron, is a hexasyllabic translation-adaptation of the <span class="italic">Sibylla Tiburtina</span>, supplemented with material from the <span class="italic">Libellus de Antichristo</span> by Adso of Montier-en-Der, and entitled <span class="italic">Le livre de Sibile</span> (<i>c</i>.1239). The attribution to Philip of an alphabetical lapidary, an apocalyptic lapidary, and the <span class="italic">D&eacute;bat de l'&acirc;me et du corps</span> is less certain.<br>
<br>Jeanette Beer </div>
