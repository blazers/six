<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ballantyne,  Thomas</span>  (1806&#150;1871), <span class="occ">newspaper editor</span>, was born in Paisley, where his first employment was as a weaver. He then became editor of the <span class="italic">Bolton Free Press</span>, and was active in promoting radical causes. He became closely associated with Richard Cobden and John Bright in their agitation against the corn laws, and in 1841 he published the <span class="italic">Corn Law Repealer's Handbook</span>. In 1841 he joined the staff of the <span class="italic">Manchester Guardian</span>, but his path to promotion was blocked by Russell Scott Taylor. Accordingly, in 1845 he joined Bright and two others to found the <span class="italic">Manchester Examiner</span> as a more radical paper than the <span class="italic">Guardian</span>; he edited it for nearly three years. After the fusion of the <span class="italic">Examiner</span> with the <span class="italic">Manchester Times</span> in 1848, he became editor of the <span class="italic">Liverpool Journal</span>, and later of the <span class="italic">Liverpool Mercury</span>. Subsequently he moved to London to edit <span class="italic">The Leader</span>, and was for a time associated with Charles Mackay in the editorial department of the <span class="italic">Illustrated London News</span>. He also started <span class="italic">The Statesman</span>, which he edited until its close, when he became editor of the <span class="italic">St James's Chronicle</span>.<br>
<br>In addition to this rather unsettled editorial career, Ballantyne wrote papers for various periodicals and published several books, including <span class="italic">Passages Selected from the Writings of Thomas Carlyle, with a Biographical Memoir</span> (1855), <span class="italic">Prophecy for 1855, Selected from Carlyle's Latter-Day Pamphlets</span> (1855), <span class="italic">Ideas, Opinions, and Facts</span> (1865), and <span class="italic">Essays in Mosaic</span> (1870). Carlyle recognized Ballantyne's skill as a compiler; he had, he wrote, &#145;a real talent for excerpting significant passages from books, magazines, newspapers (that contain <i>any</i> such), and for presenting them in lucid arrangement, and in their most interesting and readable form&#146;. Ballantyne died at Tufnell Park, Holloway, London, on 30 August 1871.<br>
<br>T. F. Henderson<span class="italic">, rev. </span> H. C. G. Matthew </div>
