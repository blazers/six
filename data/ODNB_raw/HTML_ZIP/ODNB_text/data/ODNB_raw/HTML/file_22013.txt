<span class="headword">Hibernia,  Peter  de</span> <span class="st"> [Peter Hibernicus]</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1239&#150;1266), <span class="occ">natural philosopher</span>, was probably of Anglo-Irish origins, a member of a family that had settled in the Norman kingdom of Sicily. Little is recorded of the events of his life, and that little has in the past been obfuscated by scribal and exegetical errors confusing him with his contemporary and near namesake, the jurist Peter de Isernia. There can be no doubt that it was the latter whom Frederick II, probably in 1224, invited to become professor of civil law at the University of Naples. It has been plausibly argued that Peter de Hibernia trained in the natural sciences at Oxford, and in logic at Paris, and it is possible that he joined the Franciscans at their Bologna convent, but nothing certain is known of his career until the period 1239&#150;45, when, as a master at the University of Naples, he taught logic and natural philosophy to the young Thomas Aquinas. Aquinas later referred to an opinion of his former master, when commenting on the <span class="italic">Sentences</span> at Paris. Peter came to enjoy a high reputation at Naples. The proem to a <i>determinatio</i> which he delivered before King Manfred, some time between 1258 and 1266, describes him as &#145;the jewel of masters&#146;, while to Rabbi Moses ben Solomon of Salerno, who a few years earlier discussed with him proposals for a Latin translation of the <span class="italic">Guide for the Perplexed</span> of Maimonides, Peter was &#145;a wise Christian&#146;.<br>
<br>Three of Peter de Hibernia's philosophical works are known to survive, his <i>determinatio</i> before Manfred, and commentaries on the <span class="italic">Perihermeneias</span> and <span class="italic">De longitudine</span> of Aristotle. Three other attributions have been rejected. Heavily influenced by Averroes, though not to the extent of accepting the latter's heterodox opinions, Peter's commentaries show him to have been a man of wide reading, especially in Arabic and Jewish sources, and some independence of mind. A striking characteristic of his philosophical work is its lack of reference to Christian doctrine&#151;just a single allusion to Jesus' bloody sweat, as proof of his humanity. A few eccentricities like a belief that barnacle geese are hatched from trees notwithstanding, Peter was a natural philosopher of impressive learning and depth, and a master of the form of commentary that combined exposition (<i>lectio</i>) with discussion of disputed or difficult points (<i>quaestiones</i>). His analysis of the <span class="italic">Perihermeneias</span> probably influenced that of his famous pupil. Thomas Tanner records that Peter de Hibernia, whom he describes as the author of theological quodlibets (not known to survive), apparently died at Naples. This seems likely enough, but neither the place nor the date of Peter's death is now capable of demonstration.<br>
<br>Henry Summerson </div>
