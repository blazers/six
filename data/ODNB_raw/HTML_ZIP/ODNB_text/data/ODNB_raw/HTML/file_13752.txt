<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hopkins,  Richard</span>  (<em>b. </em><em>c.</em>1546, <em>d.</em> in or before 1596), <span class="occ">Roman Catholic exile and translator</span>, son and heir of Richard Hopkins, was admitted to the Middle Temple on 9 October 1560. He was a commoner of Magdalen College, Oxford (not St Alban Hall, as sometimes stated) by 1564. About 1566 Hopkins became &#145;wearied with the heresy&#146; of Middle Temple, according to Anthony Wood  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span>, 1.567), and he went into exile at Louvain, where he was befriended by Thomas Harding. After a period of study in Spain he returned to Louvain by 1579, when he lodged with Anne, Lady Hungerford, the sister of Jane Dormer, duchess of Feria.<br>
<br>Following the advice of Thomas Harding, who had earlier urged Hopkins to undertake the translation of Spanish devotional works as being spiritually more profitable than controversy, he published at Paris in 1582 a translation of part one of the <span class="italic">Libro de la oraci&oacute;n y meditaci&oacute;n</span> by the Spanish Dominican Luis de Granada. This was dedicated to the benchers of the inns of court, as was his translation of part one of the same author's <span class="italic">Memorial de la vida Christiana</span>, published at Rouen in 1586. The two works, based on a scholarly recension of the Spanish text, illustrated and designed for the use of the laity, enjoyed wide popularity. The first work, <span class="italic">Of Prayer and Meditation</span>, went into eleven protestant editions between 1592 and 1634, and at least three protestant editions of the <span class="italic">Memoriall of a Christian Life</span> had been published by the end of Elizabeth's reign. The florid devotional style is thought to have influenced the language of Crashaw and Vaughan. Hopkins's translation of part two of the <span class="italic">Memorial</span> was published after his death in John Heigham's <span class="italic">Six Spirituall Bookes</span> (1611).<br>
<br>From 1579 Hopkins was a close associate of William Allen, and he was a leading member of the English Catholic &eacute;migr&eacute; community in Flanders. As Allen's agent Hopkins was possibly involved in clandestine negotiations for peace between England and Spain about 1594. In a report drawn up for the Archduke Ernest, governor of the Low Countries, in 1594, Hopkins was described as &#145;a man of great loyalty and zeal in the service of God and the King&#146;  (Loomie, &#145;Creswell's <i>Informaci&oacute;n</i>&#146;, 472, 476). In 1596, having died in Louvain, he was listed as a deceased pensionary of the Spanish crown.<br>
<br>G. Martin Murphy </div>
