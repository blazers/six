<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Leigh,  (Elizabeth) Medora</span>  (1814&#150;1849), <span class="occ">alleged daughter of Lord Byron</span>, was born on 15 April 1814 at Six Mile Bottom, near Cambridge, acknowledged as the fourth of the seven children of Colonel George Leigh (1770/71&#150;1850) and his wife, the Hon. Augusta Mary, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Byron (1784&#150;1851). She was named Medora after the heroine of the romance <span class="italic">The Corsair</span>, begun by <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/4279/', "George Gordon, Lord Byron (1788&#150;1824)")</script>, in December 1813 at the height of his relationship with Augusta Leigh, his half-sister. The allegation that Medora was the child of their incestuous union is not proven, but an &#145;incestuous&#146; motif marked Medora's life. In 1829 she became pregnant by a cousin, Henry Trevanion (1805?&#150;1855?), the husband of her eldest sister, Georgiana, and the couple eloped to France in 1831 using the alias Aubin. There was one surviving child of the liaison, Marie (1834&#150;1873), later Sister Saint Hilaire. Medora's relationship with Trevanion had broken down by 1838. She was now dependent on sporadic and inadequate financial assistance from her hard-pressed mother. In 1840 she became the resistant object of Lady Byron's controlling financial magnanimity, and the recipient of Lady Byron's account of her incestuous conception. Her memoir of her life, down to 1843, was written at this time to justify her position (it was published by Charles Mackay from the MS in 1869). In 1844 she recovered some freedom of action by a loan of &pound;500 on a deed of appointment for &pound;3000 (on the reversionary interest of Byron's marriage settlement). On 23 August 1848 she married Jean-Louis Taillefer (1810?&#150;1877), a retired French soldier of the hussars with whom she had one son, Elie (1846&#150;1900). She died of smallpox on 28 August 1849 at Lapeyre in Aveyron, southern France, where she is buried.<br>
<br>Malcolm Kelsall </div>
