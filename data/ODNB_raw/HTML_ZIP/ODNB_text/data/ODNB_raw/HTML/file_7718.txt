<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Dobson <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Dawson]</span>,  Susannah</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1795), <span class="occ">translator</span>, was the daughter of John Dawson of the parish of St Dunstan, London. In 1759 she married <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/55275/', "Matthew Dobson (1732&#150;1784)")</script>, a physician and natural philosopher. They had three children: Susannah (<i>b</i>. 1764), Dawson (<i>b</i>. 1766), and Elisa (1760/61&#150;1778).<br>
<br>Susannah Dobson's first published work, in 1775, was an abridged translation in two volumes of <span class="italic">M&eacute;moires pour la vie de Fran&ccedil;ois P&eacute;trarque</span>, by Jacques Fran&ccedil;ois Paul Aldonce, abb&eacute; de Sade. According to Samuel Johnson, &#145;Mrs Dobson, the Directress of rational conversation, did not translate Petrarch; but epitomised a very bulky French life of Petrarch&#146;  (<span class="italic">Letters of Samuel Johnson</span>, 249). De Sade's work was extremely controversial: he claimed to prove the true identity of Petrarch's Laura and identified her with the Laura de Noves, wife of Hugues de Sade, on the basis of a sonnet found in her tomb in the De la Croix Chapel in Avignon. His work attracted the attention of Horace Walpole and Alexander Fraser Tytler, Lord Woodhouselee. The latter rejected de Sade's theory in his <span class="italic">Essay on the Life and Character of Petrarch</span>.<br>
<br>Susannah Dobson might have become interested in the work through Baretti's praise of it in his <span class="italic">Manners and Customs of Italy</span> (1768). Her reviewer in the <span class="italic">Gentleman's Magazine</span> praised Dobson's translation for maintaining &#145;that pathos and spirit of the original&#146; and for a preface by Dobson intended, now that Laura was revealed to have been a married woman, &#145;to guard against the impression which such an attachment might otherwise make on susceptible hearts&#146;  (<span class="italic">GM</span>, 45, 1775, 186). Dobson's translation enjoyed long-lasting popularity, and by 1805 there had been one Irish and six English editions, many of which were illustrated with appropriate engravings. In 1780 she claimed to have earned &pound;400 from it  (<span class="italic">Diary and Letters</span>, 1.369). According to Roderick Marshall, &#145;this book restored Petrarch, after a hundred and fifty years of banishment, to an active part in English literature&#146;  (Marshall, 124).<br>
<br>In 1777 Susannah Dobson published a <span class="italic">Dialogue on Friendship and Society</span>. She then translated two works by the French Academician Jean-Baptiste de la Curne de Sainte-Palaye (1697&#150;1781): <span class="italic">Literary History of the Troubadours</span> (1779) and <span class="italic">Memoirs of Ancient Chivalry</span> (1784). In 1791 she published a translation of Petrarch's <span class="italic">De remediis utriusque fortunae</span> as <span class="italic">Petrarch's View of Human Life</span>. In the last year of her life she published <span class="italic">Historical Anecdotes of Heraldry and Chivalry</span> (1795).<br>
<br>In 1780 Susannah Dobson followed her husband to Bath, where he had retired on the grounds of ill health. The novelist Fanny Burney mentions her in her diary for the same year. According to Burney, Susannah Dobson had &#145;long been trying to make acquaintance with Mrs Thrale, but Mrs Thrale, not liking her advances, has always shrunk from them&#146;  (<span class="italic">Diary and Letters</span>, 1.360). Eventually Susannah Dobson was introduced to Mrs Thrale, but does not seem to have succeeded in winning her friendship. While Fanny Burney admitted that, &#145;though coarse, low-bred, forward, self-sufficient, and flaunting, she seems to have a strong masculine understanding&#146;  (ibid., 1.370), Mrs Thrale wrote that &#145;Mrs Dobson &hellip; persecutes me strangely as if with violent &amp; undesired Friendship; yet Mrs Lewis says She is jealous&#146;  (<span class="italic">Thraliana</span>, 1.595). By this time Susannah's husband had become Mrs Thrale's practitioner. Susannah Dobson died in London on 30 September 1795 and was buried at St Paul's, Covent Garden.<br>
<br>Antonella Braida </div>
