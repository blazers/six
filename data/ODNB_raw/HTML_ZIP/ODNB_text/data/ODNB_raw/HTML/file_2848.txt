<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Bonneau,  Jacob</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1786), <span class="occ">painter</span>, moved to England about 1741, with his father, an engraver, who worked in London for booksellers during the mid-eighteenth century. Probably trained by his father, Bonneau also worked for booksellers, for whom he engraved the heads prefixed to the <span class="italic">History of the American Buccaneers</span> published in London in 1741. Whitley noted that on the retirement of Alexander Cozens in 1754 the<blockquote>artists of London petitioned the Governors of Christ's Hospital to give the post [of drawing master] to Jacob Bonneau, afterwards one of the drawing masters to the Royal Family, who, although his reputation as a painter was small, received some support when a candidate for the Associateship of the Royal Academy twenty years later. Bonneau must have been extremely popular among his brother artists, for the petition, drawn up by Joseph Highmore, is signed by nearly all the important painters, sculptors and engravers of the time including Hogarth, Reynolds&#151;then a young man not long returned from Italy&#151;and Roubiliac. (Whitley, 316&#150;17)</blockquote>Bonneau was described in the petition, which is cited by Whitley in full, as &#145;being a person in every way qualified for that Trust&#146;  (ibid., 317). In the ballot he failed to obtain the post, losing by 15 votes to 40 to an artist named Bisse. From 1765 to 1778 Bonneau exhibited landscapes at the Society of British Artists, of which he was a member. From 1770 to 1781 he exhibited seven landscape paintings and the watercolour drawing <span class="italic">St John</span> at the Royal Academy. Bonneau worked chiefly in watercolour, and his principal occupation was that of a teacher of drawing and perspective. He died at Kentish Town, London, on 18 March 1786.<br>
<br>Walter Hepworth<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Asia Haut </div>
