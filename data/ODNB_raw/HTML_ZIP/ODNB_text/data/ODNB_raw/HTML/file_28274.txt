<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Vidal,  Robert Studley</span>  (1770&#150;1841), <span class="occ">antiquary</span>, was the son of Robert Studley Vidal (<i>d</i>. 1796) of Exeter, formerly a solicitor in London. He was called to the bar at the Middle Temple on 22 August 1795. He had antiquarian tastes, and communicated two papers on trial by ordeal and on the site of Kenwith Castle, Devon, to the Society of Antiquaries through his friend Henry Wansey: they were published in <span class="italic">Archaeologia</span>, volume 15. His chief work was the translation of J. L. von Mosheim, <span class="italic">Commentaries on the Affairs of the Christians before the Time of Constantine</span>. The first two volumes were published in 1813, but the third did not appear until 1835. In the intervening years Vidal prepared the third edition of Charles Watkins's <span class="italic">A Treatise on Copyholds</span>, published in 1821, and the fifth edition of Sir Geoffrey Gilbert's <span class="italic">The Law of Tenure</span>, published in 1824. His projected edition of Ralph Cudworth's <span class="italic">The Intellectual System of the Universe</span> (1678) was not published. Vidal formed a valuable collection of coins and medals, which was sold by Leigh and Sotheby on 18 June 1842 after his death. He kept a pack of harriers at Cornborough, near Bideford, Devon. He died there at his home, Cornborough House, on 21 November 1841. By his will he founded two scholarships of &pound;20 a year each at St John's College, Cambridge, which were charged upon his manor of Abbotsham.<br>
<br>Campbell Dodgson<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Joanne Potier </div>
