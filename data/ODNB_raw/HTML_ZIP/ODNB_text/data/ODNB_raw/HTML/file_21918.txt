<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Perceval <span class="st"> [Percival]</span>,  Jean</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1561), <span class="occ">Carthusian theologian</span>, was a native of Paris who became a Carthusian monk there in 1522. In 1530 he published a theological work, <span class="italic">Compendium divini amoris</span>, also at Paris. According to John Bale, followed by later British biobibliographers, Perceval studied at English universities before making his profession; Anthony Wood inevitably claimed that he did so specifically at Oxford. No evidence survives to support these assertions, which probably stem from confusion with an older near-contemporary, the English Franciscan John Percival, who studied at both Oxford and Cambridge. Perceval's <span class="italic">Compendium</span> is based upon much theological learning, but he would not have needed to attend an English university in order to acquire this. His attacks on the heresies of the Beguines and Beghards, members of a movement of lay spirituality unrecorded in England, his admiring references to Jean Gerson, formerly chancellor of the University of Paris, and his anecdote of how a few years earlier he had himself been told by a woman of Paris of her son's defying his parents in order to become a Carthusian&#151;all point to a continental and Parisian background. Made prior of the Dijon Charterhouse in 1534, Perceval was appointed a co-visitor of the French Carthusian province in 1546 and sole visitor two years later. He died on 12 September 1561.<br>
<br>Henry Summerson </div>
