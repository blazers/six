<div class="para">
<span class="headword">King,  Robert</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1726?), <span class="occ">composer and concert promoter</span>, was admitted to the royal private musick as a violinist on 6 February 1680. In December 1689 he was granted a royal licence to promote concerts and apparently did so with the composer Johann Franck, first at the Two Golden Balls in Bow Street, London, and from January 1691 at Charles Street, Covent Garden, in a purpose-built room soon to be used also for auctions of paintings and called The Vendu. In September 1691 it was &#145;inlarging to a far greater Dimension for the Convenience of Mr. FRANK's and Mr. KING's Musick&#146;  (<span class="italic">LondG</span>, 14&#150;17 Sept 1691). King graduated MusB at Cambridge in 1696. James Brydges, later duke of Chandos, wrote in his diary of visits by King in 1697 &#145;to play with me on the flute [recorder]&#146;  (Baker and Baker, 37). By January 1698 King was organizing concerts with John Banister at York Buildings, Villiers Street, and in June 1699 the music publisher John Walsh advertised &#145;a choice Collection of new Musick, made for Mr. <i>Banister's</i> and Mr. <i>King's</i> Consort, performed by Gentlemen at Exeter Exchange&#146;  (<span class="italic">Flying Post</span>, 8&#150;10 June 1699). In July 1700 King and Banister were selling &#145;The New Sonata's of the famous Signior Archangelo Corelli&#146;  (<span class="italic">LondG</span>, 8&#150;11 July 1700) and they advertised sonatas by Nicola Cosimi in November 1702.<br>
<br>King composed songs and instrumental music for the theatre, concerts, and amateur performers. His songs appeared in numerous collections, including <span class="italic">Choice Ayres and Songs</span> (1684), <span class="italic">The Theater of Music</span> (1685&#150;7), <span class="italic">Comes Amoris</span> (1687&#150;93), <span class="italic">The Banquet of Music</span> (1688&#150;90), and <span class="italic">Thesaurus musicus</span> (1693&#150;5), in issues of the <span class="italic">Gentleman's Journal</span> (1692&#150;4), and in <span class="italic">Mercurius Musicus</span> (1699). Many of these songs were republished in editions of <span class="italic">Wit and Mirth</span> between 1700 and 1720. The manuscripts of some of his songs and incidental music from the 1690s are in the British Library. About 1690 King, displeased by incorrect and badly printed versions of his songs &#145;in the Common Printed Books about Town&#146;, published his <span class="italic">Songs for One Two and Three Voices</span>, writing in his preface that he had &#145;imitated the Italians in their manner of Ariettas; who for there Excellence in Vocal Musick are (in my Judgment) the best Paterns&#146;  (Smith, 6). He published another thirty-three pieces in <span class="italic">A Second Booke of Songs Together with a Pastorall Elegy, on &hellip; Queen Mary</span> (1698?). Instrumental music by him appeared in <span class="italic">Tripla concordia</span> (1677), <span class="italic">Thesaurus musicus</span>, <span class="italic">The Theater of Music</span>, and other publications up to 1705. He was replaced in the private musick on 6 November 1726, but details of his death are not known. Manuscripts of his songs and other compositions are in the British Library; the Bodleian Library, Oxford; Christ Church, Oxford; the Royal College of Music, London; and the University of Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania.<br>
<br>Olive Baldwin and Thelma Wilson </div>
