<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hodgson,  Joseph</span>  (1756&#150;1821), <span class="occ">Roman Catholic priest</span>, son of George Hodgson and his wife, Mary Hurd, of London, was born on 14 August 1756. He was educated at Sedgley Park School, Staffordshire, from 1766 to 1769 and at the English College of Douai, which he entered on 18 December 1769. He was retained in the college as professor, first of philosophy, and then of divinity. He was appointed vice-president to John Daniel in 1792. French revolutionary forces seized the college in February 1793 and he was imprisoned, later in the year, with the rest of the professors and students, first at Arras and afterwards at Doullens.<br>
<br>On their liberation in 1795 Hodgson went to London, and was appointed one of the priests at St George-in-the-Fields, Southwark. In 1803 he was appointed vicar-general to Bishop Douglass, and moved to the bishop's residence at 4 Castle Street, Holborn. He was later vicar-general to Bishop Poynter and secretary to the chapter. He also had the spiritual care of the ladies' school at Brook Green, Hammersmith, where he died on 30 November 1821. He was buried in Hammersmith parish churchyard.<br>
<br>Hodgson's account of the last days of Douai, <span class="italic">Narrative of the seizure of Douay College, and of the deportation of the seniors, professors, and students to Doullens</span>, was printed in volumes one and two of the <span class="italic">Catholic Magazine and Review</span> (1831&#150;2); a later edited version was printed in the <span class="italic">Ushaw Magazine</span> (1929&#150;32). The original manuscript is in the archives of the archbishop of Westminster.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> G. Bradley </div>
