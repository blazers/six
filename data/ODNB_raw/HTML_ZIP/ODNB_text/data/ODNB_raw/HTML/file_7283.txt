<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Davis,  James</span>  (1706/7&#150;1755), <span class="occ">satirical writer and physician</span>, was the son of Walter Davis of Chepstow, Monmouthshire. He matriculated at Jesus College, Oxford, on 18 February 1723, aged sixteen, and graduated BA on 13 October 1726 and MA on 9 July 1729. He then decided to study medicine, and graduated MB on 7 December 1732. He practised as a physician in Devizes, Wiltshire, until his death.<br>
<br>Davis is remembered for the publication of <span class="italic">Origines Divisianae, or, The antiquities of the Devizes in some familiar letters to a friend wrote in the years 1750 and 1751</span> (1754). It is described as a &#145;very humourous collection&#146; by Nichols who none the less perpetuates &#145;upon authority which I cannot resist&#146; a wrong ascription of the work to Dr Sneyd Davis  (Nichols, <span class="italic">Illustrations</span>, 1.682). This work is a vigorous and well-sustained caricature of the antiquarian and etymological studies of Musgrave, Stukeley, and others. It has a playful sense of the absurd:<blockquote>An old woman, who shew'd Lord Bathurst's fine place by Cirencester, was ask'd by a Gentleman that came to see it&#151;Pray what building is that?&#151;Oh Sir, that is a ruin a thousand years old, which my Lord built last year; and he proposes to build one this year half as old again. (Davis, 9)</blockquote>Similarly, the letters end with:<blockquote>The foregoing papers were wrote by no man living,&#151;for the Author dy'd some months ago. He long entertain'd a disrelish for the modern sort of scholarship and was not unwilling for these papers to go to the press to prevent many larger from going there. (ibid., 89&#150;90)</blockquote>The erudite solemnity of Davis's jokes deceived later writers who took his fancies as facts of local history.<br>
<br>Davis died in Devizes on 13 July 1755, but was buried in the parish church at Chepstow on 17 July.<br>
<br>William R. Jones </div>
