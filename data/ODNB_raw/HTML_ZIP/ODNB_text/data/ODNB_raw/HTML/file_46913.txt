<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Munda,  Constantia</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1617), <span class="occ">author</span>, wrote <span class="italic">The worming of a mad dogge, or, A soppe for Cerberus the jaylor of hell: no confutation but a sharpe redargution of the bayter of women</span> (1617), the third and last response to Joseph Swetnam's <span class="italic">The araignment of lewde, idle, froward, and unconstant women, or, The vanitie of them choose you whether</span> (1615).<br>
<br>It is impossible to determine the gender of the writer behind <span class="italic">The Worming</span>. In <span class="italic">Mortalities Memorandum</span> (1621) Rachel Speght refers to Constantia as a woman, but the knowledge of Greek, Latin, and Italian texts and of legal language demonstrated in <span class="italic">The Worming</span> makes this doubtful. The ranting use of language (&#145;your turbulent mind is defecated&#146;; Munda, sig. C1<i>r</i>) also makes one question Munda's alleged female gender. Constantia does, however, dedicate <span class="italic">The Worming</span> to &#145;The Right Worshipful Lady her most deare Mother, the Lady Prudentia Munda&#146; and offers &#145;her writing hand&#146; that penned this pamphlet &#145;in recompense&#146; for the &#145;pangs of sorrow you sustain'd / In child-birth&#146;  (ibid., sig. A3<i>r</i>). Constantia establishes her persona as the daughter of a lady; if her social status is aristocratic, this could account for her learned references.<br>
<br>Although Constantia includes some of the traditional arguments for the worth of women, such as that women are &#145;the meanes / Of all mens being&#146;, <span class="italic">The Worming</span> is more of a satirical attack on Swetnam and on his desire &#145;to please / The giddy-headed vulgar&#146; than a defence of women. Constantia attacks the popular press, which has &#145;become the receptacle of every dissolute Pamphlet&#146;  (Munda, sig. B2<i>r</i>) and the public theatre, where &#145;every fantasticke Poetaster &hellip; will strive to represent unseemely figments imputed to our sex&#146;  (ibid., sig. B3<i>r</i>). Calling Swetnam an &#145;ill-favoured hunks&#146;  (ibid., sig. E1<i>v</i>), Constantia accuses him of plagiarism, repeatedly corrects his inaccurate allusions, and charges him with &#145;Nero-like &hellip; ripping up the bowels of thine own mother&#146; when he says that all women are evil  (ibid., sig. D2<i>r</i>). She speculates that Swetnam knows so much about brothels because he has spent so much time in them  (ibid., sig. C3<i>r</i>).<br>
<br>
<span class="italic">The Worming</span> was printed for Laurence Hayes by G. Purslowe, the same printer who produced the second edition of Swetnam's <span class="italic">Araignment</span>; it appears to be a rushed job to take advantage of the profits accruing to Swetnam's bookseller. Hayes entered <span class="italic">The Worming</span> in the Stationers' register on 29 April 1617, without indicating an author, which has led to speculation as to whether the author of the pamphlet was in his employ.<br>
<br>Susan Gushee O'Malley </div>
