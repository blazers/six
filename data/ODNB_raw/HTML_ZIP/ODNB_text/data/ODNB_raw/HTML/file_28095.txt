<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lemens,  Balthasar  van</span>  (1637&#150;1704), <span class="occ">painter</span>, was born in Antwerp. His &#145;early genius [having] soon distinguishd it self&#146;, he went to England not long after the restoration of Charles II in 1660. Vertue records that &#145;Mr. Le Mense learnt under Decretz&#146;  (Vertue, <span class="italic">Note books</span>, 1.127); presumably this was Emanuel de Critz. Vertue also noted that van Lemens was &#145;expecting to meet with suitable encouragement&#146;  (ibid., 2.136). This does not appear to have been forthcoming, however. Early commentators hint at misfortunes &#145;in the latter part of his life, wherein he was often in trouble&#146;  (Buckeridge, 405), which made it necessary for him to undertake work for other people, drawing and making sketches to assist both painters and engravers; chief among these was Paul van Somer, the mezzotint engraver. Bainbrigg Buckeridge, writing <i>c</i>.1706, thought van Lemens's drawings and sketches &#145;excellent, and by some thought much better than many of his finished pieces&#146;  (ibid.), while Vertue judged &#145;his Invention&#146; as &#145;very fruitfull being as serviceable in his drawings for others as to himself&#146; and his sketches &#145;free &amp; masterly&#146; and &#145;of a good manner of Design&#146;  (Vertue, <span class="italic">Note books</span>, 2.136&#150;7).<br>
<br>Van Lemens had some success in painting small history pieces, allegories, and landscapes. Buckeridge describes these as &#145;very pleasing and well coloured. His manner was free, and often very graceful&#146;  (Buckeridge, 404). His works, known through mezzotint engravings by John Smith, include <span class="italic">St George</span>, several cupids, and <span class="italic">Venus and Adonis</span>; his <span class="italic">Acteon Changed into a Stag by Diana</span> was engraved by Nicolas de Larmessin the younger. For his patron, the amateur architect William Emmett, he painted portraits of Emmett and his wife, a ceiling painting of the nine muses (after 1698; destroyed) for their house in Bromley, Kent, and religious subjects. According to Vertue, van Lemens had a brother who practised in Brussels and who painted his portrait. He died in London in 1704, of &#145;a Diabetis in the 66 year of his Age &hellip; &amp; was buried in the Chappell Church yard Westminster&#146;  (Vertue, <span class="italic">Note books</span>, 2.137).<br>
<br>L. H. Cust<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Arianne Burnette </div>
