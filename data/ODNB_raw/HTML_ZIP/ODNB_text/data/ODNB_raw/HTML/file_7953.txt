<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Dover,  John</span>  (1644&#150;1725), <span class="occ">lawyer and playwright</span>, was born in October 1644 at Shirley Farm, Barton on the Heath, Warwickshire, and baptized on 28 October, the second son of Captain John Dover (<i>bap</i>. 1614, <i>d</i>. 1696) and his wife, Elizabeth Vade (<i>d</i>. 1700). The physician and privateer <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/7955/', "Thomas Dover (bap. 1662, d. 1742)")</script> was a younger brother. In 1661 he was admitted as a foundation scholar of Magdalen College, Oxford, matriculating on 12 July, and left the university in 1665 without taking a degree. He entered Gray's Inn on 19 May 1664 and was called to the bar on 21 June 1672.<br>
<br>In 1667 Dover's play, <span class="italic">The Roman Generalls, or, The Distressed Ladies</span>, an unacted tragedy in heroic verse, was published. In his dedication to his patron, Robert, Lord Brook, he declared that he had written the play to lessen the severity of his legal studies, &#145;for after I had read a sect or two in Littleton, I then to divert my self took Caesar's Commentaries, or read the Lives of my Roman Generalls out of Plutarch&#146;  (dedication). Dover practised law at Banbury, Oxfordshire. Wood stated that he wrote &#145;one or two more plays&#146;  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span>, 4.597), one of which may have been the comedy <span class="italic">The Mall, or, The Modish Lovers</span> (1674). In 1680 <span class="italic">The White Rose, or, A Word for the House of York, Vindicating the Right of Succession: in a Letter from Scotland, 9 March 1679</span> was published. On 30 August 1682, at the herald's visitation of Warwickshire, Dover made an unsuccessful attempt to claim that his grandfather <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/7954/', "Robert Dover (1581/2&#150;1652)")</script> had been granted supporters and augmentations to a Dover coat of arms by James I because of his Cotswold Olimpick Games. He provided Wood with information about Robert Dover for his <span class="italic">Athenae Oxonienses</span> (1691)  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span>, 4.222).<br>
<br>About 1684 Dover took holy orders and from 1688 was rector of Drayton near Banbury, where, to quote Wood, he was &#145;resorted to by fanatical people&#146;  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span>, 4.597), probably Anabaptists. He died unmarried at his Drayton rectory on 3 November 1725, aged eighty-one, and was buried on 6 November in the chancel of the church where there is an inscription to him.<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> F. D. A. Burns </div>
