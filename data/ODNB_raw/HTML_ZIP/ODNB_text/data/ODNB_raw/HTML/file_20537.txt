<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ua Diummusaig,  Diarmait</span> <span class="st"> [Dermot O'Dempsey]</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1193), <span class="occ">king of U&iacute; Failge</span>, was son of C&uacute; Broga Ua Diummusaig of Clann M&aacute;el Ugra, slain in 1162 at Killeigh, Offaly, by M&aacute;el Sechlainn mac Congailig Ua Conchobair, king of U&iacute; Failge. The surname Ua Diummusaig derived from Diummasach, an eleventh-century dynast of Clann M&aacute;el Ugra which, in turn, derived its name from the mid-ninth-century M&aacute;el Ugrai. The Clann M&aacute;el Ugra claimed descent from Rus Failge, the eponymous ancestor of the U&iacute; Failge, and were therefore considered to be a branch of the U&iacute; Failge, the overkingship of which generally was monopolized by the U&iacute; Conchobair Failge. The lands of the Clann M&aacute;el Ugra were situated along either side of the River Barrow in Laois and Offaly. Diarmait probably succeeded to the kingship of Clann M&aacute;el Ugra in 1162 and subsequently gained control also of the overkingship of U&iacute; Failge, possibly because of dynastic rivalry among the U&iacute; Conchobair Failge following the death of M&aacute;el Sechlainn Ua Conchobair at the hands of the Clann M&aacute;el Ugra in 1164.<br>
<br>In 1173 Diarmait Ua Diummusaig, who occurs as king of U&iacute; Failge in the so-called <span class="italic">Song of Dermot and the Earl</span>, mounted an attack on an Anglo-Norman raiding party in U&iacute; Failge led by Richard fitz Gilbert, earl of Pembroke and lord of Striguil, known as Strongbow. As the Anglo-Normans passed through a narrow defile, their rearguard was attacked and, among others, Strongbow's son-in-law, Robert de Quincy, was killed. Ua Diummusaig is styled <i>rex Offalie</i> (&#145;king of U&iacute; Failge&#146;) in a charter which he issued to a Benedictine (later Cistercian) community between 1177 and 1181, which granted the early Irish ecclesiastical site of Monasterevin (or Mainister Eimhin), reputedly founded by St Eimin in the sixth century and also known as Rosglas, in Kildare. The charter states that the grant was made with the assent of Muirchertach Ua Conchobair, suggesting that he may have been Diarmait's successor designate in the kingship of U&iacute; Failge. Diarmait Ua Diummusaig died in 1193. His son, M&aacute;el Sechlainn, was killed in 1216 by the U&iacute; M&aacute;el Muaid in Fir Chell along with followers of Meiler fitz Henry.<br>
<br>M. T. Flanagan </div>
