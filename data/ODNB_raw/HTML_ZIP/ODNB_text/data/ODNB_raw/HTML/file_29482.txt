<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ware,  William  of</span> <span class="st"> [<em>called </em> the Doctor Fundatus]</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1290&#150;1305), <span class="occ">Franciscan friar and theologian</span>, was born at Ware in Hertfordshire. He entered the Franciscan order as a child oblate, according to the testimony of William Woodford, writing less than a century later. Almost certainly William of Ware studied at Oxford and lectured on the <span class="italic">Sentences</span> there, but he is not listed among the Oxford masters and so he probably never incepted as a master of theology there. There is some evidence, but no certainty, that he also taught at Paris, perhaps lecturing there too on the <span class="italic">Sentences</span>. He was known as the Doctor Fundatus and less commonly the Doctor Praeclarus or the Doctor Profundus.<br>
<br>Only one work can reliably be attributed to him, a commentary on the <span class="italic">Sentences</span> which survives in many manuscripts: only small parts have been edited, by the Franciscans of Quaracchi (1904), and by A. Daniels (1909, 1913), P. Muscat (1927), J.- M. Bissen (1927), and L. H&ouml;dl (1990). William does not try to discuss every distinction, but concentrates on the topics he finds most important, devoting over 100 <i>quaestiones</i> to book 1 and just 129 to the remaining three books. Among the theologians whose views William discusses are Henri de Gand, Godefroi de Fontaines, Giles of Rome, and Richard of Middleton&#151;a choice that indicates 1290&#150;1305 as the time of composition. Since there exist three, or perhaps even four, recensions of the work, its composition and revision may well have been spread over these years.<br>
<br>Traditionally William of Ware has been considered the master of Duns Scotus. This view can be supported by some early evidence: in his treatise on the immaculate conception (<i>c</i>.1373) the Franciscan Thomas Rossy refers to William as the <i>magister Scoti</i>, as does Bartolomeo da Pisa in his <span class="italic">De conformitate vitae beati Francisci ad vitam domini Jesu</span> of the late 1380s. Perhaps <i>magister</i> here is not meant in the strict sense, since William does not seem ever to have become a master. Certainly, there is little doubt that William influenced Scotus, who definitely refers to his views on the nature of theology. Other aspects of his theology, which develops the Franciscan tradition in the context of the debates of the 1290s, are also close to Scotus. Most strikingly, both in discussing the attributes of God and elsewhere, he invokes a type of distinction which neither involves a real difference nor is merely a matter of the understanding. This is very close to Scotus's formal distinction, although William does not clearly give it this label.<br>
<br>John Marenbon </div>
