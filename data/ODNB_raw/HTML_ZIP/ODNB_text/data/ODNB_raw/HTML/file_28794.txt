<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Warren,  William</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1578&#150;1581), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was the author of <span class="italic">A pithie and pleasaunt discourse dialoguewyse betwene a welthie citizen and a miserable souldiour briefelye touchinge the commodyties and discommodyties bothe of warre and peace</span> (1578). The work was entered in the Stationers' register, licensed to Richard Jones, on 7 November 1578, but no copies are known to have survived.<br>
<br>Warren is also the author of <span class="italic">A pleasant new fancie of a fondlings device, intitled and cald the nurcerie of names, wherein is presented (to the order of our alphabet) the brandishing brightnes of our English gentlewomen</span> (1581). The prefatory matter of the volume consists of some short Latin poems and a euphuistic &#145;Proaeme to the Gentleman Readers&#146;, signed &#145;W. Warren, Gent.&#146;, as well as an &#145;Address to the Gentlewomen of England&#146;. In the latter Warren speaks of himself as &#145;your poor Poet and your olde friend&#146;. The body of the work consists of extravagant poems, in fourteen-syllable verse, on women's names. The poem on Elizabeth is an excellent example of the contemporary style of compliment to the queen. Nothing is known of Warren's life. It is possible that he may have been one of the two William Warrens recorded as having matriculated at Oxford University in the 1570s, but this is purely speculative.<br>
<br>Ronald Bayne<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Elizabeth Goldring </div>
