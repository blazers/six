<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Percival,  Thomas</span>  (1719&#150;1762), <span class="occ">antiquary</span>, son of Richard Percival of Royton Hall, near Oldham, Lancashire, was born there on 1 September 1719. He was brought up a Presbyterian, but joined the Church of England; he was a whig in politics, and he welcomed the Hanoverian succession. On 14 March 1738 he married Martha (1714/15&#150;1760), daughter of Major Benjamin Gregge of Chamber Hall, Oldham, at St Mary's, Oldham.<br>
<br>In 1748 Percival wrote two pamphlets opposing the high-church clergy and the nonjurors of Manchester. In 1758 he took part with some operative weavers in a dispute with their employers about wages; he subsequently published <span class="italic">A letter to a friend occasioned by the late dispute betwixt the check-makers of Manchester and their weavers; and the check-makers' ill-usage of the author</span> (1759). On 13 June 1751 Percival read his paper &#145;Observations on the Roman colonies and stations in Cheshire and Lancashire&#146; to the Royal Society. This prompted William Stukeley to describe him as &#145;a learned person who lives in the north, and has taken a good deal of pains by travelling to search out the Roman roads and stations mentioned thereabouts&#146;  (<span class="italic">Family Memoirs</span>, 2.244); a shorter paper on the same subject was published in <span class="italic">Archaeologia</span>, volume 1. He discovered that Kinderton was the site of Roman Condate; and his paper entitled &#145;Account of a double child&#146; was published in the <span class="italic">Philosophical Transactions</span> (68, 1752). Some of the plans in John Aikin's <span class="italic">A Description of the Country &hellip; Round Manchester</span> (1795) were drawn by Percival. He was elected FRS on 25 November 1756, and FSA on 12 June 1760.<br>
<br>Percival died in December 1762, and was buried in the vault of St Paul's Chapel, Royton, where his wife had been buried in March 1760; she had died aged forty-five. Their only child and heir, Katherine, married Joseph Pickford of Alt Hill, Lancashire. Her husband was later known as Sir Joseph Radcliffe of Milnesbridge, Yorkshire, into whose possession Percival's collection of manuscript pedigrees and other papers passed. Thomas Percival the antiquary should be distinguished from his namesake, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/21921/', "Thomas Percival (1740&#150;1804)")</script>, the physician, with whom he is often confused.<br>
<br>C. W. Sutton<span class="italic">, rev. </span> J. A. Marchand </div>
