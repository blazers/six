<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Walter,  William</span>  (<em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>1525&#150;1533), <span class="occ">poet and translator</span>, is a man about whose life little is known. He describes himself on the title-page of <span class="italic">Tytus and Gesyppus</span> (<i>c</i>.1525) as &#145;somtyme servaunte to syr Henry Marney knyght chaunceler of the duchy of Lancastre&#146;. The title-pages of <span class="italic">Guystarde and Sygysmonde</span> (1532) and <span class="italic">The Spectacle of Lovers</span> (1533?) repeat this claim. All of Walter's known writings postdate Marney's death in 1523.<br>
<br>
<span class="italic">Tytus and Gesyppus</span> (812 lines) is a narrative of two friends who were &#145;confederate in amyte&#146;  (456) to the extent that Gesyppus ceded his wife to Titus; subsequently both take the blame for a murder neither committed but of which each suspected the other to be guilty. The narrative's conclusion leaves the two in &#145;love &amp; parfyte amyte&#146;  (805).<br>
<br>
<span class="italic">Guystarde and Sygysmonde</span> (642 lines) recounts the murder of Guystarde by Tancred, the father of his love Sygysmonde, whose heart he subsequently gives to his grieving daughter &#145;in a cuppe of golde&#146;  (477), whereupon she dies. The relative brevity of the narrative is expanded in various interpolations ascribed to Wynkyn de Worde's associate, Robert Copland, who provides a prologue, envoy, and several interjections amounting to about a fifth of the entire work (lines 1&#150;28, 239&#150;59, 442&#150;62, 505&#150;25, 610&#150;44).<br>
<br>Both of these narratives are based on parts of Boccaccio's <span class="italic">Decameron</span> and are the earliest printed English translations of parts of that work. They do not, however, appear to be direct translations but to be based on Latin versions by Leonardo Bruni and Philip Beroaldo respectively. Both are in rhyme-royal stanzas.<br>
<br>Walter's other surviving work, <span class="italic">The Spectacle of Lovers</span>, again is in rhyme royal. It is subtitled &#145;a lytell contravers dyalogue bytwene love and councell&#146;, a characterization that gives some indication of its anti-feminist cast. After a <i>chanson d'aventure</i> opening it consists of a dialogue between Consultator (the narrator) and Amator (a complaining lover) in which the former rebuts the arguments in favour of love advanced by the latter. The following passage is representative:<blockquote>
<div>Ye lovers delyte in slouthe and sadnesse<br>
</div>
<div>In stede of myrthe ye syghe contynually<br>
</div>
<div>Despare dothe ofte encrese your hevynesse<br>
</div>
<div>Ye be also troubled by Jalousy<br>
</div>
<div>By such meanes ye be brought in fransy.<br>
</div> (<span class="italic">Spectacle of Lovers</span>, Biii)</blockquote>Copland again provides an envoy.<br>
<br>All of Walter's works survive only in editions by Wynkyn de Worde, who valued the Boccaccio translations sufficiently to commission woodcuts specifically for them. Some stanzas from the <span class="italic">Spectacle of Lovers</span> also appear in the Bodleian Library's MS Rawlinson C. 813, possibly copied from a printed edition.<br>
<br>A. S. G. Edwards </div>
