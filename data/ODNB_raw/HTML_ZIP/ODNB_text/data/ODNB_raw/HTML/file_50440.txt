<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Dolby,  Richard</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1826&#150;1833), <span class="occ">writer on cookery</span>, of whose early life little is known, was cook at the Thatched House tavern, St James's Street, London (<i>c</i>.1826&#150;<i>c</i>.1832). He was registered by the Vintners' Company as a freeman by servitude on 5 April 1826. His residence was the Thatched House tavern, which was renowned for its clubbable atmosphere and which enjoyed the patronage of the duke of Clarence, later William IV. Dolby's <span class="italic">The Cook's Dictionary and Housekeeper's Directory</span> was published in 1830, receiving outstanding press reviews. &#145;It appears to contain all that the veriest gourmand in Christendom could sigh for, in a life like Methusalem's, with a throat a yard long and palate all the way&#146;  (Allibone, <span class="italic">Dict.</span>). Two further revised editions were published in 1832 and 1833, by which time Dolby was advertised as &#145;Late Cook at the Thatched House Tavern&#146;. Dolby acknowledged his dictionary owed much to others in the profession. French influences can be traced back to Antonin Car&egrave;me's works as well as Louis Eustache Ude's <span class="italic">The French Cook</span>. Dolby also made use of John Farley's <span class="italic">The London Art of Cookery</span>, Duncan Macdonald's <span class="italic">The New London Family Cook</span>, and Mrs Rundell's <span class="italic">Domestic Cookery</span>. His 5000 recipes were necessarily very much abbreviated.<br>
<br>Fiona Lucraft </div>
