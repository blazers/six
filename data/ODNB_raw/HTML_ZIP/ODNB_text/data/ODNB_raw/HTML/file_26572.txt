<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Stone,  Gilbert</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1417), <span class="occ">ecclesiastic and letter writer</span>, was born at Stone in Staffordshire, where he later gave the Augustinian priory a beautiful copy of the <span class="italic">Catholicon</span> by Johannes Januensis  (now BL, Burney MS 323). Probably educated at Oxford, by 1376 he was a notary public, while also being appointed chancellor and registrar first to Robert Wyville, bishop of Salisbury, and then to Ralph Erghum, successively bishop of Salisbury and of Bath and Wells. He was keeper of spiritualities during the episcopal vacancy of 1400&#150;01 at Bath and Wells, where Richard Clifford (<i>d</i>. 1421) was an unsuccessful candidate, and accompanied Clifford to Worcester, where he became chancellor and registrar in 1401 and vicar-general in 1402. He briefly opened a school in Oxford in 1401&#150;2, where he probably taught business studies. Before 1389 he held the church of Tydecombe in Salisbury diocese. He held the prebend of Buckland Denham in Wells in 1394 and briefly from early 1400 that of Wedmore Secunda, and lived in a canon's house &#145;hard by the street known as Terre lane&#146;. By 1400 he also held the prebend of Wilnecote in Tamworth, Staffordshire. In March 1401 he exchanged Wedmore and the deanery of the king's free chapel of Hastings (presented in August 1398) for the prebend of Eigne in Hereford.<br>
<br>In March 1412 Stone became a canon of St Paul's with the prebend of Portpool, Richard Clifford now being bishop of London. In October 1414 he exchanged Eigne for the wardenship of Imber free chapel, Wiltshire, while in 1415 he was admitted as prebendary of Tytherington and Horningham in Heytesbury, Wiltshire, in exchange for Tamworth.<br>
<br>Stone was admired and chiefly remembered for his letters, which were preserved as precedents and are typical of the style prized at the time by those who taught <i>dictamen</i> (literary composition) to would-be administrators. A letter-book based on one of his survives  (Bodl. Oxf., MS Bodley 859, fols. 1&#150;42). Several of its letters contain interesting information on relations with the Roman curia; letters both from Stone and from Erghum and Clifford, as well as from Richard II, are included. There are also references to Cardinal Adam Easton, who died in 1397  (fols. 18<i>v</i>&#150;19, 24&#150;5<i>v</i>, the last two printed by Perroy, nos. 214A and B). The book later belonged to Edmund Lacy, bishop of Exeter (1420&#150;55); the letters were copies of a collection originally sent to Stone's friend John Langrysh, prior of Witham Charterhouse, some of whose work is included. Other stray letters from Stone are found in BL, Harley MS 431, fols. 25<i>r</i>&#150;<i>v</i>, 108<i>r</i>&#150;<i>v</i>, 110<i>r</i>&#150;<i>v</i> (to Thomas Polton, as chancellor of Lincoln, who had shared with him service under Erghum), and 111&#150;12. A further letter is in BL, Cotton MS Vitellius E.x, fols. 134&#150;135<i>v</i>, to an archbishop of Canterbury, containing reflections on relations between church and state. He also wrote a prayer and possibly a legend of St Gwenfrewi (Winifred). Stone had died by 1 June 1417.<br>
<br>Margaret Harvey </div>
