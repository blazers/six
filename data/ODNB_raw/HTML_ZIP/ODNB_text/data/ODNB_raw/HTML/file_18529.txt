<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Mellis,  John</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1593?), <span class="occ">writer on arithmetic and bookkeeping</span>, came from Norwich, but few details of his life are known. He had (according to himself) a natural genius for drawing proportions, maps, cards, buildings, and plates. He attended a Dr Robert Forth at Trinity Hall, Cambridge, and went to the arithmetic lecture in the common school. He left the service of Forth, who afterwards became a master in chancery, about 1564. Subsequently he kept a school for writing and arithmetic at Mayes Gate, near Battle Bridge, in the parish of St Olave, Southwark.<br>
<br>In 1582 Mellis published an edition of Robert Recorde's <span class="italic">Grounde of Artes</span>, which he dedicated to Robert Forth and to which he added his own &#145;Brief rules, called rules of practize, of rare, pleasant, and commodious effect, abridged into a briefer method than hitherto hath bene published. With diuers other very necessarie rules, tables, and questions, not only profitable for merchants, but also for gentlemen and all other occupiers whatsouer&#146;. His other main work, <span class="italic">A briefe instruction and maner how to keepe bookes of accompts after the order of debitor and creditor, and as well for proper accompts partible</span> (1588), was described by Mellis as a reissue &#145;of an auncient old copie printed here in London the 14 of August, 1543&#146;, from the pen of &#145;Hugh Oldcastle, Scholemaster&#146;. No copy of Oldcastle's original is known to be extant but there is some independent evidence that it existed, and it would have been the first printed book in the English language on double-entry bookkeeping. Mellis's <span class="italic">A Briefe Instruction</span> was only the sixth book of its kind. It seems highly likely that Mellis died from the plague in or about September 1593; the register for St Olave's, Southwark, records the burial of one &#145;John Mellys, Scholemaister&#146;, on 11 September of that year. The will of &#145;John Mellis of the prishe of St Olaves&#146; had been written three days earlier. It names &#145;Anne, my wife&#146; as Mellis's sole executor and provides a bequest to his son, Simon.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> H. K. Higton </div>
