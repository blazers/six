<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Huddleston <span class="st"> [<em>alias </em>Dormer, Shirley]</span>,  John</span>  (1636&#150;1700), <span class="occ">Jesuit</span>, was born on 27 December 1636 in the village of Clavering, Essex, the son of Sir Robert Huddleston. He was brought up in London until the age of twelve. In 1649 he was sent to the English College, St Omer, where he remained until he entered the English College, Rome, on 9 September 1655 under the alias Shirley. He left Rome to join the noviciate at Bonn in 1656, was probably ordained in 1669 and was at the English College in Li&egrave;ge in 1670&#150;72. He left for the English mission in 1673 and lived in the College of St Thomas (1673&#150;4), the College of St Ignatius (1674&#150;5), and the College of St Hugh (1676&#150;8). In 1678 he was serving on the mission at Blyborough in Lincolnshire. He published <span class="italic">A New Plot</span> (1679) defending the martyrs of the Popish Plot, and wrote <span class="italic">A Brief Instruction Touching the Oath of Allegiance</span> directed against the oath. He appears in Paris in 1681&#150;2 and at Li&egrave;ge in 1683. He returned to England, to the College of St Hugh, in 1684. James II had a great regard for him and appointed him one of the royal preachers at the court of St James. Several of his sermons between 1686 and 1688 were published, including a call to repentance in <span class="italic">A Sermon of Judgement</span> (1688) and <span class="italic">Rebellion Arraign'd</span> (1688), preached on the anniversary of the execution of Charles I and condemning the use of religion as a justification for rebellion. On the outbreak of the revolution in 1688 he escaped to the continent and was chosen rector of the college at Li&egrave;ge on 4 November 1689. His government of the college was criticized and he left Li&egrave;ge on 23 April 1691 and returned to England, living mostly in the College of St Ignatius, covering the London district. His <span class="italic">Usury Explain'd, or, Conscience Quieted in the Case of Putting out Mony at Interest</span> (1695&#150;6), published under the name Philopenes, may have been written against Bishop James Smith's treatise on the subject and was condemned by the Holy Office in 1703. He died in London on 16 January 1700.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Ruth Jordan </div>
