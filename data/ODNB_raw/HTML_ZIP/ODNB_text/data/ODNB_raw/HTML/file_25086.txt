<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Sengham,  William</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1252), <span class="occ">Augustinian friar</span>, is likely to have come from the village of Sengham in Norfolk. However, there is no contemporary evidence for his life and activities. Sengham is mentioned in John Bale's <span class="italic">Scriptorum catalogus</span>, but even this information was not obtained at first hand, being derived from the writings of the early fifteenth-century Carmelite Thomas Colby. According to Colby, William Sengham came from a humble social background and was educated in the arts as well as theology. Probably after the end of his formal training he went to northern Italy, where he joined the Johnbonites, one of several loosely organized groups of hermits who&#151;largely owing to the initiative of Cardinal Ricardo Annibaldi&#151;were merged into the order of hermit friars of St Augustine in 1256. According to Bale, Sengham had already returned to England in 1252, obeying the command of the Johnbonites' prior-general, Lanfranc, who wanted his order to extend into England. It is likely that Sengham was one of the first representatives of the Johnbonites, and later of the Augustinian friars, in England. A reference to &#145;Guilhelmus Sengham&#146;, one of those &#145;saints and doctors who extended the order into England in the thirteenth century&#146; by Pamphilus  (Pamphilus, 25<i>v</i>), who published a history of the Augustinian friars in Rome in 1581, may refer to Bale's work (published at Basel in 1557), but could equally be an independent corroboration.<br>
<br>Sengham is credited with the authorship of four treatises whose titles are known (<span class="italic">De fide et legibus</span>, <span class="italic">De claustro animae</span>, <span class="italic">De professione novitiorum</span>, and <span class="italic">De tentationum remediis</span>) but whose texts are lost. According to Roth, the author of the first treatise is unknown, and Sengham probably only wrote an index to the book.<br>
<br>Jens R&ouml;hrkasten </div>
