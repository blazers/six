<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Howison,  John</span>  (1797&#150;1859), <span class="occ">writer and surgeon</span>, was born on 10 May 1797 in Edinburgh, third and youngest child of William Howison, financial writer, and his wife, Janet Bogle. He was the brother of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/13993/', "William Howison (b. 1796?)")</script>. From 1818 to 1820 Howison travelled in Quebec and Ontario, Canada, and practised medicine in St Catharines, Ontario. His <span class="italic">Sketches of Upper Canada, domestic, local, and characteristic: to which are added, practical details for the information of emigrants of every class</span> (1821) was an immediate success and is one of the best documentary sources for pioneer life in Canada. It went into three editions, and was translated into German. The first edition was reprinted in 1965 and 1970.<br>
<br>In 1820 Howison left Canada via the United States, and in 1822 he went to Bombay as an assistant surgeon with the East India Company. He published eight short stories in <span class="italic">Blackwood's Edinburgh Magazine</span> at this time, two of which were later republished in <span class="italic">Nimmo's Popular Tales</span> (1858). He was stationed in Poona in 1825, and belonged to the literary society there. Descriptions of the life of the foreign colony in Bombay and a journey through the Deccan with Major-General Sir Lionel Smith can be found in Howison's <span class="italic">Foreign Scenes and Travelling Recreations</span> (1825); this work also contains an account of Cuba which continues to interest French and Hispanic historians. His <span class="italic">Tales of the Colonies</span> (1830) consists of Gothic stories set in the West Indies. Howison became a member of the Bombay branch of the Royal Asiatic Society, and served as vice-president in 1832. He demonstrated that he was more than an author of light travel works and fiction with his unjustly neglected <span class="italic">European Colonies &hellip; Viewed in their Social, Moral, and Physical Condition</span> (1834). In this, his most ambitious work, Howison's analysis ranges over Africa, India, the Caribbean, and the Arctic. In 1835 the East India Company promoted him to the post of full surgeon.<br>
<br>Howison's sketch of &#145;the Bengal assistant-surgeon, with his fists in his pockets, and habited in a dress half military, half civilian&#146; on promenade at the Cape of Good Hope is probably a self-portrait  (J. Howison, <span class="italic">Foreign Scenes</span>, 1825, 2.216). He seems never again to have experienced the &#145;freedom &hellip; independence &hellip; and &hellip; joyousness, connected with&#146; Canada  (J. Howison, <span class="italic">Sketches</span>, 1821, 263), and certainly India presented &#145;a succession of struggles against personal inconveniences and bodily uneasiness&#146;  (<span class="italic">Foreign Scenes</span>, 2.74). He concluded that those who pursue &#145;foreign adventure&#146; lose &#145;in contentment and serenity what they gain in wealth and knowledge of the world&#146;  (ibid., 2.115). Howison retired in 1842 and died on 8 February 1859 at his home, 1 Pelham Place, Brompton, London.<br>
<br>Bonnie Shannon McMullen </div>
