<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Corbould,  Richard</span>  (1757&#150;1831), <span class="occ">artist</span>, was born in London on 18 April 1757, and baptized at St Edmund the King and Martyr, Lombard Street, on 24 April, the son of George Corbould and his wife, Anne. He painted, both in oils and watercolours, portraits, landscapes, still life, and history; miniatures on enamel and ivory, also on porcelain; and occasionally he etched. He was thought very clever at imitating the style of the old masters, and yet could show an originality of his own. He first appears as an exhibitor in 1776 at the Free Society of Artists, to which he sent <span class="italic">The Morning</span>, after Claude Lorrain, a stained drawing, <span class="italic">A Bunch of Grapes</span>, and another landscape. In 1777 he sent a miniature to the exhibition of the Royal Academy, and continued to exhibit there numerous pictures in varied styles up to 1811. These included <span class="italic">Cottagers Gathering Sticks</span> (1793); <span class="italic">The Millennial Age: Isaiah Xi. 6, 8</span> (1801), a picture very much admired at the time; and <span class="italic">Hero and Leander</span> (1803). With his wife, Charlotte, he had two sons, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/6301/', "Henry Corbould (1787&#150;1844)")</script> and George Corbould, also notable painters. He last appeared as an exhibitor in 1817 at the British Institution.<br>
<br>It is, however, as a designer of illustrations for books that Corbould is most widely known. He was largely employed by publishers, and his illustrations, engraved by the best artists, showed both skill and judgement. He contributed to Cooke's pocket editions of English classics (1795&#150;1800), including illustrations for Richardson's <span class="italic">Pamela</span>. He also illustrated children's books and non-fiction. Corbould lived for some years in John Street, Tottenham Court Road, but later in life moved to the north of London. He died at Highgate on 26 July 1831, and was buried in the churchyard of St Andrew's, Holborn, London.<br>
<br>L. H. Cust<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Ruth Stewart </div>
