<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Thomas <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Wolferstan]</span>,  Elizabeth</span>  (1770/71&#150;1855), <span class="occ">novelist and poet</span>, was baptized on 7 October 1771 at Hartland in Devon, the daughter of Mary (<i>d</i>. 1818) and Edward Wolferstan (<i>d</i>. 1788) of Berry House there. Little is known of her early life. She married the Revd Thomas Thomas (<i>d</i>. 1838), vicar of Tidenham, Gloucestershire, about 1795. Elizabeth Thomas wrote religious verse which was variously received, praised on one hand for its &#145;purity, correctness, and piety&#146;  (<span class="italic">Theatrical Inquisitor</span>, February 1819), but denigrated on the other for its trite or blasphemous versifications of biblical stories  (<span class="italic">Monthly Review</span>, August 1819). Her anonymous collection <span class="italic">The Confession, or, The Novice of St Clare, and other Poems</span> (1818) was widely reviewed, particularly for the titillating anti-Catholicism of its title poem. Its long preface defends the author's anonymity, arguing: &#145;her name is so common nothing can be made of it &hellip; her character is of the same stamp &hellip; she is, in short, quite a creature of ordinary life&#146;, disingenuous disclaimers since the title-page advertises the work as penned by the author of <span class="italic">Purity of Heart, or, The Ancient Costume</span> (1816), a virulent, polemical novel addressed to the anonymous author of <span class="italic">Glenarvon</span>, the 1816 <i>succ&egrave;s de scandale</i>. In her preface to <span class="italic">Purity of Heart</span>, under the pseudonym &#145;an old wife of twenty years&#146;, Elizabeth Thomas confesses she could not resist ridiculing <span class="italic">Glenarvon</span> in order to counteract &#145;its horrible tendency&#146; and &#145;its dangerous and perverting sophistry&#146;. Estimates of the novel's success in advancing public morals and virtue (the &#145;ancient costume&#146; of her subtitle) differed, from praise as &#145;a clever little novel&#146;  (<span class="italic">Theatrical Inquisitor</span>, Dec 1816), to criticism of the &#145;old wife&#146; for missing her target and digressing into a personally hostile attack on <span class="italic">Glenarvon</span>'s presumed author in its &#145;strange extravagant portrait&#146;  (<span class="italic">European Magazine</span>, 1817) of Lady Calintha Limb (Lady Caroline Lamb).<br>
<br>Elizabeth Thomas has also been identified as &#145;Mrs Bridget Bluemantle&#146;, author of nine Minerva Press novels from 1806 to 1818. While this author's conservative disavowal of any identification with learned women, or underdressed &#145;Bluestockings&#146;, may well be in character, bibliographical problems remain in securely fixing Elizabeth Thomas's name to these works. Virginia Blain rightly points to a confusion in attribution with the novelist Maria Elizabeth Budden (&#145;M. E. B.&#146;), and perhaps these Minerva novels should remain where Blakey left them in 1939, as by an unidentified, pseudonymous author. Elizabeth Thomas died, aged eighty-four, of bronchitis on 1 June 1855 at her home, Foxdown, Parkham, near Bideford, Devon.<br>
<br>Deirdre Coleman </div>
