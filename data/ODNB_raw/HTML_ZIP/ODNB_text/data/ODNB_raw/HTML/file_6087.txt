<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Connor,  Charles</span>  (<em>c.</em>1788&#150;1826), <span class="occ">actor</span>, was born in co. Cork, the son of James Connor. He was educated at Trinity College, Dublin, where he was admitted in 1805 at the age of seventeen. He is believed to have played Euphrasia in <span class="italic">The Grecian Daughter</span> while still at school, and to have acquired some popularity at the Dublin theatre before travelling to England. There he made his d&eacute;but as an actor at Bath about 1807, as Fitzharding in <span class="italic">The Curfew</span>. Connor's first appearance in London did not take place until 18 September 1816, in the character of Sir Patrick McGuire in Oulton's <span class="italic">The Sleep Walker</span>. From this period until 14 June 1826, when as Kenrick in Colman's <span class="italic">The Heir-at-Law</span> he took a benefit and made his last recorded appearance, he played a round of characters at Covent Garden. These consisted of Irish types, servants, villains, and similar roles, the most prominent being Sir Callaghan in Charles Macklin's <span class="italic">Love &agrave; la Mode</span>, Foigard in Farquhar's <span class="italic">The Beaux' Stratagem</span>, Sir William Davison in an adaptation of Schiller's <span class="italic">Mary Stuart</span>, Julio in Barry Cornwall's <span class="italic">Mirandola</span>, Dennis Brulgruddery in the younger Colman's <span class="italic">John Bull</span>, and Filch in <span class="italic">The Beggar's Opera</span>. He also played characters in various adaptations of Scott's novels. The original parts assigned him included Terry O'Rourke, otherwise Dr O'Toole, in <span class="italic">The Irish Tutor</span>, written expressly for him, at Cheltenham and Covent Garden in 1822; and Dr O'Rafferty in <span class="italic">Cent per Cent</span>, in 1823. He also played Sir Lucius O'Trigger in <span class="italic">The Rivals</span>.<br>
<br>Connor's wife (whose name is unknown) was also on the stage and is said to have acted at the Haymarket as Grace Gaylove in Colman's <span class="italic">The Review</span>. She performed at Covent Garden, taking the roles of Manse Hedrigge in <span class="italic">The Battle of Bothwell Brigg</span> (May 1820), in which Connor was Graham of Claverhouse; the Duchess of York in <span class="italic">Richard III</span> (March 1821); and Servia in Sheridan Knowles's <span class="italic">Virginius</span> (December 1821), to her husband's Appius. A benefit was given for her at the English Opera House (Lyceum) after her husband's death. Connor died suddenly of heart disease on 7 October 1826 while crossing St James's Park to his home in Pimlico, and was buried on 13 October 1826 at the New Church, Chelsea. A Roman Catholic, he was survived by his wife and two children. Connor had a good face, figure, and voice, and, while his career in London cannot be regarded as a great success, he was fairly popular in the roles he performed.<br>
<br>Joseph Knight<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Nilanjana Banerji </div>
