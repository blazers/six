<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hooton,  Charles</span>  (1810?&#150;1847), <span class="occ">novelist and journalist</span>, was probably born on 10 May 1810. According to later applications submitted to the Royal Literary Fund, Hooton studied at the Royal Academy of Arts, London. In 1836, while editing a newspaper in Leeds, Hooton published <span class="italic">The Adventures of Bilberry Thurland</span>. In 1837 he moved to London and published in <span class="italic">Bentley's Miscellany</span> a novel called <span class="italic">Colin Clink</span> (republished 1841). As the sub-editor of the <span class="italic">True Sun</span>, Hooton wrote a series of articles on the subject of political economy. In 1840 he edited another short-lived paper, <span class="italic">The Woolsack</span>, in which he attacked the abuses of the court of chancery. Following the collapse of <span class="italic">The Woolsack</span>, Hooton left for Texas, where for nine months he attempted to live off the land, farming, hunting, and fishing. He afterwards undertook newspaper work in New Orleans, New York, and Montreal, but his failing health prompted him to return to England. He wrote a series of ballads for the <span class="italic">New Monthly Magazine</span> illustrative of American life and literature, and a novel called <span class="italic">Launcelot Wedge</span>, which was running in <span class="italic">Ainsworth's Magazine</span> at the time of his death (republished 1849). Hooton's account of his experiences in the United States and Canada was published posthumously as <span class="italic">St Louis' Isle, or, Texiana</span> (1847). With his wife, Emily (<i>d</i>. after 1847), he had a child; the Royal Literary Fund archive records that his wife left him after he had attempted to kill her and their child. He died on 16 February 1847 from an overdose of morphine at his father's residence in Nottingham, to which he had been moved having been certified insane while in Texas. According to a late twentieth-century assessment of his writing, &#145;his sub-Dickensian fiction, though quirky, has its powerfully atmospheric and comic moments&#146;  (Sutherland, 305).<br>
<br>Francis Watt<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Charles Brayne </div>
