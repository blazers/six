<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Madan <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Cowper]</span>,  Judith</span>  (1702&#150;1781), <span class="occ">gentlewoman and poet</span>, was born on 26 August 1702, probably at the family estate, Hertingfordbury Park, Hertfordshire, the only daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/6507/', "Spencer Cowper (1670&#150;1728)")</script>, lawyer, judge, and MP, and his wife, Pennington, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Goodere (<i>bap</i>. 1665, <i>d</i>. 1727).<br>
<br>From her mid-teens Judith Cowper composed verse, mainly in heroic couplets, characteristically in imitation and celebration of the poets she was reading. She probably met Pope while sitting to his friend Charles Jervas for her portrait. He compared her favourably with Lady Mary Wortley Montagu:<blockquote>
<div>Tho sprightly Sappho force our Love &amp; Praise,<br>
</div>
<div>A softer Wonder my pleasd soul surveys,<br>
</div>
<div>The mild Erinna, blushing in her Bays.<br>
</div> (<span class="italic">Correspondence of Alexander Pope</span>, 2.138&#150;9)</blockquote>The poem concludes with a version of the comparison of sunset to moonrise, to the latter's advantage, which was later to be applied to Martha Blount at the close of <span class="italic">Characters of Women</span>. In 1723 he sent Judith &#145;To a Lady on her Birthday&#146;, which she seems to have assumed to have been addressed to her, whereas the &#145;lady&#146; was actually Martha  (ibid., 2.180, 3.18&#150;19). Pope suggested poetic projects as diversions to counter the depression to which Judith was subject, and in 1723 proposed that she should write &#145;something in the descriptive way on any Subject you please, mixd with Vision &amp; Moral&#146;: if she wrote the descriptions, he offered, &#145;I would undertake to find a Tale that shoud bring em all together&#146;  (ibid., 2.202&#150;3).<br>
<br>In 1723 an outburst of rapturously lyrical poems proclaimed Judith's love for Lysander, alias Colonel Martin Madan (1700&#150;1756), whom she married on 7 December. They had nine children, including <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/17748/', "Martin Madan (1725&#150;1790)")</script> and <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/17749/', "Spencer Madan (1729&#150;1813)")</script>. After her marriage her writing of poetry came to a virtual standstill. Money was short and her husband often abroad, and she suffered recurrent depression. She was confirmed about 1749 and joined the Methodist circle of John Wesley and Selina Hastings, countess of Huntingdon, corresponding with her like-minded nephew the poet William Cowper. Her daughter Maria also published religious verse. Madan was praised as &#145;Cornalia&#146; in John Duncombe's <span class="italic">Feminiad</span> (1754), and a few of her poems appeared in the miscellanies of Matthew Concaren (1731), George Colman and Bonnell Thornton (1755), and Robert Dodsley (1755); &#145;Abelard to Eloisa&#146;, written in 1720 in response to Pope's poem, appeared in William Pattison's <span class="italic">Poetical Works</span> (1728) as his own. However, much of Madan's most interesting work remains unpublished.<br>
<br>Madan died on 7 December 1781 at her home in Stafford Row (now part of Buckingham Gate), London, and was buried in the churchyard of Grosvenor Chapel, Mount Street, Grosvenor Square.<br>
<br>Valerie Rumbold </div>
