<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Phipps, Sir  Charles Beaumont</span>  (1801&#150;1866), <span class="occ">court official</span>, second son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/22191/', "Henry Phipps, first earl of Mulgrave and Viscount Normanby (1755&#150;1831)")</script>, and his wife, Martha Sophia Maling (<i>d</i>. 1849), was born at Mulgrave Castle, Yorkshire, on 27 December 1801, and educated at Harrow School. He joined the army as an ensign and lieutenant in the Scots Fusilier Guards in 1820, rising to the rank of lieutenant-colonel in 1837. He was placed on half pay in 1847, and retired from active service in 1851, and was thenceforth a colonel unattached. He acted as private secretary to his brother, <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/22187/', "Constantine Henry Phipps, first marquess of Normanby")</script>, when governor of Jamaica, 1832, and as steward of the viceregal household, when Normanby went to Ireland as lord lieutenant, 1835&#150;9. On 25 June 1835 he married Margaret Anne (<i>d</i>. 1874), daughter of Henry Bathurst, archdeacon of York, and Fanny Mackenzie; they had two daughters and two sons.<br>
<br>On 1 August 1846, Phipps was appointed equerry to Queen Victoria, and thereafter acquired an increasingly important role in the royal household. He became treasurer and private secretary to Prince Albert in 1847, and keeper of the queen's privy purse in 1849. Although he was never officially accorded the title, he acted with General Charles Grey as Victoria's private secretary (particularly after Albert's death), and as such was much in the political confidence of the queen and prince consort. He was made CB in 1853 and KCB in 1858. He additionally held office as treasurer and cofferer to the prince of Wales from 1849 and as one of his council from 1863. He was receiver-general of the duchy of Cornwall from 1862, and keeper of the signet to the prince of Wales as steward of Scotland from 1864. He died of bronchitis at Ambassadors' Court, St James's Palace, on 24 February 1866, and was buried on 2 March in St George's Chapel, Windsor.<br>
<br>
<a name="cosubject_42142"></a><span class="headword">Harriet Lepel Phipps</span>  (1841&#150;1922)<span class="occ"></span>, his younger daughter, was one of Victoria's longest-serving confidential attendants, as maid of honour (1862&#150;89) and bedchamber woman (1889&#150;1901). Used by the ageing queen as a go-between, she was privy to many secrets, political and personal, none of which she revealed. On her instructions, all her papers were burnt after her death.<br>
<br>K. D. Reynolds </div>
