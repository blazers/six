<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Baines,  John</span>  (1787&#150;1838), <span class="occ">mathematician</span>, was born at Westfield Farm, Horbury, near Wakefield. He showed early a strong mathematical bias and became proficient also in natural science (especially botany), Latin, and Greek. His teaching career took him from Horbury Bridge (<i>c</i>.1810&#150;1813) to Reading (<i>c</i>.1816) as a mathematics master, then to Nottingham (<i>c</i>.1818), Dewsbury (<i>c</i>.1819), and finally (<i>c</i>.1829) Thornhill, near Wakefield.<br>
<br>From 1810 at least, Baines sent mathematical contributions to periodicals, including the <span class="italic">Ladies' Diary</span>, the <span class="italic">Gentleman's Diary</span>, <span class="italic">The Enquirer</span>, and <span class="italic">York Miscellany</span>. He wrote to the <span class="italic">Northern Star</span> from Nottingham. The <span class="italic">Ladies' Diary</span> for 1833 carried a more substantial article on Cuvier's <span class="italic">Theory of the Earth</span>, written to prove its confirmation of the Mosaic account. Most of Baines's work shows considerable talent. He was an early friend of the mathematician T. S. Davies (1794&#150;1851). Baines died at Thornhill on 1 May 1838, after nine years as master of the grammar school, and was buried at Horbury.<br>
<br>R. E. Anderson<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Ruth Wallis </div>
