<div class="para">
<span class="headword">King,  Matthew Peter</span>  (<em>c.</em>1773&#150;1823), <span class="occ">composer</span>, was born in London. Very little is known of him beyond a catalogue of his works. Apparently he studied composition with Charles Frederick Horn, and the appearance of his earliest compositions under the name of &#145;Master King&#146; could mean, as suggested by Cudworth (1980), that he was a child prodigy. He seems to have spent most of his life in London.<br>
<br>Early works, before 1800, include a series of piano sonatas and other pieces for keyboard; some theoretical treatises dating from around the turn of the century concentrate on the practice of thoroughbass and challenge the theories of A. F. C. Kollmann. King then turned his efforts to vocal music, especially for the stage, and in this area he enjoyed some success. Between 1804 and 1819 he produced several stage works, sometimes in collaboration with Michael Kelly, John Braham, or John Davy, to librettos generally by James Kenney or S. J. Arnold. These were performed at Covent Garden, Drury Lane, and the English Opera at the Lyceum Theatre, and include the comic opera <span class="italic">Matrimony</span> (with Kenney; Drury Lane, 20 November 1804); the melodrama <span class="italic">Timour the Tartar</span> (with M. G. Lewis; Covent Garden, 29 April 1811); and the musical farce <span class="italic">Turn out!</span> (with Kenney; Lyceum, 7 March 1812). Among other compositions are a number of glees, ballads, madrigals, and piano pieces, and an oratorio, <span class="italic">The Intercession</span>, which was produced at Covent Garden on 1 June 1816. An aria from this work, &#145;Eve's Lamentation&#146;, became very popular for a time.<br>
<br>King was a gifted musician and his operas enjoyed considerable vogue. He died in London in January 1823. His son, C. M. King, composed and published some songs and piano pieces.<br>
<br>R. F. Sharp<span class="italic">, rev. </span> David J. Golby </div>
