<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Langtry,  Lillie</span> <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Emilie Charlotte Le Breton]</span>  (1853&#150;1929), <span class="occ">actress</span>, was born on 13 October 1853 at St Saviour's rectory, Jersey, the only daughter and sixth of the seven children of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/47317/', "William Corbet Le Breton (1815&#150;1888)")</script>, dean of Jersey, and his wife, Emilie Davis Martin. Educated at home, she developed into a socially ambitious and remarkably beautiful young woman. In 1874 she married Edward Langtry, son of a Belfast shipowner. Within three years the couple were established in London, where Lillie became celebrated, not only as a &#145;professional beauty&#146;&#151;a society woman whose photographic likenesses were on sale to the public&#151;but as the mistress of Albert Edward, prince of Wales [<i>see </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/32975/', "Edward VII")</script>]. Between 1877 and 1880 she enjoyed a period of heady social success. Her portrait was painted by most leading artists of the day; the best-known is <span class="italic">A Jersey Lily</span> by John Everett Millais, in the possession of the Soci&eacute;t&eacute; Jersiaise in Jersey.<br>
<br>Towards the end of her three-year liaison with the prince of Wales, Lillie met Prince Louis of Battenberg (1854&#150;1921) [<i>see </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/35134/', "Mountbatten, Louis Alexander")</script>]. In Paris, in March 1881, she gave birth to their daughter, who was christened Jeanne-Marie, with the surname of Langtry. The birth of this child coincided with the bankruptcy of Edward Langtry and the breakup of Lillie's marriage. Despite the continued support of the prince of Wales, she was ostracized by society, though she maintained a well-publicized friendship with W. E. Gladstone.<br>
<br>Exceptionally resilient, Lillie embarked on a new career&#151;as an actress. Her appearance in a charity performance led to an offer to join the Bancrofts' company at the Haymarket Theatre. She made her professional d&eacute;but as Kate Hardcastle in <span class="italic">She Stoops to Conquer</span> on 15 December 1881. Appreciating that it was her notoriety, as the former mistress of the prince of Wales, that was attracting audiences, Lillie soon founded her own company. Between 1882 and 1889 she divided her time between tours of the United States, where she owned a ranch in California, and appearances on various London stages. Although never more than a competent actress, she was an extremely decorative and popular one, at her best in plays about upper-class society. In 1889 she returned permanently to England. To her career as an actress, the always practical Lillie now added another: that of a highly successful racehorse owner; she won the Cesarewitch twice, with Merman and Yutoi.<br>
<br>In 1899, two years after Edward Langtry had died in destitution, Lillie married Hugo Gerald (1871&#150;1940), the eldest son of Sir Henry de Bathe, fourth baronet. On the death of her father-in-law in 1907, Lillie became Lady De Bathe.<br>
<br>After the failure of her attempt to establish a company at the Imperial Theatre, Westminster, Lillie resumed touring. After the First World War she bought a villa, Le Lys, in Monaco. In 1925 she published a highly selective book of memoirs. She died&#151;by no means destitute&#151;in Monte Carlo on 12 February 1929 and was buried in St Saviour's churchyard, Jersey.<br>
<br>Theo Aronson<span class="italic">, rev. </span>
