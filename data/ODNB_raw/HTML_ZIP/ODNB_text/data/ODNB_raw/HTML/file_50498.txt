<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Treece,  Henry William</span>  (1911&#150;1966), <span class="occ">writer and schoolteacher</span>, was born on 22 December 1911 at Oxford Terrace, Oxford Street, New Town, Wednesbury, Staffordshire, the younger of the two children of Richard Treece (<i>b</i>. <i>c</i>.1872), bridge engineer, and his wife, Mary Fisher, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Mason (<i>b</i>. <i>c</i>.1875). He attended Wednesbury High School for Boys and won a scholarship to Birmingham University, where he read English, history, and Spanish, graduating in 1933. He entered teaching, eventually becoming English master at the grammar school, Barton-on-Humber, Lincolnshire, in late 1938. He married fellow teacher Nelly Mary (1911&#150;1985), daughter of William Woodman, on 6 April 1939. They had three children: David (1945&#150;1946), Jennifer (<i>b</i>. 1947), and Richard (<i>b</i>. 1949). Treece joined the volunteer reserve of the Royal Air Force in 1941, attaining the rank of flight lieutenant but serving mainly as an intelligence officer. He returned to teaching at Barton in 1946 as senior English master, retiring on health grounds in late 1959.<br>
<br>Treece started to publish poems, and then short stories, from the mid-1930s in journals such as <span class="italic">Seven</span>. He produced six collections, the first, <span class="italic">38 Poems</span>, in 1940, and the last, <span class="italic">The Exiles</span>, in 1952. However, he was best-known for founding with the Scottish poet J. F. Hendry (1912&#150;1986) the short-lived movement which came to be known as the <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/theme/96344', "New Apocalypse")</script> and, later, the New Romantics. Characterized by its use of myth and imagery, it was a fairly loose association of young poets based mainly outside London. The pair edited three anthologies: <span class="italic">The New Apocalypse</span> (1939), <span class="italic">The White Horseman</span> (1941), and <span class="italic">The Crown and the Sickle</span> (1943). A strong influence was Dylan Thomas, and Treece later produced the first study of him: <span class="italic">Dylan Thomas: Dog among the Fairies</span> (1949). In late 1941 Treece, with Alan Rook and Stefan Schimanski, took over the editorship of <span class="italic">Kingdom Come</span>, succeeded in 1943 by <span class="italic">Transformation</span> (1943&#150;7), a series of four anthologies of prose, poetry, and plays edited by Treece and Schimanski. In the early 1950s Treece embarked on a new course as a historical novelist, producing ten titles between 1952 (<span class="italic">The Dark Island</span>) and 1966 (<span class="italic">The Green Man</span>). He enjoyed his greatest success with his numerous books (including over thirty novels) for children, particularly his viking stories, such as <span class="italic">Viking's Dawn</span> (1955) and <span class="italic">The Last of the Vikings</span> (1964). He frequently broadcast on radio, for which he also produced plays and features. He won an Arts Council play prize in 1955 for his <span class="italic">Carnival King</span>.<br>
<br>Treece was about 5 feet 10 inches tall, well-built, with glasses, and a natural&#151;but not dominating&#151;air of authority. He was very sociable, and mixed well with people from all walks of life. Nominally an Anglican and politically Conservative, he had wide interests, including boxing, music (he was an accomplished pianist and flamenco guitarist), the theatre, and painting. He died in Scunthorpe General Hospital following a heart attack on 10 June 1966 shortly after completing another children's novel, <span class="italic">The Dream-Time</span>, which was published posthumously in 1967. His body was cremated at Grimsby crematorium.<br>
<br>Brian Dyson </div>
