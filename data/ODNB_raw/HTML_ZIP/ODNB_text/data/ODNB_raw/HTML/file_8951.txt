<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Evans,  Edward</span>  (<em>b. </em>1573/4), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman</span>, was the son of a clergyman from West Meon, Hampshire. He was educated at Winchester College, matriculated at New College, Oxford, on 10 October 1594, aged twenty, and was admitted a fellow there in 1595, graduating BA in 1598 and proceeding MA in 1602. On the presentation of the college he was vicar of Heckfield, Hampshire, briefly in 1601&#150;2, and of Chesterton, Oxfordshire, between 1604 and 1610.<br>
<br>Since he was apparently &#145;a noted preacher of his time in the university&#146;  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span> 2.168), it was almost certainly he who published in 1615 <span class="italic">Verba Dierum, or, The Dayes Report of Gods Glory</span>, based on sermons or lectures given in Oxford, rather than the Edward Evans (<i>b</i>. <i>c</i>.1582) of Denbighshire and Christ Church to whom Wood gave credit for the work  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span>: <span class="italic">Fasti</span>, 1.299). Dedicated only to &#145;the honour and glory of the holiest of all, the Almightie and most high King of Kings and Lord of Lords, Father of Lights, Maker of all Things, fountain of all glory&#146;, and stiff with explanations of Greek, Hebrew, and Arabic meanings, the work celebrates the knowledge of God written in the world as well as the <span class="roman">Bible</span>, approves &#145;rites and ceremonies &hellip; royaltie and magnificence&#146; as &#145;very singular means of the advancement of the glory of the most glorious&#146;, affirms holy days, and stresses the importance of the laity's acting as a spiritual priesthood, speaking and preaching by their good lives and conversation, for &#145;beautifying ourselves sets forth the glory of God&#146;.<br>
<br>Describing himself on the title-page as &#145;Preacher and Minister of Godsword&#146;, Evans does not at that date appear to have been beneficed, but is probably the same man who was vicar of Leckford, Hampshire, in 1627. There is no other indication of his later career or date of death.<br>
<br>Gordon Goodwin<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Vivienne Larminie </div>
