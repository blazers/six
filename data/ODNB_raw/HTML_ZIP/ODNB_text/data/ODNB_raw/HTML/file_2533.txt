<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Blackrie,  Alexander</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1702, <em>d.</em> 1772), <span class="occ">surgeon-apothecary</span>, was baptized on 14 June 1702 at the church of St Nicholas, Aberdeen, the seventh of the eleven children of William Blackrie and his wife, Isobel, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Fordyce. A merchant's son, he was educated at the grammar school and Marischal College, Aberdeen (1718&#150;22), and went on to be surgeon-general on the Indian establishment. Among his friends was an East India Company director, John Hawkesworth of Bromley, Kent; Blackrie moved to the town in the late 1730s and practised there for the rest of his life. He married about 1740; he and his wife, Ann (<i>d</i>. 1749), had a son, William, and a daughter, Elizabeth, who, with a &pound;20,000 dowry, became the first wife of John Scott, the political agent of Warren Hastings.<br>
<br>When in March 1761 Blackrie took Samuel Saunders as his apprentice for seven years with the substantial premium of &pound;84, his practice was described as that of Alexander Blackrie &amp; Co., suggesting an early example of a medical partnership. In October 1763 Blackrie contributed a letter to the <span class="italic">Gentleman's Magazine</span> criticizing Walter Chittick of Bath (<i>d</i>. 1761) for his recently published cure for renal calculi. Blackrie expanded this into <span class="italic">A disquisition on medicines that dissolve the stone. In which Dr. Chittick's secret is considered and discovered</span> (1766). In 1771 a second part appeared, with a &#145;considerably improved&#146; version of the first edition. In 1775 the work was translated into French. Initially Blackrie's <span class="italic">Disquisition</span> attracted only 216 subscribers, but more than 1000 applied for the second edition.<br>
<br>In his will (made on 19 November 1770) Blackrie bequeathed &pound;5 5<i>s</i>. to John Hawkesworth and a similar sum to Thomas Wellings, a London druggist. His daughter, the only other beneficiary, inherited his portrait and the copyright of his <span class="italic">Disquisition</span>. He died in Bromley on 29 May 1772 and was buried on 4 June in the church of St Peter and St Paul, Bromley.<br>
<br>Joan Lane </div>
