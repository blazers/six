<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Weaver,  Robert</span>  (1773&#150;1852), <span class="occ">Congregational minister and antiquary</span>, born at Trowbridge, in Wiltshire, on 23 January 1773, was the son of Richard Weaver, clothier, and his wife, Mary. Aiming at the Congregational ministry, he chose study over the tailoring apprenticeship offered by his father, and entered Rotherham Independent college early in 1794, where he resided with the president, Edward Williams (1750&#150;1813). On 15 February 1802 he became pastor at Mansfield in Nottinghamshire, where he remained until his death; he reconstituted the dispersed congregation in 1805, and twice enlarged the place of worship, in 1812 and in 1829.<br>
<br>Weaver was a keen student of the <span class="roman">Greek Testament</span>, and taught the subject to residential students. The educational theme was pursued in his publications, particularly <span class="italic">Education Based on Scriptural Principles, the True Source of Individual and Social Happiness</span> (1838). Other theological writings included <span class="italic">A Complete View of Puseyism</span> (1843), <span class="italic">Dissent: its Character</span> (1844), and, in the year of the <span class="roman">Ecclesiastical Titles Act</span>, <span class="italic">Popery, Calmly, Closely, and Comprehensively Considered</span>. (1851). He also took an interest in antiquities, and in 1840 published <span class="italic">Monumenta antiqua, or, The Stone Monuments of Antiquity yet Remaining in the British Isles</span>, in which he ascribed the remains of pre-Roman times to Phoenician influence and supported his theory by the particulars of similar Canaanite and Jewish monuments given in the <span class="roman">Bible</span>. Weaver died at Mansfield on 12 October 1852, and was buried in the ground attached to Mansfield Independent Chapel.<br>
<br>E. I. Carlyle<span class="italic">, rev. </span> J. M. V. Quinn </div>
