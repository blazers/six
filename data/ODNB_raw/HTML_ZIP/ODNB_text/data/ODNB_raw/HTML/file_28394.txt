<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Wager,  Lewis</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1562), <span class="occ">playwright</span>, is of unknown origins. He first appears as a Franciscan friar in the Oxford convent, where he became a subdeacon on 21 July 1521. On 24 March 1536 he received a special dispensation to wear the habit of a Franciscan beneath the garb of a secular priest; two years later, the houses of the friars in Oxford were suppressed. Some time before 1538 the Church of England clergyman and playwright <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/28395/', "William Wager (1537/8?&#150;1591)")</script> was born, who may very probably have been Wager's son. On 5 April 1560 Wager became rector of the parish of St James Garlickhythe, London. He died over two years later, and was buried on 18 July 1562 in his parish. His property was assigned to his widow, Elenore.<br>
<br>The only work which is certainly by Wager, <span class="italic">A new enterlude &hellip; of the life and repentaunce of Marie Magdalene, not onlie godlie, learned and fruitefull, but also well furnished with pleasaunt myrth and pastime, very delectable for those which shall hear or reade the same</span>, was entered in the Stationers' register late in 1566 by John Charlewood, who printed it in 1566 and 1567. The play, doubled for four actors, was acted at the universities, and was influenced by Calvin's &#145;Institutes&#146;. The Stationers' register attributed the fragmentary interlude &#145;The Cruel Debtor&#146;, entered about March 1566, to Wager (without forename), but this may be the William Wager who is thought to be his son.<br>
<br>Peter Happ&eacute; </div>
