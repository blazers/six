<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Blomfild <span class="st"> [Blomefield]</span>,  William</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1529&#150;1574), <span class="occ">alchemist and priest</span>, was born in Bury St Edmunds. He was a Benedictine monk and priest at Bury in his youth. In 1529 he was questioned at London regarding certain protestant tenets (which he abjured) although he seems later in life to have gravitated towards Calvinism. In 1543, at which time he was living in London, he was imprisoned; subsequently, in 1546, he was arraigned as a conjuror, but apparently acquitted  (TNA: PRO, SP 1/222, fol. 132). In 1557 he wrote his best-known work, <span class="italic">Bloomfield's Blossoms, or, The Campe of Philosophy</span>, an alchemical work in verse, first published by Elias Ashmole in 1652 in <span class="italic">Theatrum chemicum Britannicum</span> (pp. 305&#150;23); numerous manuscript copies survive. The work consists of eighty-four stanzas in rhyme royal, and concludes with nine couplets.<br>
<br>Blomfild was made vicar of the parish of Sts Simon and Jude at Norwich in April 1569 by Bishop John Parkhurst, but was forced out in less than a year. In a dedicatory epistle to Queen Elizabeth prefixed to another alchemical work, <span class="italic">Blomefyld's Quintaessens, or, The Regiment of Lyfe</span> (written about 1574), he complains of being forced to &#145;forsake my lyvyng&#146; by &#145;obstinat papistes&#146; who &#145;troubled &amp; molested me for religions sake&#146;  (Blomfild, fol. 1). These &#145;papistes&#146; were presumably Anglicans who objected to Blomfild's puritanical tendencies and &#145;godly exercises&#146;  (Schuler, &#145;Blomfild&#146;, 80&#150;85).<br>
<br>Blomfild has frequently been confused with <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/2665/', "Myles Blomefylde (Blomefield) (1525&#150;1603)")</script> who copied the <span class="italic">Quintaessens</span>. The authorship of <span class="italic">Bloomfield's Blossoms</span> has sometimes erroneously been attributed to Myles, but the initial letters of the first verses of each of the three sections of text spell out the name William Blomfild. Myles Blomefylde appended a brief but valuable biographical note to what is now the Cambridge Library copy of <span class="italic">Quintaessens</span> in which he claims William was versed in many languages both ancient and modern, and &#145;in Alchimistri &amp; Distillation he hath not left his lyke in this Nation&#146;. It is probable that the two men were related (both having been born at Bury St Edmunds), as Ashmole suggested in <span class="italic">Theatrum chemicum</span>. Blomfild was well regarded as an alchemist through the seventeenth century, and at least seven brief tracts (besides the <span class="italic">Blossoms</span> and <span class="italic">Quintaessens</span>) are attributed to him. It is not known when he died, although the date 1585 has often been cited.<br>
<br>Lawrence M. Principe </div>
