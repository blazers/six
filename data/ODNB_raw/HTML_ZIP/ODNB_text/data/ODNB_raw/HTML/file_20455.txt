<div class="para">
<span class="headword">O'Brien,  Henry</span>  (1808&#150;1835), <span class="occ">antiquary</span>, was born in co. Kerry. His parentage is unknown. He was educated at Trinity College, Dublin, where he graduated BA in 1831. In 1832 he wrote a dissertation on &#145;The round towers of Ireland&#146; for the prize offered by the Royal Irish Academy. Although he did not win the prize, he was awarded a small gratuity. In 1833 he published a translation of a work by Estengo Villanueva, under the title <span class="italic">Phoenician Ireland</span>, but his introduction and notes were ridiculed as fanciful by a reviewer in the <span class="italic">Gentleman's Magazine</span>. In 1834 he published <span class="italic">The round towers of Ireland, or, The mysteries of freemasonry, of Sabaism, and of Budhism for the first time unveiled</span>. The object of this work (which was an expanded version of his prize essay) was to show that the round towers were Buddhistic remains. The book was condemned as wild and extravagant in the <span class="italic">Gentleman's Magazine</span> and by George Petrie, a fellow Irish antiquary. Thomas Moore wrote a hostile review in the <span class="italic">Edinburgh Review</span>, and, in response O'Brien accused Moore of appropriating his discoveries in his <span class="italic">History of Ireland</span>. F. S. Mahony, known as Father Prout, a warm friend and admirer of O'Brien's, came to his defence in <span class="italic">The Reliques of Father Prout</span> (1836).<br>
<br>Enthusiastic and self-deluding, O'Brien spoke of compiling a dictionary of Celtic, a subject of which he knew nothing. He also announced that he was writing a book on the pyramids of Egypt but it was never published. He died on 28 June 1835 in the house of a friend, at The Hermitage, Hanwell, Middlesex. He was buried in Hanwell churchyard. A fanciful sketch by Daniel Maclise of O'Brien lying on his deathbed appeared in Prout's <span class="italic">Reliques</span>.<br>
<br>W. W. Wroth<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Marie-Louise Legg </div>
