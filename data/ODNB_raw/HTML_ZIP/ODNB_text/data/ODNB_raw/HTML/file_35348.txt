<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Owen,  Robert</span>  (1820&#150;1902), <span class="occ">theologian and antiquary</span>, born at Dolgellau, Merioneth, on 13 May 1820, was the third son of David Owen, surgeon, and Ann, youngest daughter of Hugh Evans of Fronfelen and Esgairgeiliog, near Machynlleth. Educated at Ruthin grammar school, he matriculated from Jesus College, Oxford, on 22 November 1838, graduating BA in 1842 with a third class in classical finals, and proceeding MA in 1845 and BD in 1852. He was fellow of his college from 1845 until 1864, and public examiner in law and modern history in 1859&#150;60.<br>
<br>Although he was ordained by Christopher Bethell, bishop of Bangor, in 1843, and served a curacy until 1845 at Tremeirchion, Owen held no preferment. Coming under the influence of the Tractarians, he maintained an occasional correspondence with Newman, even after the latter seceded to Rome. In 1847 he edited, for the <span class="roman">Anglo-Catholic Library</span>, John Johnson's work <span class="italic">The Unbloody Sacrifice</span>, first published in 1714. He came to believe that establishment and endowment were destructive of the catholic character of the Church of England, and gave his support to Welsh disestablishment.<br>
<br>Owen has a claim to be considered the most erudite of the nineteenth-century fellows of his college. His principal theological works were <span class="italic">An Introduction to the Study of Dogmatic Theology</span> (1858), which displays his patristic learning, and <span class="italic">Institutes of Canon Law</span> (1884), written at the instance of Walter Kerr Hamilton, bishop of Salisbury. His <span class="italic">Sanctorale Catholicum, or, Book of Saints</span> (1880) is notable for its record of Welsh saints, and was the first such work to include &#145;just men&#146; of the Anglican church. <span class="italic">The Kymry: their Origin, History and International Relations</span> draws on a lifelong study of Welsh philology and ecclesiastical history, while his <span class="italic">Pilgrimage to Rome</span> (1883) is a curious annotated record in verse of a long vacation <i>voyage litt&eacute;raire</i>.<br>
<br>In 1864, owing to an allegation of immorality, Owen was obliged to resign his fellowship, and shortly afterwards retired to Vron-y-graig, Barmouth, where he owned a small estate. There he died unmarried on 6 April 1902, and was buried at Llanaber.<br>
<br>D. L. Thomas<span class="italic">, rev. </span> G. Martin Murphy </div>
