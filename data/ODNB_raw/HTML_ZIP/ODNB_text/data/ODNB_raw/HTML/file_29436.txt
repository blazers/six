<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Wilks,  Samuel Charles</span>  (1788&#150;1872), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman and journalist</span>, was born in Churchyard Row, Newington Butts, Surrey, on 11 December 1788, the son of Samuel Charles Wilks (1760&#150;1819) of Newington, Surrey, and his wife, Elizabeth, daughter of Stephen Barber of Walsall. He matriculated from St Edmund Hall, Oxford, on 8 June 1810, aged twenty-one, and graduated BA in 1814 and MA in 1816. While an undergraduate he won in 1811 the premium of the Society for Promoting Christian Knowledge and Church Union in the diocese of St David's for an <span class="italic">Essay on the Signs of Conversion and Unconversion in Ministers of the Church</span>, which was published in 1814. Through this achievement and through his grandfather, also Samuel Wilks, who had risen high in the East India Company, he came to the notice of members of the influential evangelical Clapham Sect. In consequence, following ordination he was appointed lecturer at St Swithin, London, and in 1816 succeeded Zachary Macaulay as editor of the <span class="italic">Christian Observer</span>, the sect's journal. On 23 June 1818 he married Rebecca Emma Langstone Earle (1792&#150;1861), daughter of Joseph Earle. They had at least two sons and two daughters.<br>
<br>In 1817 Wilks dedicated to Hannah More two volumes of <span class="italic">Christian Essays</span>, and in 1835 published a new edition of Lord Teignmouth's <span class="italic">Memoirs of Sir William Jones</span>, to which he prefixed a life of Teignmouth. He continued to edit the <span class="italic">Christian Observer</span> until 1847, when he became rector of Nursling, near Southampton. Wilks was the author of many tracts, essays, and letters of a religious and theological character, mostly reprinted from the <span class="italic">Christian Observer</span>. He also acquired considerable knowledge of contemporary geology, rejecting the natural theology still prevalent at the English universities, as defended in the <span class="roman">Bridgewater Treatises</span>. Wilks died at Nursling on 23 December 1872, in his eighty-fourth year.<br>
<br>[Anon.]<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Stephen Gregory </div>
