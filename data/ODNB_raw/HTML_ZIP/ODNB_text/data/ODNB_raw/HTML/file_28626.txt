<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Walsingham,  John</span>  (<em>supp. </em><em>fl. </em>1326), <span class="occ">supposed Carmelite friar and theologian</span>, is first recorded in Johann Tritheim's <span class="italic">Catalogus scriptorum ecclesiasticorum sive illustrium virorum</span> of 1531. Bale's <span class="italic">Scriptorum illustrium maioris Brytanniae, quam nunc Angliam et Scotiam vocant, Catalogus</span> (published in Basel in 1559), Pits's <span class="italic">Relationum historicarum de rebus Anglicis tomus primus</span> (published at Paris in 1619), Casanate's <span class="italic">Paradisus Carmelici decoris</span> (published at Leiden in 1639), and Leland's <span class="italic">Commentarii de scriptoribus Britannicis</span> (published at Oxford in 1709) all add details. They tell of his education at Norwich and Oxford, his growing fame as a theologian, his election in 1326 to be provincial of the English Carmelites, and his services to the papacy in the cause of doctrinal orthodoxy. The information provided by these authors, as the article on John Walsingham in the <span class="italic">Dictionary of National Biography</span> cautiously indicates, is often uncorroborated. The detail given for his life could easily have been inferred from general information about the Carmelite order, student life at Oxford and Paris, and from theological discussions of papal authority in the relevant era. As the <span class="italic">Dictionary of National Biography</span> article and its sources wisely indicate, moreover, there are great inconsistencies in the various accounts of John Walsingham's life and works. The <span class="italic">Dictionary of National Biography</span>, while not going so far as to question John's existence, nevertheless concluded that &#145;the silence of his contemporaries attests that Walsingham's writings exercised no influence on his age&#146;. <br>
<br>B. M. Xiberta, in his <span class="italic">De scriptoribus scholasticis saeculi XIV ex ordine Carmelitarum</span>, after diligently examining the manuscript evidence given by John Bale on page 378 of his <span class="italic">Catalogus</span>, concluded emphatically that John Walsingham never existed. He found that many of the details of the life of John were details that also fit the life of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/52688/', "Robert Walsingham")</script>. Other items belonged in reality to John Baconthorpe or John Walsham. Even the seemingly more solid manuscript evidence lost its strength before Xiberta's careful eye. Joannes W. had wrongly been inferred to be Joannes Walsingham. MS Digby 41 in the Bodleian Library, Oxford, mentions a Joannes Walssinbham, but the work is a treatise against the Lollards and belongs to a much later time than that of the supposed early fourteenth-century Carmelite. William Bintre (<i>d</i>. 1493) is said to have named a John Walsingham among the defenders of transubstantiation, but since this work is now lost and it is Bale who provides the reference, this information, like all the rest, lacks a guarantee.<br>
<br>S. F. Brown </div>
