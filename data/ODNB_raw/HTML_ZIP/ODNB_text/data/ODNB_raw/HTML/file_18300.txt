<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Massey,  William</span>  (1691&#150;1764?), <span class="occ">writer and translator</span>, born in January 1691 of Quaker parents, learned Latin, Greek, and French at a private grammar school kept by William Thompson at Nottingham, and afterwards took lessons in Hebrew from a clerk of the parish of St Gregory, Norwich. In 1712 he became Latin usher in a boarding-school at Half-Farthing House, Wandsworth, Surrey, kept by Richard Scoryer, after whose death in 1714 he continued in the same employment for about a year under Scoryer's successor, Edward Powell, a noted writing-master and accountant. Afterwards he ran a boarding-school of his own for many years at Wandsworth which proved popular with Massey's fellow Quakers.<br>
<br>Massey's first published work was <span class="italic">Musa paraenetica, or, A Tractate of Christian Epistles, on Sundry Occasions, in Verse</span> (1717, repr. 1746), and he went on to compose further works of a religious or scholarly nature. These include a translation of Ovid's <span class="italic">Fasti</span>, and <span class="italic">Synopsis sacerrima, or, An Epitomy of the Holy Scriptures, in English Verse</span> (1719, repr. 1801). The latter is a whistle stop tour of the <span class="roman">Bible</span>, &#145;chiefly designed for children, to be got by heart when they have learn'd to read&#146;. Also of interest are his <span class="italic">Remarks upon Milton's Paradise Lost, Historical, Geographical, Critical, and Explanatory</span> (1761) and <span class="italic">Corruptae Latinitatis index</span> (1755). The latter is an attack on post-classical Latin neologisms; of <i>flatuosus</i>, for example, he remarks: &#145;This word is not, that I can find, in the Classics. Whatever Occasion some Modern Physicians may have for it, other Writers ought to reject it.&#146; Massey's most substantial work is <span class="italic">The Origin and Progress of Letters, an Essay</span> (1763). The first part deals with the origin of writing and includes discussions of writing materials, printing, and alphabets. The second part, treating of calligraphy, is &#145;a compendious account of the most celebrated English penmen&#146;.<br>
<br>Dr Birch notes that on 24 March 1764 Massey was seized with the dead palsy on his right side, and on 28 August he adds: &#145;I visited him at his house on Cambridge Heath, near Hackney, and found him very ill of the stone, added to the palsy.&#146; Probably he died shortly afterwards.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Sarah Annes Brown </div>
