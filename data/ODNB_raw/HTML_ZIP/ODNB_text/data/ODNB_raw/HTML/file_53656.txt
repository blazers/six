<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Bate,  John</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1626&#150;1635), <span class="occ">writer on mechanics</span>, is an obscure figure; his birth and death dates, parentage, and virtually all personal details remain unknown. Bate's <span class="italic">The Mysteries of Nature and Art</span> (1634) is the first comprehensive illustrated English book on waterworks and hydraulic machinery. It also includes sections on drawing, painting, recipes, and folk remedies, as well as one on fireworks and incendiary devices largely derivative of earlier English and continental works on the subject. A second, greatly expanded edition in 1635 was followed by a third edition, printed in 1654, which has few changes from the second edition.<br>
<br>Judging from the engraved portrait by George Gifford in the second edition, Bate was probably born in the first decade of the century, and probably died before the third edition, possibly as an Irish colonist (a John Bate died there before 1653, leaving four daughters in London, which may explain the third edition the following year). Beyond his book, the only possible hint to his career is John Smith's recommendation of a gunner's scale made by &#145;John Bates&#146;, an instrument maker on Tower Hill  (<span class="italic">Seaman's Grammar</span>, 1626). This supposition is strengthened by Bate's dedicatory poem to his &#145;worthy friend&#146;, the gunner John Babbington, in the latter's book on fireworks  (<span class="italic">Pyrotechnia</span>, 1635). Both this book and Bate's first and second editions were printed by the same printer, Ralph Mabb. Bate's influence extended to the young Isaac Newton, who owned a copy of <span class="italic">Mysteries</span>, copied extracts from Bate's section on drawing, and was probably inspired by his section on waterworks.<br>
<br>Steven A. Walton </div>
