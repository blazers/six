<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Herbert, Lady  Lucy</span>  (1669&#150;1744), <span class="occ">prioress of St Augustine, Bruges, and devotional writer</span>, was born in Redcastle or Powis Castle in Montgomeryshire, the fourth daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/13060/', "William Herbert, first marquess and titular duke of Powis (c.1626&#150;1696)")</script>, politician, and his wife, Elizabeth Somerset (<i>c</i>.1634&#150;1691), younger daughter of Edward Somerset, second marquess of Worcester, and governess to James II's children.<br>
<br>Born into a Roman Catholic family, Lady Lucy decided that she wanted to become a nun, and she spent some time touring English convents on the continent. In February 1692 she visited the priory of the English canonesses of St Augustine at Bruges and, struck by their simplicity, she decided to enter their order. She was admitted the following month and was professed on 1 June 1693, taking the name in religion of Sister Teresa Joseph.<br>
<br>Lady Lucy had a long and successful life in the convent. Having been elected prioress on 5 March 1709 she made many improvements, increasing convent membership, erecting a new chaplain's house, and rebuilding the convent church. In 1738 she also installed a fine marble altar imported from Rome. After the Jacobite rising in 1715 her sisters, Lady Montagu and Lady Nithsdale [<i>see </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/18417/', "Maxwell, Winifred, countess of Nithsdale")</script>], paid her a long visit at the convent.<br>
<br>Lady Lucy published two devotional works, <span class="italic">Several Excellent Methods of Hearing Mass</span> (1722) and <span class="italic">Several Methods and Practises of Devotion</span> (1743), which were reprinted throughout the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. She died in Bruges on 19 January 1744 and was buried, probably in the convent, on 23 January.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Emma Major </div>
