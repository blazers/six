<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Thomas,  Ernest Chester</span>  (1850&#150;1892), <span class="occ">bibliographer and lawyer</span>, the eldest son of John Withiel Thomas, was born on 28 October 1850 at Birkenhead, Cheshire. He was educated at Manchester grammar school, and matriculated on 17 October 1870 from Trinity College, Oxford, where he graduated BA in June 1875. He became a student at Gray's Inn on 7 May 1874, and, having won the Bacon scholarship of the inn in May 1875, published the following year a volume on <span class="italic">Leading Cases in Constitutional Law Briefly Stated</span>. In 1875 and 1876 Thomas studied at the universities of Jena and Bonn, and in 1877 he produced the first volume of a translation of Friedrich Lange's <span class="italic">Geschichte des Materialismus</span>, the second volume of which appeared in 1880, and the third in 1881. In 1878 he published <span class="italic">Leading Statutes Summarised for the Use of Students</span>, and in the same year became joint honorary secretary of the Library Association with Henry Richard Tedder (1850&#150;1924), with whom he collaborated in writing the article &#145;Libraries&#146; in the ninth edition of the <span class="italic">Encyclopaedia Britannica</span> (1882). He held this position for twelve years (1878&#150;1890). He also edited the <span class="italic">Monthly Notes</span> of the Library Association for 1882 and several other volumes, contributing many articles and papers to the association's proceedings and journals. He published in January 1884 the first number of the <span class="italic">Library Chronicle: a Journal of Librarianship and Bibliography</span>, which he carried on at his own expense until 1888. Thomas's most significant work was his edition of the <span class="italic">Philobiblon of Richard de Bury, Bishop of Durham, Treasurer and Chancellor of Edward III</span> (1888), the first really critical text, based upon the early editions and a personal examination of twenty-eight manuscripts of the work; it continues to be used by scholars.<br>
<br>Thomas had at one time a small practice at the bar, but his life was chiefly devoted to literature and librarianship. He was a man of extensive reading, a brilliant talker, a keen debater, and an excellent writer. In the last two or three years of his life he took up business in the City, but his health was undermined by an attack of typhoid fever and he died at Redlands, near Tonbridge, Kent, on 5 February 1892.<br>
<br>H. R. Tedder<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Nilanjana Banerji </div>
