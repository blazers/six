<div class="para">
<span class="headword">O'Conor,  Matthew</span>  (1773&#150;1844), <span class="occ">historian</span>, the sixth son of Denis O'Conor (1732&#150;1804) of Belanagare and Catherine, daughter of Martin Browne of Cloonfad, co. Roscommon, was born in co. Roscommon on 18 September 1773. Like his brother <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/20526/', "Charles O'Conor (1764&#150;1828)")</script>, he was intended for the priesthood, and studied in the English College at Rome, but unlike Charles, who duly entered the church, Matthew O'Conor adopted the legal profession and became eminent therein. He supplemented his practice at the bar by studying and writing upon subjects in connection with Irish history and there is some evidence that he always hankered after a military career. He married Priscilla Forbes in 1804 and they had five children: Denis (1808&#150;1872), of Mount Druid, who was sheriff of his county in 1836; Arthur (<i>d</i>. 1870), of the Palace, Elphin; Matthew, of Mount Allen; and two daughters, about whom nothing is known.<br>
<br>O'Conor was the author of <span class="italic">The history of the Irish Catholics from the settlement in 1691, with a view of the state of Ireland from the invasion of Henry II to the revolution</span> (Dublin, 1813). This work, which was polemical in tone, was based upon some valuable documents in the possession of the writer's grandfather <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/20525/', "Charles O'Conor")</script> of Belanagare, an antiquary and an early activist on behalf of Irish Catholics.<br>
<br>The posthumously published <span class="italic">Military history of the Irish nation; comprising memoirs of the Irish brigade in the service of France, with an appendix of official papers relative to the brigade from the archives at Paris</span> (Dublin, 1845) was part of a larger work contemplated by O'Conor. It goes down only to 1738, and, as a consequence of not being revised by the author, contains errors. But the work is based upon genuine research and on material presented to O'Conor by Colonel Montmorency Morres. It was a valuable contribution to military history, and was critically commended at the time, but was soon superseded by <span class="italic">Irish Brigades in the Service of France</span> (1851), by John Cornelius O'Callaghan, and by later works. O'Conor died on 8 May 1844, probably at his seat, Mount Druid, co. Roscommon.<br>
<br>Thomas Seccombe<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Thomas Bartlett </div>
