<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Mayo,  John</span>  (1761&#150;1818), <span class="occ">physician</span>, son of Thomas Mayo, was born in Hereford on 10 December 1761. He matriculated at Oxford in 1778 from Brasenose College, graduated BA in 1782, was elected fellow of Oriel College on 16 April 1784, and proceeded MA in 1785, MB in 1787, and DM in 1788. He became FRCP on 30 September 1789, and was censor in 1790, 1795, 1804, and 1808; he was Harveian orator in 1795, elect on 10 April 1807, and resigned this last position on 6 October 1813. He served in London as physician to the Foundling Hospital from July 1787 to 1809 and as physician to the Middlesex Hospital from 6 November 1788 until 11 January 1803, and he was also physician-in-ordinary to the princess of Wales. At a meeting of the board of the Middlesex Hospital in December 1802, it was resolved that Mayo, &#145;who had been physician to this hospital with equal advantage to the charity and honour to himself for fourteen years, be solicited to attend the cancer ward as physician extraordinary&#146;  (Wilson, 200). With his first wife, Ann, daughter of Thomas Cock of Tottenham, whom he married on 5 February 1789, he had three sons: <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/18462/', "Thomas Mayo (1790&#150;1871)")</script>, subsequently president of the Royal College of Physicians; John, who took holy orders; and <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/18458/', "Herbert Mayo (1796&#150;1852)")</script>, physiologist. His second wife was Frances Lavinia, daughter of William Fellowes of Ramsey Abbey, MP for Ludlow and Andover.<br>
<br>Mayo habitually divided his time between London and Tunbridge Wells, practising at the latter during the summer months. There he enjoyed &#145;the undisputed lead in medical business and emoluments&#146;  (Munk, 395). After resigning his hospital appointments in 1817, he lived wholly at Tunbridge Wells, where he died on 29 November 1818; he was buried at Speldhurst, Kent. His eldest son published <span class="italic">Remarks on Insanity, Founded on the Practice of J. Mayo, M.D.</span> (1817).<br>
<br>C. H. Mayo<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Michael Bevan </div>
