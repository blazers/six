<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Teulon,  Samuel Sanders</span>  (1812&#150;1873), <span class="occ">architect</span>, was born on 2 March 1812 at Hillside, Crooms Hill, Greenwich, the eldest of the four sons of Samuel Teulon (<i>b</i>. 1785), cabinet-maker (later a surveyor) of Greenwich, and his wife, Louisa Sanders from Rotherhithe. His father's family was of Huguenot origin and he retained an allegiance to this French connection. In 1835 Teulon married Harriet Bayne (<i>d</i>. 1866), with whom he had six sons (two died in infancy and the eldest became principal of Chichester Theological College) and four daughters.<br>
<br>Teulon attended the Royal Academy Schools and was then articled to the architects George Legg and George Porter, before starting his own practice in London in 1838. He spent much of 1841&#150;2 travelling and sketching on the continent with, among others, Ewan Christian and Horace Jones, both later architects of distinction.<br>
<br>Teulon developed a vigorous and idiosyncratic Gothic style: indeed he has come to be regarded as the chief among the rogue architects of the mid-Victorian Gothic revival  (cf. H. R. Goodhart Rendel, <span class="italic">Journal of the Royal Institute of British Architects</span>, 56, 1949, 251). He obtained few commissions for public buildings because, after his early success, he declined to enter the competitions, but he acquired connections with the landed and wealthy, including the royal family, and as a result designed several spectacular country houses. Clients valued his highly individual command of the Gothic language and came to respect him as a reliable man of business. Among the most notable of his houses were: Shadwell, Norfolk (1856&#150;60), for Lady Buxton; Tortworth Court, Gloucestershire (1849&#150;53), built for the second earl of Ducie; Bestwood Park, Nottinghamshire (1862&#150;4), for the tenth duke of St Albans; and Elvetham Hall, Hampshire (1859&#150;62), for Lord Calthorpe. All these are in the most energetic high Victorian style, with a rich multiplicity of towers and gables, and a studiously observed asymmetry. His many churches are equally eclectic, with frequent use of the patterned brickwork and polychromy more often associated with William Butterfield. The most striking surviving examples are: at Burringham, Lincolnshire (1856&#150;7); his vigorous recasting of St Mary's, Ealing (1863&#150;74); Huntley, Gloucestershire (1861&#150;3), with clear echoes of Burges at Cardiff Castle; Hunstanworth, co. Durham (1862&#150;3); Leckhampsted, Berkshire (1859&#150;60); St Mark's, Silvertown, in London's docklands (1862); St Thomas's, Wells (1856&#150;7); as well as Woodchester, Gloucestershire (1862&#150;3), and his two essays in neo-Romanesque at Hawkley, Hampshire (1865), and Oare, Wiltshire (1857&#150;8). His masterpiece was St Stephen's, Rosslyn Hill, Hampstead (1869&#150;75), finished after Teulon's death by Ewan Christian. Teulon's most prominently sited non-ecclesiastical work is the Buxton memorial fountain (1861&#150;6) in Victoria Tower Gardens, Westminster, a richly decorated structure with Gothic arches and a much repaired spire of iron and enamel.<br>
<br>Teulon died on 2 May 1873 in his house, Tensleys, The Green, Hampstead, and was buried under a simple tomb in Highgate cemetery. He left a substantial estate of nearly &pound;30,000.<br>
<br>J. M. Richards<span class="italic">, rev. </span> M. J. Saunders </div>
