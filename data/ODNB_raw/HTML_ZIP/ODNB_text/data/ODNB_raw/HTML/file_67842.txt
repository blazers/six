<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Mitchelson <span class="st"> [Mitchel]</span>,  Margaret</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1638), <span class="occ">prophetess</span>, is said to have been the daughter of a parish minister, possibly James Mitchelson (1585&#150;1625), minister of Yester. She achieved fame in September 1638 through having fits during which she prophesied success for the national covenant and denounced the rival king's covenant. Henry Rollock, an Edinburgh minister, proclaimed his belief that she spoke with the voice of God, and many flocked to hear her words. &#145;Great numbers of all rankes of people wer her dayly hearers; and many of the devouter sexe, the women, prayed, and wept with joy and wonder, to heare her speacke&#146;, while her words were taken down in &#145;brachgraphy&#146; (shorthand) and distributed in manuscript. Opponents suggested that &#145;the Shee Prophetesse&#146; was mad or an impostor  (Gordon, 1.131&#150;2), while Gilbert Burnet took her to be &#145;a devout person &hellip; troubled with Vapours&#146; whose illness caused her to &#145;speak as one transported&#146;  (Burnet, 83). On 13 September Rollock took Archibald Johnston of Wariston to hear the &#145;poor damsel&#146;, and he was much impressed by &#145;the wonderful work&#146; of one whose &#145;saule was full to the brim and to the overflouing of the most sensible conceptions and expressions&#146;. On 2 October Johnston recorded that she was &#145;transported in heavinly raptures&#146; to the astonishment of &#145;many thousand&#146; including doubting nobles who were converted to the cause of the covenant  (<span class="italic">Diary of Sir Archibald Johnston</span>, 385, 393). &#145;But this blazing starr quickly vanished, and her prophecyes wer never printed&#146;  (Gordon, 1.132). After the end of 1638 Mitchelson vanished back into obscurity, but her brief career as a prophetess had been influential in stirring up zeal for the covenanters.<br>
<br>David Stevenson </div>
