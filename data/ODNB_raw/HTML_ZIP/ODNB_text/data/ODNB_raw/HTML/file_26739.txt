<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Stubbs,  Thomas</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1343&#150;1381), <span class="occ">Dominican friar and supposed chronicler</span>, is described by John Bale as the author of the continuation of the chronicle of the archbishops of York known as <span class="italic">Chronica pontificum ecclesiae Eboracensis</span>; but there is no contemporary ascription to him, nor is he mentioned in any of the extant manuscripts, and the authorship remains undecided. The continuation in question extends from the time of Henry Murdac (1147&#150;53) to the pontificates of William Zouche (<i>d</i>. 1352) and John Thoresby (<i>d</i>. 1373), and it is possible, from more detailed comments made in the chronicle on archbishops Melton (1317&#150;40) and Zouche, that the writer had personal knowledge of them, or at least knew members of their entourage. An edition of the chronicle by James Raine was published in the <span class="roman">Rolls Series</span> in 1886.<br>
<br>The available information about Stubbs is very slight. Bale states that he was a Yorkshireman, but so far research has failed to produce any confirmatory evidence. The first detail there is records his ordination to the priesthood in Durham Cathedral on 20 December 1343 by Richard, titular bishop Bisaciensis, acting as the suffragan of the bishop of Durham. There were two Dominican convents in the diocese of Durham, one at Bamburgh, the other at Newcastle, and presumably Stubbs was a member of one of these convents at the time of his ordination. Earlier confusion over a Franciscan friar of the same name is the result of a misreading of the Durham ordination register by the editor of the chronicle continuation. Stubbs was in the Oxford Dominican convent by 1354&#150;5, but a reference to him as being a member of the order's convent in York in the late 1350s is given without any source  (<span class="italic">VCH Yorkshire</span>, 3.285) and more recent research on the mendicants in the York diocese has failed to locate him there. The latest reference found to him is in early 1381, when he was appointed one of the nine executors of the will of Thomas Hatfield, bishop of Durham (1345&#150;81), in a list headed by his fellow Dominican John Gilbert, bishop of Hereford (1375&#150;89). In the bishop's will Stubbs is described as <i>sacrae paginae professor</i>, showing that he had attained the degree of doctor of theology. Bale, John Leland, and John Pits ascribe to Stubbs the following theological works, in addition to the continuation of the York chronicle: <span class="italic">Statutum contra impugnantes ecclesiasticas constitutiones</span>; <span class="italic">De stipendiis praedicatoribus verbi debitis</span>; <span class="italic">De perfectione vitae solitariae</span>; <span class="italic">De arte moriendi</span>; <span class="italic">Meditationes quaedam pro consolatione contemplativorum</span>; <span class="italic">In revelaciones Brigidae</span>; <span class="italic">De misericordia Dei</span>; <span class="italic">Super cantica canticorum</span>; <span class="italic">Sermones de sanctis</span>; <span class="italic">Sermones de tempore</span>; <span class="italic">Officium completum cum missa de nomine Jesu</span>; <span class="italic">Officium de beata Anna</span>; <span class="italic">De poenis peregrinationis huius vitae</span>. None of these can now be identified.<br>
<br>David M. Smith </div>
