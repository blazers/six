<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Stopford,  James</span>  (<em>c.</em>1697&#150;1759), <span class="occ">Church of Ireland bishop of Cloyne</span>, was born in London, the son of Joseph Stopford, army officer. He entered Trinity College, Dublin, in 1710, became a scholar in 1713, and graduated BA in 1715. On 25 March 1717 he was elected a fellow of Trinity, and proceeded MA in the following year. In 1727 he resigned his fellowship on being appointed vicar of Finglas, near Dublin, by Lord Carteret, the lord lieutenant. On 16 December of the same year he married Anne, second daughter of James Stopford of Tara Hill, co. Meath, and sister of James, first earl of Courtown; among the couple's children were three sons, William, James, and Joseph. On 11 July 1730 Stopford was installed provost of Tuam, co. Galway, on 10 July 1736 he was collated archdeacon of Killaloe, and on 8 January 1748 he was instituted dean of Kilmacduagh. He held these preferments until February 1753, when he was appointed bishop of Cloyne.<br>
<br>Stopford was a close friend of Jonathan Swift, who on one occasion described him as &#145;the most valuable young man of this kingdom&#146;  (<span class="italic">Correspondence</span>, 3.22). Swift assisted in Stopford's promotion within the church and appointed him one of his executors, bequeathing him a Van Dyck portrait of Charles I, which Stopford himself had originally given to Swift. Stopford died on 23 August 1759 and was buried at St Anne's Church, Dublin, where a tablet to his memory was erected on the outside of the church's south wall.<br>
<br>E. I. Carlyle<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Philip Carter </div>
