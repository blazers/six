<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hopkins,  John</span>  (<em>b. </em>1675), <span class="occ">poet</span>, was born on 1 January 1675, the second son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/13746/', "Ezekiel Hopkins (1634&#150;1690)")</script>, bishop of Derry, and younger brother of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/13744/', "Charles Hopkins")</script>. A John Hopkins was admitted sizar at Jesus College, Cambridge, where he graduated BA in 1693&#150;4, and proceeded MA in 1698. This John Hopkins was ordained deacon at Norwich in March 1698, and priest at York in September that year.<br>
<br>In 1698 Hopkins published two Pindaric poems: <span class="italic">The triumphs of peace, or, The glories of Nassaw &hellip; written at the time of his grace the duke of Ormond's entrance into Dublin</span>, and <span class="italic">The Victory of Death, or, The Fall of Beauty</span>, on the death of Lady Cutts. In the following year he issued <span class="italic">Milton's &#145;Paradise Lost&#146; imitated in rhyme, in the fourth sixth and ninth Books: containing the primitive loves, the battel of the angels, the fall of man</span>, apologizing in the preface for his audacity on the ground that &#145;<i>when I did it, I did not so well Percieve the Majesty and Noble air of</i> Mr. Milton's <i>style as now I do</i>&#146;  (J. Hopkins, <span class="italic">Milton's &#145;Paradise Lost&#146;</span>, 1699, 1).<br>
<br>Hopkins's last work was a collection of indifferent love-verses and translations from Ovid, <span class="italic">Amasia, or, The Works of the Muses</span> (1700), with an &#145;Epistle Dedicatory&#146; to the duchess of Grafton, and dedications of particular sections to various persons of distinction. Referring in the preface to his brother's renderings of Ovid (&#145;very well perform'd&#146;) he observes, &#145;mine were written in an other Kingdom before I knew of his&#146;  (sig. <span class="smallcap">A</span>4). The author's portrait, engraved by F. H. Van Hove and subscribed with his assumed name, Sylvius, is prefixed to this work.<br>
<br>A. H. Bullen<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Freya Johnston </div>
