<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Truefitt,  George</span>  (1824&#150;1902), <span class="occ">architect</span>, was born in Manchester and articled in London from 1839 to 1844 to the scholarly Gothic revivalist L. N. Cottingham (1787&#150;1847). He worked briefly in the offices of Sancton Wood (1815&#150;1886) and Harvey Eginton (1809&#150;1849) of Worcester before establishing himself in independent practice in London <i>c</i>.1846. After a walking tour in France and Germany he published <span class="italic">Architectural Sketches on the Continent</span> (1847), which both responded to a growing interest in European Gothic and urged the development of the revival beyond medieval precedents. Shrewdly, it was dedicated to A. J. Beresford Hope (1820&#150;1887), chairman of the Ecclesiological Society, which Truefitt joined in 1848. His Gothic design for London's Army and Navy Club, though unsuccessful in competition, was well publicized and particularly impressed the banker William Cunliffe Brooks (1819&#150;1900), who became his principal patron. Pursuing ecclesiastical Gothic, Truefitt's <span class="italic">Designs for Country Churches</span> (1850) welcomed the revival's emergence from antiquarian &#145;copyism&#146; into &#145;original conception&#146;  (&#145;Preface&#146;), and provided twenty imaginary designs, the geometrical simplification and compositional boldness of which&#151;praised by <span class="italic">The Ecclesiologist</span> for their &#145;vigour and spirit&#146;  (vol. 11, 1850, 48)&#151;anticipated much of the aesthetic of high Victorian Gothic.<br>
<br>However, Truefitt, an evangelical, was unsympathetic to the high-church arrangements demanded by ecclesiology's leaders, and his career developed on secular rather than ecclesiastical lines. His first major job, the now demolished Newbury Savings Bank (1848&#150;9), was followed by various commissions: Bridgnorth cemetery (1854), a school in Neath (1857), estate buildings for Cunliffe Brooks, and&#151;probably&#151;work on Brooks's Manchester house, Barlow Hall. On 15 January 1856 his son George Haywood Truefitt was baptized at the Old Church, St Pancras, London; his mother's name was there recorded as Mary. When he was elected FRIBA in 1860, Truefitt was engaged in developing the suburban Tufnell Park estate, London, to which he was surveyor from 1865. Here he designed robust, stylistically eclectic villas, including his own house in Middleton Road, and his most important church, St George's (1866&#150;8), where bare Gothic detailing consorts with a circular nave and terracotta-clad iron columns. Its aesthetic toughness recurs in the evangelical citadel of St George's, Worthing (1868), and his later churches at Bromley, Kent (1879), and Davyhulme, Lancashire (1889). Truefitt's stylistic inventiveness and resourceful use of materials are most evident in the three banks he designed in 1869&#150;70 for Cunliffe, Brooks &amp; Co.: a classical and Gothic mix for Manchester, a Romanesque&#150;Renaissance medley at Blackburn, and elaborate timber-framed revival in Altrincham, Cheshire&#151;all strikingly composed and idiosyncratically ornamented. In 1890 Truefitt's London practice was taken over by his son, though he continued as architect to Brooks's Glen Tana estate in Aberdeenshire.<br>
<br>Truefitt's output never rivalled those of the great Victorian architects, but he contributed significantly to high Victorian eclecticism, and his artistic independence resulted in designs of considerable individuality across a wide range of building types. He died at his home, the Old House, Worthing, on 11 August 1902, leaving a widow, Constance, a son, Lieutenant-Colonel Lewis Haywood, and two daughters, Mary Louisa Warleigh and Connie Georgie Truefitt.<br>
<br>Chris Brooks </div>
