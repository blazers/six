<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ker,  John</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1741), <span class="occ">Latin poet</span>, was born at Dunblane, Perthshire. He was for a time schoolmaster at Crieff, Perthshire, and on 25 March 1713 became a master at the Royal High School, Edinburgh. He composed memorial verses on the death of Archibald Pitcairne (1652&#150;1713), the Latin poet and most important Jacobite literary figure in contemporary Scotland. In 1717 Ker became professor of Greek at King's College, Aberdeen, the first special teacher of the subject there.<br>
<br>In 1725 Ker published in Edinburgh <span class="italic">Donaides, sive, Musarum Aberdonensium de eximia J. Fraseri munificentia carmen eucharisticum</span>, a poem celebrating the distinguished dons and alumni of Aberdeen University. Further memorial verses include those on the Latin lyricist Sir William Scott of Thirlestane (1624?&#150;1725) and, for the university, <span class="italic">Fraserides, sive, Funebris oratio et elegia in laudem J. Fraserii</span>, in 1731. In 1727 appeared his paraphrase of the <span class="roman">Song of Solomon</span>, <span class="italic">Cantici Solomonis: prior vario carminum genere, altera Sapphicis versibus prescripta &hellip; auctore Joanne Kerro</span>. The volume also contained Arthur Johnson's paraphrase of the same biblical text, dedicated to Charles Edward Stuart as king of Great Britain. Both works were reprinted in 1739, together with a number of original Latin poems, some with similar dedications, by Patrick Adamson, William Barclay, Robert Boyd, George Eglisham, Arthur Johnston, John Johnston, and William Hogg in an anthology, <span class="italic">Poetarum Scotorum musae sacrae</span>, edited by William Lauder. Ker's strong Jacobite sympathies seem to have been no barrier to higher academic appointments, for on 2 October 1734 he succeeded Adam Watt as professor of Latin at Edinburgh University. Here he studied law and associated again with friends of high school days, enjoying both popularity and the great respect of his students. He also taught the humanity course and was praised by Alexander Carlyle as &#145;very much master of his business&#146;  (<span class="italic">Autobiography</span>, ed. Burton, 31).<br>
<br>Ker contributed greatly to the growth of antiquarianism so characteristic of the eighteenth century and had a distinct influence in reviving exact Latin scholarship in Scotland. The Latin ballad on the battle of Killiecrankie versified in English by Walter Scott is most probably Ker's. He died in Edinburgh on 19 November 1741.<br>
<br>T. W. Bayne<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Richard Riddell </div>
