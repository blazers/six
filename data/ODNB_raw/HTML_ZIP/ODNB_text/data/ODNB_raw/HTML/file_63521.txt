<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Fortnum <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>King]</span>,  Sophia</span>  (<em>b. </em>1781/2, <em>d.</em> in or after 1805), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was the daughter of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/67336/', "John King (c.1753&#150;1824)")</script>, moneylender and radical writer known in London society as Jew King, and his wife, Sara, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Lara. In 1798 Sophia, with her sister Charlotte [<i>see </i><script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/63519/', "Byrne, Charlotte (1782?&#150;1825)")</script>], dedicated to him <span class="italic">Trifles of Helicon</span>, to show &#145;the education you have afforded us has not been totally lost&#146;. Later in the year she published a melodramatic novel, <span class="italic">Waldorf, or, The Dangers of Philosophy</span>, in which sceptical doctrines propel all the main characters through unauthorized sexual behaviour to madness and death.<br>
<br>In 1799 <span class="italic">Cordelia, or, A Romance of Real Life</span> was published at the Minerva Press; <span class="italic">The Victim of Friendship</span>, &#145;a German romance&#146;, followed in 1800. <span class="italic">The Fatal Secret, or, Unknown Warrior</span> (1801) again links sex and death in the figure of a destructive demon lover. In the preface Sophia King describes herself as a &#145;weak sapling of nineteen years growth&#146; and complains about the &#145;extortionate&#146; mechanics of the book trade (the book was printed &#145;for the author&#146;). She also mocks her own love of the Gothic formulae developed by Ann Radcliffe. On 19 July 1801 she married Charles Fortnum. As Sappho she wrote newspaper verse, some of which was republished in <span class="italic">Poems, Legendary, Pathetic and Descriptive</span> (1804) alongside pieces from <span class="italic">Trifles of Helicon</span>. In the preface she celebrates Matthew Lewis's <span class="italic">Tales of Wonder</span> and espouses &#145;the fairy world of ghosts, and of magic&#146; where &#145;the fantastic imagination roves unshackled&#146;. The volume was dedicated to a Miss Sawbridge. There were sixty-five subscribers, including many aristocrats; John Penn, the MP to whom her sister dedicated her <span class="italic">Hours of Solitude</span> (1805), subscribed &pound;20. Sophia Fortnum's last known work was <span class="italic">The Adventures of Victor Allen</span> (1805). Nothing is known of her after this date.<br>
<br>Paul Baines </div>
