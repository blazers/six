<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Mardeley,  John</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1548&#150;1558), <span class="occ">government official and author</span>, wrote a series of polemical protestant poems, including <span class="italic">A shorte resytal of certayne holy doctours whych proveth that the naturall body of Christ is not conteyned in the sacrament of the Lordes supper but fyguratyvely</span>, partly written in Skeltonic metre  (Collier, 1.515&#150;16). It was published by Thomas Raynolde probably in 1548 and, according to W. T. Lowndes, was &#145;written in answer to John Aungell's <span class="italic">Agreement of the Holy Fathers and Doctors</span>&#146;  (Lowndes, 3.1471).<br>
<br>Mardeley's critique of transubstantiation was continued in a prose treatise, <span class="italic">A Declaration of the Power of Gods Worde Concerning the Holy Supper of the Lord</span>, published by Raynolde in 1548 and dedicated to Protector Somerset. It attacked those bishops resistant to Edward VI's religious reforms and who sought &#145;His godly reformacion, utterly to delay&#146;  (sig. A1<i>v</i>). Mardeley was committed to the idea of a reformed church, and a further prose treatise, <span class="italic">A Necessary Instruction for All Courteous Men</span>, published by Raynolde probably in 1548, provided opportunity for a caustic aside on how &#145;gready al kinde of men&#146; &#145;be in these dayes, as wel the clergye as the layitie&#146;  (sig. A2<i>r</i>).<br>
<br>Under Edward VI, Mardeley was clerk of the mint. Unsurprisingly, considering his strong affiliations to protestantism, he published nothing during the reign of Mary, but greeted the accession of Elizabeth in 1558 with an extended poetic treatise on &#145;The supper of the Lord&#146; presented to the queen in manuscript form  (BL, MS 17 B.xxxvii). In this work Mardeley celebrated England's release from Marian persecutions and urged the new monarch to &#145;Redresse the abuses of the spiritualitie&#146;  (fol. 17<i>v</i>). As a poet, Mardeley tapped into vernacular traditions of Skeltonic metre and the idioms of Wyatt's epistolary satires.<br>
<br>Bale credits Mardeley with various works (now lost) in verse and prose, including a book &#145;On the ingratitude of the Scots&#146;, verse translation of twenty-four psalms, and &#145;The complaint of the English republic&#146;, probably the anonymous <span class="italic">A Ruful Complaynt of the Publyke Weale to Englande</span>, printed between 1547 and 1553?, owing to which work, according to Bale, Mardeley &#145;only evaded death with great difficulty&#146;  (Bale, <span class="italic">Cat.</span>, 2.106&#150;7). Some verse translations in the psalter of 1562 signed M and attributed by Haslewood to Mardeley are by John Marckant.<br>
<br>Mardeley may have been the John Mardeley of the parish of St Katharine Cree, London, whose wife, Eleanor, was granted administration of his estate on 25 January 1560.<br>
<br>Cathy Shrank </div>
