<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Vaughan,  Thomas</span>  (<em>fl. </em>1772&#150;1820), <span class="occ">playwright</span>, was the son of a lawyer, and was educated in the same profession. He obtained the post of clerk to the commission of peace of the city of Westminster, and about 1782 became captain of a company of the Westminster Volunteers. He had a great love of the stage, and devoted much of his leisure time to dramatic literature. In 1772 he wrote a series of essays in the <span class="italic">Morning Post</span> on the Richmond Theatre. In 1776 he produced a farce entitled <span class="italic">Love's Metamorphoses</span>, which was acted for Mrs Wrighten's benefit at Drury Lane on 15 April. It was afterwards rejected by John Philip Kemble, manager of Drury Lane, in 1789, and by George Colman the younger, manager of the Haymarket, in 1791. Vaughan published it in 1791, under the title <span class="italic">Love's Vagaries</span>, with a dedication to the rejectors. In 1776 he published another farce, entitled <span class="italic">The Hotel, or, The Double Valet</span>, which appeared at Drury Lane on 21 November. His next dramatic venture was <span class="italic">Deception</span>, a political comedy, which was acted at Drury Lane on 28 September 1784. None of Vaughan's plays possessed much merit, and they met with no success. He was the author of a novel entitled <span class="italic">Fashionable Follies</span> (1782), which had some vogue; one reviewer called it &#145;The best of his writings&#146;  (<span class="italic">European Magazine</span>, 58). He republished it in 1810 with considerable additions, and with a dedication to Colman, with whom he had formerly quarrelled, and who bestowed on him the nickname of Dapper. Though much earlier, <span class="italic">The Retort</span> (1761), a reply to Churchill's <span class="italic">Rosciad</span>, which contained an allusion to Vaughan as &#145;Dapper&#146;, is also assigned to him (Churchill, 31n.; <span class="italic">New Cambridge Bibliography of English Literature</span>, 2.859). He was a friend of Sheridan, and is said to have been the original of Dangle in <span class="italic">The Critic</span>.<br>
<br>E. I. Carlyle<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Grant P. Cerny </div>
