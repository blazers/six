<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Squire,  William</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1677), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman</span>, was born in Yorkshire, the son of a proctor in the archbishop of York's court. He entered Trinity Hall, Cambridge, in 1647. After graduating BA in 1650 or 1651 he &#145;went thence for preferment&#146; to Oxford, where he entered Brasenose College as a batteler, was incorporated on either 16 June 1652 or 16 January 1653, and proceeded MA on 25 April 1653  (Wood, <span class="italic">Ath. Oxon.</span>, 3.1115). Soon afterwards he became a chaplain at All Souls College and a fellow of University College. Through the influence of Gilbert Sheldon, archbishop of Canterbury, Squire was presented, in 1675, to the rectory of Rolleston, Derbyshire, and on 23 July of the same year was instituted to the prebend of Hansacre, in Lichfield Cathedral.<br>
<br>Squire wrote two books against Roman Catholicism, published in 1672 and 1674. Among the well-worn arguments deployed in them the following may be singled out as reflecting something of the pragmatic and questioning spirit which had emerged in recent times:<blockquote>their pretended traditions are nothing to us, they may be records of their own making &hellip; they bring us no other proof than the testimony of men who are parties themselves: we have learnt to call no man on earth, master, or take that for gospel, which any sect, or party of men under what name soever impose on us. Let them prove what they require us to believe. (W. Squire, <span class="italic">Some More Considerations Proving the Unreasonableness of the Romanists</span>, 1674, sig. A3)</blockquote>William Squire died at Rolleston at the beginning of September 1677, and was buried on 4 September in the chancel of the parish church under a black marble stone.<br>
<br>Stephen Wright </div>
