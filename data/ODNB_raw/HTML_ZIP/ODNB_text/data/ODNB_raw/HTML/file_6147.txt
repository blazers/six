<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Cook,  Richard</span>  (1784&#150;1857), <span class="occ">history painter</span>, was born in London and was baptized on 17 October 1784 at St Giles Cripplegate, London, the son of Richard Cook (<i>fl</i>. 1785&#150;1804), painter, and Jane, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Tuck whom he married at St Leonard, Shoreditch, on 22 July 1784. Farington recorded in his <span class="italic">Diary</span> for 18 November 1816 that, according to the sculptor John Rossi, &#145;Cooke [<i>sic</i>] the new Associate, is a native of Gloucestershire. His father, who was a farmer, has retired from business, having sufficient to live upon&#146; would appear to be inaccurate  (Farington, <span class="italic">Diary</span> 14, 4926). The work of both Cooks has been &#145;inextricably confused&#146;  (Stewart and Cutten, 142). He obtained admission into the schools of the Royal Academy when sixteen years of age, and received the Society of Arts gold medal in 1832. He first exhibited at the Royal Academy <span class="italic">A Landscape</span>, in 1808. At that period he resided at 41 North Audley Street, Grosvenor Square; in the same year he sent to the British Institution <span class="italic">The Agony of Christ</span> and <span class="italic">Hector Reproving Paris</span>. In 1814 he exhibited at the Royal Academy a portrait of G. F. Cooke, and <span class="italic">Acis and Galatea</span>, afterwards engraved by W. Taylor. He then lived at 12 Greek Street, Soho Square. In 1816, being elected an associate of the Royal Academy, he sent from 50 Upper Marylebone Street five pictures: four derived from Scott's <span class="italic">Lady of the Lake</span>, and <span class="italic">Ceres, disconsolate for the loss of Proserpine, rejects the solicitation of Iris, sent to her by Jupiter</span>. In 1822 Cook was elected Royal Academician, and from that time forward he almost seems to have relinquished his profession. He married a lady of fortune, which enabled him to entertain liberally his brother artists. He died in Cumberland Place, Hyde Park, London, on 11 March 1857. A sale of his pictures, sketches, and prints at Christie and Manson and Woods on 1 June 1857 included Thomas Stothard's <span class="italic">George III and Queen, Sitting, Surrounded by a Family of Boys and Girls</span>. The British Museum preserves several of Cook's drawings, chiefly studies for book illustrations, executed in 1806; a large study for the <span class="italic">Lady of the Lake</span>; a tinted pencil portrait of Mrs Cook, and a folio volume containing numerous carefully drawn figures, furniture, and arms, from the eighth to the fifteenth century. In addition to illustrating several classics published by John Sharpe, Cook provided illustrations to works by F&eacute;nelon, Shakespeare, Homer, Goldsmith, Ovid, Dryden, and Tasso. Further examples of Cook's work are in Swansea Art Gallery; one of his sketchbooks is in the Mitchell Library, Glasgow.<br>
<br>L. A. Fagan<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Annette Peach </div>
