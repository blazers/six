<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Martin,  Matthew</span>  (1748&#150;1838), <span class="occ">natural historian and philanthropist</span>, born in Somerset, was at first engaged in trade at Exeter. He married on 9 July 1773; his wife, Penelope, died on 9 August 1827. An early interest in natural history led Martin to publish at Exeter <span class="italic">The Aurelian's Vade-Mecum</span> (1785), listing those plants on which caterpillars fed, &#145;aurelian&#146; being the Latin term for a chrysalis, and <span class="italic">Observations on Marine Vermes, Insects, &amp;c</span> (1786). He had been a member of the Bath Philosophical Society, but moved to London and was living at Half Moon Street, Piccadilly, in 1794 when he was elected fellow of the Royal Society.<br>
<br>In 1796 Martin began inquiring into the circumstances of beggars in the metropolis, encouraging several of these unfortunates to explain their circumstances to him. He became secretary to the Society for Bettering the Condition and Improving the Comforts of the Poor, founded in December 1796, and lived above its offices at 3 Parliament Street. Martin proposed a systematic inquiry into the nature and extent of begging in London, and in 1800 obtained a grant of &pound;1000 from the Treasury. His report was published in 1803 as <span class="italic">Letter to Lord Pelham on the State of Mendicity in the Metropolis</span> and reissued by the society in 1811. The institution in January 1805 of the Bath Society for the Investigation and Relief of Occasional Distress was partly due to his efforts. Martin appears to have undertaken a further inquiry, supported partly by a government grant and partly by contributions raised by his &#145;Appeal to public benevolence for the relief of beggars&#146;.<br>
<br>Probably about 1826 Martin obtained the post of secretary to a commission for adjusting San Domingo claims, set up to deal with the claims of merchants disadvantaged by the independence of Haiti from France, and he settled in a house adjoining Poets' Corner, near Westminster Abbey. He then retired to Blackwater Park, Charlton, Kent, which was probably where he died, on 20 November 1838, survived by three sons and two daughters.<br>
<br>[Anon.]<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Anita McConnell </div>
