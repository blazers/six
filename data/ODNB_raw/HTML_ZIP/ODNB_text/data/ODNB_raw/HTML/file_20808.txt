<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Ord,  John Walker</span>  (1811&#150;1853), <span class="occ">poet and journalist</span>, was born at Guisborough, Yorkshire, on 5 March 1811, the son of Richard Ord, principal partner in the firm of Richard Ord &amp; Son, tanners and leather merchants of that place, and his wife, Ann, <i>n&eacute;e</i> Ovington. He entered the University of Edinburgh, and, being intended for the medical profession, was apprenticed to Robert Knox, the lecturer on anatomy who employed the body snatchers Burke and Hare. While at Edinburgh he was friendly with John Wilson, the essayist, and James Hogg, the &#145;Ettrick Shepherd&#146;. Eventually he abandoned the study of medicine, and, moving to London in 1834, he started, two years later, the <span class="italic">Metropolitan Conservative Journal</span>, a paper which was afterwards merged with <span class="italic">The Britannia</span>. His literary labours brought him into contact with Thomas Campbell, Sheridan Knowles, Douglas Jerrold, the countess of Blessington, and other eminent literary figures of the day.<br>
<br>Ord's first publication was <span class="italic">England: a Historical Poem</span> (2 vols., 1834&#150;5). This was followed by <span class="italic">Remarks on the sympathetic condition existing between the body and the mind, especially during disease</span> (1836), forming a supplement to the <span class="italic">Metropolitan Literary Journal</span>; <span class="italic">The Bard, and Minor Poems</span> (1841); <span class="italic">Rural Sketches and Poems, Chiefly Relating to Cleveland</span> (1845); and <span class="italic">The History and Antiquities of Cleveland</span> (1846). The varied nature of his interests is also reflected in the positions he held, including member of the council of the British Archaeological Association, fellow of the Genealogical Society of London, and president of the Charing Cross Medical and Scientific Society. In his later years Ord also edited the <span class="italic">Northern Times</span> and a poem by Thomas Pierson, <span class="italic">Roseberry Topping</span>, published in 1847. He retired to his native county and began work on <span class="italic">The Bible Oracles</span>, which was left unfinished when he died at Guisborough on 29 August 1853. He was buried in the churchyard at Guisborough.<br>
<br>Thompson Cooper<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Nilanjana Banerji </div>
