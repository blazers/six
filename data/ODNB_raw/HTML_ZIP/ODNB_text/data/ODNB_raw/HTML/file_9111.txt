<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Falconer,  John</span>  (<em>d.</em> 1560), <span class="occ">botanist</span>, was the first English person known to possess a herbarium. His antecedents are unknown: a suggestion  (Garrett, 152) that he came from Rutland lacks confirmation. Falconer was tutor in William Lucy's household at Charlecote, Warwickshire, until 1545, when his friend John Foxe succeeded him there. He was at Ferrara, and a fellow student of William Turner at Bologna at some time between 1540 and 1547. Back in England, he was ordained deacon (1550) but fled to Strasbourg in 1554; by the autumn of 1556 he had moved to Frankfurt am Main, with property valued at 500 florins.<br>
<br>Herbaria had appeared, as valuable complements to accurate botanical drawings, at least by 1551, when Luca Ghini of Bologna possessed one. &#145;Amatus Lusitanus&#146; (Jo&atilde;o Rodrigues de Castelo Branco), who had received specimens&#151;including <i>Symphyto petraeo</i>, common comfrey&#151;from Falconer, referred to the latter's collection of dried plants using &#145;codici cuidam consitas ac agglutinatas&#146; (&#145;special sheets sewn and glued&#146;; 337): &#145;maister Falkonners boke&#146; contained an Italian specimen of sea trefoil (sea milkwort)  (Turner, <span class="italic">Seconde Parte</span>, fol. 11<i>v</i>). Falconer had discovered rose parsley (pasqueflower, recorded by 1551) and his collection included <i>Faba aegypta</i> (a <i>Vicia narbonensis</i>), <i>Elaphobosco</i> (both 1554), and a species of <i>Stachys</i> (woundwort; 1562).<br>
<br>Seemingly, Falconer never married, and his will, made on 16 April 1558, mentions no relatives. He left to John Hales &#145;my Avicenna with the index&#146; (presumably an edition of <span class="italic">The Canon of Medicine</span>, and Palamedes's <span class="italic">Index</span>); to &#145;Mr Railton&#146;, <span class="italic">Imagines quadrupedum et avium</span> (perhaps Conrad Gesner's two books of <span class="italic">Icones</span>); and to &#145;Mr Dr Turner, my herball [i.e. herbarium]&#146;. Following some small bequests, including one of 40 shillings to John Foxe in Basel, the residue of his goods, &#145;here on this side the seas and in England&#146; was left to his executrix, Elizabeth Lucy. If she died before he had &#145;finished this my journey&#146;, her brother Thomas was to officiate instead. Falconer was then in Frankfurt, with John Hales and Gregory Railton, staying in Thomas Watts's house.<br>
<br>John Falconer, &#145;phisicyon&#146; (presumed, perhaps, from his knowledge of simples), was buried in St Stephen's Church, Coleman Street, London, on 11 May 1560; on 22 May, John Jewel wrote to P. M. Vermigli, saying &#145;Our friend Falconer is dead&#146;  (<span class="italic">Zurich Letters</span> 1.79). Probate was granted to Elizabeth Lucy on 24 May 1560. Falconer's colleagues testified to his learning, and the <i>Falconera</i> Salisb. (perhaps <i>Albuca</i> in the Hyacinthaceae) was apparently named after him.<br>
<br>John Bennell </div>
