<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Lyne,  Richard</span>  (<em>fl. </em><em>c.</em>1570&#150;<em>c.</em>1600), <span class="occ">painter and engraver</span>, was one of the earliest English artists whose works have been preserved. Vertue noted that he was one of the engravers employed by Matthew Parker, archbishop of Canterbury, and worked for him, in company with Remigius Hogenberg, at Cambridge and at Lambeth Palace. John Ingamells considered that the portrait of Parker at Lambeth, of which a small engraving on copper was made by Hogenberg, is:<blockquote>more likely to derive from the print which was intended for Parker's <span class="italic">De Antiquitate Britannicae ecclesi&aelig;</span> 1572, which was based on the miniature portrait stuck in the Corpus Christi &#145;Black Book of Corpus Christi College&#146; (MS 582, the Parker Library, Corpus Christi College, Cambridge). (Ingamells, 317)</blockquote>Lyne drew and engraved at Parker's expense a very interesting map of the University of Cambridge for John Caius's <span class="italic">Historia Cantabrigiensis</span> (1574). He also painted and engraved in that year a large genealogical chart of the history of Great Britain (&#145;of this Hogenberg. is graved. a small. plate&#146;  (Vertue, <span class="italic">Note books</span>, 2.71) which appeared in Alexander Neville's <span class="italic">De furoribus Norfolciensium Ketto duce</span> (1575). Vertue noted further that the original was &#145;done in wood, but very plain &amp; well. to which the name is set. &#147;Richardus Lyne' servus D. Matths. Archp Cant. Sculpsit 1574&#148;&#146;  (ibid., 71&#150;2). Lyne is mentioned by Francis Meres in his <span class="italic">Palladis tamia</span> (1598) as among the leading painters of the time.<br>
<br>L. H. Cust<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Annette Peach </div>
