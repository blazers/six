<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Holden,  George</span>  (1783&#150;1865), <span class="occ">Church of England clergyman and biblical scholar</span>, only son of the Revd George Holden LLD, headmaster of the free grammar school at Horton in Ribblesdale, Yorkshire, was born there in 1783. He was educated at Glasgow University, where he graduated. In 1811 he was presented to the perpetual curacy of the village of Maghull, near Liverpool; living there in seclusion he read and wrote much. He succeeded his father as vicar of Horton in 1821, but resigned that living in 1825, preferring to devote himself to Maghull. He died suddenly at Maghull on 19 March 1865, aged eighty-one. He was not married, and his large library and more than half of his property were left for the benefit of clergy of the diocese of Ripon.<br>
<br>Holden's fifteen works show him to have been a competent Hebraist and an able Christian apologist. His early works include <span class="italic">An Attempt towards an Improved Version of the Proverbs of Solomon</span> (1819), <span class="italic">The Scripture Testimonies to the Divinity of our Lord Jesus Christ</span> (1820), <span class="italic">An Attempt to Illustrate the Book of Ecclesiastes</span> (1822), and <span class="italic">A Dissertation on the Fall of Man</span> (1823). In the 1830s he published several expositions of scripture, and <span class="italic">The Authority of Tradition in Matters of Religion</span> (1838). His later works include <span class="italic">The Anglican Catechist</span> (1855) and <span class="italic">An Essay on the Angels of the Church</span> (1862).<br>
<br>For many years Holden compiled the <span class="italic">Liverpool Tide Tables</span>, which were begun by his grandfather and continued by his father.<br>
<br>C. W. Sutton<span class="italic">, rev. </span> H. C. G. Matthew </div>
