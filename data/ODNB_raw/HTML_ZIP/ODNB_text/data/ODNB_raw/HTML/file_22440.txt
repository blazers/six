<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Pointer,  John</span>  (<em>bap. </em>1668, <em>d.</em> 1754), <span class="occ">antiquary</span>, was born at Alkerton, Oxfordshire, and baptized on 31 March 1668. He was the son of John Pointer (1630/31&#150;1710), rector of Alkerton, and his wife, Elizabeth Hobell (1644?&#150;1709), daughter of John Hobell, a London merchant. He was educated at Banbury grammar school and at Preston Capes School, Northamptonshire, after which he matriculated at Merton College, Oxford, on 24 January 1687. He graduated BA in 1691 and MA in 1694. Ordained deacon on 24 December 1693 and priest on 23 September 1694, he was chaplain of his college from 1693 to 1732. On 27 September 1694 he was instituted rector of Slapton, Northamptonshire.<br>
<br>Pointer produced a variety of antiquarian studies and collections and was known also as a collector of natural and antiquarian objects. His circle of friends included antiquaries such as White Kennett, bishop of Peterborough, William Musgrave, and John Morton. In 1713 he became involved in a lively antiquarian debate with his successful first work, <span class="italic">An Account of a Roman Pavement Lately Found at Stunsfield in Oxford-Shire</span>, in which he attacked and poked fun at the interpretation of the pavement published by the Oxford antiquary Thomas Hearne in 1712. Pointer's conclusions about the iconography of Bacchus, the god depicted on the pavement, were correct and represented the opinion of the majority. Yet he felt the need to defend his work in an advertisement in the <span class="italic">Evening Post</span> of 1720. Hearne dismissed, not without some personal bitterness, most of Pointer's subsequent publications as of little importance. Pointer's critical judgement, however, is still accepted by commentators today.<br>
<br>In 1714 Pointer published his <span class="italic">Chronological History of England</span>, a two-volume historical compilation which was particularly comprehensive in its description of events from 1660 until the death of Queen Anne. Six supplements, each containing the incidents of a year, and the last two published under the name Mr Brockwel, carried it on to the close of July 1720. According to Hearne:<blockquote>John Poynter hath taken his silly Book call'd A Chronological History of Engl. from a Book call'd the Historian's Guide (wch he hath printed verbatim,) &amp; from Sr. Geo. Wharton's Almanacks &amp; other Pieces, &amp; from some silly, trivial Pieces published since. (<span class="italic">Remarks and Collections</span>, 4.401)</blockquote>Pointer also published a variety of collections and guidebooks, among them <span class="italic">Miscellanea in usum juventutis academicae</span> (1718) and <span class="italic">A Rational Account of the Weather</span> (1723; enlarged edn, 1738). In 1724 he returned to the study of Roman Britain with <span class="italic">Britannia Romana, or, Roman Antiquities in Britain, viz., Coins, Camps, and Publick Roads</span>. In 1729 he applied unsuccessfully for the vacant position of keeper of the Ashmolean Museum, thinking of donating his collection of antiquities and curiosities to that institution. In 1732 he was removed from his chaplaincy at Merton for, according to Hearne, committing sodomy. In 1738 Pointer sent Richard Rawlinson a &#145;Vindication&#146; of his grandfather John Pointer, canon of Christ Church  (MS Rawl. J., fol. 4, 224&#150;8), wishing Rawlinson to correct Anthony Wood's aspersions in his planned continuation of Wood's <span class="italic">Athenae Oxonienses</span>.<br>
<br>In 1743 Pointer published <span class="italic">Britannia Triumphans, or, An Historical Account of Some of the Most Signal Naval Victories Obtained by the English over the Spaniards</span>, and finally, in 1749, <span class="italic">Oxoniensis academia, or, The Antiquities and Curiosities of the University of Oxford</span>. Like John Ayliffe's <span class="italic">Ancient and Present State of the University of Oxford</span> (1714), this work was largely derivative, though not without new details of some interest. In it Pointer identified the author of the account of the Bodleian Library in Ayliffe's book as John Hudson, Bodley's librarian. He died, unmarried, on 16 January 1754 in the house of his niece, Mrs Bradborne, of Chesterton in Worfield, Shropshire. He was buried in the chancel of Worfield parish church on 19 January.<br>
<br>Theodor Harmsen </div>
