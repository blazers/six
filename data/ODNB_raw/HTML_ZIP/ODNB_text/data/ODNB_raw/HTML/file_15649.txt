<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Kirk,  John</span>  (1724?&#150;1778?), <span class="occ">medallist</span>, probably became a pupil of the medallist James Anthony Dassier about 1740. It is difficult to be certain of his life because of the profusion of medallists and engravers named John Kirk. From about 1740 until 1778 he produced a large number of medals signed KIRK or I. KIRK. In 1745 he was probably living in St Paul's Churchyard, and in 1745&#150;6 several medals were struck by John Kirk and A. Kirk. The <span class="italic">Gentleman's Magazine</span> reported the death on 19 November 1761 of a &#145;Mr Kirk senior&#146; of that address, which may have been his father or brother. In 1762&#150;3 he received premiums from the Society of Artists and in 1773 and 1775&#150;6 exhibited medals of the royal family and others. Many of his medals commemorate the victories of the War of the Austrian Succession and the Seven Years' War, though others are for royalty, politicians, and civic associations such as the British Fishery Society (1751).<br>
<br>Although some authorities list Kirk's death as 27 November 1776, this appears unlikely, as he probably produced several medals in 1778, including a commemoration of the earl of Chatham. He may have been the John Kirk, &#145;engraver&#146;, of St James's, who made his will on 13 November 1778, and which was proved by his widow, Catherine, on 5 December 1778. A further confusion may arise from the report in the <span class="italic">Gentleman's Magazine</span> on 31 March 1791 of the death of &#145;Mr Kirk, seal-engraver, in St Paul's Churchyard&#146;.<br>
<br>Stuart Handley </div>
