<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Hilton,  John</span>  (1599&#150;1657), <span class="occ">organist and composer</span>, was probably born in Cambridge, the son of John Hilton, composer and organist of Trinity College, Cambridge, from 1594 to 1607. Having according to his own account studied music for ten years, he graduated MusB from Trinity on 1 July 1626 and published <span class="italic">Ayres or Fa Las for Three Voyces</span> the following year. On 15 June 1627 he married Frances Trapp at St Margaret's, Westminster, and in 1628 was appointed organist and parish clerk there, receiving for the former office a salary of &pound;6 13<i>s</i>. 4<i>d</i>., or 10 marks a year.<br>
<br>In addition to the <span class="italic">Ayres or Fa Las</span> Hilton's works include songs, dialogues, and catches, as well as a certain amount of church music which is hard to disentangle from his father's. There is also an elegy on the death of his friend William Lawes (&#145;Bound by the near conjunction of our souls&#146;), published in Henry Lawes's <span class="italic">Choice Psalmes</span> (1648), and some consort music, including fourteen three-part fantasias. Hilton is best known as the compiler of <span class="italic">Catch that Catch Can</span>, a collection of rounds and catches published by John Playford in 1652 and subsequently enlarged in 1658 and 1663. It contains twelve canons and thirty catches by him together with similar compositions by numerous other composers. Among those by Hilton which have remained popular over the years are &#145;Come follow, follow&#146; and &#145;Turn Amaryllis to thy swain&#146;. Most of his songs and dialogues survive in manuscript: the dialogues, especially those on the judgment of Paris (&#145;Rise princely shepherd&#146;), the judgment of Solomon (&#145;When Israel's sweet singer slept&#146;), and the temptation of Job (&#145;Amongst my children dares the fiend appear&#146;), are among the most interesting manifestations of this musico-dramatic form in England. The solo songs, which include settings of Donne's &#145;Hymne to God the Father&#146; (&#145;Wilt thou forgive the sin where I begun?&#146;) and Wotton's &#145;On his Mistress the Empress of Bohemia&#146; (&#145;You meaner beauties of the night&#146;), are, for the time, somewhat old-fashioned in style but none the less attractive. In contrast to the seriousness of the songs, the consort music shows a lightness of style that has more in common with the <span class="italic">Ayres and Fa Las</span>. Hilton died in 1657 and was buried in St Margaret's, Westminster, on 21 March.<br>
<br>Ian Spink </div>
