<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Bond,  George</span>  (1750&#150;1796), <span class="occ">lawyer</span>, was the younger son of George Bond (<i>d</i>. 1792), of Farnham, Surrey. His mother was a daughter of Sir Thomas Chitty (<i>d</i>. 1792). He and his elder brother, Thomas, were both called to the bar on 12 February 1779 at the Middle Temple.<br>
<br>Bond became famous as a counsel in criminal cases, and obtained a large practice at the Surrey sessions. He often played on his local associations, referring to &#145;my native county&#146;, which rarely failed to sway Surrey juries, who were enthralled by his coarse and vulgar humour. A common verdict at the Surrey sessions was: &#145;We finds for Serjeant Bond and costs&#146;  (Polson, 207). He was made a serjeant in 1786, and a king's serjeant in 1795. He married Kitty Cooke on 1 April 1793 at St George's, Hanover Square, London. His wife was a granddaughter of one of the protonotaries of the common pleas and they had two children. Bond died on 19 March 1796, aged only forty-six, from a rheumatic fever. He was survived by his wife.<br>
<br>J. M. Rigg<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Robert Brown </div>
