<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Neville <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Manners]</span>,  Frances</span>, Lady Bergavenny  (<em>d.</em> 1576), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was the third daughter of the ten children of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/17963/', "Thomas Manners, first earl of Rutland (d. 1543)")</script>, and his second wife, Eleanor Paston (<i>d</i>. 1550). By 1554 she had married Henry Neville, fourth Lord Bergavenny or Abergavenny (<i>d</i>. 1587), and they had one daughter, Mary (1554&#150;1626), who married Sir Thomas Fane and unsuccessfully claimed the barony on the death of her father. Collinson cites a letter by the puritan preacher Edward Dering attacking Lord Abergavenny for &#145;ungodly talke&#146;, &#145;unclennes&#146;, and &#145;riot&#146;  (Collinson, 301). Lady Abergavenny wrote pious works. Her <span class="italic">Praiers</span> in prose and verse were later published in the second lamp of <span class="italic">The Monument of Matrones</span> (1582), Thomas Bentley's anthology of protestant women writers' prayers. In a deathbed dedication of her work to her daughter, she calls her work &#145;a jewell of health for the soule, and a perfect path to paradise, verie profitable to be used of everie faithfull Christian man and woman&#146;  (Aburgavennie, 139). Her collection includes sixty-seven pages of prose prayers for private use and public worship linked to varied occasions and times of day; a metrical prayer against vices; a five-page acrostic prayer based on Mary Fane's name; and a concluding acrostic prayer based on her own name. She died in 1576.<br>
<br>Elaine V. Beilin </div>
