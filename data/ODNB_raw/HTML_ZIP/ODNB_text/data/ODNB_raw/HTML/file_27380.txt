<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Thorpe,  John</span>  (1715/16&#150;1792), <span class="occ">antiquary</span>, was probably born in Rochester, Kent, the only son of <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/27379/', "John Thorpe (1682&#150;1750)")</script>, physician and antiquary, and Elizabeth Woodhouse. He was educated nearby at Luddesdown under the Revd Samuel Thornton, and at the age of sixteen matriculated from University College, Oxford, on 22 March 1732, graduating BA in 1735 and MA in 1738. In 1746 he married Catharina (<i>d</i>. 1789), daughter of Laurence Holker, physician, of Gravesend, and Katharine Allen; they had two daughters, Catharine and Ethelinda.<br>
<br>Thorpe commenced the study of medicine but abandoned it, and, like his father, devoted himself to antiquarian research. In 1755 he was elected a fellow of the Society of Antiquaries. In 1769 he published, with the assistance of John Baynard of the Navy Office, his father's <span class="italic">Registrum Roffense</span>, a collection of materials relating to Rochester. In 1788 he supplemented the work by publishing the <span class="italic">Custumale Roffense</span> from the original manuscript, adding other memorials from the cathedral. For this purpose he used his father's papers and, like other antiquaries, undertook visits to Darenth church (1768), Scotgrove chantry (1769), Higham Priory (1776), the lost village of Merston (1776), Horsted (1777), Manor Farm, Ruxley, and Shorne. He wrote the <span class="italic">Custumale Roffense</span> while residing at Highstreet House, Bexley, from where, after the death of his first wife on 10 January 1789, he moved to Richmond Green, Surrey, and then to Chippenham, Wiltshire. Catharina's grave is in the churchyard adjoining Highstreet House. On 6 July 1790 he married his second wife, Mrs Holland, his housekeeper and &#145;the widow of an old collegiate acquaintance&#146;. Thorpe also contributed &#145;Illustrations of several antiquities in Kent which have hitherto remained undescribed&#146; to the first volume of the <span class="italic">Bibliotheca Topographica Britannica</span>. A letter from him to Andrew Colt&eacute;e Ducarel in which he argued, contrary to Daines Barrington, that the cherry is indigenous to England, was published in the <span class="italic">Philosophical Transactions</span> of the Royal Society (1771, 152). He frequently made contributions on antiquarian subjects to the <span class="italic">Gentleman's Magazine</span>. He was a friend of the great Kent historian Edward Hasted, with whom he corresponded and to whom he lent family manuscripts, as Hasted recorded in &#145;The following Notes, Coats of Arms, Drawings of Monuments and churches were copied by me from a curious MS in the possession of John Thorp [<i>sic</i>] of Bexley, Esqr. in the year 1768&#146;  (BL, Hasted MSS, Add. MS 5479 D.IV). As a great Kent antiquary Thorpe followed in the footsteps of his father, being prominent among many who rendered advice and assistance to Hasted. Thorpe died at Chippenham on 2 August 1792 and was buried in the churchyard of the neighbouring village of Hardenhuish.<br>
<br>E. I. Carlyle<span class="italic">, rev. </span> John Whyman </div>
