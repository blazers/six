<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Balfour,  Francis</span>  (<em>d.</em> in or after 1816), <span class="occ">surgeon and orientalist</span>, was born in Edinburgh and took his MD degree there in 1767. He entered the East India Company's service in Bengal as assistant surgeon on 3 July 1769, and was appointed full surgeon on 10 August 1777.<br>
<br>Balfour was a close acquaintance of Warren Hastings and dedicated a book&#151;<span class="italic">The Forms of Herkern</span>&#151;to him in 1781. In 1783, while at Benares, he corresponded frequently with Hastings in an abortive attempt to disclose a plot between the resident of Benares, Francis Fowke, and Raja Cheyte Sing, which he claimed to have discovered.<br>
<br>Balfour devoted much time to oriental studies. <span class="italic">The Forms of Herkern</span>, translated into English by Balfour, was published in Calcutta in 1781 and in London in 1804. The work comprised the endeavours of a state letter-writer in Persian; a vocabulary is given by Balfour at the end and the book is by far the most valuable of his publications. Balfour was one of the earliest members of the Bengal Asiatic Society, founded in 1784, and he contributed two papers to its <span class="italic">Transactions</span> (in 1790 and 1805) on oriental languages and literature, and a third (in 1795) on the barometer. Of his medical works one of the most remarkable was <span class="italic">A Treatise on Sol-Lunar Influence in Fevers</span> (1784), which went to several editions and saw a German translation. In it Balfour expounded his favourite theory, that fevers are under the direct influence of the moon, and reach their critical stage with the full moon, a theme he returned to in a paper of 1805. Contemporaries regarded his views as rather extreme.<br>
<br>Balfour retired from the East India Company's service on 16 September 1807 and returned to Edinburgh. He is thought to have died in or after 1816.<br>
<br>Sidney Lee<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Elizabeth Baigent </div>
