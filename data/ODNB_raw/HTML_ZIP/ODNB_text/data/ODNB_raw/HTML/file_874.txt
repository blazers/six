<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Atterbury,  Luffman</span>  (1740&#150;1796), <span class="occ">composer and builder</span>, was probably the son of Abraham and Elizabeth Atterbury who was baptized at St Sepulchre, London, on 5 January 1740. A carpenter and builder by trade, based at Turn Again Lane, Fleet Market, London, he acquired considerable proficiency in music, and when he was left well off by the death of his father he retired to Teddington to pursue this interest. Between 1778 and 1780 he obtained five prizes for catches and glees from the Catch Club. Many of his works appeared in E. T. Warren's <span class="italic">Collection of Catches, Canons and Glees</span> (1763&#150;94). &#145;Come let us all a-Maying&#146; and &#145;Hot cross buns&#146; enjoyed long popularity. He was appointed a musician-in-ordinary to George III, and was a performing member of the Madrigal Society. His oratorio <span class="italic">Goliath</span> was performed at the Haymarket Theatre on 5 May 1773 and again at West Wycombe church on 13 August 1775 on the occasion of the burial of the heart of the satirist Paul Whitehead in the mausoleum, but it was not a success.<br>
<br>In 1780 Atterbury was employed by Francis Dashwood, eleventh Baron Le Despencer, to build the rectory (later Mere House) at Mereworth, Kent, from the design of Nicholas Revett. This was Atterbury's only recorded new building. He also worked for Le Despencer in 1780 at Hall Place, Bexley, Kent.<br>
<br>Atterbury sang in the chorus at the Handel commemoration in 1784, and in 1790 was one of the original members of the glee club at the Newcastle Coffee House, Castle Street, Strand. He published <span class="italic">A Collection of Twelve Glees</span> in 1790 and on 25 September that year he married Elizabeth Ancell at St Martin-in-the-Fields. He was obliged, by improvidence, to move from Teddington to Marsham Street, Westminster, and to give concerts to improve his finances. In the middle of one of these concerts he died, on 11 June 1796.<br>
<br>W. B. Squire<span class="italic">, rev. </span> K. D. Reynolds </div>
