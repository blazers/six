<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Watt,  James Henry</span>  (1799&#150;1867), <span class="occ">line engraver</span>, born in London, was probably the elder brother of the portrait engraver William Henry Watt (<i>b</i>. 1804). He was educated at Mensall's academy, Kentish Town, and at the age of eighteen became a pupil of the engraver Charles Heath (1785&#150;1848). Watt engraved many beautiful vignettes for <span class="italic">The Amulet</span>, the <span class="italic">Literary Souvenir</span>, and similar productions from designs by Robert Smirke, Richard Westall, and others, as well as several plates for the official publication <span class="italic">Ancient Marbles in the British Museum</span>. His larger works were mostly executed in pure line on copper, and include <span class="italic">The Flitch of Bacon</span>, after T. Stothard (1832), <span class="italic">May Day in the Time of Queen Elizabeth</span>, after C. R. Leslie (1836), <span class="italic">Highland Drovers' Departure</span> and <span class="italic">Courtyard in the Olden Time</span>, both after E. Landseer, and <span class="italic">Christ Blessing Little Children</span>, after C. L. Eastlake (1859). The last named was exhibited at the Royal Academy in 1859. Watt suffered ill health for a number of years, and as a consequence of that and domestic problems he was said to have been something of a recluse. He died in London on 18 May 1867.<br>
<br>F. M. O'Donoghue<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Dennis Harrington </div>
