<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Wallace <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Maxwell]</span>,  Eglantine</span>, styled Lady Wallace  (<em>d.</em> 1803), <span class="occ">writer</span>, was the youngest daughter of Sir William Maxwell, third baronet (<i>d</i>. 1771), of Monreith, Wigtownshire, and his wife, Magdalene Blair. Her sister was <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/11059/', "Jane Gordon, duchess of Gordon (1748/9&#150;1812)")</script>. A boisterous hoyden in her youth, and a woman of violent temper in her maturer years, Eglantine Maxwell was married on 4 September 1770 to Thomas Dunlop, son of John Dunlop of Dunlop, and Frances Anna, daughter and heir of Sir Thomas Wallace, fourth and last baronet (1702&#150;1770), of Craigie. On his grandfather's death Dunlop inherited Craigie, took the name of Wallace, and assumed the style of fifth baronet; but the property was deeply indebted, and in 1783 he was obliged to sell all that remained of Craigie. His wife obtained a legal separation in 1778, on the ground, it is said, of her husband's cruelty, but the quarrel may have been due to pecuniary embarrassment.<br>
<br>Lady Wallace was herself a little later summoned for assaulting a woman&#151;apparently a humble companion&#151;and was directed by the magistrate to compound the matter. She left Edinburgh, and seems to have settled in London, but when her play <span class="italic">The Whim: a Comedy</span> (1795) was banned from the stage by the licenser, she left England in disgust. In October 1789 she was arrested at Paris as a British agent, and narrowly escaped with her life. In 1792 she was in Brussels. There she contracted a friendship with General Charles Fran&ccedil;ois Dumouriez, whom in 1793 she entertained in London, where she seems to have been well received in society.<br>
<br>Lady Wallace was author of <span class="italic">Letter to a Friend, with a Poem called The Ghost of Werter</span> (1787), <span class="italic">Diamond Cut Diamond: a Comedy</span> (from the French, 1787), and <span class="italic">The Ton: a Comedy</span> (1788), which was produced at Covent Garden on 8 April 1788 with a good cast, but was a failure. Other works include <span class="italic">The Conduct of the King of Prussia and General Dumouriez</span> (1793) and <span class="italic">An Address to the People on Peace and Reform</span> (1798).<br>
<br>Lady Wallace died at Munich on 28 March 1803, leaving two sons, the elder of whom was <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/28537/', "General Sir John Alexander Dunlop Agnew Wallace (1774/5&#150;1857)")</script>.<br>
<br>J. K. Laughton<span class="italic">, rev. </span> Rebecca Mills </div>
