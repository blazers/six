<div class="para">
<span class="headword">Mansfield <span class="st"> [<em>n&eacute;e </em>Fellowes]</span>,  Margaret</span>, Lady Sandhurst  (<em>bap. </em>1827, <em>d.</em> 1892), <span class="occ">women's suffragist and spiritualist</span>, was the youngest of the seven children of Robert Fellowes (1779&#150;1869) of Shotesham Park, Norfolk, and his second wife, Jane Louisa Sheldon (<i>d</i>. 1871); his father also had a child from his first marriage. She was baptized at Tredington, Worcestershire, on 14 November 1827. On 2 November 1854 she married <script>writeSeealsoLink('../view/article/17996/', "Lieutenant-Colonel William Rose Mansfield (1819&#150;1876)")</script>, who was knighted in 1858 and created Baron Sandhurst in 1871; they spent eleven years in India and had four sons and a daughter.<br>
<br>After her husband's death in 1876 Lady Sandhurst became increasingly involved in both spiritualism and Liberal politics, which interests came together in 1884 when she played host to a private s&eacute;ance for the benefit of the prime minister, W. E. Gladstone. Gladstone commented that she had &#145;explained to me the spirit &amp; purpose, wholly Christian &amp; biblical, of her proceedings in this matter&#146;  (Gladstone, <span class="italic">Diaries</span>, 18 Nov 1884). From 1886 she was active in the Women's Liberal Federation, and in January 1889 she was the first woman elected to the London County Council, as member for Brixton. She was, however, unseated on petition in May, when the objection of her Conservative opponent, Beresford Hope (who maintained that, though empowered to vote in municipal elections, women were ineligible to serve as councillors), was upheld. In the same year she was given the freedom of the city of Dublin, in recognition of her sympathy towards Ireland. She died suddenly in London on 7 January 1892, at her home, 29 Park Road, Regent's Park, and was buried with her husband at Digswell, Hertfordshire.<br>
<br>K. D. Reynolds </div>
